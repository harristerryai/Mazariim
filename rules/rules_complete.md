## How to Play

The core of this system is simple and resolves every situation the same way:

1. The player tells the GM what action they want to take, how they plan to do it, and any dialogue they wish to deliver.
2. The GM determines if the action is routine (automatic success) or if there is a chance of failure. If failure is possible, the GM identifies which stat is relevant — Might, Speed, or Intellect — and sets a difficulty from 1 to 10.
3. The player and GM determine what modifiers apply. Ranks from skills, assets, and Effort all raise the player's effective result. Inabilities and adverse circumstances lower it. In this system, ranks always count upward — raising the player's effective result to meet the difficulty — rather than lowering the difficulty number.
4. If the player's total ranks equal or exceed the difficulty, the task succeeds automatically with no roll needed. If the task still isn't routine, the remaining difficulty (after subtracting ranks) determines the target number, which is always three times the remaining difficulty.
5. The player rolls a d20. If the result meets or exceeds the target number, the task succeeds.

That procedure covers everything — swinging a sword at an ogre, identifying a magical rune, calming a raging barbarian, climbing a treacherous cliff, or outwitting a dragon.

### The Player Always Rolls

The GM never rolls dice. If a PC attacks a creature, the player rolls to hit. If a creature attacks a PC, the player rolls to dodge or resist. If a rockslide falls on the PCs, the players roll to get out of the way. Since the PC always rolls whether attacking or defending, modifiers to either side affect the player's roll. A PC using a wall for cover gains a rank on defense rolls. If the foe uses that wall for cover, the player's attack roll loses a rank.

## Key Concepts

**Action:** Anything a character does that is significant — attacking, defending, leaping a chasm, activating a special ability, and so on. Each character takes one action per round on their turn.

**Character:** Any creature in the game, whether a player character (PC) or a nonplayer character (NPC) run by the game master (GM).

**Level:** A measure of the strength, difficulty, power, or challenge of something in the game. Everything has a level — creatures, objects, locks, traps, and tasks. Levels range from 1 to 10 for most things, though exceptionally powerful creatures can exceed this (see GM Reference: Creature Rules). In standard Cypher System, player characters advance through 6 tiers. In this system, tiers are replaced by 10 character levels. Whenever any source material references a "tier," read it as a "level" instead and consult the Character Progression Charts for the appropriate values.

**Difficulty:** How hard a task is, rated from 1 (simple) to 10 (impossible for most). The difficulty determines the target number — always three times the difficulty. Difficulty often equates directly with level: attacking a level 3 creature or picking a level 3 lock is typically a difficulty 3 task.

**Rank:** The term for all modifiers that work in the player's favor on a task. Ranks from skills, assets, and Effort stack to raise the player's effective result toward the difficulty. We always count upward — if a character has 3 total ranks against a difficulty 5 task, the effective remaining difficulty is 2 (target number 6).

**Stat:** One of three defining characteristics: Might, Speed, or Intellect. Each stat has a Pool and an Edge.

**Pool:** The basic measurement of a stat, representing raw innate ability. Pool points are spent to use Effort and activate special abilities. Taking damage reduces Pool points. If a Pool reaches 0, the character moves down the damage track. Rest and recovery rolls can restore lost points.

**Edge:** Reduces the cost of spending Pool points from the associated stat. Might Edge reduces Might costs, Speed Edge reduces Speed costs, Intellect Edge reduces Intellect costs. Edge from one stat never applies to another stat. When you must spend points from a stat Pool, subtract your Edge for that stat from the total cost. If Edge reduces the cost to 0, the expenditure is free. Edge applies only once per action, regardless of how many separate costs that action involves.

**Effort:** You can apply Effort to gain additional ranks on a task by spending Pool points. Each level of Effort costs 2 points from the relevant stat Pool and provides one rank. You can apply up to your Effort score in levels per task. Subtract your Edge from the total cost. You must decide to apply Effort before you roll. You cannot apply Effort while surprised. Note that some character progression charts may modify the cost per level of Effort (e.g., the Gritty/OSR chart increases the cost to 3 per level), and different charts set different maximum Effort scores at each character level.

**Inability:** A pointed weakness. Having an inability in a task lowers your effective result by one rank for that task. If you become trained in a task you have an inability in, they cancel out and you are practiced.

**Practiced:** The default skill level. No modification to the task. Your type determines what weapons and armor you're practiced with; if you aren't practiced with a type of weapon, you have an inability with it.

**Trained:** One rank of proficiency in a skill. Available at any character level.

**Specialized:** Two ranks of proficiency in a skill. Available at character Levels 4–6 and above.

**Expert:** Three ranks of proficiency — the maximum skill level. Available at character Levels 7–10 through specific abilities or extraordinary circumstances determined by the GM.

**Asset:** A beneficial circumstance, piece of equipment, or ally assistance that provides a rank toward a task. The standard maximum is two ranks from assets on any single task, but this limit can vary by progression chart — some charts allow only 1 asset rank, while others allow up to 3. Check your campaign's chosen progression chart.

**Enabler:** A special ability that is not an action unto itself. It either functions constantly (such as being able to wear heavy armor) or happens as part of another action (such as adding damage to an attack). Enablers say "Enabler" at the end of their description.

**Round:** About five to ten seconds. Roughly ten rounds in a minute. Each character gets one turn per round.

**Turn:** The part of the round when a character takes its action.

**Task:** Any action a PC attempts that requires a roll. The GM determines the difficulty.

**Roll:** A d20 roll made to determine success. When the text says "a roll," it always means a d20.

## Taking Action

Each character gets one turn per round. On your turn, you take one action. All actions relate to one of the three stats. Many actions require a die roll.

Every action performs a task, and every task has a difficulty that determines the target number. Most routine tasks have a difficulty of 0 and succeed automatically. Tasks that are difficult or made difficult by circumstances require a roll.

Some actions require a minimum expenditure of Pool points. If a character cannot spend the minimum points needed, they automatically fail.

### Determining Task Stat

Every task relates to one stat. Physical activities requiring strength, power, or endurance are Might tasks. Physical activities requiring agility, coordination, or reflexes are Speed tasks. Mental activities requiring knowledge, willpower, or social acumen are Intellect tasks. The task's stat determines which Pool you spend Effort from and which Edge applies.

---

## Determining Task Difficulty

The GM sets a task's difficulty on a scale from 1 to 10. The target number is always three times the difficulty. A character's level does not determine task difficulty — the GM rates the task on its own merits.

| Difficulty | Description | Target Number | Base Success Rate |
|------------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 0 | Routine | 0 | Automatic |
| 1 | Simple | 3 | 90% |
| 2 | Standard | 6 | 75% |
| 3 | Demanding | 9 | 60% |
| 4 | Difficult | 12 | 45% |
| 5 | Challenging | 15 | 30% |
| 6 | Intimidating | 18 | 15% |
| 7 | Formidable | 21 | Impossible without ranks |
| 8 | Heroic | 24 | Impossible without ranks |
| 9 | Immortal | 27 | Impossible without ranks |
| 10 | Impossible | 30 | Impossible without ranks |

Tasks of difficulty 7 or higher cannot be accomplished with a d20 alone — the character must have ranks from skills, assets, or Effort. Reducing the effective difficulty to 0 means the task is routine and no roll is needed.

---

## Modifying the Difficulty

After the GM sets the difficulty, the player determines what ranks their character brings to the task. There are three sources of ranks: skills, assets, and Effort. Total ranks are subtracted from the difficulty. If this reduces the difficulty to 0, the task succeeds automatically. Otherwise, the remaining difficulty determines the target number.

### Skills

Skills provide ranks toward related tasks:

| Skill Level | Ranks Provided | Availability |
|-------------|----------------|--------------|
| Inability | −1 rank | Assigned by type, descriptor, or circumstances |
| Practiced | 0 ranks | Default |
| Trained | +1 rank | Available at any level |
| Specialized | +2 ranks | Available at Levels 4–6+ |
| Expert | +3 ranks | Available at Levels 7–10 through specific abilities |

Skills can never provide more than three ranks. Only skills gained through character type abilities or other specific instances allow proficiency in attack or defense tasks.

#### Fantasy Skill List

Fantasy campaigns use the following uniform skill list: Acrobatics, Appraise, Animal Handling, Athletics, Craft, Disable Device, Deception, Insight, Intimidation, Investigation, Knowledge (Arcana, History, Nature, Religion), Language, Navigation, Perception, Performance, Persuasion, Profession, Research, Sleight of Hand, Spellcraft, Stealth, Survival, Tools, and Vehicle.

#### Untrained Skills

For skills requiring specific knowledge (such as Spellcraft or Research), the maximum level of success an untrained PC can achieve is difficulty 3.

### Assets

An asset is anything that helps — a good tool, favorable conditions, or assistance from an ally. Each asset provides one rank. The standard maximum is two ranks from assets on a single task, but the active progression chart may raise or lower this limit (the Gritty/OSR chart limits assets to 1 rank; the Heroic chart allows up to 3).

### Effort

Each level of Effort costs 2 points from the relevant stat Pool and provides one rank toward the task. A character can apply up to their Effort score in levels per task. After totaling all Pool costs for the action (Effort, ability costs, initial costs), subtract Edge once.

The maximum Effort a character can apply is set by their progression chart at each level. Some charts also modify the per-level cost — the Gritty/OSR chart increases the cost to 3 points per level of Effort.

A beginning Level 1 character typically has an Effort of 1. More experienced characters have higher scores.

**Free Level of Effort:** Some abilities grant a free level of Effort (usually requiring you to apply at least one paid level first). This free level can exceed your Effort limit but not the six-rank maximum from Effort on any task.

**Effort and Surprise:** You cannot apply Effort while surprised. If attacked before you have acted and you were unaware of the threat, you cannot spend Pool points on Effort for defense.

### Effort and Damage

Instead of applying Effort for ranks on an attack roll, you can apply it to increase damage. Each level of Effort on damage adds 3 points to a single-target attack or 2 points to an area attack (applied to all targets). Even targets who resist an area attack still take 1 point of damage.

### Multiple Uses of Effort and Edge

If your Effort score is 2 or higher, you can split Effort across aspects of a single action (e.g., one level on the attack roll, one on damage). Total Effort on any action cannot exceed your Effort score. Edge applies once per action, regardless of how many costs are involved.

## Rolling the Die

If a task isn't routine after applying ranks, the player rolls a d20. The target number is three times the remaining difficulty. Roll equal to or higher to succeed.

If a character's modifiers add up to +3, treat them as one rank instead of adding to the roll. If there is no possibility of success (not even a natural 20 can reach the target number), no roll is made.

### Special Rolls

| d20 Result | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| **1** | Something goes wrong — the GM introduces a complication. Edge does not apply to any Pool costs for this action. This cost cannot reduce any Pool below 1. |
| **17** | +1 damage (in combat) |
| **18** | +2 damage (in combat) |
| **19** | **Minor effect** OR +3 damage. If not in combat, minor effect only. |
| **20** | **Major effect** OR +4 damage. Pool points spent on the action are refunded. For spells, the spell costs no Pool points OR gains an additional beneficial effect (player's choice). |

**Minor Effects** are subtle but useful benefits: knock the foe back, distract them (hindering their next action), strike a specific body part, or move past them. If nothing suggests itself, take the +3 damage.

**Major Effects** are decisive outcomes: knock the foe prone, disarm them, stun them for one round (they lose their next action), or impair them (all tasks hindered for the rest of the encounter). When nothing else fits, the GM can grant an additional action that round.

Players can always suggest their own effects; both player and GM must agree.

---

## Retrying a Task After Failure

A character who fails a task can try again, but must apply at least one level of Effort on the retry. A retry is a new action and takes the same amount of time as the first attempt. The GM may rule that some tasks cannot be retried — a failed negotiation, for example, might not allow a second chance. This rule does not apply to combat attacks, as each round is a new situation.

---

## Initial Cost

The GM can assign a Pool point cost just for attempting a particularly taxing task. This initial cost is in addition to any Effort spent and does not provide ranks toward the task. Edge reduces the total cost as normal, but since Edge applies only once per action, it cannot reduce both the initial cost and Effort costs separately.

## Distance and Measurement

Distances are measured in feet during combat. The following table converts named ranges to measured distances:

| Named Range | Distance | Grid Squares (5 ft each) |
|-------------|----------|--------------------------|
| Immediate | Up to 10 feet (3 m) | 2 squares |
| Short | Up to 50 feet (15 m) | 10 squares |
| Long | Up to 100 feet (30 m) | 20 squares |
| Very Long | Up to 500 feet (150 m) | 100 squares |

Beyond very long range, use real-world distances (1,000 feet, 1 mile, etc.).

When using a battle map, each square is 5 feet. Spell ranges, movement distances, and area effects are measured in feet during combat. Outside of combat, the GM can use named ranges or general descriptions.

All melee weapons have immediate range (10 ft). Ranged weapons specify their own ranges.

## Timekeeping

Keep time using minutes, hours, days, and weeks. Precision is rarely needed. In an encounter, "one minute" essentially means "the rest of the encounter." "Ten minutes" covers a short exploration, a conversation, or a brief rest.

**Round:** About five to ten seconds. Roughly ten rounds per minute.

**Common Time References:**

| Activity | Time Required |
|----------|---------------|
| Walking a mile over easy terrain | ~15 minutes |
| Walking a mile over rough terrain | ~30 minutes |
| Walking a mile over difficult terrain | ~45 minutes |
| Moving between city locations | ~15 minutes |
| Sneaking into a guarded location | ~15 minutes |
| Observing a new location for details | ~15 minutes |
| In-depth discussion | ~10 minutes |
| Resting after strenuous activity | ~10 minutes |
| Quick meal and rest | ~30 minutes |
| Making or breaking camp | ~30 minutes |
| Shopping for supplies | ~1 hour |
| Meeting with an important contact | ~30 minutes |
| Referencing a book or text | ~30 minutes |
| Searching a room thoroughly | 30 minutes to 1 hour |
| Identifying a cypher | 15–30 minutes |
| Identifying an artifact | 15 minutes to 3 hours |
| Repairing a device (with tools) | 1 hour to 1 day |
| Building a device (with tools) | 1 day to 1 week |

## Encounters, Rounds, and Initiative

An encounter is a significant scene — a fight, a chase, a negotiation, or an environmental challenge. Encounters are composed of rounds. Each round, every character gets one turn to take one action.

### Initiative

At the start of a combat encounter, each player makes a Speed roll called an initiative roll. The target number is three times the highest NPC level in the encounter. Characters who roll equal to or higher act before the NPCs. Characters who roll lower act after. The order among PCs is decided by the players.

NPCs use their level as their base initiative value. If an NPC spends Effort (which creatures can do in this system — see GM Reference: Creature Rules), the GM may increase the NPC's effective initiative.

Initiative order remains the same throughout the encounter unless circumstances change drastically, at which point the GM may call for new rolls.

Anything that lasts "for one round" ends at the start of that character's next turn.

### Surprise

If one side is unaware of the other, the aware side gets a free round of actions before initiative is rolled. During the surprise round, surprised characters cannot take actions, cannot apply Effort to defense rolls, and attacks against them gain one rank. After the surprise round, initiative proceeds normally.

## Actions

On your turn you take one action. An action is a single significant thing you can do in five to ten seconds. Drawing a sword and attacking is one action. Opening a door and attacking a guard beyond it are two actions.

You can take a 5-foot step before or after your action without it counting as a separate action and without provoking attacks of opportunity. This represents a small tactical adjustment.

### Action: Attack

An attack is anything you do to someone that they don't want. Slashing with a sword, blasting with a spell, grappling, or mentally dominating a creature are all attacks. The player rolls against the target's level (difficulty = target's level, target number = level × 3).

Attacks are melee (within 10 feet) or ranged (at a distance):

| Attack Type | Task Stat | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Melee weapon | Might or Speed (player's choice) | Must be within 10 ft |
| Ranged weapon | Speed | Range varies by weapon |
| Special ability | Usually the stat of the ability's cost | Touch abilities are melee |

Weapons deal fixed base damage:

| Weapon Category | Base Damage | Notes |
|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| Light | 2 points | Attack gains one rank; one-handed |
| Medium | 4 points | One-handed |
| Heavy | 6 points | Two hands required |

Damage increases from special rolls, Effort, and abilities.

If a touch-range special ability misses, the power is not wasted. You can try again on subsequent rounds as your action.

### Activate a Special Ability

Many special abilities require an action to use (marked "Action" in their description). Enablers function passively or as part of another action and don't require a separate action.

Activating an ability costs Pool points from the relevant stat, reduced by Edge. Costs with a "+" (e.g., "2+ Intellect points") can be increased for greater effect as described in the ability. You can end your own abilities at any time.

### Action: Move

**5-Foot Step:** Move 5 feet before or after your action. Does not count as an action. Does not provoke attacks of opportunity.

**Move a Short Distance (up to 50 ft):** Your action for the turn. Routine under normal circumstances — no roll required.

**Move a Short Distance and Act:** Move up to 50 feet and take another physical action (like an attack). Requires a difficulty 4 Speed task. On failure, you move but can't take the other action.

**Move a Long Distance (up to 100 ft):** Your entire action. Requires a difficulty 4 Speed task.

**Movement Modifiers:** Difficult terrain, obstacles, and adverse conditions hinder movement. Movement-related skills provide ranks.

### Action: Wait

Declare a trigger. If it occurs, you act first (unless acting first is illogical). If it doesn't occur, you lose your action for the round. Useful against enemies that pop out of cover or approach a guarded position.

### Action: Defend

Defending is reactive — the GM assigns it when you're attacked or subjected to a harmful effect. It doesn't count against your action on your own turn, and you can use Effort on it.

**Might Defense:** Resist poison, disease, crushing force, and effects overcome by endurance.

**Speed Defense:** Dodge attacks and escape physical danger. The most common defense.

**Intellect Defense:** Resist mental attacks, charm, fear, and mind-affecting effects.

Failing usually means taking damage. Armor and special defenses can reduce it.

### Do Something Else

Characters can attempt anything reasonable. The GM sets the stat, difficulty, and parameters. Common actions:

**Climbing:** Might or Speed task. Difficulty based on the surface. Success allows climbing up to 50 feet as an action. Failure means no progress or a fall (GM's discretion).

**Cooperative Actions:**

- *Helping:* Use your action to help an ally. Provide one asset rank, or two if you're trained or specialized in the task. Can't help if you have an inability.
- *Distract:* Successful action against a foe hinders its attacks for one round.
- *Guard:* Give up attacks to gain a rank on all defense rolls. Can attempt an eased Speed task to block an NPC from getting past you.
- *Draw the Attack:* Draw an NPC's attack to yourself. Usually automatic, but may require an Intellect task against smart foes. Your defenses are hindered by two ranks.
- *Take the Attack:* Throw yourself in front of a successful attack on an ally. Automatically hit, plus 1 extra damage. Once per round.

**Crafting, Building, and Repairing:** GM sets difficulty based on complexity. Requires tools and materials. May take extended time. See Equipment & Economy and Magical Items for crafting magical items.

**Healing:** Once per day per target. Intellect task with difficulty equal to points restored. On success, target recovers that many points to a Pool of their choice.

**Interacting with Creatures:** Social actions (persuasion, deception, intimidation) are Intellect tasks against the NPC's level, modified by disposition.

**Jumping:** Standing jump of ~4 feet across or ~1.5 feet high is difficulty 4 Speed. Each additional rank adds ~2 feet horizontal or ~1 foot vertical. Running jump provides an asset.

**Looking or Listening:** Intellect-based perception tasks. Thorough searching takes at least a minute.

**Moving a Heavy Object:** Might task; difficulty based on weight and bulk.

**Operating or Disabling a Device, or Picking a Lock:** Speed or Intellect task; difficulty equals the device or lock's level.

**Riding or Piloting:** Routine travel needs no roll. Complex maneuvers are Speed tasks.

**Sneaking:** Speed task. Difficulty is usually the level of the observer(s).

**Swimming:** Calm water is routine. Rough conditions are Might tasks.

**Understanding, Identifying, or Remembering:** Intellect tasks. Difficulty based on obscurity.

**Vehicular Movement and Combat:** Routine travel needs no roll. Maneuvers, chases, and vehicular combat are Speed or Intellect tasks.

## Attack Modifiers and Special Situations

The following modify attack and defense tasks. Each typically provides or removes one rank. The GM has final say.

### Cover

Partial cover (behind a wall, rock, furniture) gives the target one rank on defense. Full cover prevents direct targeting.

### Position

Advantageous position (higher ground, flanking) provides one rank on attack. Disadvantaged position (prone, off-balance) removes one rank.

### Surprise

Attacks against surprised targets gain one rank. Surprised characters cannot apply Effort to defense.

### Range

Beyond normal weapon range: hindered by one rank. Beyond double range: hindered by two ranks (if the GM allows the attempt).

### Illumination and Visibility

Dim light or moderate obstruction (fog, smoke): hindered by one rank. Darkness or heavy obstruction: hindered by two ranks.

### Water

Melee while submerged: hindered by one rank for non-aquatic creatures. Ranged through water: hindered by two ranks or impossible beyond 10 feet.

### Moving Targets

Attacking a target that used its last action to move a long distance (100 ft): hindered by one rank.

### Area Attacks

Affects all targets in an area. One roll compared against each target's defense. Effort on damage adds 2 points per level (not 3). Targets who resist still take 1 point.

### Attacking Objects

Objects have levels and may have hardness (functions as Armor). Attacking an object is typically a Might task at the object's level.

### Combat Between PCs

The attacking player rolls. Difficulty is based on the defending PC's relevant capabilities.

### Combat Between NPCs

The GM adjudicates based on relative levels and the narrative. No dice are rolled.

---

## Damage

When an attack hits, the target loses points from the relevant stat Pool (usually Might for physical damage), reduced by Armor. "Damage" without a type specified means Might damage. Intellect damage and Speed damage are always labeled.

NPCs have health instead of Pools. Damage reduces health.

---

## Armor

Each time you take damage, subtract your Armor from the damage before reducing your Pool. If Armor reduces damage to 0, you take none.

Physical armor categories:

| Armor Type | Armor Bonus |
|------------|-------------|
| Light | +1 Armor |
| Medium | +2 Armor |
| Heavy | +3 Armor |

Armor stacks from all sources — physical armor, abilities, cyphers, and artifacts. Track each source separately, as some attacks bypass certain types.

**Shields:** Provide one rank on Speed defense against attacks. Require a free hand.

**Elemental Armor:** Protects against a specific damage type (fire, cold, etc.). Stacks with normal Armor against that type.

**Spell Damage and Armor:** Spell damage is reduced by Armor unless noted otherwise. Ambient or lingering spell damage often bypasses physical Armor but is affected by elemental or magical Armor.

Speed damage and Intellect damage bypass physical Armor entirely. Ambient damage usually ignores Armor as well.

### Armor Speed Penalty

Wearing physical armor increases Speed Effort costs and hinders Speed-based skills (Acrobatics, Athletics, Movement, Sleight of Hand, Stealth) by ranks equal to the penalty.

| Armor Type | Bonus | Not Experienced | Experienced | Mastery |
|------------|-------|-----------------|-------------|---------|
| Light | +1 | +2 per Effort level | +1 per Effort level | 0 |
| Medium | +2 | +3 per Effort level | +2 per Effort level | +1 per Effort level |
| Heavy | +3 | +4 per Effort level | +3 per Effort level | +2 per Effort level |

Experienced in Armor reduces the penalty by 1. Mastery in Armor reduces it by a further 1.

### Ambient Damage

Damage from environmental sources (fire, acid, cold, etc.) that ignores physical Armor unless specifically protected against.

### Damage from Hazards

| Source | Damage | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Falling | 1 point per 10 ft | Ambient |
| Minor fire (torch) | 3 per round | Ambient |
| Major fire (building) | 6 per round | Ambient |
| Lava | 10 per round | Ambient |
| Acid splash | 2 per round | Ambient |
| Acid bath | 6 per round | Ambient |
| Cold (below freezing) | 1 per round | Ambient; after extended exposure |
| Severe cold (arctic) | 3 per round | Ambient |
| Electricity (minor) | 2 | Ambient |
| Electricity (major) | 6 | Ambient |
| Suffocation | 2 per round | After held breath expires; Might task to hold |
| Poison (minor) | 4 to Speed Pool | Might defense to resist |
| Poison (major) | 8 to Speed Pool | Might defense to resist |
| Disease | Varies | Might defense; effects over time |

---

## The Damage Track

**Hale:** Normal state. Everything functions normally.

**Impaired:** One stat Pool has reached 0. Effort costs increase by 1 per level. Minor and major effects on rolls of 17–20 are ignored; only bonus damage (+1 to +4) applies.

**Debilitated:** Two stat Pools have reached 0. You can only move up to 10 feet. If Speed is at 0, you can't move at all.

**Dead:** All three Pools at 0. The character is dead.

### The Effects of Taking Damage

Damage is subtracted from the relevant Pool (usually Might). If that Pool reaches 0, remaining damage carries over: Might → Speed → Intellect. Armor and protections still apply to carried-over damage.

---

## Recovering Points in a Pool

Recovery rolls restore Pool points. Roll 1d6 + your character level. Distribute recovered points among your Pools as you wish. Four recovery rolls per day, used in order:

| Recovery Roll | Time Required |
|---------------|---------------|
| First | One action |
| Second | Ten minutes |
| Third | One hour |
| Fourth | Ten hours |

---

## Restoring the Damage Track

Recovery rolls restore Pool points but do not restore your position on the damage track. Rest is required:

**Impaired → Hale:** 1 day of rest.

**Debilitated → Impaired:** 1 week of rest.

This reflects the grittier nature of fantasy campaigns. Badly hurt characters need real downtime.

---

## Special Damage

Some attacks inflict conditions beyond Pool loss. See the Conditions appendix for complete details. Common conditions:

**Dazed:** All tasks hindered. **Stunned:** Lose next action. **Poisoned:** All tasks hindered; may deal ongoing damage. **Paralyzed:** Cannot move or act; auto-fail Might/Speed tasks; attacks gain two ranks. **Blinded:** Auto-fail sight tasks; attacks and defenses hindered by two ranks. **Deafened:** Auto-fail hearing tasks. **Frightened:** Tasks hindered while fear source is visible; cannot approach source. **Prone:** Can only crawl; attacks hindered; defense hindered against attackers within 10 ft, gains rank against those farther.

---

## Attacks of Opportunity

Certain actions provoke a free melee attack from an adjacent enemy (within 10 ft), outside normal turn order.

**Provokes:**

- Casting a spell within 10 ft of an enemy (unless using Defensive Casting)
- Using a potion or item within 10 ft of an enemy
- Standing up from prone within 10 ft of an enemy
- Moving beyond 10 ft from an enemy without a 5-foot step
- Retrieving and/or using a scroll within 10 ft of an enemy
- Retrieving and/or using a wand within 10 ft of an enemy

**Does NOT provoke:**

- Taking a 5-foot step
- Making a melee or ranged attack
- Using a power from magical weapon or armor
- Using a power from a magical rod

---

## Dual Wielding

Two weapons, two attacks per action, both hindered by one rank. Requires appropriate proficiency:

- **Dual Light Wield:** Two light weapons. This ability removes the hindrance penalty.
- **Dual Medium Wield:** One light and one medium weapon.

## Spellcasting in Combat

### Magical Training

Every spellcaster receives this ability free. It grants training in magic basics (artifacts/cyphers) and the Spellcraft skill. All spells require somatic components (one free hand) and verbal components (Draconic syllables). Spellcasters cannot cast in physical armor they aren't trained in. Enabler.

### Spell Slots

Spellcasting classes gain spell slots per level. Full casters (Bard, Druid, Mage, Priest) gain more than half casters (Magus, Paladin, Ranger).

### Preparing Spells

Requires a spellbook or holy text. Swapping prepared spells takes one uninterrupted hour of study soon after a ten-hour recovery.

### Spellcasting Skills

**Spell Focus:** Trained in attacks with one school of magic, damage type, or focus. Provides one rank on those attacks. If not an attack, reduces Intellect cost by 1. Enabler.

**Greater Spell Focus:** Specialized in attacks with a chosen school/type/focus (requires Spell Focus in the same area). Provides two ranks. If not an attack, reduces Intellect cost by 2. Enabler.

**Spellcraft Skill:** Used for identifying spells, analyzing/disarming magical traps, Defensive Casting (difficulty 4), and Concentration Checks.

### Magic Rolls

**Rolling a 1:** Edge does not apply to the Intellect cost. Cost cannot reduce the Pool below 1.

**Rolling a 20:** Spell costs no Pool points OR gains an additional beneficial effect (player's choice).

### Defensive Casting

Difficulty 4 Spellcraft or Intellect task to avoid provoking an attack of opportunity when casting within 10 ft of an enemy. Eased by the Combat Casting ability. On failure, the enemy gets a free attack; if that attack deals damage, the spell is disrupted and lost (Pool points still spent).

### Concentration Checks

If damaged while casting, make a Spellcraft check with difficulty equal to the damage taken. On failure, the spell is disrupted and lost (Pool points still spent).

### Spell Potency

Spend Effort to make spells harder to dispel/counter: +1 level of potency per level of Effort.

### Effort on Spells

**Attacks (Heighten):** Effort on a spell attack provides ranks on the attack roll, as with any task.

**Damage (Single-Target):** +3 per level of Effort.

**Damage (Area):** +2 per level of Effort.

**Area/Duration:** Requires metamagic abilities (Enlarge Spell, Extend Spell, Widen Spell).

### Spamming Spells

Only one instance of a spell can be active at once. Casting the same spell again ends the first.

### Spell Components

All spells require verbal (Draconic syllables), somatic (one free hand), and material components (typically not tracked).

### Intellect Costs

Generally, Intellect cost = original D&D spell level +1 (low/mid level) or +2 (high level). Specific costs from the Spell Compendium.

### Anti-Stacking

Spell bonuses are treated as assets. Only one instance of most buffs/effects per target. Magical items duplicating class abilities don't stack, even if differently named.

### Metamagic Abilities

**Enlarge Spell:** One level of Effort to increase range one step (touch → immediate → short → long → very long). Enabler.

**Extend Spell:** One level of Effort to increase duration (1/10 min → 1 hour → 8 hours → 24 hours). Enabler.

**Widen Spell:** Two levels of Effort to double the area. Enabler.

**Subtle Spell:** One level of Effort to cast without verbal or somatic components. Enabler.

**Energy Substitution:** One level of Effort to change damage type (Fire, Cold, Lightning, Acid, Thunder, etc.). Enabler.

## Equipment and Economy

### Gold Pieces

This system uses gold piece (gp) values instead of the standard Cypher price categories. Use standard D&D gold piece values for equipment and treasure.

### Starting Gear

Characters begin with class-appropriate equipment plus gold based on background (typically 100–200 gp).

### Artifacts vs Cyphers

Whenever an ability references how many cyphers a character can carry, apply it instead to artifacts. Artifacts (permanent magical items) interfere with each other. Cyphers (potions, scrolls, wands) can be carried in any number.

### Bonuses and Stacking

When in doubt, count a bonus as an asset. Magical items that duplicate class abilities or spells do not stack with those abilities, even if differently named.

## Experience and Advancement

This system uses milestone or session-based advancement instead of the standard Cypher XP system. The GM determines when characters advance to the next level. See the Character Progression Charts for what each level provides.

## Character Progression Charts

Four progression charts are available, each tuned for a different campaign style. The GM selects one at campaign start.

**Standard** is the default balanced progression suitable for most fantasy campaigns — a steady growth of power that keeps challenges meaningful at every level.

**Gritty / OSR** creates a harsher, more dangerous game reminiscent of old-school play. Lower pools, slower advancement, higher Effort costs (3 per level instead of 2), and restricted assets and magical items make every encounter a serious threat.

**Heroic** produces powerful characters from the start who grow into legends. Higher pools, faster advancement, generous assets (up to 3), and unrestricted magical items support epic, high-fantasy storytelling.

**Veteran** is designed for experienced players who want to skip the fragility of the lowest levels and the extreme power of the highest. Characters begin with higher Edge and Effort, creating competent adventurers from session one, but cap at moderate power — ideal for campaigns focused on story and challenge rather than either survival or spectacle.

### Reading the Progression Charts

**Level:** Character level (1–10), replacing standard tiers.

**Edge:** Total Edge the character has at this level (distributed among stats per type or player choice).

**Effort:** Maximum Effort score at this level.

**Pool:** Additional stat Pool points gained at this level (distributed among Might, Speed, and Intellect).

**Max Rank:** Maximum skill rank available (1 = trained at Levels 1–3, 2 = specialized at Levels 4–6, 3 = expert at Levels 7–10).

**Abilities:** Number of new type abilities gained.

**Focus:** Whether a focus ability is gained.

**Skill & Combat:** Whether a new skill training or combat proficiency is gained.

## Martial Skills

Martial skills are gained through character type abilities and tracked separately from general skills:

**Skill with Attacks:** Training in a weapon group (e.g., light bladed, swords, bows). Overlapping groups don't stack.

**Skill with Defense:** Training in Might, Speed, or Intellect defense.

**Experienced in Armor:** Removes the first level of Armor Speed Penalty.

**Mastery in Armor:** Removes the second level of Armor Speed Penalty.

**Mastery with Attacks:** Specialized in a weapon group already trained in.

**Mastery with Defense:** Specialized in a defense skill already trained in.

**Extra Attack:** Second melee or ranged attack per round.

**Triple Attack:** Third attack per round (Fighter only, Level 10).

## GM Reference: Creature Rules

This section provides guidelines for building and running creatures.

### Creature Difficulty Scale

While player-facing task difficulty ranges from 1 to 10, creatures in the game world can range from level 1 up to level 15 for the most powerful beings. The same mathematical formula applies — target number always equals level × 3. Creatures above level 10 represent world-shaking threats: ancient dragons, demon lords, and godlike entities.

| Creature Level | Target Number | Description |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | 3 | Common vermin, minor hazards |
| 2 | 6 | Trained guards, large predators |
| 3 | 9 | Veteran soldiers, dangerous beasts |
| 4 | 12 | Skilled champions, lesser monsters |
| 5 | 15 | Hardened elite, formidable creatures |
| 6 | 18 | Exceptional beings, powerful monsters |
| 7 | 21 | Legendary warriors, greater monsters |
| 8 | 24 | Heroic threats, elder creatures |
| 9 | 27 | Mythic beings, ancient horrors |
| 10 | 30 | Near-divine entities, apex predators |
| 11 | 33 | Demigods, ancient wyrms |
| 12 | 36 | Archfiends, great wyrms |
| 13 | 39 | Lesser deities, titan-class threats |
| 14 | 42 | Greater deities, cosmic entities |
| 15 | 45 | Supreme beings, forces of nature |

Creatures above level 10 cannot be defeated without significant ranks from skills, assets, Effort, and likely magical assistance. These encounters should be rare, climactic events.

### Health Determination

Base health is calculated by Level × Size multiplier:

| Size | Health per Level |
|------|-----------------|
| Small | 3 per level |
| Medium | 4 per level |
| Large | 5 per level |
| Huge+ | 6 per level |

**Might Bonus:** +1 Health per level for creatures with +1 to Might tasks; +2 per level for +2 to Might tasks.

|----------|-------------|
| 0–1 HD | Level 1 |
| 2–3 HD | Level 2 |
| 4–5 HD | Level 3 |
| 6–8 HD | Level 4 |
| 9–10 HD | Level 5 |
| 11–12 HD | Level 6 |
| 13–15 HD | Level 7 |
| 16–18 HD | Level 8 |
| 19–21 HD | Level 9 |

| 28–30 HD | Level 12 |

**Special Modifiers:** Extra-planar creatures: +1 Level. Dragons: +2 Levels.

### Combat and Stat Modifiers

Task level adjustments stack. A level 2 creature with +1 Speed and +1 Stealth acts as level 4 for Stealth.

### Armor Conversion (from D&D AC)

| D&D AC | Cypher Armor |
|--------|-------------|
| AC 10–12 | 0 Armor |
| AC 13–15 | 1 Armor |
| AC 16–19 | 2 Armor |
| AC 20–23 | 3 Armor |
| Every +4 beyond 23 | +1 additional |

**Shields:** One rank on Speed defense vs. attacks.

**Size Penalties:** Large: −1 to Speed defense. Huge+: −2 to Speed defense.

### Monster Effort Rules

Unlike standard Cypher System, creatures in this system can use Effort. They possess one level of Effort per creature level, applied to damage, attacks, defense, or initiative:

| Creature Level | Max Effort at Once | Uses per Encounter |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Level 1–3 | 1 level | 1–3 times |
| Level 4–6 | Up to 2 levels | 2–5 times |
| Level 7–10 | Up to 3 levels | 3–10 times |
| Level 11–15 | Up to 4 levels | 5–15 times |

When a creature uses Effort on initiative, its effective initiative increases accordingly.

### NPC Combat Summary

NPCs don't roll — the player always rolls. When an NPC attacks, the player makes a defense roll against the NPC's level. When a PC attacks, the player rolls against the NPC's level. NPC health equals level × 3 by default, modified by size and stat bonuses.

When NPCs fight each other without PC involvement, the GM adjudicates by level comparison and narrative logic.
