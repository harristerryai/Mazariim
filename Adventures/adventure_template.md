# Cypher System Fantasy — Adventure Preparation Template

## How to Use This Template

This template converts any adventure concept — whether a published module, a homebrew idea, or a narrative outline — into a fully prepared Cypher System Fantasy game document. It is designed for a **3-session adventure** with **2 scenes per session** (6 scenes total), though it can be adapted for shorter or longer adventures.

**Before you begin**, confirm the following with your group:

- Which **Progression Chart** is in use (Standard, Gritty/OSR, Heroic, or Veteran)
- The **party level range** and number of PCs
- Any **house rules** beyond those in the Cypher System Fantasy supplements

**Reference documents you will need:**

- **Cypher System Fantasy Creature Compendium** — All creature and monster stat blocks
- **Cypher System Fantasy Spell Compendium** — All spell descriptions, costs, and effects
- **Cypher System Fantasy Magical Item Compendium** — All magical item descriptions and properties
- **Cypher System Fantasy Ability Index** — Complete searchable list of all character abilities
- **Cypher System Fantasy Ancestries & Types** — Character types, ancestries, and type-specific abilities
- **Rules Reference files** — Core mechanics, conditions, foci, flavors, spell lists, and progression charts

**Preparation workflow:**

1. Complete the Adventure Overview
2. Design each scene using the Scene Template
3. Build creature stat blocks using the Creature Workshop (referencing the Creature Compendium)
4. Map skill checks using the Skill Check Matrix
5. Calibrate difficulties using the Difficulty Guidelines
6. Plan rewards using the Reward Framework (referencing the Magical Item Compendium)
7. Review pacing using the Resource & Recovery Tracker
8. Prepare contingencies for each scene

**Two-Pass Preparation:** Prepare all adventures using this template
in two passes. On the first pass, complete all structural elements:
the Adventure Overview, NPC & Faction Roster, all scenes (settings,
goals, stakes, skill check matrices, combat encounter layouts, social
encounters, environmental hazards, treasure lists, and transitions),
difficulty calibration, resource tracking, reward framework,
contingency planning, and session checklists. For creatures, spells,
and magical items encountered in scenes, record the name, level,
type, tactical role, and location, marking detailed mechanical
entries with [STAT BLOCK PENDING], [SPELL DETAILS PENDING], or
[ITEM DETAILS PENDING]. On the second pass, return to each pending
marker and populate full stat blocks from the Creature Compendium,
spell descriptions and costs from the Spell Compendium, and item
properties and crafting details from the Magical Item Compendium.
These three reference documents are extensive, and attempting to
resolve their contents inline during scene design disrupts the
creative flow and risks inconsistencies in narrative structure,
pacing, and difficulty curve. Complete the adventure first; fill in
the mechanical details second.

### Pacing Guidance

**Session 1 — Setup & Hook:** Introduce the world, establish stakes, give players reasons to care. Scene 1 should be heavy on roleplay and investigation. Scene 2 should escalate tension with the first real challenge — a combat encounter, a dangerous exploration, or a tense social confrontation that makes clear the adventure is underway.

**Session 2 — Escalation & Complications:** Deepen the mystery or raise the stakes. Scene 3 should involve skill-heavy exploration or travel, giving every character a chance to contribute with their non-combat abilities. Scene 4 is the turning point — a major combat, a betrayal revealed, a critical choice that determines how the climax will play out.

**Session 3 — Climax & Resolution:** Everything comes to a head. Scene 5 is the final approach — the last gauntlet, puzzle, or negotiation before the climax. Scene 6 is the boss fight, the final negotiation, or the dramatic resolution, followed by a brief denouement.

### Quick Creature Conversion (from D&D)

If converting creatures from a D&D source, check the Creature Compendium first — the creature may already be converted. If not:

1. **Level**: Convert Hit Dice using the table (0–1 HD = Level 1, 2–3 HD = Level 2, etc.). Add +1 for extra-planar creatures, +2 for dragons.
2. **Health**: Level × Size multiplier. Add +1 or +2 per level for strong creatures.
3. **Armor**: Convert AC (10–12 = 0, 13–15 = 1, 16–19 = 2, 20–23 = 3). Shields give +1 rank to Speed defense.
4. **Damage**: Simplify to a single damage value appropriate to the creature's level (roughly level × 1 to level × 1.5 for standard attacks).
5. **Size Defense Penalty**: Large creatures get −1 to Speed defense. Huge+ get −2.
6. **Special Abilities**: Convert the most iconic 2–3 abilities. Reference the Ability Index and Spell Compendium for existing conversions. Not everything needs to convert — focus on what makes the creature memorable and tactically interesting.

## Part 5: Difficulty Calibration Guide

Setting appropriate difficulties is critical. Too easy and there's no tension; too hard and players feel helpless. The right difficulty depends on the party's level and the progression chart in use.

### Difficulty by Party Level

**Low Tier (Levels 1–3):** Max Rank 1 (Trained), Effort 1–2, Edge 1–2.

| Difficulty | Description | Achievability |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Simple | Automatic with any training |
| 2 | Standard | Routine with training |
| 3 | Demanding | Comfortable with training + Effort or asset |
| 4 | Difficult | Challenging — requires Effort or multiple ranks |
| 5 | Challenging | Hard — requires Effort AND asset or training |
| 6+ | Intimidating+ | Nearly impossible without extraordinary preparation |

*Sweet spot: Difficulties 2–4. Use difficulty 5 for dramatic moments. Avoid 6+ except as "you need to find another way" signals.*

**Mid Tier (Levels 4–6):** Max Rank 2 (Specialized), Effort 2–3, Edge 2–4.

| Difficulty | Description | Achievability |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| 2 | Standard | Routine with specialization |
| 3 | Demanding | Comfortable with training + Effort |
| 4 | Difficult | Manageable with specialization + Effort |
| 5 | Challenging | Challenging — requires multiple sources of ranks |
| 6 | Intimidating | Hard — requires Effort, training, and assets |
| 7 | Formidable | Requires maximum investment; still risky |

*Sweet spot: Difficulties 3–5. Use difficulty 6–7 for dramatic peaks.*

**High Tier (Levels 7–10):** Max Rank 3 (Expert), Effort 3–4+, Edge 4–5+.

| Difficulty | Description | Achievability |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| 4 | Difficult | Routine with expertise |
| 5 | Challenging | Comfortable with expert + Effort |
| 6 | Intimidating | Manageable with full investment |
| 7 | Formidable | Challenging but achievable |
| 8 | Heroic | Hard — requires expert + max Effort + assets |
| 9–10 | Immortal/Impossible | Climactic — only with extraordinary preparation |

*Sweet spot: Difficulties 5–7. Use difficulty 8–10 for climactic encounters.*

### Difficulty Modifiers for Common Situations

| Situation | Modifier |
|-----------|----------|
| Favorable circumstances (good tools, calm environment) | −1 difficulty |
| Unfavorable circumstances (bad weather, time pressure) | +1 difficulty |
| NPC disposition is Friendly | −1 difficulty for social tasks |
| NPC disposition is Hostile | +1 difficulty for social tasks |
| PC has relevant background/profession | Consider granting an asset |
| Task is attempted under fire or time pressure | +1 difficulty |
| PC has studied or observed the subject previously | Consider granting an asset |

### The "Untrained Ceiling" Rule

For skills requiring specific knowledge — Spellcraft, Research, Knowledge (any), Language — untrained PCs cannot succeed on tasks above difficulty 3. This is a hard rule. Design scenes with this in mind: if a knowledge check is critical to the plot, either set it at difficulty 3 or lower, ensure at least one PC is likely to be trained, or provide an alternative path to the information.

## Part 6: Skill Usage Guide

This section provides detailed guidance on when and how to call for skill checks during play. Use it as a reference during session preparation and during the game itself.

### Awareness & Information Skills

#### Perception (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- The PCs enter a new area and might notice something hidden or unusual
- Enemies are attempting to surprise the party (directly ties to Surprise rules: aware side gets a free round)
- Environmental details contain clues that aren't immediately obvious
- Something changes in the environment that alert PCs would notice

**When NOT to call for it:**
- Obvious features of a room or area (describe these freely)
- Information the PCs need to progress (give it to them or set the difficulty very low)

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Noticing something in plain sight that isn't being hidden: Difficulty 0 (routine, no roll)
- Spotting something partially concealed or subtle: Difficulty 2–3
- Detecting a well-hidden creature or object: Difficulty 4–5 (or creature's level for Stealth)
- Sensing an invisible or magically concealed presence: Difficulty 6+ (or spell level)

**Interaction with other rules:**
- A successful Perception check can grant the party the benefit of not being surprised
- Thorough searching takes at least a minute
- Dim light or moderate obstruction hinders Perception by 1 rank; darkness or heavy obstruction by 2 ranks

#### Insight (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- An NPC is lying, hiding their true feelings, or has a hidden agenda
- The PCs are trying to read the mood of a crowd or gauge political tensions
- Multiple clues exist and the PCs need to connect them
- During negotiations, to sense what the other party really wants versus what they claim

**When NOT to call for it:**
- The NPC's emotions or intentions are obvious
- The player has already figured out the deception through roleplay

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Sensing that someone is nervous or uneasy: Difficulty 2
- Detecting a specific lie from someone who isn't a practiced liar: Difficulty 3
- Reading the true motivation of a skilled diplomat or spy: Difficulty 5–6
- Seeing through a magical disguise or charm: Difficulty equal to the spell level or caster's level

#### Investigation (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- The PCs are actively searching a room, crime scene, or area for clues
- They are examining a document, artifact, or mechanism to understand it
- They want to reconstruct what happened in a location

**When NOT to call for it:**
- Casual observation (use Perception instead)
- Searching an area where there is simply nothing to find

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Finding something hidden in a messy but not deliberately concealed location: Difficulty 2
- Discovering a clue at a crime scene: Difficulty 3–4
- Finding a secret door or hidden compartment: Difficulty 4–5
- Reconstructing a complex sequence of events from scattered evidence: Difficulty 5–6

**Time requirements:** Thorough searching takes 30 minutes to 1 hour per room. Quick searches can be attempted as an action but at higher difficulty.

### Knowledge & Lore Skills

#### Knowledge — Arcana (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Identifying a magical effect, aura, or phenomenon
- Recognizing a magical creature by its properties or behavior
- Understanding the theory behind a magical trap, ward, or enchantment
- Recalling information about magical traditions or arcane history

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Common magical knowledge: Difficulty 2
- Identifying a specific spell school or tradition: Difficulty 3
- Recognizing an obscure magical creature or artifact: Difficulty 4–5
- Understanding ancient or forbidden magical lore: Difficulty 6–7

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

#### Knowledge — History (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Recognizing historical significance of a location, artifact, or event
- Identifying heraldry, noble lineages, or political allegiances
- Understanding the context of a conflict, treaty, or tradition

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Common historical knowledge: Difficulty 1–2
- Regional history or notable events from the past century: Difficulty 3
- Obscure historical details or events from centuries ago: Difficulty 4–5
- Ancient history or deliberately suppressed information: Difficulty 6–7

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

#### Knowledge — Nature (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Identifying plants, animals, or terrain types
- Understanding natural phenomena
- Recognizing unnatural corruption of natural environments
- Knowing the properties of natural materials

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Common flora and fauna of the local region: Difficulty 1–2
- Identifying unusual or rare natural specimens: Difficulty 3–4
- Recognizing exotic creatures or plants from distant lands: Difficulty 5
- Identifying magically altered or corrupted natural elements: Difficulty 5–6

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

#### Knowledge — Religion (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Recognizing religious symbols, rituals, or holy sites
- Understanding the tenets and practices of a specific faith
- Identifying undead, fiendish, or celestial creatures by their nature

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Common religious knowledge: Difficulty 1–2
- Identifying a specific cult, sect, or obscure deity: Difficulty 3–4
- Understanding forbidden or heretical religious practices: Difficulty 5–6
- Recognizing signs of divine or infernal intervention: Difficulty 4–5

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

#### Spellcraft (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Identifying a spell as it is being cast
- Analyzing a magical trap to understand and potentially disarm it
- Defensive Casting: Avoiding an attack of opportunity when casting within 10 ft of an enemy (fixed difficulty 4, eased by Combat Casting)
- Concentration Check: Maintaining a spell after taking damage (difficulty equals damage taken)
- Understanding the properties of a magical item before identification

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Identifying a common low-tier spell: Difficulty 2–3
- Identifying a mid-tier spell: Difficulty 4–5
- Identifying a high-tier spell: Difficulty 6–7
- Analyzing a magical trap: Difficulty equals the trap's level
- Defensive Casting: Always difficulty 4 (eased by Combat Casting ability)
- Concentration Check: Difficulty equals damage taken

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

**Reference:** See the Spell Compendium for all spell descriptions and costs.

#### Research (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- The PCs have access to a library, archive, or collection of texts
- They need to find specific information from written sources
- They are cross-referencing multiple documents to find a pattern

**Time requirements:** Referencing a book or text takes about 30 minutes. Identifying an artifact takes 15 minutes to 3 hours. Deep research might take days.

**Untrained ceiling:** Difficulty 3 maximum for untrained PCs.

#### Appraise (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- The PCs find treasure and want to know its value
- They are buying or selling goods and want to avoid being cheated
- They encounter an item and want to determine if it's genuine or counterfeit
- They want to assess the quality of craftsmanship or materials

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Appraising common goods: Difficulty 2
- Appraising unusual or rare items: Difficulty 3–4
- Appraising magical items (determining tier and approximate value): Difficulty 4–5
- Detecting a skilled forgery: Difficulty 5–6

**Design note:** Use Appraise to make treasure discovery interactive rather than passive.

### Social Skills

#### Persuasion (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Convincing an NPC to help, cooperate, or change their mind
- Negotiating prices, terms, or agreements
- Making diplomatic appeals or rallying allies

**Difficulty:** Base difficulty equals the NPC's level, modified by disposition (Friendly: −1, Unfriendly: +1, Hostile: +2 or impossible without first changing disposition).

#### Deception (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Telling a lie and hoping it's believed
- Running a con or scam
- Disguising intentions during a negotiation

**Difficulty:** Base difficulty equals the NPC's level. NPCs trained in Insight effectively increase the difficulty.

#### Intimidation (Intellect or Might)

**When to call for it:**
- Threatening someone to get information or compliance
- Establishing dominance in a social situation
- Frightening enemies into surrendering or fleeing

**Difficulty:** Base difficulty equals the NPC's level. May be Intellect (verbal threats) or Might (physical menace) at the GM's discretion.

**Note on consequences:** Intimidation gets results quickly but poisons relationships. NPCs who are intimidated may become hostile, seek revenge, or sabotage the PCs later.

#### Performance (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Entertaining a crowd as a distraction while allies act
- Inspiring troops before battle
- Creating a diversion at a social event

**Difficulty:** Difficulty 2 for a receptive audience, 3–4 for an indifferent crowd, 5+ for a hostile audience.

### Physical Skills

#### Acrobatics (Speed)

**When to call for it:**
- Balancing on narrow surfaces
- Tumbling through a space to avoid obstacles or enemies
- Landing safely from a fall or jump
- Performing acrobatic maneuvers in combat

**Armor penalty:** Hindered by ranks equal to the armor's Speed penalty.

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Walking along a wide ledge: Difficulty 2
- Balancing on a narrow beam: Difficulty 4
- Performing combat acrobatics: Difficulty 4–5
- Extreme acrobatics (tightrope in wind): Difficulty 6

#### Athletics (Might or Speed)

**When to call for it:**
- Climbing walls, cliffs, or structures
- Swimming in rough water (Might)
- Jumping gaps or reaching heights (Speed; standing jump ~4 ft across = difficulty 4; each rank adds ~2 ft horizontal or ~1 ft vertical; running jump provides an asset)
- Forcing open stuck doors or moving heavy objects (Might)

**Armor penalty:** Hindered by ranks equal to the armor's Speed penalty.

#### Stealth (Speed)

**When to call for it:**
- Sneaking past guards or enemies
- Hiding in shadows or cover
- Following someone without being noticed

**Difficulty:** Usually equal to the level of the observer(s). Modified by lighting (dim light eases by 1; darkness eases by 2).

**Armor penalty:** Hindered by ranks equal to the armor's Speed penalty.

**Interaction with Surprise:** If the PCs succeed at Stealth against enemies' Perception, they gain a surprise round.

#### Sleight of Hand (Speed)

**When to call for it:**
- Picking pockets
- Palming small objects
- Planting an item on someone

**Armor penalty:** Hindered by ranks equal to the armor's Speed penalty.

#### Disable Device (Speed or Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Picking a lock
- Disarming a mechanical trap
- Sabotaging a mechanism or device

**Difficulty:** Equals the device or lock's level.

### Wilderness & Travel Skills

#### Survival (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Foraging for food and water in the wilderness
- Finding or building shelter
- Identifying natural hazards
- Tracking creatures or people through wilderness
- Reading weather patterns

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Foraging in a temperate forest: Difficulty 2
- Foraging in a barren or hostile environment: Difficulty 4–5
- Tracking over soft ground: Difficulty 2–3
- Tracking over hard ground or after rain: Difficulty 5–6
- Predicting weather for the next few hours: Difficulty 2–3

#### Navigation (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Finding your way through unfamiliar wilderness, dungeons, or cities
- Choosing the best route between two locations
- Avoiding getting lost
- Reading a map or interpreting directions

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Following a well-marked road: Difficulty 0 (routine)
- Navigating trackless wilderness with visible landmarks: Difficulty 2–3
- Navigating in poor visibility: Difficulty 4–5
- Finding a hidden or concealed location: Difficulty 5–6
- Navigating in a featureless environment: Difficulty 6

#### Animal Handling (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Calming a frightened or aggressive animal
- Commanding a trained mount during combat
- Approaching a wild animal without provoking it

**Difficulty guidelines:**
- Commanding a trained mount in calm conditions: Difficulty 0 (routine)
- Commanding a mount during combat: Difficulty 3
- Calming a frightened wild animal: Difficulty 4–5
- Approaching a dangerous predator: Difficulty 5–6

### Crafting & Professional Skills

#### Craft (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Creating or repairing mundane equipment
- Creating magical items — reference the Magical Item Compendium for costs, requirements, and recipes
- Improvising equipment from available materials

**Time requirements:** Repairing a device takes 1 hour to 1 day. Building a device takes 1 day to 1 week. Magical item creation varies by type and level.

#### Profession (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Any situation where a character's professional background would provide relevant knowledge or capability

**Design note:** Review the PCs' Profession skills and deliberately include at least one scene where each Profession provides a meaningful advantage.

#### Language (Intellect)

**When to call for it:**
- Reading inscriptions, texts, or signs in a foreign language
- Communicating with creatures who don't speak Common
- Deciphering coded messages

**Design note:** Include at least one encounter per adventure where language matters.

## Part 7: Resource & Recovery Tracker

Pacing in Cypher System is directly tied to Pool management and recovery rolls.

### Recovery Roll Schedule

Each PC has four recovery rolls per day, used in order:

| Recovery Roll | Time Required | When to Allow |
|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| First | One action | During combat lulls or immediately after a fight |
| Second | Ten minutes | Short rest between scenes |
| Third | One hour | During a longer pause — camping, waiting, traveling |
| Fourth | Ten hours | Full night's rest between sessions or adventure days |

**Recovery Amount:** 1d6 + character level, distributed among Pools as the PC wishes.

### Damage Track Recovery

Recovery rolls restore Pool points but do NOT restore position on the damage track:

- **Impaired → Hale:** Requires 1 full day of rest
- **Debilitated → Impaired:** Requires 1 full week of rest

Plan accordingly — either provide rest opportunities or design encounters that account for weakened PCs.

**Session [#] Recovery Opportunities:**

| Between Scenes | Recovery Available | Notes |
|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| Scene [#] → Scene [#] | [Action / 10 min / 1 hour / 10 hours / None] | [Context] |

### Resource Pressure Guidelines

**Low Pressure (Heroic):** Full recovery between most scenes. Focus on dramatic moments.

**Moderate Pressure (Standard/Veteran):** Short rests between scenes but full rests are limited. By Session 3, Pools should be noticeably depleted.

**High Pressure (Gritty/OSR):** Recovery opportunities are scarce. Every Pool point matters. The higher Effort cost (3 per level) means PCs burn through resources faster.

### Treasure Guidelines by Progression Chart

**Gritty/OSR (Low tier items only, levels 1–3):**

- Artifacts: Level 1–3 only (1,000–3,000 gp); carry limit per character level
- Total gold: Conservative (50–200 gp per PC per session)

**Standard/Veteran (Low and mid tier, levels 1–6):**
- Cyphers: Up to Intellect cost 5; no carry limit; moderately available

- Total gold: Moderate (100–500 gp per PC per session)

**Heroic (Unrestricted):**
- Cyphers: All tiers available; no carry limit; commonly available
- Artifacts: Up to level 10 (100,000+ gp for the most powerful); carry limit per character level
- Total gold: Generous (250–1,000+ gp per PC per session)

**Reference:** See the Magical Item Compendium for all item descriptions, properties, crafting costs, and recipes.

### Cypher and Artifact Notes

**Cyphers (Potions, Scrolls, Wands):** Characters can carry any number of cyphers. Cyphers are single-use (potions, scrolls) or limited-use (wands with 25 charges). Their availability is setting-dependent — in some settings they are sold openly in shops; in others they are rare treasures found only in ancient ruins.

**Artifacts (Permanent Magical Items — Armor, Weapons, Rods, Staves, Wondrous Items):** Artifacts have carry limits because permanent magical energies interfere with each other. The number of artifacts a character can carry is determined by their character level. Artifacts require recipes to create (except cyphers, which do not). Reference the Magical Item Compendium for artifact abilities, level costs, and creation requirements.

### Non-Material Rewards

- **Allies:** NPCs who owe the party a favor or will help in future adventures
- **Information:** Secrets, maps, or lore that opens new adventure possibilities
- **Reputation:** The party becomes known in a region
- **Access:** Gaining entry to a faction, guild, or restricted location
- **Training:** An NPC offers to teach a skill or ability

Identify every piece of information that is essential for the adventure to continue. For each one, prepare at least three ways the PCs can discover it:

### Common Player Deviations

**The PCs skip a scene entirely:**
[What information do they miss? How can you fold it into a later scene?]

**The PCs try to fight when the scene was designed for diplomacy:**
[What happens? Can the NPC call for help? Can the scene still provide necessary information?]

**The PCs try diplomacy when the scene was designed for combat:**
[Under what conditions would the enemies negotiate? What do they want?]

**The PCs split the party:**
[How do you handle scenes with only part of the group?]

**A PC dies:**
[How does the adventure continue? Is resurrection available within the adventure's context?]

## Appendix C: Adventure Design Principles

**Every scene should have at least 3 skill check opportunities.** If a scene only involves combat, add environmental elements that reward Perception, Knowledge, or Survival. If a scene is purely social, add details that reward Investigation, Insight, or Appraise.

**Never put essential information behind a single check.** The three-source rule: every piece of critical information should be discoverable through at least three different methods.

**Failure should change the story, not end it.** A failed check should make things harder, more expensive, or more dangerous — but the adventure continues.

**Match difficulty to consequence.** Low-consequence tasks should be low difficulty. High-consequence tasks should be moderate to high. Save the highest difficulties for dramatic climaxes.

**Reward every character type.** A well-designed adventure has moments where the Fighter's strength matters, the Rogue's stealth matters, the Wizard's knowledge matters, and the Bard's charm matters. Review the party composition and ensure each PC has at least one spotlight moment per session.

**Track resource pressure deliberately.** Know when PCs can rest, when they can't, and how many recovery rolls they've used. The climactic scene should happen when resources are strained but not exhausted.

**Use the environment as a character.** Terrain, weather, lighting, and hazards are tools the GM controls. Environmental details create opportunities for non-combat skills to shine.

**Creature Effort is your drama dial.** Creatures using Effort creates dramatic spikes. Save creature Effort for dramatic moments rather than using it every round.

**Reference the compendiums.** The Creature Compendium, Spell Compendium, Magical Item Compendium, and Ability Index contain the detailed mechanical descriptions. This template structures the adventure; the compendiums provide the content.

## 1. MARTIAL NPCs (Fighters, Barbarians, Warriors, Guards, Knights)

### Passive / Always-On

- **Capable Warrior** (mid): +1 damage to all attacks
- **Precision**: +1 damage with ranged attacks
- **Weapon Master**: +1 damage with chosen weapon

- **Wear It Well** (high): Additional +1 Armor in any armor
- **Fleet of Foot**: Move short distance as part of another action; long distance as full action
- **Combat Reflexes**: Extra attacks of opportunity (one per Speed Edge)
- **Dual Light Wield**: Two light weapon attacks as one action
- **Opportunist**: Asset on attacks against foes already attacked this round
- **Horde Fighting**: Ease defense by one step when 2+ foes attack in melee
- **Sense Ambush**: Never surprised
- **Mobility** (mid): No attacks of opportunity for movement
- **Fists of Fury**: +2 damage unarmed
- **From the Shadows**: +3 damage if target unaware (Rogue)
- **Surprise Attack**: Asset + 2 damage from surprise (Rogue)
- **Nightstrike**: Free level of Effort attacking in dim/dark
- **Shield Master**: Trained in Speed defense with shield (attacks hindered that round)
- **Unarmored Fighter**: Trained in Speed defense when unarmored (Barbarian)

### Extra Attacks (Key Tier Breakpoints)
- **Extra Attack** (Fighter L3, Barbarian mid, Rogue mid, Ranger/Paladin high): Two attacks as one action
- **Triple Attack** (Fighter L10, Barbarian high, Rogue high): Three attacks as one action
- **Successive Attack** (2 Speed): Free attack on a new foe when you drop a target
- **Quick Throw** (2 Speed): Throw, draw, throw again

### Active Offense
- **Bash** (1 Might): −1 damage, target dazed 1 round (all tasks hindered)
- **Swipe** (1 Speed): −1 damage, target dazed 1 round
- **Control the Field** (1 Might): −1 damage, reposition target within immediate range
- **Lunge** (2 Might): Attack hindered, +4 damage on hit
- **Hemorrhage** (3 Might): On hit, target takes 3 more damage end of next round
- **Fury / Deadly Aim** (3 Might or 3 Speed): +2 damage to all melee/ranged attacks for 1 minute
- **Power Strike** (3+ Might): Hit knocks target prone (Barbarian)
- **Crushing Blow** (1 Might): Two-handed weapon + Effort = free Effort on damage
- **Double Strike** (3 Might): Dual wield, one roll, both weapons + 2 damage, Armor once
- **Feint** (2 Speed, mid): One action setup → next round: asset + 4 damage
- **Snipe** (2 Speed, mid): One action aim → next round: asset + 4 damage
- **Jump Attack** (5+ Might, mid): Difficulty 4 Might → +3 damage and prone on hit
- **Stun Attack** (6 Speed, mid): Difficulty 5 Speed → stun 1 round (lose turn)
- **Spin Attack** (5+ Speed, high): Attack up to 5 foes, one action, Effort adds more targets
- **Deadly Strike** (5 Might, high): Instant kill foe level 3 or lower
- **Murderer** (8+ Speed, high): Instant kill foe level 4 or lower (+1 per Effort)
- **Finishing Blow** (5 Might, high): +7 damage vs prone/stunned/helpless
- **Whirlwind of Throws** (5 Speed): Attack all in immediate area within short range, 3 damage each
- **Ambusher** (4 Speed, Rogue mid): Attack from hiding, +6 damage
- **Assassin Strike** (5 Speed, Rogue high): +6 damage vs unaware target
- **Quick Death** (2 Speed, Rogue): +4 damage, can't use two rounds in a row

### Active Defense
- **Block** (3 Speed): Auto-block next melee attack within 1 minute
- **Dodge and Resist** (3 Speed): Reroll any defense roll, take better
- **Dodge and Respond** (3 Might): If melee attack misses, immediate counterattack (1/turn)
- **Seize the Moment** (4+ Speed): On successful Speed defense, gain an action
- **Parry** (5 Speed, mid): Ease Speed defense; each additional attack eased further (max +3)
- **Divert Attacks** (4 Speed, mid): Auto-deflect ranged projectiles for 1 minute
- **Moving Like Water** (3 Speed): Defense and movement eased for 1 minute
- **Vigilance** (2 Intellect): Asset on Speed defense vs attacks; own attacks hindered
- **Nothing but Defend** (mid): Do nothing = specialized in all defense for 1 round
- **Again and Again** (5 Speed, high): Extra action each round for 1 minute

### Barbarian-Specific
- **Frenzy** (1 Intellect): +1 Might Edge and Speed Edge; can't use Intellect
- **Greater Frenzy** (4 Intellect, mid): +2 Might Edge and Speed Edge; can't use Intellect
- **Blood Fever**: Asset to attacks or defense when 1–2 Pools are empty

## 2. SPELLCASTER NPCs (Mages, Druids, Priests)

### Spellcasting Basics
- Spells prepared = 4 + Intellect Edge + level (full casters) or 2 + IE + half level (half casters)
- Require spellbook/focus + verbal/somatic components
- Can't cast in armor they aren't practiced in
- **Cantrip** (1 Intellect): Minor effects (Light, Message, Hand, Prestidigitation/Druidcraft/Thaumaturgy)

### Passive / Always-On

## 9. NPC QUICK-BUILD GUIDELINES

**Level = target number ÷ 3.** A level 4 NPC has TN 12, 12 HP, 4 damage, 1 Armor (typical).

### Suggested Ability Loadouts by NPC Role

**Grunt (level 1–3)**: 1 passive + 1 active ability
- Melee grunt: Combat Prowess + Bash or Swipe
- Ranged grunt: Precision + Overwatch

**Elite (level 4–5)**: 2 passives + 2 active abilities
- Martial elite: Combat Prowess, Experienced Defender + Feint, Successive Attack
- Caster elite: Spell Power, Combat Casting + 2–3 prepared spells + Cantrip

**Boss (level 6–8)**: 3+ passives + 3+ active abilities + 1 "capstone"
- Martial boss: Capable Warrior, Mobility, Extra Attack + Spin Attack, Finishing Blow + Parry
- Caster boss: Spell Potency, Battlecaster, Closed Mind + Divide Your Mind + key spells
- Noble boss: Natural Charisma, Will of Legend + Battle Management, Assume Control, Flee

**Legendary (level 9–10)**: Full suite; pick freely from high-tier lists
- Consider: Again and Again, Murderer, Deadly Strike, Spin Attack, Divide Your Mind
