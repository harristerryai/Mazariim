# Chapter Eleven
## The Weak Point

The report arrived at the villa on Shalazar as the
sun cleared the eastern horizon and the salt wind
carried the scent of volcanic soil and wild herbs
across the veranda where Rangithyr sat in his heavy
chair with the mana scimitar across his knees and
the morning ritual of polishing complete and the
blade's cold bluish light pulsing with the slow
rhythm that was not mechanical but organic, the
weapon responding to the proximity of the hand that
had wielded it for eight centuries the way a hound
responds to the voice of its master: with recognition,
with readiness, with the particular patience of a
thing that has been waiting and knows that the
waiting is nearly over.

The aide was young. Rangithyr assessed him in the
fraction of a second that assessment required: rank,
posture, the nervousness that junior officers carried
into his presence the way they carried their sidearms,
conspicuously and with insufficient comfort. The aide
held a communication crystal, faceted deep blue, and
beside it a leather case that Rangithyr had not seen
before, unmarked, sealed with the particular mana-lock
that Yzrat used for classified material.

"Commander Yzrat's field report, General. Delivered by
courier drake from the staging area. He requests a
personal audience at your convenience."

"My convenience," Rangithyr said, "is now."

The aide departed. The leather case remained on the
broad stone table in the map room, beside the crystal
and the stylus and the blank slate sheets that
Rangithyr used for strategic notation. The room was
spare. The walls were bare dark stone. The window
faced the ocean. The chair was the same heavy design
as the veranda chair: dark wood, unpadded, built for
a body that did not require comfort and that regarded
comfort as a concession to weakness that eight
centuries of service had not earned and would not
accept.

Rangithyr opened the case.

The contents were methodical. Yzrat's precision was
evident in the organisation: intelligence packets
sorted by category, each sealed separately, each
labelled in the compressed Orcish notation that the
military used for classified assessments. Political
geography. Military disposition. Magical capability.
Key personalities. Phase one results.

He began with political geography.

The world they had designated Tangent Twelve was, by
Orceri standards, fragmented to the point of
incoherence. No unified government. No continental
military command. The dominant political structure in
the target region, the area Yzrat had designated the
operational theatre, was a collection of independent
city-states whose relationships were governed by
commerce rather than treaty and whose capacity for
coordinated military response was, in Yzrat's
assessment, negligible. The assessment was supported
by the Phase one data: the probe at the town called
Winderbourgh had drawn a response from the anomaly
group, not from any governmental military apparatus.
Response time from the nearest city-state with
standing forces: none. No standing forces had
responded. The anomaly group had arrived through
dimensional transit within approximately four hours.
The local militia had been irrelevant.

He moved to military disposition.

The native military forces were primitive. Iron-based
metallurgy with limited magical enhancement.
Professional standing armies in the northern kingdoms
only; the southern city-states relied on mercenaries,
militia, and the intermittent deployment of
spellcasters whose coordination with conventional
forces was, per Yzrat's observation, improvised rather
than doctrinal. The one exception was the mage-city
whose defensive capabilities Yzrat assessed as
significant but whose offensive projection was
limited by institutional conservatism and the absence
of a military tradition. They were scholars, not
soldiers. Their power was real. Their willingness to
deploy it was conditional.

Magical capability was more interesting.

The native spellcasters operated at what the Orceri
classified as mid-tier: competent, versatile, but
individually insufficient to challenge Orceri
military-grade enchantment or biomantic deployment.
The exceptions were few. The mage-city contained
several high-tier practitioners whose individual
power exceeded standard Orceri officer capability.
The anomaly, the being whose dimensional signature
defied classification, represented a unique variable.
And the dragon.

Rangithyr paused at the dragon assessment.

Yzrat had tracked the dimensional disturbance of the
anomaly group's crossing to and from Ta'Puh. The
outbound crossing registered five signatures
consistent with the group's known composition. The
return crossing registered six: the original five
plus an additional signature of extraordinary
magnitude. Analysis of the mana-field density and
vibrational frequency was consistent with an ancient
draconic entity. The dragon had been on Ta'Puh. The
anomaly group had retrieved it. It was now on Tangent
Twelve.

"Interesting," Rangithyr said. The word was quiet.
The quiet was the quality that those who served him
had learned to read as the opposite of what it
seemed: not disinterest but focus, the particular
concentration of a mind that had been analyzing
threats for eight centuries and that found new
variables not alarming but engaging, the way a master
swordsman found a worthy opponent not threatening but
clarifying, the blade discovering its purpose through
the resistance of another blade.

He moved to key personalities.

Yzrat's assessment was thorough. Five primary figures.
Three mid-tier spellcasters operating in coordinated
tactical formation: an elven divine caster (Minerva
tradition, silver-white signature), a gnomish arcane
caster (standard arcane, warm gold signature, highest
raw combat output of the three), and a human bardic
combatant (diffuse signature, minimal direct magical
engagement, significant morale and tactical
contribution). A fourth figure whose dimensional
signature remained unclassifiable after months of
observation: the anomaly, an elven planar entity
whose abilities included interdimensional transit,
dimensional perception, and an undefined interaction
with the boundary fabric itself. A fifth figure
confirmed as draconic: a young green dragon operating
in humanoid disguise, whose combat signature at
Winderbourgh had registered on a frequency that
Yzrat's instruments associated with innate draconic
casting rather than learned magical tradition.

"Individually negligible," Rangithyr said. He reached
for the Phase one results. "Collectively..." He read.
The reading took three minutes. The silence in the
map room was absolute. The ocean was audible through
the window, the constant rhythm that had been his
companion for centuries and that the centuries had
rendered into background, into the ambient sound of
a life lived in a place chosen for its emptiness.

He set down the packet.

"Unpredictable."

The word was an assessment. In eight centuries of
military command, Rangithyr had learned that
unpredictability was the most dangerous quality an
enemy could possess. Strength could be matched.
Speed could be countered. Numbers could be
overwhelmed. But unpredictability required
adaptation, and adaptation in the field was expensive,
and expense in the field was measured in lives. The
anomaly group had responded to the Winderbourgh probe
with a resourcefulness that exceeded their individual
power. They had used a dimensional transit that should
have incapacitated their most dangerous member and had
compensated for the incapacitation with tactical
coordination that spoke of trust rather than training.
Trust was harder to break than training. He had seen
this before.

"On the seventh tangent," he said, to the empty room.
The memory was specific, as all his memories were,
stored with the precision of a mind that did not
permit the softening that time inflicted on lesser
consciousnesses. The seventh tangent. A small group
of native defenders who had understood their world's
vulnerability before their governments did. They had
delayed the Orceri conquest by six months through
tactical unpredictability, through the willingness to
sacrifice positions that conventional doctrine would
have held, through an understanding of their own
terrain that the Orceri's superior technology could
not compensate for. Six months. It had been the most
engaging six months of that campaign. Rangithyr had
personally led the final assault that broke them. He
had acknowledged their commander before killing him.
The acknowledgement had been sincere. The killing had
been necessary. The two were not in conflict.

He activated the communication crystal with a pulse
of mana that was less than a thought and more than a
gesture. The faceted blue surface brightened. Yzrat's
voice filled the room, compressed by the crystal's
transmission protocol into a quality that was
recognisably the skymage but stripped of the
self-satisfied warmth that characterised his natural
speech.

"General."

"Commander. Your report is thorough."

"I aim to provide sufficient data for strategic
decision-making."

"You have exceeded sufficiency." The compliment was
delivered without inflection. Rangithyr did not
distribute praise through tone. He distributed it
through specificity: if a thing was named, it was
valued. "The dragon. Assessment of combat capability."

"Ancient blue wyrm. Mana signature consistent with
a being of exceptional age and power. Currently
grounded, location uncertain, the draconic entity
designated to a concealment position by the young
green. If deployed in combat, it represents the single
greatest threat among the native forces. Individual
combat capability exceeds anything else we have
observed on Tangent Twelve." A pause. The pause was
Yzrat's precision at work: measuring the next
statement against the standard of what his general
required. "Excluding yourself, General."

"Noted." Rangithyr's voice did not change. The
comparison was not vanity. It was calibration. "The
dimensional weak point. Ozmakir's analysis."

"Confirmed viable. The scar tissue at the site
designated Gaal'Tarak provides ideal conditions for
portal construction. Boundary thickness at the site
is approximately sixty percent of standard. With
sustained application of Ozmakir's dimensional
compression apparatus, breach threshold will be
reached within thirty to forty standard days. The
apparatus is in transit to the staging area."

"The anomaly. Can it interfere with the construction?"

A longer pause. "Unknown. The anomaly's interaction
with dimensional fabric is unprecedented in eleven
catalogued tangents. If the anomaly reaches the
breach site during or after construction and applies
whatever capability it possesses to the boundary, the
result is unpredictable. Unpredictable," Yzrat
repeated, and the repetition was emphasis, and the
emphasis was the closest the precise skymage came to
expressing concern, "is not a word I use lightly."

"No," Rangithyr said. "You do not." He was quiet.
The ocean filled the silence. The scimitar lay on
the table beside the report, its cold blue light
pulsing with the slow rhythm that was patience made
visible. "Recommendation."

"Accelerate portal construction to maximum feasible
pace. Deploy a defensive perimeter at the breach
site in sufficient force to delay or prevent native
interference during the construction window. Once
the portal is operational, full deployment through
the breach to overwhelm native resistance before
coordination is possible." Yzrat paused again. "The
native political fragmentation is an asset. There is
no unified command to rally. Destroy the anomaly
group's capacity to project force and the remaining
resistance collapses within weeks."

Rangithyr considered. The consideration was not
hesitation. It was the final alignment of a campaign
plan that had been forming since the morning Ozmakir
had said "Battle" in a laboratory beneath the capital,
a plan that four hundred years of interdimensional
conquest had refined into a methodology and that
eleven conquered worlds had proven beyond the need
for further proof, and the plan required only one
decision that Yzrat's recommendation had not included,
because the recommendation was strategic and the
decision was personal, and the personal was the thing
that Rangithyr had been waiting for since the
centuries of peace had settled over him like dust
over a dragon in a cave.

"Accelerate construction. Deploy the defensive
perimeter. Standard assault force through the portal
upon completion." He paused. The pause was not for
breath. It was for the pleasure of speaking words he
had not spoken in over a century. "Full deployment,
Commander. I will lead it personally."

The crystal was silent for two seconds. Two seconds
was a very long time in a conversation between Orceri
military professionals whose communication protocol
measured efficiency in fractions of a second. The
two seconds were Yzrat processing the statement and
everything it implied.

"Full deployment, General? For a single unconquered
tangent?"

"I have been bored for a very long time, Commander."
The voice was quiet. The voice was quieter than it
had been at the beginning of the conversation, and
the quieter Rangithyr became, the more dangerous he
was, and the more dangerous he was, the more alive
he felt, and the alive-ness was the thing that had
been missing for centuries and that the morning on
the beach and the scimitar's cold blue light and the
empty ocean and the halfling ruins in the grass had
all been the absence of. "I do not intend to be bored
again."

"Understood, General. I will relay the orders."

The crystal dimmed. The room was quiet. The scimitar
pulsed. The ocean breathed.

Rangithyr stood. He moved to the armoury. The weapons
were arranged by type and era of acquisition, each
one maintained with the ritual care that was not
sentiment but respect, the acknowledgement that every
weapon in this room had been carried by a being whose
skill had been sufficient to require Rangithyr's
personal attention and whose skill had ultimately
been insufficient to survive it. The Shalazari longbow
on the wall. The crystalline lance from the third
tangent. The mithril-edged katar from the sixth. Each
one a record. Each one a memory. Each one a testament
to the truth that the universe produced beings of
extraordinary capability and that extraordinary
capability, in the end, was subject to the same
mathematics as ordinary capability: it was overcome
by superior force, superior strategy, and the
willingness to apply both without reservation.

He lifted the dark steel and adamantine armour from
its stand. The articulated plates were plain. No
ornamentation, no insignia, no heraldry. It was a
tool. He respected his tools. The weight of the
armour was substantial and the substantiality was
welcome, the body recognising the burden as
something it had been designed to carry and had been
denied for too long.

He dressed for war.

The process took seven minutes. Each piece placed
with the precision of a being who had performed the
sequence thousands of times and whose muscles
retained the memory the way stone retained the
memory of the river that had carved it. Greaves.
Cuisses. Fauld. Breastplate. Pauldrons. Gauntlets.
The heavy war-boots of reinforced leather and
mithril plate. The scimitar at his hip, its cold
blue light intensifying as it settled against the
armour, the mana within the blade responding to the
proximity of the mana within the plates, the
stripped entity energy in both recognising itself
across the boundary of separate housings and
resonating with the shared frequency of power that
had been taken from beings who no longer existed.

He walked to the veranda. The ocean stretched to
the horizon. The ruins of the halfling village were
geometric patterns in the grass behind him. The
wind carried salt and the scent of a world he had
conquered eight centuries ago and that he had
watched from this chair for most of the time since,
waiting for the silence to break, waiting for the
purpose to return, waiting for the word that would
tell him that the waiting was finished.

"Twelve," he said.

The word was spoken to the empty air. The word
carried the weight of a career that had begun in
fire and had been sustained by fire and that the
centuries of peace had banked to embers and that
the ember was now catching, now brightening, now
reaching toward the air that would feed it into
the flame that a civilisation of conquered worlds
had been built upon and that would now consume one
more world, one final world, the twelfth, the
completion of an architecture that eleven walls had
left unfinished.

The wind took the word. The ocean did not respond.
The halfling ruins in the grass were silent.

Rangithyr turned from the veranda and walked inside.
The heavy war-boots sounded on the stone floor of
the villa with the measured cadence of a being who
had waited centuries and was no longer waiting.

---

They departed Amara on a morning when the harbour
bell rang the sixth hour and the fishing boats were
putting out and the gulls were crying over the bay
and the ordinary machinery of the city turned
beneath them with the persistence of a thing that
did not know it was being left behind.

Aizune rode at the front. The layered robes of forest
green and deep grey, the mithril shirt visible at
collar and cuffs where the fabric shifted with the
horse's motion, the spirit-touched elven curved blade
at his hip with its pale shimmer dimmed by daylight,
the longbow across his back, the prayer journal heavy
at his belt. The Minerva clasp was warm at his throat.
The warmth was a constant. The warmth was the thing he
was carrying east, into a place where the warmth's
counterpart had been destroyed, and the carrying was
not metaphor but mission, and the mission was the
thing that his training and his faith and his
friendship with the gaunt elf behind him had refined
into a purpose that did not require elaboration.

Smiggles rode beside him. The burnt orange waistcoat,
the one he wore when the mood was resolute rather
than reflective, caught the morning light. The satchel
was full. The spellbook was memorised. The brandy flask
was full. The mana engine, reassembled after days of
study, rode in the satchel beside the flask, and the
proximity of the two objects, one a comfort and one a
weapon, was the gnome's entire operational philosophy
expressed in the geometry of a leather bag. His
moustache was waxed to a precision that suggested he
had spent extra time on the morning's maintenance, the
upward curves sharper than usual, the points more
defined, and whether this was vanity or defiance or
the particular gnomish tradition of presenting one's
finest work before walking into the unknown was a
question that the moustache itself answered by existing,
magnificently, in a world that was trying very hard to
destroy everything magnificent.

Iskar rode with his hat at the angle that meant
forward: the brim tilted down over his eyes, the
crown catching the morning light. The rapier was at
his hip. The sash held its full complement of throwing
knives. The concertina was in its case behind his
saddle. He had not sung since waking. The silence was
not absence. It was the sailor's quiet before a
heading is set: the held breath, the hand on the
wheel, the moment between the decision and the
execution when the body is gathering the commitment
that the mind has already made.

They passed through the Market District. Past the
Red Raven Trading Company, where Aldorph Broomspatch
stood in his doorway and raised a hand and did not
wave because waving was farewell and the merchant who
had started everything by hiring them to recover
stolen documents from an orc-infested keep did not
intend farewell to be the last gesture between them.
He raised a hand. He held it. The monocle caught the
light.

Past the Blessed Halls of Eternal Light. Past the
Shrine to the Wanderer, where a gnomish oracle whose
name meant nothing to most of the city and everything
to the people his networks had liberated from chains
watched from a window and touched the symbol of
Rivoleen at his throat and spoke a prayer in the
gnomish tongue that the wind carried away.

Past the gate. Past the road south to Blowing Rock,
where eighteen paladin-monks of Pala'dinae were
already marching north to meet them, Lionous Harker
at their head with his blonde hair pulled back and
his blue eyes burning with the light of a man whose
faith had told him that this was the stand and whose
faith he had never, in forty-five years of service,
found reason to doubt.

The road turned east. The Steppes of Broken Stone
lay ahead, the desolate scrubland where Zanthar had
built his machine and the orcs had occupied his keep
and the heroes had found the body of a monk and the
body of a spy and the vision of a world with three
moons, and the scrubland was the beginning of the
road that led to the Jinook Valley and through the
valley to the Kirak Mountains and through the
mountains to the ruins of a dwarven citadel that
had been destroyed fifty years ago by a force that
had not yet been applied.

Aleron rode among them. He sat in the saddle with
the careful balance of a man whose body required
management rather than assumption, the quarterstaff
strapped to his saddle, the tuning fork humming in
his pouch, the amulet pulsing green at his throat.
His white-streaked hair caught the morning light.
Both eyes were focused. The echoes were quiet. The
quiet was temporary, as all quiet was temporary for
a man whose perception was fractured across a dozen
realities, but the temporary was sufficient for the
morning, and the morning was sufficient for the road,
and the road was sufficient for the walking, and the
walking was what Sugnim had told him the gift was for:
not the destination but the path, not the answer but
the willingness to walk toward the question.

He did not look back at Amara. The city was behind
him. The rooftop where he had sat with Aizune and
then with Lai was behind him. The room above
Eccentricities where he had held the tuning fork and
felt the wound in the east growing was behind him.
The destroyed section of the mural was ahead of him,
and the ahead was the only direction that Thalaman's
gift had ever permitted.

Lai walked at the rear. Twenty paces from Aleron.
The distance was precise. The yellow eyes were on
the road ahead. She had said nothing since dawn. The
green-black-brown hair caught the light and shifted
colour with each step, the chromatic instability of
a being whose appearance was never quite fixed because
the being behind the appearance was larger than any
fixed form could contain. She carried no visible
weapons. She carried no visible provisions. She
carried what she had always carried: the certainty
that whatever waited at the end of the road would
find her there, and the finding would learn what it
cost to threaten a world that a dragon had chosen to
defend.

Above them, two moons fading in the brightening sky.
Silver and gold. Between them, the space was empty.

In the east, where the Kirak Mountains rose against
the horizon in a line of grey stone and white snow
that was visible from thirty miles on a clear morning,
something stirred beneath the ruins of a dwarven
citadel. The stirring was not physical. It was
dimensional. The boundary between realities, the
membrane that separated Mazariim from the infinite
alternates that occupied the same space at different
frequencies, was thinning at the point where
Gaal'Tarak had stood, thinning with the patient
acceleration of a process that was being driven from
the other side by the technology of a civilisation
that had breached eleven such boundaries before and
that was now, with the methodical precision that
characterised everything the Orceri built, preparing
to breach this one.

On the other side of the membrane, Ozmakir's
apparatus hummed. The mirror arrays in his laboratory
showed the breach site from a dozen angles. The
dimensional compression was proceeding within
projected parameters. The boundary was cooperating,
if that was the word, the scar tissue from the
original wound providing a path of least resistance
that his instruments could follow and his apparatus
could widen. In thirty days, perhaps fewer, the
breach would reach threshold. The portal would open.
And through the portal would come the army that had
conquered eleven worlds, led by a being who had
blocked a single blow eight centuries ago and changed
the course of a reality and who was now, in dark
armour on a conquered island, walking away from
centuries of boredom toward the purpose that the
boredom had been the absence of.

The heroes did not know this. They rode east with
the knowledge they possessed: the wound, the portal,
the deadline, the sacrifice that one of them suspected
and another had sensed and the rest carried as the
weight of an unspoken fear that manifested in the
particular quality of silence that settles over
people who are walking toward something they cannot
walk around.

The road stretched east. The mountains waited. The
two moons faded. The sky was empty and bright and
ordinary, the particular blue of a world that had
not yet learned what was pressing against its borders
from the other side.

But in the ruins of Gaal'Tarak, in the collapsed
halls where dwarven engineering had once held back
the mountain and where the mountain had returned to
claim what engineering had borrowed, the boundary
between realities thinned and thinned, and the
thinning carried a quality that no instrument on
either side of the membrane could measure and no
scholar on either side could explain: a wrongness
in the flow of time itself, a stutter in the
sequence of moments, as though the ruins remembered
something that had not yet happened and the
remembering was the wound and the wound was the
reason the boundary was thin and the reason was a
question that the ruins posed in the language of
collapsed stone and fractured time and that no one,
not the Orceri building their portal on one side
and not the heroes riding toward it on the other,
had yet learned to read.

The road went east.

Book Two ends.
