### Historical Background

Amara's past dates back to Sabonaar fishing villages along the Bay of
Urgot. The Dukhan Empire assimilated the coastal cities and ruled for
over seven hundred years, widening the harbor and dredging the bay.

In 213 BW, the Dukhan Empire fell when the Zebulban high priest Soros
un-Kahn's catastrophic ritual killed most major cities' populations.
Amara was destroyed and depopulated. After the War to End All Wars,
dwarves helped rebuild, and Amara was slowly repopulated over centuries.

In 291 AW, Amara was assimilated into the Empire of Simir. In 686 AW,
the Empire fell when King Tiberius Simir was assassinated. In 692 AW,
Baron Feraldo Bastien seized control, declared the "Free City of Amara,"
built defenses and economy using paid enforcers, a merchant guild, and a
thieves guild to protect investments and maintain order. He expanded the
harbor and warehouse district, attracted new trade routes, and governed
more fairly. Three generations later, Baron Armand Bastien rules one of
the biggest trade hubs in western Nigal.

In 733 AW, Moamon went to war with Amara. Both suffered heavy losses
before a truce brokered by Instar wizards and the Federation. Tensions
remain.

---

### Key Districts

#### Noble District

Wealthy merchants rather than true nobility. Finest homes, parks, and
establishments. Constant security patrols.

**Castle Amara** — Seat of power for Baron Bastien and administrative
center. Well-fortified keep overlooking the bay. **Barracks** adjacent.
**Noble's Market** — exotic and luxury goods. **Elegant Cockatrice** —
high-end restaurant and inn. **Halls of the Dark Dancer** — festhall
dedicated to Sheeva, rumored connected to the Coven of the Vengeful
Lady.

#### Market District

A maze of sellers and buyers, one to three story buildings. Shopkeepers
live above their businesses. Legitimate and underhanded dealings day and
night. The heart of Amara's commercial life.

**Grand Market** — central marketplace. **Red Raven Trading Company** —
Aldorph Broomspatch's operation; heroes' initial employer. **Over the
Mountain** — goods from dwarven lands. **Yella's Fine Cooking** —
authentic Sabonaar cuisine. **The Wayward Warrior** — weapons and armor
for adventurers. **Breath of the Forest** — elven herbal shop, potions,
natural medicines. **Underhill's Axes and Armor** — premium dwarven
smithy. **Joboba's Specialties** — curiosity shop (closed for repairs
at campaign start).

**Blessed Halls of Eternal Light** — grand temple of Tiovin, largest
religious institution. **Halls of Iron** — temple to Mordrion. **Shrine
to the Wanderer** — outwardly Rivoleen temple, secretly headquarters of
the Liberators (helps slaves escape from Moamon, Asim, Kruta).
**Hearthstone** — temple to Hinterhin. **Fortune's Embrace** — temple
and gambling den to Polyvin. **Tower of the Magical One** — magical
components and minor enchanted items. **Eccentricities** — magical
components and rare reagents. **Hirak's Fine Concoctions** — potions
and alchemy.

#### Dock District (Warehouse District)

Along the River Cho and Bay of Urgot. Warehouses, loading areas,
shipyards. Safe during day, dangerous at night.

**Ogre's Arse** — rough tavern, cheap drinks, reliable rumors.
**Marn's Respite** — cheaper inn. **Amaran Thieves' Guild** — run by
the mysterious Sergeant Knox (actually a security tool used by the
Bastien family). **Ocean's Edge** — temple to Iquinias built on pilings
over water.

---

### Key NPCs

**Baron Armand Bastien** (LN male Sabonaar Aristocrat 9) — Ruler.
Shrewd merchant lord, third generation Bastien. Balances noble houses,
guilds, and foreign powers with calculated pragmatism. Has spies in
nearby villages and Instar.

**Aldorph Broomspatch** (LN male human Rogue 3/Expert 7; Cha 15,
Int 14, Wis 14; Appraise +12, Diplomacy +13, Sense Motive +15) —
Owner of Red Raven Trading Coaster. Large, rotund, red hair, eye
monocle, long moustache and beard. Hires heroes, becomes ongoing
contact.

### Atmosphere and Tone

Vibrant, cosmopolitan trading city where gold is king. Diversity of
population (Sabonaar humans, Bryndawi dwarves, elves, halflings, gnomes)
creates a melting pot rare in the Merchant Nations. The heroes' safe
harbor between adventures. But not without danger: Dock District
treacherous at night, political undercurrents between Baron and Merchant
Guild, shadow of Moamon's Zebulbans and Asim's slavers.

In Book Three, the Defense of Amara transforms this familiar home into
a battlefield. Every district the reader has come to know is threatened.

## Zanthar's Keep

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Zanthar's Keep / The Orc Keep |
| **Type** | Ruined Wizard's Tower beneath Abandoned Keep |
| **Location** | Steppes of Broken Stone, east of Amara |
| **Era Built** | Keep: centuries prior; Tower below: ~310 BW |
| **Occupants (811 AW)** | Orc warband led by Kru'ka'ta |
| **Distance from Amara** | ~28 miles east |
| **Campaign Role** | Book One Chapters 4-5; site of Zanthar's Machine |

---

### Historical Background

#### Zanthar's Story (310 BW)

The wizard Zanthar, fleeing the Zebulban-controlled Dukhan Empire after
they killed his wife Tatyana, hid in the Steppes of Broken Stone. He
crafted a small tower disguised as boulders and excavated underground
tunnels. Within years he built the Machine: three large rings rotating
in three directions, covered in Draconic runes, metals, and gemstone
powders, with a glass globe on a central base.

First use: peered into a universe with three moons (Ta'Puh), though
undetected. Over 23 years, became obsessed with viewing alternate
versions of himself through mirrors. In 287 BW, lost control while
thinking of Tatyana, was vaporized. Tower collapsed. Underground
portions survived with the Machine and mirror room.

Over centuries, the shadows in the mirrors began to move on their own,
creating the Whisper of Zanthar (incorporeal undead).

#### The Keep Above

Hobgoblins and bugbears built a fort from tower rubble centuries after
Zanthar's death. Changed hands multiple times, fell into disuse.

#### Current Situation (811 AW)

Orcs led by Kru'ka'ta occupy the keep, raiding trade shipments between
Amara and Instar (hired by unscrupulous Amaran merchants). The orcs
discovered an excavation to the lower level and found the Machine.

Remular, a monk of Paladine from Blowing Rock, infiltrated the keep,
found the lower level and Machine. Killed by the Whisper while
activating it. His brief activation sent an interdimensional vibration
detected by both the Orceri and Aleron Windstrider.

A month later, the Orceri sent scout Kuulatch. He found the Machine
but did not use it. The keep's orcs killed him with numbers. His body
was left in the machine room.

---

### Layout

#### The Surface Keep

Small stone fortress. Multiple levels: living quarters, barracks, crude
library (Kru'ka'ta's notes about the Machine), upper level (shaman and
bodyguards). Network of secret passages through the walls, unknown to
orc occupants, with tiny spy-holes for observation. Approximately forty
orcs of varying capability.

#### The Lower Level (Zanthar's Ruins)

Accessed through excavation in keep's floor. Older, different stonework.

**The Mirror Room:** Dozens of mirrors of different sizes. Dancing
images in each showing the same face (Zanthar) living different lives.
Two dead orcs on floor, frozen in horrifying screams, emaciated (killed
by Whisper). Lit by flickering mirror-light. Shadows seem to move.

**Warded Corridors:** Draconic runes and protective phrases. Zanthar's
magical defenses, partially degraded.

**The Machine Room:** Three large rings along room borders, covered in
Draconic runes and gemstone powders, surrounding glass globe (~1 foot
wide) on central base. Activated by channeling magical energy into
globe. Rings unlock, runes glow, rings spin (vertical, horizontal,
diagonal) faster until blurred. Operator grasps globe, consciousness
projected to view other realities. Currently locked onto Ta'Puh
coordinates. Two corpses: Remular (withered husk) and Kuulatch (well-
groomed orc with extraordinary gear).

---

### What the Protagonists Find

**Zanthar's Machine** — Device viewing alternate planes. Locked onto
Ta'Puh. When activated, heroes see civilized orc world: elven lands
decimated, humans and dwarves enslaved, no elves or halflings visible,
massive orc army, three moons. Can be reactivated, though doing so
risks attracting Orceri.

**Remular's Body** — Dead monk to return to Blowing Rock.

**Kuulatch's Body and Gear** — Adamantine weapons of superior quality,
strange coins, items from another plane. Key physical evidence later
confirmed by Instar mages.

**Kru'ka'ta's Notes** — Fragmentary clues about Machine's purpose.

**The Whisper of Zanthar** — Incorporeal shadow-wizard. Atmospheric
menace. Drains strength. Killed Remular. Heroes must contend with it
during exploration.

---

### Post-Adventure Status

After heroes clear the keep, Yzrat (invisible on blue dragon mount)
observes their departure and drops a purple worm larva. Twelve hours
later, the purple worm destroys the surface ruins and much of the
underground. The Machine is lost. This occurs at the end of Book One.

---

### Atmosphere and Tone

Peeling back layers of history. Surface keep: crude orc territory,
filthy and violent. Lower level: older, stranger. Mirror Room creates
unease as dozens of flickering alternate lives play in darkness. The
Whisper makes the underground actively hostile. The Machine Room is the
culmination: a device that should not exist, showing a world that should
not be. The Orceri spy's body turns speculation into certainty.

## Blowing Rock

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Blowing Rock |
| **Type** | Small Village with Monastery |
| **Location** | South of Amara; approximately half a day's travel |
| **Population** | ~300-500 (predominantly Sabonaar human) |
| **Religions** | Paladine (dominant) |
| **Campaign Role** | Book One Chapter 7; peaceful arrival; heroes earn Lionous as ally |

---

### Description

A small, quiet village in the countryside south of Amara. Unremarkable
settlement: homes, small market, everyday rural life. Distinguished by
the Valiant Halls of the Dragon, a monastery of Paladine on the large
hill overlooking the town.

The monastery features an interior courtyard inspired by Ejiin gardens
and bridges, with a marble statue of Paladine depicted as a powerful
man with draconic features wearing elaborate plate mail and wielding a
longsword, flanked by dragon heads and crossed lances. The Valiant Halls
have been home to paladin-monks for over a century, established after
the Empire of Simir fell.

---

### Campaign Relevance (811 AW)

The heroes arrive peacefully to return Remular's body to the monastery.
Lionous Harker receives the body with grief and profound gratitude. He
tells Remular's story: a street child from Amara who escaped Moamon
slavers, wandered to Blowing Rock, grew up at the monastery, and set
out to investigate the orc raids.

Lionous is moved by the heroes' willingness to return a stranger's body.
He pledges: if any of them fall, bring the body to him and he will call
on Paladine to restore them. This establishes the raise dead safety net
and a relationship of mutual respect.

Half the monastery's paladin-monks are away on a great crusade to the
Ancient South.

---

### Key NPCs

**Lord of Justice Lionous Harker** (LG male Human Cleric 9 of Paladine)
— Leader of the Champions of Blowing Rock. Madronian man with blonde
hair pulled back, blue eyes telling of deep loss and equally deep
devotion. Looks young for forty-five. Fist-in-palm bow of his order.
Mixture of warmth and duty.

**Remular** (deceased) — Monk-paladin of Paladine whose body the heroes
return.

---

### Atmosphere and Tone

Peaceful and pastoral. The monastery on the hill with its Ejiin gardens
and serene Paladine statue represents hope and institutional goodness.
The contrast between this quiet place and the horrors the heroes have
seen (the machine's vision, the Orceri spy) amplifies what is at stake.
This is what the Orceri would destroy.

## Instar

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Instar / The City of Mages |
| **Type** | Major City; Magocracy |
| **Government** | Thaumaturgic Council |
| **Co-rulers** | Yelinda Featherstep (LN female Ya'sehrim Wizard (Enchantment) 17/Archmage 3) and Yigba (LN male Ejiin Wu-Jen 18/Archmage 5) |
| **Location** | Jinook Valley, Merchant Nations |
| **Alignment** | LN |
| **Population** | Major city (predominantly human with significant elven and other minority populations) |
| **Languages** | Common, Sabo, Draconic (scholarly), Ejao, Elda'lam |
| **Religions** | Alagalothor (dominant), Minerva, Rivoleen, Mau |
| **Primary Exports** | Magical goods, arcane education, alchemical products |
| **Progress Level** | PL2-PL3 with significant magical enhancements |
| **Campaign Role** | Book One Chapters 9-10; confirmation of Orceri items; source of planar tuning fork |

---

### Historical Background

Instar sits in the Jinook Valley, a region once controlled by the
Zebulba-worshiping Dukhan Empire. When the Empire collapsed in 213 BW
after the catastrophic ritual of the high priest Soros un-Kahn, the
cities of the Jinook Valley were devastated and depopulated. Over the
centuries that followed, Instar was slowly rebuilt and repopulated,
eventually becoming a center of arcane learning.

In 689 AW, the Thaumaturgic Council seized control of Instar,
establishing the magocracy that persists to the present day. Under
Council rule, Instar transformed from a conventional trade city into
the preeminent center of magical scholarship and arcane power in the
Merchant Nations.

In 715 AW, the Council closed most of Instar's ports for trade,
redirecting commercial traffic to Amara while focusing the city's
resources on magical research, education, and production. This decision
cemented Amara's position as the dominant trade hub of the western
Sabon Peninsula while allowing Instar to pursue its arcane ambitions
without the distractions of large-scale commerce.

In 733 AW, Instar wizards helped broker the truce between Amara and
Moamon after their costly war, demonstrating the Council's willingness
to intervene in regional affairs when it served their interests.

---

### Description

The preeminent center of magical learning and arcane power in the
Merchant Nations. The entire city is illuminated by permanent magical
lights. The skyline is dominated by wizard towers, arcane academies,
and research laboratories. Social status is determined entirely by
arcane ability; the most powerful mages command greatest respect
regardless of wealth or birth. Non-magical residents occupy a
secondary social position.

The city functions as both a university and a government. Arcane
research drives policy. Magical innovation is prized above all other
achievements. The streets are filled with spellcasters of every
tradition, from hedge wizards selling minor enchantments to archmages
whose experiments reshape the fabric of reality. Magical streetlights,
enchanted architecture, and the constant presence of arcane energy
create an atmosphere unlike any other city in the Merchant Nations.

---

### The Thaumaturgic Council

The ruling body of Instar, the Thaumaturgic Council consists of the
most powerful mages in the city. The Council has governed since 689 AW
and exercises absolute authority over civic, commercial, and academic
affairs. Membership is determined by demonstrated arcane power and
scholarly achievement.

The Council is co-led by Yelinda Featherstep and Yigba, who between
them represent both the western and eastern magical traditions and
balance elven longevity with human ambition.

#### Council Members

**Yelinda Featherstep** (LN female Ya'sehrim Elf Wizard (Enchantment)
17/Archmage 3) — Co-ruler. Elven enchantress of immense power.
Originally from the Inner Kingdoms. Has ruled for over a century,
her elven lifespan granting continuity to the Council's long-term
research programs. Specialist in enchantment magic.

**Yigba** (LN male Ejiin Human Wu-Jen 18/Archmage 5) — Co-ruler.
Ejiin mystic of extraordinary power who balances western and eastern
magical traditions. The most individually powerful spellcaster on the
Council. His wu-jen tradition draws from the Ejiin Lands of Honor,
bringing a perspective unique among the predominantly Madronian and
Sabonaar mages.

**Salrutis** — Varkonan sorcerer. Brings the ancient magical heritage
of the Varkonan people to the Council, a tradition stretching back to
the civilization of Akovar.

**Myth'raal Dragonsbane** — Yav'daerim elf evoker specializing in
force magic. One of the few elves on the Council besides Yelinda.
Valley elf heritage gives him the most experience with human-elven
cooperation among the elven members.

**Valia Jorino** — Sabonaar wizard. Represents the local Sabonaar
magical tradition on the Council.

**Snarf Copperthumb** — Gnome alchemist. Represents gnomish magical
and alchemical traditions. Likely connected to the broader gnomish
scholarly networks centered in Zhonhylite.

**Yaldian** — Ya'sehrim sorcerer. Another high elf presence on the
Council, representing innate sorcerous talent rather than studied
wizardry.

**Festin Mestinclass** — Green elven magus. Combines martial skill
with arcane magic in the magus tradition, an unusual specialty on a
Council dominated by pure spellcasters.

**River ni'Bathrun** — Silver dragon. A dragon member of the Council,
present in humanoid form per draconic shapechanging abilities. Dragons
cast as sorcerers with caster level equal to twice their age category,
making even a young dragon a formidable magical presence. Draconic
heritage would be apparent in humanoid form.

**Mikel Kailester** — Dwarven summoner/archmage. A rare dwarven
presence at the highest levels of arcane scholarship, representing
the summoning and conjuration traditions.

**Ariano Ignescreo** — Ifrit cleric of Mau. An ifrit (fire-touched
planetouched) serving the Oracle. Brings divine magic and the
perspective of Mau's church to a Council otherwise dominated by
arcane practitioners.

**Ziljiin bin Kamal** — Varkonan cleric of Alagalothor. Serves the
dragon-god of magic. A second Varkonan voice on the Council and a
bridge between divine and arcane traditions through Alagalothor's
portfolio of spellcasting.

**Vardon Vex** — Ya'sehrim bard/loremaster. Keeper of magical history
and lore. The loremaster tradition emphasizes the accumulation and
preservation of knowledge across all magical disciplines.

**Sieg Silverstein** — Hin (halfling) bard. A rare halfling presence
in the highest echelons of arcane governance. Likely brings the
halfling tradition of hearth-weaving and social magic.

**Miyeriatar Corsemal** — Yav'daerim sorcerer/monk. Combines innate
sorcerous power with monastic discipline, a highly unusual combination
reflecting the Valley Elves' proximity to diverse human cultures.

**Nelssis Larielle** — Green elven druid/ranger. Represents natural
magic on the Council, a tradition often overlooked by arcane-focused
institutions. The druidic perspective provides balance to the
Council's heavily arcane orientation.

---

### Campaign Relevance (811 AW)

The heroes and Aleron travel to Instar for two purposes:

**1. Confirmation of Orceri Items:** Mages of the Thaumaturgic Council
examine the Orceri spy's gear recovered from Zanthar's Keep. The
adamantine weapons are confirmed to have been manufactured in another
alternate prime material plane. The physical properties are finer than
anything produced in Mazariim. As the sage Barxis Brewerson in
Arkhan's Hold initially suspected, the Council's examination
transforms the machine vision from a strange experience into a
confirmed interdimensional threat. The items are not from any known
plane of existence within this universe; they originate from an
alternate prime material plane with different vibrational properties.

**2. Planar Tuning Fork:** The Council agrees to create the planar
tuning fork attuned to Ta'Puh, using the Orceri items themselves as
calibration material. The process requires approximately one week.
In exchange, the Council requires the heroes to share all intelligence
gathered during any crossing into Ta'Puh, establishing Instar as an
informed ally with its own strategic interests in the interdimensional
threat. The completed tuning fork provides the critical tool for
crossing into the Orceri world. Aleron can use the tuning fork in
conjunction with his supernatural planar travel abilities to transport
the group to Ta'Puh.

---

### Atmosphere and Tone

A city where magic is mundane and the mundane is irrelevant. Magical
streetlights, enchanted architecture, and the constant presence of
spellcasters of every tradition create an environment unlike anywhere
else in the Merchant Nations. The social hierarchy based entirely on
magical ability creates unusual dynamics: powerful but magically
ungifted characters are treated as second-class citizens regardless
of their wealth, martial prowess, or political connections.

Instar represents both the promise of arcane knowledge (the tuning
fork, the confirmation of the Orceri threat) and its limitations
(ivory-tower indifference from mages who care more about research
than external threats, institutional arrogance, a tendency to view
non-magical problems as beneath their attention).

The heroes react differently to the city: Smiggles is fascinated by
the magical innovations and feels a kinship with the scholarly
environment. Iskar is uncomfortable with the rigid magical hierarchy
and the city's detachment from the natural and mundane worlds.
Aizune navigates with social grace, his oracle abilities
granting him sufficient magical credentials to be taken seriously by
the Council's members.

The presence of a dragon (River ni'Bathrun) and an ifrit (Ariano
Ignescreo) on the Council underscores how different Instar is from
conventional human cities. In Instar, species and heritage matter
less than magical power. This cosmopolitan approach to magical
governance stands in contrast to the more insular attitudes found in
the dwarven holds and many human nations.

## Ta'Puh — The Orceri Homeworld

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Ta'Puh (roughly "Orc Kings") |
| **Type** | Alternate Prime Material Plane |
| **Nature** | Historically-divergent Mazariim where orcs won the War |
| **Point of Divergence** | Close to 1 BW — Rangithyr blocked Stoneshaper's killing blow |
| **Orceri Conquests** | 11 alternate realities over ~400 years |
| **Campaign Role** | Reconnaissance (Book Two Chapters 4-6); enemy homeland |

---

### Historical Background

Ta'Puh diverged when Rangithyr blocked Demious Stoneshaper's killing
blow against Zuel's avatar. The orcs won. Within ten years, sentient
races were nearly eradicated. Elven lands razed completely. Survivors
kept in camps for experimentation.

#### Biomancy

Far Realm-derived magical discipline reshaping living beings:

**Humans:** Selectively bred over thirty generations into stronger but
dull-witted creatures, conditioned into complete obedience. Willing
servants.

**Dwarves:** Transformed into "dwarven hulks" — nearly mindless war
machines three times normal size, adamantium and stone growths bonded
to skin, oversized magical fists.

**Elves:** Twisted into "sensoriums" — beings linked in psionic
hivemind of total submission, used as scrying/surveillance networks.
Appear as twisted, starved elves in metallic chains and armor,
detecting mana signatures.

#### The War of the Heavens

~75 years after the Great War, Zuel's forces invaded extra-dimensional
planes. Destroyed Minerva in Elysium (obliterated consciousness,
memory, identity). Over the next century, hunted down Mordrion, Quarzel,
Alagalothor, Tiovin, others. Taovan swore allegiance to Zuel.
Rangithyr earned title "Godslayer."

#### Interdimensional Conquest (~400 years ago)

In 392 AW, Ozmakir discovered alternate realities and developed the
ability to view and enter them. Systematic conquest of 11 worlds:

- Gnomish world: clockwork designs, dirigible technology
- Psionic elf world: population absorbed into hivemind
- Beast world: biomantic material for "leviathans"
- Akovarian world (~70 years ago): near-complete mana mastery,
  enabling sky citadels (mountaintop shaved, inverted, suspended
  by mana)

---

### Current State

Advanced, civilized orc world. Cities built on old empire ruins:
magical streetlights, floating carriages, cooling boxes, dirigibles
and zeppelins, mana engines, pseudo-tech combining clockwork and
magic. Giant eyeball surveillance constructs (beholder-like, severed
stalks, bat-wings) patrol skies.

Rigid military hierarchy under Zuel's near-immortal generals and
hierophants. Surveillance via elven hivemind sensoriums. Civilian
orceri enjoy comfort and prosperity. Enslaved races serve in silence.
Wildlife nearly eliminated from settled areas. Orceri are clean,
well-groomed, civilized: monocles, manicured appearances, commerce

## Conquered Shalazar (Ta'Puh)

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Shalazar (Orceri designation: Tangent Prime, Sector 7) |
| **Type** | Conquered island; personal estate of Rangithyr |
| **Original Inhabitants** | Halflings (Hin) — exterminated during the Great War |
| **Current Population** | ~2,000 Orceri (military garrison, administrative staff, servants) |
| **Location** | Large island approximately 80 miles off the southern coast of the elven continent |
| **Climate** | Mediterranean: mild rainy winters, hot dry summers |
| **Campaign Role** | Book Two Chapter 1 (Rangithyr POV); Book Two Chapter 11 (Rangithyr receives intelligence) |

---

### Physical Description

Shalazar is a rolling island of central highlands descending to
fertile coastal plains, approximately 85,000 square miles. The
volcanic soil that once made it extraordinarily productive farmland
has been left largely fallow under Orceri occupation. Eight
centuries without halfling stewardship have transformed the
landscape: former orchards have gone wild or died, terraced
hillside farms have eroded into stepped ruins, irrigation channels
are silted and dry. The climate remains mild and pleasant — warm
salt wind off the ocean, bright sun, the scent of wild herbs that
have reclaimed the abandoned fields.

The Orceri maintain a small garrison and administrative presence
but have never developed the island. It serves primarily as
Rangithyr's personal retreat, granted to him after he conquered it
during the Great War. The halfling towns were demolished centuries
ago; only foundation stones remain, overgrown with scrub grass and
wildflowers. The harbour at the former capital (Brandobaris) has
been maintained for military resupply but sees little traffic.

The island feels empty. That is the point. Rangithyr chose it for
the silence.

---

### Rangithyr's Villa

Built on the northern coast overlooking the strait between Shalazar
and the elven continent (also conquered, also empty). The villa
occupies a low bluff above a crescent beach of pale sand.

**Architecture:** Orceri military-functional adapted for comfort.
Single-story sprawl of dark stone and heavy timber, built low
against the coastal wind. No ornamentation. Thick walls, deep-set
windows facing the ocean, a covered veranda running the full length
of the seaward side. The roof is flat dark slate. Interior rooms
are large, sparsely furnished: a sleeping chamber, an armoury
(weapons maintained with ritual care, polished, oiled, arranged by
type and era), a map room with a broad stone table, and a main hall
with a cold hearth large enough to roast an ox.

The veranda is where Rangithyr spends most of his time. A single
heavy chair of dark wood faces the ocean. The view extends to the
horizon: grey-blue water, distant haze where the elven coast would
be visible on clear days. Salt wind. The sound of waves on sand.
Nothing else.

**Grounds:** A stone path descends from the veranda to the beach.
A small dock extends into the water for supply boats. Behind the
villa, the scrub-covered ruins of a halfling village are visible
as geometric patterns in the grass — foundation walls, a village
square, the outline of round halfling doors now filled with earth.
Rangithyr has never ordered them cleared. They do not bother him.
They are simply there, like the sand.

**Atmosphere:** Austere, quiet, enormous. The villa is too large
for one being and too empty for company. The silence is absolute
except for wind and waves. No servants are visible (they work in
the rear of the compound and do not approach unless summoned). The
armoury is the only room that feels alive: weapons gleaming,
recently handled, the mana scimitar on its rack glowing faintly
cold blue even at rest.

## Ozmakir's Laboratory (Ta'Puh)

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Beneath the Orceri capital city (built on ruins of what was Fort Hope in this reality) |
| **Type** | Research facility; interdimensional observation |
| **Access** | Restricted; deep underground; multiple security checkpoints |
| **Campaign Role** | Book Two Chapter 1 (Rangithyr summoned here, 392 AW flashback) |

---

### Physical Description

Ozmakir's laboratory occupies a vast chamber deep beneath the
Orceri capital. The space was originally a natural cavern,
expanded and reinforced with Orceri engineering: smooth dark stone
walls, vaulted ceiling supported by pillars of fused rock and
metal, floor of polished black basalt.

**The Mirror Array:** The chamber's centrepiece. A curved wall of
mirrors — not glass but liquid-metal surfaces held in dark iron
frames, each approximately six feet tall and four feet wide,
arranged in a gentle arc. There are dozens. Most show nothing:
flat silver surfaces reflecting the room. When Ozmakir activates
them, they show other realities: alternate versions of the same
world, each slightly different, each vibrating at its own
frequency. The images shimmer and shift, unstable, requiring
constant adjustment. The effect of many mirrors showing many
worlds simultaneously is disorienting: a dozen versions of the
same landscape, each wrong in different ways.

**Ozmakir's Workspace:** A cluttered desk near the mirror array,
covered in scrolls, instruments, and half-finished calculations
in precise Draconic script. The scent of raspberry incense and
sulphur (his personal markers). Glass apparatus, copper tubing,
crystalline lenses. Everything meticulously organised despite
appearing chaotic — Ozmakir knows where every item is.

**Mana Infrastructure:** Conduits of dark metal run along the
walls and ceiling, pulsing with cold bluish light (stripped entity
mana). These power the mirror array and the various instruments.
The ambient hum of mana circulation is constant and low, felt in
the teeth and sternum rather than heard.

**Atmosphere:** Scholarly, obsessive, quietly unsettling. The
mirrors showing empty alternate realities create the sense of
being watched from a dozen directions simultaneously. The cold
blue mana-light gives everything a clinical, preserved quality.
Ozmakir moves through it with the ease of a man in his own
kitchen. Visitors find it deeply uncomfortable.

## The Buried Pyramid — Zanzaboor Desert

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Deep Zanzaboor Desert, Varkonan continent (Ancient South) |
| **Type** | Ancient Varkonan pyramid, mostly buried by sand |
| **Current Occupant** | Sugnim, kitsune priest of Thalaman |
| **Era** | Ancient; predates most known Varkonan construction |
| **Campaign Role** | Book Two Chapter 2 (Aleron flashback, 774 AW) |

---

### The Desert Approach

The Zanzaboor is a vast expanse of sand and rock east of the
River of Life valley. Daytime temperatures kill the unprepared
within hours. The landscape is featureless dunes interspersed
with rocky outcroppings and hardpan flats where nothing grows.
No water. No shade. Wind carries fine sand that scours exposed
skin raw.

Aleron crossed this on foot, sustained by magic and the certainty
that something waited for him. Days of trudging: sand in
everything, sun bleaching the sky white, the horizon shimmering
with heat distortion. At night: sudden cold, stars thick and
brilliant, the two moons casting overlapping shadows on the dunes.

### The Pyramid Exterior

A single corner and one face of the pyramid emerge from the sand
at an angle, the rest buried beneath centuries of accumulated
dunes. The exposed limestone is weathered smooth, its original
casing long eroded to reveal darker core stone beneath. The
capstone is missing or buried. The structure is clearly enormous
— what is visible suggests the full pyramid, if excavated, would
rival the great pyramids of Nepherites. It stands alone in the
desert with no other structures visible in any direction.

The entrance is not a door but a gap where sand has partially
collapsed into an interior passage. Aleron fell through this gap,
sliding down a slope of loose sand into darkness.

### The Underground Chamber

Below the sand level, the pyramid's interior is intact. A broad
passage of smooth dressed stone, angles precise, descends into a
chamber of considerable size. The air is dry, still, and ancient
— the particular stillness of a sealed space that has not been
disturbed in centuries. Dust covers everything in a fine uniform
layer.

**The Mural Chamber:** A rectangular room approximately sixty feet
long and thirty feet wide. The walls are covered floor to ceiling
with carved and painted murals depicting hundreds of images in
sequential narrative. The pigments have faded but remain legible:
ochre, deep blue, green, gold, and black on pale limestone. The
images show a vast cycle — figures, events, cosmic forces depicted
in the stylised Varkonan manner (profile figures, hierarchical
scaling, celestial symbols).

Among the hundreds of images: an elf with dark hair and large
grey streaks, energy pouring from his hands and face, standing
before a rift in reality. This image is unmistakably Aleron. The
section of mural immediately following this image has been
deliberately destroyed — chiselled away, leaving rough bare stone.
Whatever came after the elf's act was removed. (Sugnim destroyed
this section himself, so Aleron would walk the path without
knowing its end.)

The chamber contains no furniture, no altar, no religious
trappings. Only the murals and, when Aleron arrived, Sugnim
waiting cross-legged on the dusty floor.

**Atmosphere:** Sacred in the oldest sense — not through
ornamentation but through age and silence. The weight of millennia
presses down. The murals seem to move in peripheral vision, their
faded figures shifting when not observed directly. Whether this is
a property of the paint, the chamber, or the observer's state of
mind is unclear.

## Fort Hope — Street-Level Detail

### Supplementary to Existing Location Reference

The following provides ground-level atmosphere for Aleron's
flashback (Book Two Chapter 2), specifically the market quarter
where the shockwave hits.

---

### The Market Quarter (Aleron's Experience)

Fort Hope is built on a scale that reflects its dwarven-influenced
construction: thick walls, heavy stone, broad streets designed for
cart traffic and troop movement. But in the market quarter, human
commercial life has softened the military architecture. Awnings of
coloured canvas shade storefronts. Produce carts crowd the
intersections. The smell of baking bread, roasting meat, and fresh
herbs mingles with the permanent undertone of stone dust and iron
that defines the city.

The market quarter sits between the Fort District and the Upper
Docks, a transitional zone where the governmental precision of the
fort gives way to commercial bustle. Buildings are three and four
stories of dressed stone with timber upper floors, their ground
levels open as shops and stalls. The Grand Bazaar's covered
arcades are visible from most streets — a massive iron-and-glass
roof (gnomish engineering) sheltering the central marketplace.

Aleron's decade in Fort Hope was spent in this quarter and the
adjoining residential streets. His lodgings (modest, clean, chosen
for proximity to the market where he bought bread each morning)
were on a street of mixed human and dwarven shops. The morning
routine: out the door, down two flights of stone stairs, through
an alley that smelled of the dwarven coffee house on the corner,
into the market square where a baker named Thresk sold round
loaves from a cart.

**The shockwave moment:** A bright morning. Aleron holding a loaf
of bread. The planar vibration hit him like a physical blow — he
dropped to his knees on the cobblestones, bread rolling away, the
echoes exploding into cacophony. The market continued around him.
No one else felt anything. A woman asked if he was ill. He could
not answer. The direction of the disturbance was as clear as a
compass needle in his chest: south and east. Far away. But
unmistakable.

## Giganzalt's Cave — Ta'Puh Mountains

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Northernmost mountain range, Ta'Puh |
| **Type** | Natural cave system; dragon's lair |
| **Occupant** | Giganzalt, ancient blue dragon |
| **Campaign Role** | Book Two Chapter 4 (arrival, negotiation); Chapter 6 (exposition) |

---

### The Mountains

The northernmost mountains of Ta'Puh are high, cold, and barren.
Unlike the Dragon Spine in Mazariim (where dwarven civilisation
has shaped every peak), these mountains show no sign of habitation.
The Orceri never settled them — too remote, too cold, no strategic
value. The slopes are grey rock and loose scree above the
treeline, with sparse conifer forest lower down. Snow covers the
upper peaks year-round. Wind is constant and cutting.

The mountains are the one place on Ta'Puh where the Orceri's
civilisational footprint does not reach. No mana streetlights. No
dirigibles overhead. No sensoriums patrolling. The silence is
natural rather than imposed — wind, rock, distant bird calls. For
the heroes (who arrive polymorphed as Orceri), this is the safest
landing zone: far from populated areas, far from surveillance.

### The Cave

A natural opening in a cliff face approximately 8,000 feet above
sea level, partially concealed by a rock overhang and scrub
vegetation. The entrance is wide enough for a large dragon but
does not look like a dragon's lair from the outside — no scorched
stone, no bone piles, no treasure glinting in the darkness. Just
a dark opening in grey rock.

**The Passage:** A descending tunnel of natural stone, wide enough
for two humans abreast (or one dragon moving carefully). The floor
is smooth from centuries of dragon traffic. The air temperature
rises as the passage descends — geothermal warmth from deep rock.
The passage turns twice and opens into the main chamber.

**The Main Chamber:** Enormous — natural cavern expanded by
dragon-bulk over centuries of habitation. Roughly circular,
approximately 200 feet across, with a domed ceiling lost in
darkness above. The floor is worn smooth stone with shallow
grooves where a massive body has lain in the same position for
very long periods.

Giganzalt fills the chamber. He is vast — an ancient blue wyrm
whose bulk occupies the majority of the floor space. Layers of
dust inches thick cover his scales, undisturbed by movement.
His eyes (when open) are the brightest things in the chamber:
deep electric blue, ancient beyond comprehension. The air smells
of ozone (blue dragon), dry stone, and the particular staleness
of a space that has not seen fresh air in decades. He does not
move when visitors enter. He simply opens his eyes.

No treasure hoard is visible. If Giganzalt ever accumulated
wealth, it was taken or hidden long ago. The chamber contains only
the dragon, the dust, and the darkness.

**Atmosphere:** Ancient, still, oppressive in scale. The dragon's
presence fills the space the way a thunderstorm fills a sky —
not through action but through sheer magnitude. Speaking feels
presumptuous. The dust suggests he has not moved in years. Perhaps
decades. He has been waiting for something, or waiting for nothing,
or simply existing in the way that ancient dragons exist: patient
beyond mortal comprehension.

## Orceri Town — Street Level (Ta'Puh)

### Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Name** | Not named; equivalent to a mid-sized regional centre |
| **Type** | Orceri civilian settlement |
| **Population** | ~15,000–20,000 |
| **Location** | Central lowlands, Ta'Puh (roughly where the Jinook Valley would be in Mazariim) |
| **Campaign Role** | Book Two Chapter 5 (heroes infiltrate disguised as Orceri) |

---

### Overview

A mid-sized Orceri town built on the ruins of a pre-War city.
The original architecture (human or dwarven) has been entirely
replaced or buried beneath eight centuries of Orceri construction.
The town is not a fortress or military installation — it is a
civilian centre where Orceri live, work, shop, and conduct the
business of daily life. This is the domestic face of the empire
that conquered eleven worlds.

The town is clean, orderly, and deeply wrong.

### Layout

The town is organised in concentric rings around a central plaza.

**Central Plaza:** A broad open square paved in dark stone, dominated
by a statue of Zuel rendered in dark metal — not the savage god of
Mazariim's orcs but a dignified figure in armour, one hand raised
in benediction, the other resting on an axe. The statue is well-
maintained, polished, surrounded by low-burning mana lanterns. Orceri
pass it without looking up. It is background, wallpaper, unremarkable
to those who grew up beneath it.

**Commercial Ring:** Immediately surrounding the plaza. Shops, eating
establishments, and service buildings line wide, clean streets paved
in fitted stone. The architecture is functional and uniform: two-
and three-story buildings of dark stone and timber with flat roofs,
wide doorways (sized for Orceri physiology: seven-foot frames),
and windows of thick glass. Signage is in Orcish script, painted
in precise characters. Mana streetlamps line every street — poles
of dark metal topped with glass globes emitting steady cold
bluish-white light. They never flicker. They never go out.

Shops sell goods recognisable in function if alien in detail:
clothing (Orceri-sized, dark fabrics, functional cuts), food
(unfamiliar cuts of meat, root vegetables, dense dark bread), tools,
household items, and luxury goods. A shop selling mana devices
(cooling boxes, heating elements, light-globes, communication
crystals) does steady business. Floating carriages — small platforms
of dark metal suspended three feet above the street by mana
repulsion — glide past carrying goods or passengers. They make no
sound except a low hum.

**Residential Ring:** Beyond the commercial district. Rows of
identical residential blocks, each containing multiple family units.
Architecture is uniform, clean, adequate. Small gardens behind some
units. Children (young Orceri) play in supervised groups in fenced
yards. The residential streets are quieter but not empty — Orceri
going about domestic routines, carrying groceries, walking with
purpose.

**Industrial Ring (outer):** Workshops, smithies, a small mana
processing facility. The sound of metalwork and the scent of hot
metal and ozone carry inward on the wind. Labour here is mixed:
Orceri supervisors, human slaves performing heavy physical work.

### The Enslaved

Humans are everywhere. They serve in shops (carrying goods, cleaning
floors, stocking shelves), attend Orceri on the streets (carrying
packages, holding parasols, walking three paces behind), and perform
all manual labour in the industrial ring. They are clean, adequately
clothed in simple grey tunics, and uniformly docile. Thirty
generations of selective breeding have produced humans who are
physically strong but intellectually diminished — capable of
following instructions, performing repetitive tasks, and responding
to simple commands, but showing no initiative, no curiosity, no
resistance. Their eyes are dull and accepting. They do not speak
unless addressed. They do not look up.

The Orceri treat them as furniture. Not with cruelty (cruelty
implies awareness of suffering), but with the complete indifference
of people interacting with tools. An Orceri shopkeeper directs a
human slave to sweep the floor with the same tone and attention one
would use to adjust a lamp. The humans respond instantly and without
expression.

Dwarven hulks are visible at the town's perimeter: enormous figures,
three times normal dwarf size, adamantine and stone growths bonded
to grey skin, oversized fists hanging at their sides. They stand
at intersections and gates like living statues, responding only to
commands from Orceri officers. Their eyes, buried deep in
transformed faces, show nothing.

### Surveillance

Giant eyeball constructs drift above the rooftops at irregular
intervals: beholder-like spheres with severed stalks and bat-wings,
trailing mana conduits. They scan the streets below in slow sweeps.
Orceri civilians ignore them.

Sensoriums patrol on foot in pairs: twisted, starved elves in
metallic chains and form-fitted armour, linked in psionic hivemind.
They move in perfect synchronisation, their eyes glazed and
unfocused, detecting mana signatures rather than visual targets.
Citizens step aside for them without comment. The sensoriums do
not acknowledge civilians. They are scanning for anomalies.

### What Is Missing

No wildlife. No birds, no stray animals, no insects visible. The
streets are clean in a way that goes beyond maintenance — the
biological messiness of a living ecosystem has been removed. No
weeds grow between paving stones. No moss on walls. The trees
planted along residential streets are uniform in species and size,
trimmed to identical shapes, producing neither fruit nor seed.

No elves. No halflings. These races do not exist here in any form.

No temples or religious buildings. Zuel worship is cultural and
civic (the statue, the holidays), not institutional. The Orceri
do not pray. They conquered their gods.

No poverty. No beggars, no slums, no visible inequality among
Orceri citizens. The empire provides. Every Orceri is fed, housed,
and employed. The cost of this prosperity walks three paces behind
them in grey tunics.

### Atmosphere

The wrongness is not in any single detail but in the accumulation.
Each element — the clean streets, the steady lights, the docile
humans, the absent wildlife, the synchronised sensoriums — is
individually unremarkable. Together they produce a civilisation
that functions with mechanical precision and absolute moral
vacancy. The Orceri are polite, productive, content. They have
built a society that works. It works because everything that
might resist, question, or disrupt has been enslaved, transformed,
exterminated, or bred out of existence.

The heroes walk through it wearing stolen faces, and the hardest
part is not the danger of detection. The hardest part is that it
is pleasant. The streets are clean. The food smells good. The
children laugh. And the humans sweep the floors with empty eyes.

### Basic Information

### Physical Description

A farming and waystation town on the road between Amara and the
Jinook Valley. Winderbourgh exists because of the crossroads:
the main Amara-Instar trade road intersects here with a north-
south route connecting hill farms to the river markets. The town
grew around the crossroads and has never outgrown its origins.

**Layout:** A rough cross shape following the two roads, with
most buildings concentrated at the intersection. The main road
(east-west) is wide enough for two carts; the secondary road
(north-south) is narrower. Buildings are one and two stories of
local stone and timber, with clay tile roofs in the Sabonaar
style. A low stone wall rings the town's core — built generations
ago against orc raids, now partially crumbling and used primarily
to keep livestock from wandering.

**The Crossroads:** The town's centre. A small cobbled square
where the two roads meet, containing a well, a watering trough,
and a market pavilion (wooden posts, canvas roof) where farmers
sell produce on market days. The Crossed Keys inn faces the square
from the east side: two stories, wide porch, the largest building
in town.

**Key Buildings:** The Crossed Keys inn (gathering place, largest
structure), a general store, a smithy (basic — horseshoes, tools,
not weapons), a small Tiovin chapel (single room, no resident
priest), a granary (largest by volume — stone, partially below
ground), and approximately sixty residences and farmstead
buildings scattered along the roads and immediately outside the
wall.

**Surrounding Terrain:** Flat to gently rolling farmland in every
direction. Wheat and barley fields, some already harvested (late
season). Low stone walls divide fields. A small stream runs north-
south about 200 yards west of town, crossed by a stone bridge on
the main road. A copse of old oak trees stands on a low rise 300
yards south of town — the only significant cover in the immediate
area.

### Defensive Assessment

Winderbourgh is not a military position. The wall is low,
crumbling in places, and was designed to discourage bandits and
animals, not withstand a military assault. There are no towers,
no gates (the wall simply ends where the roads pass through),
and no fighting positions. The granary is the sturdiest building
(stone walls, partially underground) and would serve as a
fallback position. The crossroads geometry means attackers can
approach from four directions simultaneously.

The town militia — farmers and tradespeople with hunting bows,
woodcutting axes, and a few inherited swords — would be
overwhelmed by any disciplined military force within minutes.

### Why the Orceri Target It

Winderbourgh is not strategically important. That is why it is
chosen. The Orceri preliminary attack is a test: probing
Mazariim's military response time, force composition, and magical
capabilities. A small, undefended town provides clean data without
risking significant Orceri forces against fortified positions. The
attack is clinical, not emotional. Winderbourgh is a test subject.

### Atmosphere

Before the attack: quiet, agricultural, unremarkable. The kind of
town that exists in the background of larger stories. After the
attack begins: chaos, smoke, screaming civilians, disciplined
Orceri soldiers moving through streets designed for farmers.
The contrast between the mundane setting and the advanced enemy
is the chapter's central horror.

## Orceri Command Infrastructure (Ta'Puh)

### Supplementary Reference

Orceri strategic command operates from the capital city (built on
the ruins of Fort Hope). However, Rangithyr does not use the
central command facilities. His status as supreme military
commander and Godslayer grants him the privilege of operating
from his villa on Shalazar, receiving intelligence via mana
communication crystals and occasional visits from subordinates.

**When Rangithyr receives intelligence (Ch. 11):** At the villa.
The map room: a broad stone table, bare except when in use. An
aide (junior Orceri officer) delivers a communication crystal
containing Yzrat's full report. Rangithyr listens alone. He
reviews the data at the map table, sketching force deployments
with a stylus on blank slate sheets. He works through the night.
By dawn, he has a preliminary campaign plan.

**Yzrat's operational base (Ch. 7):** Yzrat operates from his
dragon mount at extreme altitude, receiving and transmitting via
mana communicator (wrist device, established B1 Ch. 6). He does
not have a fixed base on Mazariim. On Ta'Puh, he reports to a
military airfield adjacent to the capital: a flat expanse of dark
stone where dirigibles dock at tall metal mooring masts and
dragon mounts are stabled in reinforced pens. The airfield's
command tower is a slim dark spire bristling with mana antenna
arrays.
