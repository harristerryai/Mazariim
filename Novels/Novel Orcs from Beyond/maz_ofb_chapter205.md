# Chapter Five
## The History of Eleven Worlds

Giganzalt began with the silence.

Not the silence of the cave, which was the silence of stone and
stillness and the particular acoustic quality of a space that had
been sealed against the world for centuries. The silence he
described was different. It was the silence that had followed the
War, the silence that had settled over a world whose voices had
been systematically removed, and the silence had lasted eight
hundred years and had not ended and would not end because the
beings who might have broken it were dead or enslaved or twisted
into things that no longer possessed the capacity for speech.

"The War ended in a ssingle afternoon," he said. "Four yearss of
carnage, and it ended in the time it takess a shadow to crosss a
plaza. Zuel'ss avatar desssstroyed Quarzel. Desssstroyed Mordrion'ss
avatar. And then the dwarf came."

His voice shifted register. The conversational tone of the
negotiation was gone. What replaced it was something older and
more formal, the deep harmonic of draconic speech pressed into
the service of testimony, the voice that dragons used when they
spoke of things that had happened and could not unhappen and must
therefore be spoken of with the precision that the unhappenable
demanded.

"Demiouss Sstoneshaper. I watched him from the ssky, noble elf.
I did not fight in the battle. I am not a creature of allianccess
and noble causess. I watched because the watching wass important,
because what happened at Fort Hope would determine the sshape of
the world I lived in, and I wished to ssee the sshape before it
ssettled."

Aizune sat on the cavern floor with his prayer journal open on
his knees and his quill in his hand and the Minerva clasp cold
at his throat. The blank page waited. The dim luminescence of
the cave's geothermal warmth provided enough light to write by,
barely, and the strain of reading his own hand in the dimness
would be considerable, but he did not call light. He could not
call light. The cantrip that had served him since adolescence,
the soft silvery radiance that Minerva's gift channelled through
his open palm, required the entity's presence to function, and
the entity's presence was eight hundred years absent from this
reality, and the silence where her light should have been was a
wound he carried in his chest beside the quill and the journal
and the attention he was giving to the dragon's words.

He wrote. The quill moved across the page in the measured script
that the Cundo'elda had trained into his hand, precise and even,
the letters forming in Elda'lam because that was the language his
mind used when it processed information of theological or
historical weight, the language closest to the divine, the
language Minerva had helped shape three thousand years ago in a
world where she was alive.

"The dwarf reached Zuel'ss avatar," Giganzalt continued. "The
warhammer blazed. The blow would have landed. And Rangithyr
sstepped from the Ethereal Plane."

The name settled into the cave like a stone into deep water.
Aizune had heard it before, in fragments: Aleron's references,
the compendium notes Smiggles had assembled from the machine's
vision, the implications that accumulated around the title
Godslayer the way sediment accumulated around an obstruction in
a stream. But he had not heard it spoken by a being who had
watched the moment from the sky, who had seen the parry with
eyes that could resolve individual figures from miles above a
battlefield, who had felt the consequences of that parry
reverberate through eight centuries of aftermath.

"One parry," Giganzalt said. "The ssscimitar blocked the
warhammer. Zuel'ss avatar turned. The dwarf died. The alliance
broke. And the world..." The pause was not rhetorical. It was
the pause of a being whose memory was perfect and whose
willingness to revisit certain memories was not. "The world
became thiss."

Aizune wrote: *One parry. Two worlds.*

"Tell me what followed," he said.

---

What followed was extermination.

Giganzalt told it without emotion, and the absence of emotion
was worse than emotion would have been, because emotion implied
engagement, and engagement implied that the events described
still possessed the capacity to affect the being describing
them, and what Giganzalt's flat, measured recitation communicated
was something beyond the reach of affect: the settled, calcite
certainty of a consciousness that had processed the death of a
world so many times that the processing had become geological,
layers of understanding compressed into stone, the sharp edges
worn smooth by the sheer duration of the contemplation.

"The elven landss were razed firsst," he said. "The Ya'sehrim.
The Tawarim. The Gaearim. Every sssubrace, every nation, every
sssettlement. Zuel'ss generalsss moved acrrosss Vand'ardhon
with the ssystemmatic precision of a fire consuming a foressst,
and when they were finisshed there wass nothing. Not ruinss. Not
refugeess. Nothing. The continent wass bare earth and assh from
coassst to coassst, and the assh wass the assh of treess that
had sstood for millennia and librariess that had contained the
collected wisssdom of a civilisssation older than any other on
thiss world, and the assh blew out to ssea and the ssea
dissssolved it and the world forgot that elves had lived there,
because there wass nothing left to remember them by."

Aizune's quill stopped.

The words on the page blurred. Not because his eyes were failing
but because the eyes were full, and the fullness was not tears,
not yet, but the pressure that preceded tears, the hydraulic
weight of a grief that was too large for the body that contained
it, and the body was trying to accommodate it and was finding
that the accommodation required the displacement of other things:
composure, and distance, and the professional detachment that a
priest of Minerva brought to the recording of terrible events,
and these things were being pushed aside by the weight of what
the dragon was describing, and what the dragon was describing was
the death of everything Aizune loved.

He had known. Aleron had told him. The machine had shown him.
The abstract knowledge had lived in his mind for weeks, a fact
among facts, terrible but contained, the way a fact about a
distant disaster was contained: known, acknowledged, processed
through the intellect rather than the body. But hearing it from
a witness, from a being whose ancient eyes had watched the
smoke rise from a continent that had been his people's home for
ten thousand years, hearing the specific details, the bare earth,
the ash, the sea dissolving the last physical evidence of elven
civilisation, the intellectual containment shattered, and what
replaced it was not understanding but experience, the vicarious
experience of a destruction so total that the word destruction
was inadequate, because destruction implied that something
remained afterward, rubble or ruin or scar, and what Giganzalt
described was not destruction but erasure, the removal of a
civilisation from the record of the world as though it had never
been written there.

He touched the clasp. Cold silver. Cold symbol. The open eye
that saw nothing in this world because the entity behind the
eye had been destroyed, and the destruction of the entity was
the next thing Giganzalt would tell him, and Aizune knew it, and
the knowing was a wound opening inside a wound, the smaller grief
nested within the larger grief like a chamber within a pyramid.

"Continue," he said. His voice was steady. The steadiness cost
him.

---

"The halflingss followed," Giganzalt said. "Ssshalazar. The
island of the Hin. Rangithyr took it perssssonally, because the
island wass ssstrategically positioned and because the halfling
population wass not..." The sibilants coiled around the word that
followed, tasting it, finding it bitter. "Usseful enough to
justify presservation. They were sssmall. They were not sstrong.
Their magic wass hearth-magic, community-magic, the sssmall
protectionss of a people who had never needed large onessss. The
Orceri asssssessed their military value at nothing and their
labour value at lesss than nothing and their island'ss value at
conssiderable, and the arithmetic wass performed and the
concluussion wass drawn and the halflingss were killed. All of
them. Every community. Every family. Every child." A breath that
was not a breath but a pause in which the cave's silence
deepened. "Rangithyr built hiss villa on the northern coassst.
He left the foundationss of the halfling villagess visssible in
the grasss. He did not order them cleared. I do not believe thiss
wass sssentiment. I believe it wass indifference. The dissstinction
iss worth noting."

Aizune wrote. The quill formed the words in Elda'lam, and the
words were factual and precise and carried none of the weight that
the dragon's voice carried, because ink on paper could not carry
the subsonic vibration that made the cavern walls hum, and the
weight had to live somewhere, and it lived in Aizune's chest,
beside the silence, beside the cold clasp, in the space that was
filling with the history of a world where everything he served
had been undone.

"Humanss were herded into campss," Giganzalt continued. "The
campss were not prissonss. They were laboratoriesss. The Orceri
had dissscovered biomancy, the disscipline derived from the Far
Realm, the art of resshaping living beingss at the fundamental
level. The humanss were the firsst sssubjectss. Thirty
generationss of sselective breeding, combined with biomantic
modification, produced what you will ssee in the lowlandss if
your companionss are ssuccessful: creaturess that ressemble
humanss in form and differ from humanss in everything elsse.
Sstrong. Docile. Intellectually diminisshed to the point where
the concept of ressisstance is not sssuppressed but abssent,
becausse the capacity for the concept hass been removed from
the lineage."

He paused. The blue eyes found Aizune's with the weight of
attention that the priest had learned, in the hours since
their negotiation, was the dragon's equivalent of emphasis:
the gaze that said what the words around it did not.

"They did not hate the racess they conquered," Giganzalt said.
"Hatred requiress recognition of perssonhood. They sssimply...
optimissed."

The word hung in the air between them, suspended on the subsonic
resonance, and Aizune heard it and wrote it and understood it
and the understanding was the worst thing the dragon had given
him so far, because hatred he could comprehend, hatred was a
relationship between persons, and where there was relationship
there was the possibility of change, and where there was the
possibility of change there was hope. But optimisation was not
a relationship. Optimisation was a process. And processes did
not change because you hoped they would. Processes changed
because the inputs changed or the objectives changed or the
system that performed them was dismantled, and none of these
changes were accomplished by hope.

He wrote: *Not hatred. Optimisation. No personhood recognized.
No relationship to appeal to. Process, not passion.*

---

The War of the Heavens.

"Approximately ssseventy-five yearss after the Great War,"
Giganzalt said, and his voice changed again, dropping into a
register that Aizune had not heard before, a register that was
deeper and slower and carried a vibration that was not the usual
subsonic resonance but something that came from further down, from
the place in the dragon's body where the oldest memories lived,
the memories that were not recalled but inhabited, permanent
residents of a consciousness that had witnessed them and been
altered by the witnessing in ways that eight centuries had not
repaired.

"Zuel'ss forcess invaded the extra-dimensional planess."

The words were simple. Six words. The reality they described was
not.

"The asssault on Elysssium wass the firsst," Giganzalt said. "I
did not witnessss it directly. No mortal did. But I felt it." The
blue eyes closed. The dust on his scales shifted as something
deep in his body responded to the memory, a vibration that was
not voluntary but reflexive, the physical echo of a trauma that
transcended the physical. "When Minerva died, the world
sshuddered. Not the ground. The world. The thing that holdss
reality together flinched, ass though the universe itsself felt
pain."

The quill stopped. Aizune's hand stopped. His breath stopped.

The word died occupied the cave. Not destroyed. Not diminished.
Not weakened or dispersed or scattered or reduced to a fragment
that might one day reconstitute. Died. The dragon used the word
with the finality of a being that understood the difference
between the various forms of cessation that entities could
experience and had chosen the one that left no room for
qualification.

Minerva died.

The entity whose symbol hung at his throat, cold and dark, in a
reality where her absence was a wound in the dimensional fabric
that Aleron had described and that Aizune now felt with every
breath. The entity whose Enlightening had lifted the elven
people from primitive awareness to civilisation, whose teaching
of respect for nature and the absence of possessive greed had
shaped a culture that endured for ten thousand years. The entity
whose sight moved through his perception when he prayed, whose
warmth filled his palm when he healed, whose light answered his
call when he reached for it in the dark.

Died.

Not her avatar. Her avatar's destruction would have been
bearable. An avatar's destruction was a setback, a century of
silence, a wound that healed. This was not that. This was the
destruction of the entity herself: consciousness, memory,
identity, the accumulated wisdom of an awareness that had
existed since before the elven people had learned to speak,
obliterated by forces wielded by beings who viewed her not as
a goddess or a guide or a mother of civilisation but as a
resource, a concentration of mana that could be harvested and
repurposed and distributed through conduits and implants and
streetlights and the surveillance networks built from the
twisted bodies of her own people.

Aizune sat in the dark and felt the cold clasp at his throat
and understood, for the first time fully, what the cold meant.
It was not the absence of connection. It was the absence of the
thing the connection connected to. In this reality, there was
no Minerva to connect to. The thread that ran from priest to
entity across the dimensional architecture of Elysium had not
been cut. The other end had been destroyed. The thread ran
from his chest into nothing, into the place where nothing was
not emptiness but wound, the scar that Ariana Ignescreo had
described in Instar, the dimensional scar where something vast
had been removed with violence.

"Her plane collapsed," Giganzalt said. The voice was gentle.
The sibilants were soft, the coiling quality subdued, the
ancient intelligence behind the words aware of what it was
doing to the elf who sat before it and unwilling to cause
more damage than the truth required. "Elysssium. The forests.
The light. Everything that wass Minerva'ss creation ceased to
exisst when sshe did. The space where her plane had been iss...
a hole. Not empty. A hole. A wound in the fabric that hass not
healed in eight centuriess and will not heal becausse there iss
nothing left to heal it."

Aizune's hand found the clasp. The silver was cold. His fingers
closed around it and held, and the holding was not prayer,
because prayer required a recipient, and in this reality there
was no recipient. The holding was something else. Something that
had no name in the theology he had been trained in, because the
theology assumed the entity's existence and had not developed
vocabulary for a relationship that persisted in the entity's
absence.

He held the cold silver and breathed and did not break.

He did not break because he was a priest of Minerva, and
Minerva's teaching was that wisdom survived what could not be
survived, and this was a thing that could not be survived, and
he was surviving it, and the survival was not strength but
stubbornness, the stubbornness of a man who had been told that
the foundation of his life had been destroyed and who refused
to fall because falling would be a concession that the
destruction was final, and the destruction was final in this
reality but not in his, and his reality was the one he would
return to, and in his reality Minerva was alive and whole and
her light shone and her gift flowed and the clasp at his throat
would be warm again.

He released the clasp. He picked up the quill. He wrote.

*Minerva destroyed. Elysium collapsed. Dimensional scar
permanent. Eight centuries. No healing.*

The words were steady. The hand that wrote them was not.

The recitation continued.

"He fought well. It did not matter."

Aizune absorbed this. The theological implications branched in

vassals. In Mazariim's reality, Taovan ruled Hell independently,
his evil balanced against the good of other entities in the
cosmic architecture that maintained the world's stability. Here,
the balance was gone. The good entities were dead. Evil served
evil, and the architecture had not collapsed because the Orceri
had built their own architecture, one that did not require
balance, one that required only power, and power they had in
abundance.

"Quarzel," Aizune said. "The entity of war."

The interdimensional conquests came next, and the telling shifted
from testimony to analysis, Giganzalt's voice losing the weight
of witnessed trauma and gaining the precision of observed data,
the difference between a being describing events that had broken
something inside him and a being describing events that had
fascinated something inside him, because the conquests, unlike
the War and the War of the Heavens, had not been personal. They
had been the slow, methodical expansion of an empire into
territories that the dragon had never seen and whose inhabitants
he had never known, and the distance allowed him the clarity
that proximity had denied.

Eleven worlds.

Giganzalt catalogued them with the systematic attention of a
being who had spent centuries assembling information from
whatever sources his mountain exile permitted: overheard
communications, the behaviour patterns of Orceri military
movements visible from altitude, the occasional captured
intelligence that filtered up from the lowlands through means
he did not disclose.

Three months. A civilisation built across centuries, consumed in
three months, its innovations absorbed, its people processed,
its identity dissolved into the machinery of an empire that
viewed it not as a civilisation but as a resource.

Aizune's quill paused. He looked up. The blue eyes met his
across the dim cavern, and in the meeting was a shared
understanding that did not require articulation: the dragon
was telling him this because it mattered, because the
connection between the conquered psionic elves and the enslaved
sensorium elves was not incidental but architectural, a
deliberate design decision made by beings who saw no meaningful
distinction between one set of elves and another, because the
distinction required the recognition of personhood, and the
Orceri did not recognise personhood in anything that was not
Orceri.

"The third," Giganzalt continued. "A world of beasstss. Large

Each world a capability. Each conquest a tool added to the
arsenal. The gnomish world gave them engineering. The psionic
world gave them surveillance. The beast world gave them weapons.
And the pattern was clear, the pattern was systematic, the
pattern was the operational logic of a civilisation that viewed
conquest not as an end but as a means, not as glory but as
acquisition, each new reality a shelf in a store from which
the Orceri took what they needed and discarded what they did not.

"An Akovarian world. A reality where the Akovarian civilissation

Aizune wrote. His journal was filling, page after page, the
Elda'lam script growing smaller as the history grew larger, as
the scope of what the Orceri had built expanded beyond the
boundaries of what any single reality could contain, because
the Orceri's empire did not exist in a single reality. It
existed across eleven, and the combined resources of those
eleven realities, absorbed and integrated and optimised over
four centuries, had produced a civilisation whose power was not
measured in armies or spells or territory but in the
accumulated capability of every world it had consumed.

---

"And Mazariim," Aizune said. The word felt strange in his
mouth: the name of his own world spoken in a cave in a world
where his own world was a target, a designation, a number in
a sequence. "The twelfth."

"The twelfth." Giganzalt's voice carried something new on the
word, something that had not been present during the
enumeration of the previous eleven. Not concern, precisely.
Dragons did not express concern the way mortal beings
expressed it, through the tightening of the voice and the
narrowing of the eyes and the involuntary signals of a body
registering proximity to threat. What Giganzalt's voice
carried was attention, the focused engagement of an
intelligence that had spent centuries in passive observation
and was now, for the first time in that span, actively
interested in an outcome.

The assessment was delivered without emotion. It was not a
warning. It was not a boast. It was not the dramatised
escalation of a storyteller building tension. It was the
clinical evaluation of a being whose survival for eight
centuries had depended on the accurate assessment of threats,
and whose assessment of this particular threat carried the
weight of a lifetime spent measuring power and finding, in
this one instance, that the measurement exceeded the scale.

Aizune wrote the assessment in his journal. The words were
small and precise and they described something vast and
terrible and the smallness of the words and the vastness of
the thing they described created a dissonance that lived on
the page the way the cold of the clasp lived at his throat:
a fact that could be recorded but not contained.

*Rangithyr. Godslayer. Personally destroyed entities.
Giganzalt's assessment: most dangerous mortal being in
existence. Possibly unkillable.*

---

The hours passed. The cave's dim luminescence shifted as
the geothermal warmth pulsed in cycles that Aizune could
not measure but could feel, the stone beneath him growing
warmer and cooler in a rhythm that was the earth's breathing,
slow and vast, the respiration of a body so large that its
exhalations were measured in geological time.

Giganzalt spoke of biomancy in detail: the Far Realm
derivation, the techniques of genetic manipulation that
transcended anything Mazariim's scholars had conceived, the
systematic application of biological engineering to the
problem of optimising conquered populations. He spoke of the
sensorium network: its architecture, its limitations, its
sensitivity to mana signatures that deviated from established
parameters. He spoke of the military hierarchy: the command
structure beneath Rangithyr, the generals and the biomancers
and the skymages and the assassins who constituted the empire's
operational capability.

And Aizune wrote. He filled page after page of his prayer
journal, the Elda'lam script becoming a map of an empire that
spanned realities, a diagram of the machine that had consumed
eleven worlds and was preparing to consume a twelfth. The
writing was an act of discipline. Every word he recorded was
a word he had heard and processed and understood, and the
understanding was painful, and the pain was useful, because
pain confirmed that he was present and engaged and that the
information was reaching the part of his mind that would use
it, the analytical part that Minerva's training had sharpened
and that operated now, in her absence, on the reserves of a
lifetime of practice.

When Giganzalt fell silent, the silence was different from the
silence that had preceded the telling. It was heavier. It was
the silence of a room in which a great weight had been
deposited, and the weight was knowledge, and knowledge occupied
space, and the space it occupied in Aizune's mind was the space
where certainty lived, the space that had been partially filled
by the machine's vision and the spy's gear and Aleron's
testimony and was now completely filled by the comprehensive,
devastating, irrefutable testimony of a being who had watched
it happen.

He knew now. He knew what they faced. He knew the scale of it
and the nature of it and the history behind it and the capability
within it. He knew that the empire they were preparing to
resist had four hundred years of interdimensional conquest
experience and technology from eleven absorbed civilisations
and biomantic weapons and mana-stripped divine energy and sky
citadels and a military commanded by a being who had personally
destroyed multiple entities. He knew all of this with the
settled, immovable certainty of a man who had received testimony
from an unimpeachable witness, and the certainty did not comfort
him, because certainty about a catastrophe was not comfort but
clarity, and clarity without the power to act on it was the
particular torment of the informed.

He closed the journal. The pages were full. His hand ached from
the hours of writing. The cavern was quiet, the dragon's vast
form motionless in the dust, the blue eyes watching him with the
patience that was not patience but existence, the dragon's
default state, the way of being that had sustained him through
eight centuries of hiding and would sustain him through whatever
came next.

"You have given me more than intelligence," Aizune said. He
looked at the dragon and the dragon looked at him and the
exchange was direct and unmediated by pretence, two beings
facing each other across the rubble of a world that one of them
had watched fall and the other was trying to prevent from
falling. "You have given me certainty. I know now what we face.
And knowing is the beginning of standing against it."

"Knowing," Giganzalt said, and the sibilants were quiet, almost
absent, the ancient voice reduced to something that was nearly,
for a dragon, intimate, "iss also the beginning of dessspair.
The dissstinction between the two beginningss iss a choice, and
the choice iss yourss, and I am..." The blue eyes closed. Opened.
The slowest blink. "I am curiousss to ssee which you choosse."

Aizune stood. His legs protested: hours of sitting on stone in a
body that was not accustomed to immobility. His robes were
stiff with dust. The prayer journal hung heavy at his hip, full
of a history that was the heaviest thing he had ever carried,
heavier than the spirit-touched blade at his other hip, heavier
than the cold clasp at his throat.

He looked at the clasp. He touched it. Cold. It would be cold
until they crossed back, until the boundary between realities
was breached again and the connection that ran from his chest
to Elysium found its other end, found the living entity, found
the warmth and the light and the wisdom that eight centuries of
absence had not diminished in the reality where she survived.

He carried the knowledge of her death in a world where she was
dead and the faith in her life in a world where she was alive,
and the two facts coexisted in his chest without contradiction,
because faith was not the denial of facts but the refusal to
let facts determine the shape of one's response, and his
response was not despair.

His response was the choice that the dragon was curious to see.

"Respectfully, good dragon," Aizune said, and the formality
was his anchor, the measured diction of a priest of Minerva
addressing a being of power, the words carrying the composure
that his heart did not feel and his training required and his
will enforced, "I did not come here to despair. I came here
to learn what we face so that we may stand against it. You
have taught me. And when my companions return, and we carry
you to our world, the knowledge you have given me will be
the foundation on which we build whatever defence our world
can muster."

He paused. The cave was very quiet. The dust settled.

"Minerva teaches that wisdom survives what cannot be survived.
In this world, she is dead, and her teaching survives in me,
because I carry it, and I will carry it back to the world
where she is alive, and in that world her teaching will not
be a memory but a practice, and the practice will be the
standing against what is coming."

Giganzalt regarded him for a long time. The blue eyes were
the brightest things in the chamber: ancient, deep, carrying
the weight of centuries and the particular quality of
attention that developed in a consciousness that had
evaluated the courage of countless beings across the span
of its existence and had learned to distinguish the courage
that sustained from the courage that burned out.

"You are either very brave or very foolissh," the dragon said.
The sibilants were soft. The coiling quality was gentle. And
beneath both, so far beneath that it might have been
imagined, something that was neither amusement nor assessment
but the ancient, enormous, carefully concealed phenomenon of
a dragon who had spent eight hundred years expecting nothing
discovering, in a cave in the mountains of a conquered world,
something that exceeded the expectation.

"I have told you," Giganzalt said. "The dissstinction iss
academic."

The dust settled. The cave breathed. The clasp was cold.

And Aizune Silverwind, priest of a goddess who was dead in
this world and alive in his, stood in the darkness with his
journal full and his faith intact and his understanding
rebuilt from the foundations up, and waited for his
companions to return from the world the orcs had made.
