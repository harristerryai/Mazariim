# Chapter Eight
## The Enemy on Our Soil

The dream was not a dream.

Aleron knew this because dreams, even the dreams that bled
through the fractures of his perception from realities adjacent
to his own, carried a particular quality of displacement, a
looseness at the edges where the image frayed into the fabric
of the unconscious, and what woke him in the small hours of a
Mazariim night in the room above Eccentricities carried no
looseness at all. It was sharp. It was specific. It was the
sensation of something being forced through a barrier that was
not designed to open, the dimensional equivalent of a blade
driven through leather: precise, deliberate, and wrong in a way
that bypassed the intellect and went directly to the part of
his body where Thalaman's gift lived, the part that felt the
shape of reality the way a sailor felt the shape of the sea,
and the shape had changed.

He sat upright. The quarterstaff was in his hand before his
eyes were fully open, the pale ashwood finding his palm with
the automatic certainty of seventy years of dependence, and
his other hand went to the amulet at his throat, the silver
and gold wire warm against his fingers, the three rubies
pulsing green in the darkness with the steady rhythm that
matched his heartbeat, and his heartbeat was fast now, faster
than the amulet's containment preferred, and the green pulse
stuttered once before finding the new tempo.

The echoes were screaming.

Not the usual murmur of adjacent realities bleeding through
the fractures, not the translucent figures and half-heard
conversations that were the constant background noise of his
existence, but a cacophony, the dimensional fabric around him
vibrating with the resonance of something that had been
punched through it from the other side. He could feel the
direction the way he had felt Remular's activation from Fort
Hope: a vector burned into his chest with the precision of a
compass needle, northeast, and the distance was not hundreds
of miles but dozens, close enough that the vibration carried
detail, and the detail was military.

Not one crossing. Many. Forty, perhaps more, individual
signatures punching through in rapid sequence, each one a
small violence against the boundary, each one leaving a wound
that would heal but that the healing could not erase entirely,
the scar tissue accumulating the way scar tissue accumulated
on flesh, and the pattern was not random but engineered, the
crossings spaced at intervals that spoke of technology and
discipline and the systematic application of a process that
had been refined across four hundred years of practice.

He was on his feet. His legs protested. The baseline that had
settled after the return from Ta'Puh, the new diminished
normal that was all his body could sustain after carrying
seven beings across the dimensional boundary, registered the
sudden demand and responded with a tremor that started in his
knees and climbed to his hands and would have reached his
voice if he had allowed it.

He did not allow it.

He crossed the corridor and knocked on Aizune's door. The
knock was three raps, hard, the sound of a man who did not
have time for courtesy. The door opened in four seconds.
Aizune stood in his mithril shirt with his robes half-
gathered, the Minerva clasp warm and bright at his throat,
his dark grey eyes sharp with the alertness of someone who
slept lightly and woke completely, the way Cundo'elda
training had taught him.

"They are here." Aleron's voice was steady. Both eyes were
focused, grey irises locked on the priest's face with the
startling clarity that his companions had learned to
recognise as his most capable state: the lucid window, the
fractures aligned, the intelligence behind them fully
present. "They have crossed. A small force. Northeast. Two
days' ride." He paused. The steadiness held. "We do not have
two days."

Aizune did not ask how he knew. He touched the clasp at his
throat, the warm silver a confirmation that needed no
articulation, and said: "Wake the others."

---

They gathered in the common room in minutes.

Smiggles arrived first, which was characteristic, the gnome's
sleep patterns governed less by biological rhythm than by
the restless turning of an analytical mind that considered
unconsciousness a waste of processing time. He wore his
burgundy waistcoat over his nightshirt, which was the only
concession to the hour; his boots were on, his satchel was
over his shoulder, and his moustache, somehow, was waxed.
The magnificent Salvador Dali curves caught the candlelight
and held it with the defiance of a man whose vanity did not
recognise emergencies.

"How many," he said. Not a question. A demand for data.

"Forty signatures. Perhaps more. The crossing pattern is
engineered. Intervals suggest military deployment protocol."
Aleron stood at the centre of the room with his staff
planted and both hands wrapped around it and the tremor in
his fingers visible only to those who knew to look for it.
"Northeast. The vibration carries the particular density of
the Orceri dimensional frequency. I felt it in Ta'Puh. This
is the same. They are here."

Iskar came through the door with his rapier already belted,
his long coat settling around his shoulders, his hat in his
hand. His dark eyes were sharp and his jaw was set and the
rolling gait that was his resting state carried none of its
usual looseness. He had slept in his boots. This was not
paranoia. This was the habit of a sailor who had learned that
the sea did not announce its storms with sufficient notice
for the luxury of dressing.

"Where?" he said.

"Northeast. On the road toward the Jinook Valley. A small
town. I can feel the boundary wounds clustering around a
fixed point." Aleron closed his eyes. Opened them. One eye
drifted briefly toward something that was not there, then
returned. "They are not moving. They have arrived at their
target and they are holding position. This is not a raid. It
is a deployment."

"Winderbourgh," Iskar said. The name came without hesitation,
the bard's knowledge of the trade routes between Amara and
Instar producing the answer before the question was fully
formed. "Crossroads town. Two days northeast. Farming
community. A thousand people, perhaps twelve hundred. Low
wall. No garrison. No mage. Nothing."

The word nothing settled into the room.

Smiggles' fingers drummed once on the table, fast, the speed
of calculation rather than nervousness. "Phase one," he said.
"Rangithyr's probe. Assess response time, force composition,
magical capability. This is the intelligence-gathering strike
that any competent military commander would deploy before a
full invasion." His hazel eyes were sharp and focused and
showed none of the warmth that sometimes softened them when
the analytical engine behind them was not running at full
capacity. "They chose Winderbourgh because it does not
matter. A small town with no strategic value. Clean data.
Minimal risk to the probe force."

"Then we go," Aizune said. He was dressed. The layered robes
of forest green and deep grey, the mithril shirt visible at
collar and cuffs, the spirit-touched elven curved blade at
his hip with its pale shimmer barely visible in the
candlelight, the longbow and quiver across his back. His
prayer journal was at his belt. The clasp was warm. He was
ready. "Aleron. Can you carry us?"

The question was simple. The answer was not.

Aleron looked at the priest, and in the looking was every
transit that had come before, the cumulative cost written in
the translucence of his skin and the tremor of his hands and
the new baseline that would not recover, the permanent
diminishment that each major use of the gift extracted from
a body that had been paying for seventy years and whose
reserves were no longer reserves but the last coins in a
purse that would not be refilled.

"I can carry you," he said. "The cost will be significant."

"You said that before Ta'Puh," Smiggles said. "And the cost
was 'everything.' What is significant, on a scale where
everything is the previous maximum?"

"Less than everything." The ghost of a smile. "I will be
functional afterward. I will not be useful."

Lai appeared in the doorway. She had not been in the
building. Where she had been was, as with many things
concerning her, unexplained. She wore her dark brown leather
armour and her green-black-brown hair was wild and her yellow
eyes were wide and bright in the candlelight, the vertical
pupils dilated, and she said: "I felt it. The boundary. It
tore." She looked at Aleron. The twenty paces between them
was maintained even in the narrow room, her position at the
threshold precise, the buffer that their incompatible natures
demanded honoured even in urgency. "How many?"

"Forty."

"Disciplined?"

"Engineered crossing intervals. Military pattern."

She nodded once. The playful energy that was her usual
register was absent. What stood in the doorway was something
older and less comfortable: the predator, assessing the
territory, calculating the threat. "Then we should move.
Before they finish whatever they came to do."

---

The transit broke him.

Not metaphorically. The word broke was precise and clinical
and described what happened to the internal architecture of
a man who had been carrying a gift that was slowly consuming
him for seven decades and who now, in a room above a shop in
Amara, gathered five people within arm's reach and held a
tuning fork that was not attuned to the dimensional frequency
of the target but to the frequency of the boundary wound
itself, the scar that the Orceri crossing had left in the
fabric of reality northeast of Amara, and used that scar as
a beacon the way a moth used a flame, and the comparison was
apt because the moth did not survive the arrival.

The fork sang. The note was displacement. The room stretched
and compressed simultaneously. Amara vanished.

Cold air. Smoke. The smell of burning thatch and something
beneath it that was sharper, chemical, the acrid signature
of mana-enhanced combustion that did not occur in nature
and did not belong on Mazariim soil. Sound: distant
screaming, the thin high sound of civilians who had been
woken by violence and had no framework for understanding
what was happening to them. Closer: the rhythmic crunch of
boots on cobblestone, dozens of feet moving in unison, the
sound of disciplined infantry advancing through a town that
had been designed for farmers and was being occupied by an
army.

They stood at the western edge of Winderbourgh, on the road
where it passed through a gap in the low crumbling wall. The
crossroads town assembled itself in the predawn darkness: one
and two story buildings of local stone and timber, clay tile
roofs in the Sabonaar style, the market pavilion at the
central square visible as a dark silhouette against the glow
of fires that should not have been burning. The Crossed Keys
inn was ablaze, its upper floor engulfed, the flames casting
orange light across the cobbled square and the bodies that
lay on the cobbles, three, four, five figures in nightclothes
who had come out to see what was happening and had found the
answer.

Aleron went down.

His knees struck the road and his staff clattered from his
hands and his body folded with the boneless descent that his
companions had seen twice before, once in the mountains of
Ta'Puh and once on the mountainside east of Amara, but this
was worse than both because both of those collapses had left
something, some residual function, some base layer of
consciousness that could be called upon to speak or move or
at least acknowledge the world around him, and this collapse
left nothing visible. His eyes were open. His mouth moved.
The words that came from it belonged to conversations in
realities that had nothing to do with Winderbourgh, fragments
of a dozen overlapping planes flooding through fractures that
the transit had blown wide: "... cannot hold the wall if
the... no, the water is rising, tell them the water is...
where is she, where did she... I told you the bridge would
not..."

Aizune knelt beside him. His hand found Aleron's face and
turned it, the grey eyes open and seeing everything and
nothing, and Aizune's other hand pressed against the elf's
forehead and the silver-white light of Minerva's healing
bloomed from his palm, clean and fierce and gentle, the
divine energy flowing through a connection that had been
severed in Ta'Puh and restored in Mazariim and was now,
in the predawn darkness of a town under attack, the only
thing standing between Aleron Windstrider and the complete
dissolution of a consciousness that had been fractured for
seventy years and was now shattering.

"Aleron. You are here. Winderbourgh. Mazariim. The stone is
real." The words were the anchor. Aizune had learned them in
the mountains and on the mountainside and he spoke them now
with the steady measured authority that was not from his
throat alone but from the entity whose warmth flowed through
his palm. "Stay with me."

"Stay," Aleron whispered. The word was from this reality.
The eyes focused, briefly, on Aizune's face. "Stay. Yes. I
will... stay."

"He cannot fight," Smiggles said. The assessment was rapid,
clinical, delivered without cruelty and without softness
because both were luxuries the moment did not afford. "He
cannot stand. He is out. We are four."

"We are four," Iskar confirmed. His rapier was drawn. The
Sabonaar steel caught the firelight, the wave pattern on
the guard rippling with reflected flame, and his dark eyes
were on the square where the Orceri moved, and his face
carried the expression that his companions had learned to
recognise not as fear and not as excitement but as the
particular state of a man whose body had been trained for
violence and whose heart had been trained for kindness and
who was, in this moment, choosing which training to deploy.

"Four and a town," Aizune said. He looked up from Aleron.
The grey eyes that occasionally flashed silver when Minerva's
sight moved through him flashed silver now, the divine lens
engaging, the deeper structure of the scene revealing itself
in layers that mortal perception could not access. "There
are people alive in those buildings. There are people with
weapons. Hunting bows. Axes. This town has a militia."

"Oy," Smiggles said. "It has farmers with sharp objects."

"Then we use what we have." Aizune stood. He placed Aleron
against the wall beside the gap, the elf's body propped on
the stone with his staff laid across his knees, the amulet
pulsing green at his throat, the echoes still murmuring
from his lips but quieter now, the healing light having
purchased a stability that was fragile and temporary and
better than nothing. "Smiggles. Elevation. The granary roof
gives you line of sight to the square. Iskar. The militia.
Rally them. Get them organised. Lai." He looked at her. She
stood at the gap in the wall with her yellow eyes reflecting
the firelight and her hands empty because her hands did not
need to hold weapons. "Do what you do."

"Respectfully," Lai said, and the word carried the musical
lightness of her usual register layered over something
deeper, something that resonated in a lower harmonic than
a wood elf's voice should have been capable of producing,
"I intend to."

---

Smiggles reached the granary roof in ninety seconds.

The building was the sturdiest in Winderbourgh: stone walls,
partially below ground, a flat roof accessible by an exterior
ladder that a gnome could climb faster than a human because
a gnome's centre of gravity was lower and a gnome's hands,
precise and quick and shaped by decades of spellwork and
clockwork and the particular fine-motor discipline of a
people who built watches smaller than a thumb, found the
rungs with an efficiency that his three-foot frame belied.
He crested the roof and dropped to his stomach and the town
spread before him in the firelight: the burning inn, the
cobbled square, the dark shapes of buildings, and the Orceri.

They were not what he expected.

He had seen Orceri in their town. He had walked among them in
borrowed skin and watched them buy provisions and ignore
their enslaved and pass beneath surveillance constructs with
the casual indifference of citizens in a civilisation that
had won everything. Those Orceri had been civilians.
Shopkeepers. Parents. Beings going about the mundane business
of daily life in a world that ran on the stolen power of dead
entities.

These were not civilians.

They moved through the square in units of eight, each unit
operating with the coordinated precision of a machine whose
components had been designed to function together and had been
functioning together for long enough that the functioning was
not cooperation but instinct. Their armour was dark, form-
fitted, covering torso and limbs without the bulk that
Mazariim's heaviest plate imposed, the material absorbing the
firelight rather than reflecting it. Their weapons were
standardised: heavy curved blades at the hip, short stabbing
spears in hand, the blades carrying the faint cold shimmer
of mana enhancement that Smiggles recognised from Kuulatch's
gear, from the streetlamps, from the scimitar in Rangithyr's
hand, the bluish light that was the signature of a
civilisation that had stripped the divine and repurposed it
as infrastructure.

Five units visible. Forty soldiers. They had secured the
square and were moving outward in paired sweeps, two units
north, two south, one holding the centre. The movement
pattern was systematic: clear, hold, advance. Each doorway
checked. Each building entered briefly and exited. They were
not killing indiscriminately. Smiggles noted this with the
clinical detachment of an analyst who understood that the
absence of indiscriminate killing was not mercy but method.
They were gathering data. Testing response. Recording
capability. The bodies on the cobbles were not victims of
rage but of resistance: people who had come out and been
assessed as threats and been eliminated with the minimum
force necessary to remove the threat and no more, because
excess force was waste, and the Orceri did not waste.

A sixth figure stood apart.

Taller than the infantry, robed rather than armoured, its
hands moving in patterns that Smiggles recognised instantly
because the patterns were spellcasting, the particular
geometries of someone manipulating mana through somatic
components, and the mana that responded was not the warm
gold of arcane tradition or the cool silver of divine
practice but something else, something that registered on
Smiggles' habitual detection sweep as a frequency he had
not encountered before: dense, layered, carrying the
biosignature of living tissue reshaped at the fundamental
level.

The biomancer.

Its hands completed a gesture and the cobblestones of the
square rippled. Not cracked, not shattered, but rippled,
the stone becoming temporarily fluid, the surface
undulating in a wave that spread outward from the
biomancer's position and solidified in a new configuration:
a low barrier of raised stone, two feet tall, curving
around the central unit's position, providing cover that
had not existed three seconds earlier. The biomancer had
reshaped the ground the way a potter reshaped clay,
transmuting the physical structure of the road with a
casual precision that spoke of a discipline Mazariim's
mages had never developed because the discipline required
knowledge from a plane of existence that Mazariim's
scholars had never accessed.

Smiggles filed this. The analytical engine turned. The
threat matrix assembled itself with the rapid efficiency
of a mind that processed danger the way other minds
processed arithmetic: automatically, precisely, and
without the interference of the fear that the mind was
simultaneously experiencing and that the engine required
to wait its turn.

Forty infantry. One biomancer. And somewhere in the town,
the acolyte that Orceri doctrine attached to every
company-strength deployment: a divine caster whose
channelling would be negative energy, whose healing would
sustain the infantry, whose presence elevated a dangerous
force to a lethal one.

He placed his hands flat on the granary roof. The stone
was cool beneath his palms. His fingers spread into the
precise geometry that three syllables of Draconic
required, and the three syllables were the foundation of
the working that had served him in the Khessar Tunnels
and at Zanthar's Keep and in every engagement since he
had completed his training and discovered that the
particular intersection of intellect and violence that
combat spellcasting demanded was, despite everything his
instructors at the Academy had said about his temperament,
something he was very, very good at.

"Right," he said. The word was inaudible. The moustache
twitched. "Let us see how their mana-enhanced armour
handles thermal overload."

---

Iskar found the militia in the granary basement.

Twenty-three of them. Farmers. A blacksmith. Two women
who carried hunting bows with the practised ease of people
who had been shooting rabbits since childhood and whose
understanding of the weapon was practical rather than
martial. A boy of perhaps sixteen holding a woodcutting
axe whose head was larger than his own. An old man with
a short sword that had belonged to his grandfather and
that he held with the particular grip of someone who had
been shown once how to hold it and had remembered the
lesson imperfectly.

They were terrified. The terror was visible in the way
they held their weapons: too tight, the knuckles white,
the muscles of forearm and shoulder locked in the
particular rigidity of bodies that had been seized by
fear and that fear had not released. Their eyes were wide
and their breathing was fast and the smell of the
granary, grain dust and cool stone and the sharp
undertone of sweat, was thick with the accumulated
exhalation of two dozen people who had been hiding in
the dark and listening to the sounds of their town being
taken.

"My frrriends," Iskar said.

The warmth was deliberate. The voice was pitched to
carry, not through volume but through the particular
resonance that a bard's training cultivated, the quality
that was not magic exactly but was adjacent to it, the
capacity of the human voice to enter a room and change
what the room contained. The Sabonaar accent rolled the
R and softened the consonants that followed it, and the
effect was physical, measurable, the locked muscles in
two dozen bodies easing by a fraction that was
insufficient for combat but sufficient for listening.

"I know this is not the morning you planned." He moved
among them, his boots quiet on the stone floor, his
rapier sheathed, his hands visible and open, the posture
of a man who was not here to command but to stand beside.
"I know you are not soldiers. I know your wall is low
and your weapons are what you had to hand and the things
in your square are not the things your wall was built to
stop." He stopped beside the boy with the axe. The boy
looked up. Dark eyes. Sabonaar features. The face of
every boy Iskar had seen on the docks of Amara, the
face of the child in the Orceri town carrying bread in
a grey tunic, except this child's eyes were not empty.
This child's eyes were full. Full of fear, and fear was
a thing that could be used, because fear meant the child
still cared whether he lived, and caring was the raw
material from which courage was manufactured.

"What is your name?" Iskar asked.

"Tovar," the boy said.

"Tovar. That is a good name. Tovar, how long have you
been cutting wood?"

"Three years."

"Three years." Iskar looked at the axe. "In three years,
have you ever missed?"

"No."

"Then you will not miss tonight." He placed his hand on
the boy's shoulder, brief, firm, the contact carrying
the warmth that the Sabonaar maritime tradition invested
in physical gesture, the handshake and the shoulder-grip
and the embrace that sailors exchanged before storms
because the body remembered comfort longer than the mind
remembered words. "Stay close to me. Swing when I tell
you. Do not think about what you are swinging at. Think
about the three years of wood that taught your arms where
to go."

He turned to the room. Twenty-three faces. Twenty-three
weapons. Twenty-three reasons to be elsewhere and one
reason, the only one that mattered, to be here: the
town above them, the homes above them, the people above
them who were hiding in cellars and attics and behind
locked doors, the ordinary architecture of ordinary lives
that was not ordinary anymore and would not be ordinary
again unless the twenty-three people in this basement and
the four people who had arrived from nowhere decided that
ordinary was worth defending.

"I am Iskar of the Cobalt Tide," he said. The declaration
was not a battle cry. It was an introduction. "I have
come a very long way to stand in this basement with you,
and I intend to walk out of it with all of you beside
me. The things in your square are strong. They are
disciplined. They are not from this world." A murmur.
Fear, sharpening. "But they bleed. My friends are above
us and my friends have the particular talent of making
things bleed that would prefer not to. What I need from
you is simple. When the bleeding starts, stand with us.
Hold the line I give you. Do not advance. Do not retreat.
Hold, and let my friends do the work that they were
trained for."

The blacksmith, a Sabonaar woman whose arms were thicker
than Iskar's thighs, said: "And if they push through
your line?"

Iskar looked at her. His dark eyes were warm and his
smile was the smile that he had deployed in a hundred
taverns and a dozen boarding actions and one common room
in Amara when he had first met a gnome whose moustache
defied the laws of physics, and the smile said: I am
here, and being here is what I do, and you are not alone.

"Then I suggest swinging verrry hard."

The blacksmith's mouth twitched. Not a smile. Not yet.
But the muscle that controlled the smile had been
engaged, and the engagement was permission, and
permission was the first step toward the thing that
twenty-three terrified people in a granary basement
needed more than weapons or armour or walls.

It was the belief that they could fight.

---

The fireball announced the battle.

Six syllables of Draconic, each consonant struck clean,
pitched low and hard, the words shaped by hands that
were spread flat on the granary roof in the precise
geometry that the working demanded, and the bead of
orange-white light that formed between Smiggles' palms
was small and hot and carried the compressed fury of a
gnome who had walked through an Orceri town and seen
what civilisation looked like when it was built on the
bones of every other civilisation and who was, in this
moment, applying the only response his training and his
temperament and his magnificent moustache permitted.

The bead crossed the square in a flat arc and detonated
in the centre of the Orceri formation.

The thunderclap of superheated air shattered every
remaining window facing the square. The fireball
expanded in a sphere of orange-white fury, twenty feet
in diameter, the heat visible as a shimmer that distorted
the air and the firelight and the shapes of the Orceri
soldiers who occupied the space where the sphere bloomed.
Eight soldiers in the central unit caught the full force.
Their mana-enhanced armour absorbed a portion that would
have killed unprotected beings instantly, the dark
material flaring with bluish resistance as the stripped
entity power within it fought the thermal overload, but
the portion was not sufficient. Three went down. Two more
staggered, their armour smoking, the mana enhancement
exhausted by the single impact. The remaining three
pivoted toward the granary with the reflexive discipline
of soldiers whose training included the response to
arcane bombardment, and the pivoting was fast, faster
than Smiggles had calculated, and the calculation
revised itself in the fraction of a second between the
detonation and the first Orceri crossbow bolt striking
the granary wall six inches from his left ear.

"Gears and grindstones," Smiggles said. He rolled left.
A second bolt passed through the space his head had
occupied. He came up on one knee and his hands were
already moving, the somatic geometry shifting from the
broad bilateral spread of the thermal working to the
tight interlocking pattern that three syllables of a
different Draconic formula required, and the three golden
bolts that streaked from his fingertips struck the
nearest Orceri soldier in the chest with the precise,
unerring accuracy that the working guaranteed, and the
soldier staggered but did not fall, and the not-falling
was the data point that Smiggles filed with the grim
satisfaction of an analyst confirming a hypothesis he
had hoped was wrong.

They were tough. The infantry absorbed the force bolts
the way a stone wall absorbed a thrown pebble: with
damage, with acknowledgement, and with the structural
integrity to remain standing afterward. Their mana
resistance was higher than anything Smiggles had
encountered in the Merchant Nations. His Mid Tier
workings, which had dispatched Kru'ka'ta's orcs with
casual efficiency, were adequate here. Not overwhelming.
Adequate. And adequate, against forty disciplined
soldiers from a civilisation that had conquered eleven
worlds, was a margin so thin that the word margin was
generous.

"That doesn't calibrate," he muttered. "Their armour
mana is higher than Kuulatch's personal gear. They have
improved since we last looked." He flattened himself as
a third bolt struck the roof beside him. "Fine. Fine.
Thermal overload works but requires concentration. Force
works but requires volume. This is going to be
expensive."

---

Iskar led the militia out of the granary as the second
fireball struck.

The timing was not accidental. Smiggles' assault drew
the Orceri's attention upward and eastward, toward the
granary, and the drawing created a window on the western
approach where twenty-three people with hunting bows and
woodcutting axes and one Sabonaar bard with a rapier and
a voice that carried further than steel could reach
emerged from the stone building and formed the line that
Iskar had described in the basement: a shallow arc across
the western road, the buildings on either side channelling
any advance into a corridor that the bows could cover.

"Hold," Iskar said. The word was quiet and it carried.
The militia held. Their weapons were raised. Their
knuckles were white. But they held.

The Orceri northern sweep, two units of eight, turned
south toward the engagement. They moved through the
residential streets with the systematic pace that
Smiggles had observed from the roof: clear, hold,
advance. The first unit reached the western road and
the lead soldier, a being seven feet tall with dark
armour and a mana-enhanced curved blade and the composed
face of a professional who had conducted operations
like this on other worlds and found them routine, saw
the militia and assessed them in the time it took his
eyes to sweep the line, and the assessment was visible
in his posture: the slight relaxation, the blade
lowering by a degree, the body language of a predator
that had identified prey and was not concerned.

Iskar saw the assessment. The bard in him, the man
whose life had been built on reading people, on gauging
the shift of a shoulder and the angle of a blade and
the quality of attention in an enemy's eyes, read the
relaxation and understood what it meant: the Orceri
officer had looked at twenty-three farmers and a man
with a rapier and concluded that the engagement was
already won.

"You have," Iskar said, and his voice changed, the
Sabonaar warmth igniting into something hotter, the
bardic resonance that was not magic exactly but was
adjacent to it filling the street the way the firelight
filled the square, "the singularr misforrtune of
facing Iskar of the Cobalt Tide."

Two things happened simultaneously.

The first was that the militia, which had been trembling,
stopped trembling. Not because the fear left them but
because the voice that had entered the street had brought
something with it that was stronger than fear, a quality
that the bardic tradition called inspiration and that was,
in practice, the simple and devastating power of a human
being who believed in what he was doing telling other
human beings that they could believe too, and the telling
was not rhetoric but frequency, the voice carrying a
resonance that settled into the muscles and the nerves
and the part of the brain where courage lived and
activated it the way a key activated a lock.

The second was that Lai dropped from a rooftop onto the
Orceri officer's back.

She did not shapechange. The calculation behind the
decision was visible in the two seconds between her
departure from the roof and her arrival on the officer's
shoulders: dragon form in a residential street would
destroy buildings and kill civilians hiding inside them;
humanoid form preserved the architecture and limited the
collateral. She landed with her knees on the officer's
pauldrons and both hands on either side of his helmeted
head and she spoke a single word of Draconic, one
syllable, and the word was not a spell name because
spell names did not exist in prose but the effect was
immediate and devastating: the officer's body locked, the
muscles seizing, the mana-enhanced armour flaring with
bluish light as the draconic working punched through
its resistance with a force that the armour had not been
designed to withstand because the armour had been
designed to resist arcane and divine assault and what
Lai delivered was neither, it was draconic, and draconic
magic operated on a frequency that the Orceri, for all
their four hundred years of conquest, had not
encountered from a source this close and this angry.

The officer went rigid and fell. Lai rolled clear and
was on her feet before the body hit the cobbles, and
the seven remaining soldiers of the first unit saw what
had happened to their commander and the assessment that
had relaxed them evaporated, and what replaced it was
the recalibration of professionals who had just been
shown that the engagement was not routine.

"Bows," Iskar called. "Now."

Twenty arrows arced from the militia line. The distance
was thirty feet. At thirty feet, a hunting bow in the
hands of someone who had been shooting rabbits for
twenty years did not miss. The arrows struck mana-
enhanced armour and most bounced or shattered, the
dark material absorbing the impact, but five found
gaps, joints, the neck above the gorget, the inner
thigh below the tasset, and the Orceri who were struck
staggered and the staggering was the proof that Iskar
had needed and the militia had needed: they bleed.

"Again."

Twenty arrows. The Orceri raised shields, interlocking,
the defensive formation assembling with the mechanical
speed of a reflex trained into the body across years of
drill. Arrows rattled off dark metal. But the formation
was static, and static was what Smiggles had been
waiting for.

Three syllables from the granary roof. Three golden
bolts. Then three more. Then three more. The force
workings struck the shield formation at angles that the
shields could not simultaneously cover, the unerring
accuracy of the formula finding the gaps between the
interlocked surfaces, and two more soldiers fell and
the formation broke and the breaking was the moment
Iskar had been waiting for.

"Hold the line," he told the militia. "Do not advance.
Hold."

He advanced.

The rapier was a blur. The Sabonaar style was not the
heavy, committed strikes of a longswordsman or the
measured precision of an elven blade-dancer but something
faster and more fluid, the blade finding openings and
withdrawing and finding new openings with the restless
efficiency of water finding cracks in stone. An Orceri
soldier, seven feet tall, twice Iskar's mass, swung a
curved blade that would have split him from shoulder to
hip. He was not there. The rolling gait that concealed
his readiness expressed itself as lateral movement so
smooth it seemed he had always been standing two feet to
the left, and his rapier punched through the gap beneath
the soldier's raised arm and into the joint where the
armour's coverage failed, and the soldier grunted, the
sound deep and surprised, the sound of a professional
encountering a level of opposition that the briefing
had not prepared him for.

Iskar withdrew the blade and parried the second soldier's
strike and his left hand drew a throwing knife from his
sash and sent it spinning into the throat of a third
who was raising a crossbow, and the knife found the gap
above the gorget with the accuracy of a man who had been
throwing knives since the age of fourteen when the bosun
of the Cobalt Tide had shown him the trick of it and
told him that the sea rewarded people who could put
sharp things where they needed to go.

"Sangre y sal," Iskar breathed. The words were not a
battle cry. They were a prayer.

---

Aizune found the acolyte.

He found it because Minerva's sight, the divine lens
that the entity's renewed connection provided, perceived
the world in layers that mortal vision could not access,
and the layer that showed the flow of divine energy
showed him a node of power in the residential streets
north of the square: a concentration of channelled
negative energy that pulsed with the rhythm of someone
actively casting, sustaining, maintaining the working
that kept the Orceri infantry at combat readiness despite
the wounds they were accumulating.

The acolyte was in a doorway. Robed, like the biomancer,
but smaller, its hands raised in a gesture that was
prayer and was not prayer, the divine energy flowing
through conduits that were recognisable in structure if
alien in source. It was channelling. The negative energy
radiated outward in a sphere that encompassed the
nearest Orceri units, and within that sphere the soldiers
moved faster, struck harder, their wounds closing at a
rate that was not regeneration but accelerated knitting,
the cellular repair that healing magic facilitated pushed
to a tempo that should not have been sustainable and was
sustained by the acolyte's continuous channelling.

Aizune advanced through a residential alley. The spirit-
touched blade was in his right hand, its pale luminescence
brightening as it neared the concentration of negative
energy, the ghost-light responding to the proximity of
forces it had been forged to oppose. His left hand
touched the clasp at his throat. Warm silver. The
connection was open and the power flowed and the prayer
that formed in his mind was not words but intention, the
precise theological frequency that Minerva's tradition
channelled through the Cundo'elda, and the intention was:
enough.

He spoke four syllables of Draconic combined with two
words of Elda'lam, the divine formula emerging from his
throat with an authority that was not his alone, and the
wave of silver-white light that expanded from his
position struck the acolyte's channelling field and
shattered it.

The sound was not audible. The effect was. Every Orceri
soldier within the acolyte's sphere of influence
staggered simultaneously, the accelerated healing
ceasing, the wounds they had been accumulating suddenly
present and unmitigated, and three who had been fighting
through injuries that the channelling had been
suppressing collapsed as the suppression failed.

The acolyte turned toward Aizune. Its eyes were amber.
Its face was composed. It raised its hands and the
negative energy gathered between its palms with the
dense violet-black light that was the antithesis of
everything the clasp at Aizune's throat represented,
and the gathering was fast, professional, the response
of a combat caster who had been trained to fight and
was fighting now.

Aizune did not stop. He closed the distance at a run,
the spirit-touched blade leading, and the blade's pale
light blazed as it entered the field of negative energy,
the ghost-light and the dark light meeting in a
collision of frequencies that was felt in the teeth and
the sternum and the foundations of the buildings on
either side of the alley, and Aizune's blade found the
acolyte's chest and the pale light pierced the dark
and the acolyte fell and the channelling ceased and the
silence that followed was the silence of a wound in the
world's divine architecture that had been closed.

His hands were steady. His breathing was controlled.
The blade's luminescence faded to its resting shimmer.
The clasp was warm.

He said nothing. He turned south toward the square
where the remaining Orceri were discovering what a
battle felt like when the divine support that sustained
them was gone.

---

The biomancer was the last to fall.

Smiggles killed it.

The creature had been reshaping the battlefield since
the engagement began: raising stone barriers, liquefying
the road beneath defenders' feet, transmuting a section
of wall into something that crumbled when the militia
tried to use it as cover. It was versatile, creative,
and operating at a level of spellcraft that Smiggles
recognised as equivalent to his own, which was the
precise piece of information he needed because
equivalence meant vulnerability, and vulnerability
meant that the same countermeasures that worked against
a Mid Tier caster in the Merchant Nations would work
against a Mid Tier caster from a civilisation that had
conquered eleven worlds if the countermeasures were
applied with sufficient precision and sufficient
conviction and, ideally, sufficient violence.

Six syllables. The working was not thermal and was not
force but was the particular intersection of arcane
theory and practical application that the Zhonhylite
Academy had called disruptive resonance and that
Smiggles called the thing that made other mages' spells
stop working. A pulse of warm gold light expanded from
his position on the granary roof and washed over the
biomancer's active working, the stone wall it was
raising from the road halfway through its formation,
and the gold light met the dense layered frequency of
the biomantic casting and the two frequencies collided
and the collision resolved in Smiggles' favour because
the dispelling was precise and the biomantic working
was broad and precision beat breadth the way a key
beat a door.

The half-formed wall collapsed. The biomancer looked
up. Their eyes met across the square: gnome on a
rooftop, Orceri in a street, two mages of equivalent
power from civilisations that would never understand
each other, and the understanding that passed between
them in the fraction of a second before Smiggles' next
working was not respect and not hatred but recognition,
the mutual acknowledgement of practitioners who had
measured each other and found the measurement equal.

Smiggles spoke six syllables. The bead of orange-white
light crossed the square.

The biomancer raised a barrier. Smiggles had expected
this. The bead struck the barrier and detonated against
it, the thermal overload focused on a single point
rather than spread across a sphere, and the barrier held
for one second and then two and then the mana that
sustained it was exhausted and the remaining force of
the detonation passed through and the biomancer was
gone.

Smiggles lowered his hands. His fingers were trembling.
Not from fear. From the mana expenditure that six Mid
Tier workings in rapid sequence demanded of a body that
was three feet tall and ninety-four years old and
running on adrenaline and the particular gnomish
stubbornness that refused to acknowledge physical
limitations until the limitations acknowledged
themselves.

"Finely ground," he said. The words were quiet and
satisfied and carried no triumph because triumph
implied that the outcome had been in doubt, and it
had been in doubt, and the doubt was the thing he
would not think about until the moustache was in a
brandy glass and the brandy was in his throat and
the night was over.

---

Dawn came.

The Orceri force was destroyed. Not routed, not
scattered, but destroyed: forty soldiers, one biomancer,
one acolyte, eliminated in an engagement that had lasted
less than an hour and that had cost Winderbourgh eleven
civilian dead, four militia wounded, and the structural
integrity of the Crossed Keys inn, which continued to
burn as the eastern sky lightened from black to grey
to the pale gold that the Sabonaar called the colour
of promises.

Iskar sat on the edge of the well in the market square.
His rapier lay across his knees, the blade dark with
blood that was not his. His coat was torn at the
shoulder where an Orceri blade had found the leather
and cut through it to the shirt beneath, and the shirt
was cut and the skin beneath the shirt was cut and the
cut was shallow and long and would scar and he had not
noticed it until the fighting stopped. Tovar sat beside
him, the boy's woodcutting axe on the ground, the boy's
hands shaking, the boy's face carrying the expression
that every person who survived their first battle
carried: the expression of someone who had discovered
that they were capable of something they had not
imagined and who was not yet certain whether the
discovery was a gift or a wound.

"You did well," Iskar said. The warmth was back. Not
the full theatrical performance, not the rolled Rs and
the grand declarations, but the simpler thing beneath
them: the genuine kindness of a man from the docks of
Amara whose mother ran a chandlery and whose father
sailed the trade routes and whose life had been built
on the understanding that people needed to hear, after
the worst was over, that they had been enough.

"I was scared," Tovar said.

"I know. I was scared too."

"You did not look scared."

"That," Iskar said, "is the trick of it."

Aizune knelt beside Aleron at the wall. The elf was
conscious. Barely. His grey eyes were focused on
Aizune's face with the effortful attention of someone
who was holding onto a single point of reference because
all the other points had become unreliable, and the
holding was costing him something that was visible in
the tendons of his neck and the set of his jaw and the
way his hands, still trembling, gripped the quarterstaff
across his knees with a force that turned his knuckles
white.

"How many?" Aleron asked. The words were thin. Steady,
but thin.

"Forty Orceri. A biomancer. An acolyte. All destroyed."

"Casualties?"

"Eleven dead. Civilians. Four militia wounded." Aizune
paused. The silver-white light was in his palm again,
warm and gentle, the healing energy flowing into
Aleron's body through the contact of hand on shoulder,
the divine gift spending itself on a man whose body
could accept the gift but whose underlying condition,
the permanent diminishment, the baseline that would not
rise, was beyond any healing that Minerva's mid-tier
workings could address. "Rest."

"They learned what they came to learn." Aleron's voice
was quiet. The echoes murmured at the edges of his
perception, fragments of other conversations, other
realities, the constant noise that was his, chosen for
him by an entity that had not asked his permission. "I
felt them during the engagement. Not the soldiers. The
observer. High above. Invisible. Recording." His grey
eyes found the sky, the pale dawn, the two moons
fading as the light rose. "The skymage. He watched
everything."

Smiggles descended from the granary. His waistcoat was
scorched where a crossbow bolt had grazed the fabric
and left a burn across the burgundy that he would,
under any other circumstances, have found personally
offensive. His moustache was intact. His hands were
steady now, the trembling resolved, the mana
expenditure settling into the deep fatigue that would
hit him in approximately two hours and that he would
manage, as he always managed, with brandy and
stubbornness.

He looked at the square. The Orceri bodies lay where
they had fallen. Dark armour. Mana-enhanced weapons.
The faint residual shimmer of the stripped entity power
that had sustained their technology and their
civilisation and their absolute certainty that the
worlds they entered would fall.

"They sent forty," Smiggles said. His voice was clipped.
The gnomish craft idiom, the struck consonants and the
first-syllable emphasis of Zhonhylite diction, surfaced
the way it always did when the analytical engine had
finished processing and was delivering its conclusion.
"Forty soldiers. One biomancer. One acolyte. Against a
town with no garrison. This was a stripped-thread test.
They were not trying to conquer. They were trying to
measure." He looked at his companions. The hazel eyes
were sharp and carried the weight of an assessment that
the analytical engine had delivered and that the man
behind the engine did not enjoy repeating. "And they
measured us. Response time: less than one hour. Force
composition: three mid-tier casters, one bardic
combatant, one planar anomaly incapacitated by transit.
Capability: sufficient to destroy a company-strength
probe. All of this is data. All of this is being
recorded. And all of this is going into a report that
will land on Rangithyr's map table."

The silence that followed was the silence of people
who had won a battle and knew that winning was the
worst possible outcome, because winning provided the
enemy with exactly the information the enemy had come
to collect.

Aleron spoke from the wall. His voice was thin and his
eyes were half-focused and the words came with the
deliberate care of a man spending currency he could
not afford. "The next time will not be forty. The next
time will be thousands. And the next time, they will
not send a probe." He closed his eyes. The amulet
pulsed green at his throat. The echoes murmured.
"They will send Rangithyr."

Above Winderbourgh, invisible at an altitude that no
native mage could reach, Yzrat observed the aftermath
through enhanced goggles that painted the scene in the
jewelled colours of mana taxonomy. The skymage noted
the response time. He noted the force composition. He
noted the three mid-tier signatures, the bardic
combatant, the draconic caster whose frequency his
instruments still struggled to fully classify, and the
planar anomaly who had transported the group to the
engagement site and was now incapacitated at the base
of a wall.

He touched the communicator at his wrist. The dark
metal pulsed with cold blue light.

"Phase one complete," he said. "Data acquisition
successful. Recommend proceeding to phase two."

The mount banked. The wind at altitude carried no
sound. Below, the town of Winderbourgh burned and the
heroes who had saved it stood among the dead and the
dawn rose gold and silver over a world that had just
learned what was coming.

The two moons faded in the brightening sky.

Between them, the space was empty.

For now.
