# Chapter Three
## The Free City

The Free City of Amara woke the way it always woke: with noise.

Dawn came late to the Market District, where three-story buildings of
weathered sandstone and salt-bleached timber crowded so tightly that
the sun had to fight for every inch of street it touched. But the
merchants did not wait for the sun. They never had. By the time the
first pale light crept across the tiled rooftops and found the narrow
alleys below, the Grand Market was already alive with the particular
brand of organized chaos that Amara had perfected over three
generations of Bastien rule. Canvas awnings snapped open. Shutters
banged against walls. The smell of Yella's morning bread, fresh from
clay ovens, warm and yeasty and thick enough to taste, fought for
dominance against the sharper scents of lamp oil, tanned leather, and
the ever-present salt wind that climbed from the Bay of Urgot and
threaded through every street and alley like a rumor that refused to
die.

Aizune Silverwind stood at the window of a rented room above
Eccentricities, the magical components shop at the eastern edge of the
Market District, and watched the city come to life.

He had been awake for two hours. Elven reverie required less time than
human sleep, and what time it saved in rest it often spent in
reflection. This morning's reflections had been unproductive. The orc
raids on the eastern trade routes had been growing bolder for months,
and the job that Aldorph Broomspatch had offered was straightforward
in principle and troubling in its implications. Orcs raiding caravans
was common enough in the borderlands between the Merchant Nations and
the wild territories to the east. Orcs raiding caravans with
sufficient organization to target specific shipments, carry the stolen
goods back to a permanent fortification, and evade both the Baron's
patrols and the Merchant Guild's hired trackers suggested something
more than the usual tribal opportunism.

He turned from the window. The room was modest, clean, and impersonal.
His layered robes of forest green and deep gray hung on a hook by the
door, the mithril shirt beneath them catching the early light with a
faint silvered gleam. His ghost touch blade leaned against the wall
beside his longbow and quiver, the curved elven steel shimmering with
the pale luminescence that was visible only in dim light. His prayer
journal lay open on the small writing desk, the most recent entry
half-finished: a methodical analysis of what little they knew about
the orc raiders' patterns, annotated with questions he had not yet
been able to answer.

He dressed with the unhurried precision of long habit, settling the
mithril shirt against his chest, layering the robes over it, fastening
the silver oak-and-eye clasp of Minerva at his throat. His fingers
lingered on the clasp for a moment. A whispered prayer, barely more
than a breath. Guidance. Clarity. The faint shimmer that crossed his
vision when Minerva's gift responded was so familiar he barely noticed
it anymore.

He collected his weapons, his journal, and his composure, and went
downstairs to find his colleague already making a nuisance of himself.

---

Smiggles von Hoofel was engaged in a dispute with the proprietor of
Eccentricities over the quality of a jar of powdered moonstone, and
from the sound of it, the dispute was not going well for the
proprietor.

"Oy! I can see the impurities from here," the gnome was saying, one
thick finger jabbing toward the offending jar on the counter. His
other hand hovered beside the glass, palm flat, fingers spread, and
the faintest shimmer of warm gold light played across his knuckles,
so brief and so habitual that he almost certainly did not know he was
doing it. The shimmer faded. His expression did not improve. He was
standing on the shop's customer step, a wooden platform installed
along the counter's length for the convenience of shorter patrons,
which placed his magnificent moustache at approximately the eye level
of the human shopkeeper. The moustache was, as always, immaculate:
auburn, waxed to precise upward-curling points, each end defying
gravity with the disciplined confidence of its owner. The rest of
Smiggles was slightly less composed. His waistcoat, a deep burgundy
that complemented the auburn of his hair, was unbuttoned at the top.
His sleeves were rolled to the elbow, revealing ink-stained fingers
that gestured with increasing agitation. A cup of something hot sat
forgotten on the counter beside him, steam curling from its surface.

"Master von Hoofel, I assure you, our moonstone is sourced from
reputable..."

"Reputable." Smiggles repeated the word as though it had personally
offended him. "Reputable suppliers don't sell moonstone with feldspar
inclusions. I can see them. Right there. Those aren't lunar
refractions, those are mineral contaminants that'll destabilize any
transmutation matrix you try to build with them. You'd get better
results grinding up a cobblestone. That doesn't calibrate with
'reputable' by any measure I know."

The shopkeeper opened his mouth. Smiggles held up a hand.

"I'm not saying you're cheating anyone. I'm saying your supplier is
cheating you, which means you're passing defective material to your
customers, which means someone is going to try to build a ward with
this dross and it's going to fail at the worst possible moment. Do you
want that on your conscience? Do you want that on your shop's
reputation? In this city?"

The shopkeeper closed his mouth. In Amara, reputation was currency, and
currency was sacred.

"Good morning," Aizune said from the foot of the stairs.

Smiggles glanced over his shoulder. "Morning. Don't buy the moonstone."

"I was not planning to," Aizune said, and the gnome had already turned
back to the shopkeeper with the expression of a man settling in for a
prolonged siege.

"Now. Do you have anything in the back room that didn't come from the
same shipment, or should I walk down to Hirak's and tell him you're
selling contaminated components at full price?"

Aizune waited. He had learned, over the course of a year's professional
association with Smiggles von Hoofel, that the gnome's morning
negotiations were a force of nature best observed from a safe distance.
Interference only prolonged them. The shopkeeper produced a second jar
from beneath the counter. Smiggles examined it with the focused
intensity of a jeweler appraising a diamond, his hazel eyes shifting
from green to brown as he tilted the jar toward the light. He grunted.
Paid. Tucked the jar into the leather satchel that hung at his hip,
its gnomish-engineered clasp clicking shut with mechanical precision.

"Right," he said, hopping down from the customer step and adjusting his
waistcoat with a sharp tug. "Broomspatch's office. You eaten?"

"I have not."

"We'll stop at Yella's. I want to get there before Aldorph starts
talking, because once that man starts talking, no one eats for an
hour."

They stepped out into the Market District morning, and the street was
already full. Amara's population was a mosaic of the Merchant Nations
entire: Sabonaar humans with their dark skin and easy laughter,
Bryndawi dwarves with their red and blonde beards and their blunt
opinions, elves from half a dozen subraces moving through the crowd
with the quiet grace of people accustomed to being stared at, halflings
darting between the legs of taller folk with baskets of produce balanced
on their heads, gnomes examining merchandise with the critical squint
of a people for whom quality was a moral imperative. A pair of Nond
sailors, pale-skinned and massive, haggled with a Varkonan spice
merchant over a crate of cinnamon. A Chak'ti horse-trader led a string
of beautiful mares through the press, her animals drawing admiring
glances from every direction. The Temple of Tiovin's morning bells
rolled across the rooftops, deep and bronze, answered a moment later
by the sharper chime of the Halls of Iron, Mordrion's house.

Smiggles navigated the crowd with the practised efficiency of someone
who had spent years at gnome-height in a world built for taller people,
his compact frame slipping through gaps that Aizune had to step around.
They reached Yella's Fine Cooking, a narrow establishment wedged
between a cobbler and a candle-maker, its doorway fragrant with cumin
and saffron and slow-roasted peppers.

"Two," Smiggles said to the Sabonaar woman behind the counter, holding
up fingers. She served them clay bowls of spiced eggs and flatbread
without a word. They ate standing at the counter, which was set at a
height that required Smiggles to raise his elbows to an undignified
angle and Aizune to hunch. Neither commented on this. They had eaten
here before. Smiggles muttered two syllables of Draconic into his cup
without looking at it, and the steam rising from the surface thickened
and curled. He took a sip and nodded, satisfied. Aizune did not ask
what he had done to it. He had learned that the answer was always
either "nothing" or a twelve-minute explanation of thermal mana
equilibrium, and neither was worth pursuing before breakfast was
finished.

"Broomspatch said he'd have the route maps ready this morning,"
Smiggles said between bites. "The ones showing where his caravans were
hit. I want to see the pattern before we commit to anything."

"Agreed. The raids are too organized for opportunistic raiding. Someone
is directing them."

"Someone, or something. Kru'ka'ta, if the name the Guild's scouts
pulled is accurate. Orc shaman. Cleric, probably, from the reports of
magical darkness during two of the attacks. Which means we're not just
clearing a nest. We're dealing with a leader who can channel."

"That is concerning."

"It's more than concerning. It's expensive. Broomspatch is paying us to
retrieve documents and goods, not to assault a fortified position held
by a spellcasting orc and however many warriors he's commanding. The
price goes up."

"You may raise that with Aldorph. I would prefer to hear the full
briefing before discussing compensation."

Smiggles snorted. "Of course you would."

They finished eating. Smiggles left coins on the counter, precisely
calculated, and they walked east through the Market District toward the
Red Raven Trading Company.

---

Aldorph Broomspatch's office occupied the second floor of the Red Raven
Trading Company's headquarters, a solid three-story building of dressed
stone and dark timber that anchored the southeastern corner of the
Grand Market. The Red Raven's signboard, a crimson bird clutching a
gold coin in each talon, swung above the main entrance on iron brackets
polished to a mirror shine. Inside, the ground floor was a controlled
riot of commerce: clerks tallying inventories, runners carrying
messages, merchants examining sample goods spread across long tables.
The smell was ink and sealing wax and the particular dry sweetness of
trade goods in transit: bolts of fabric, casks of dried fruit, bundles
of cured leather.

The office upstairs was quieter but no less energetic. Aldorph himself
was the center of it, a large man who occupied space with the cheerful
confidence of someone who had earned every square inch. His red hair
was thick and wild, his beard long and pointed, and his moustache
curved upward in two dramatic points that rivaled Smiggles' own, a
fact neither of them had ever acknowledged aloud but both were acutely
aware of. A monocle glinted over his right eye. His clothes were
expensive, colorful, and slightly too tight, suggesting either recent
weight gain or a tailor who had been paid to flatter rather than to
measure accurately.

"Gentlemen!" He rose from behind a desk covered in maps, ledgers, and
the remnants of his own breakfast. "Gentlemen, good morning, excellent,
you're here, good, good."

He came around the desk and clasped Aizune's hand with both of his,
pumping it with an enthusiasm that moved the elf's entire arm. Smiggles
received the same treatment, though his shorter stature meant Aldorph
had to bend nearly double. The gnome endured it with the expression of
a man submitting to a necessary indignity.

"I have the maps," Aldorph said, spreading them across the desk with
the sweeping gesture of a man unveiling a masterwork. "Three raids in
the last four months. Here, here, and here." His thick finger stabbed
the parchment at three points along the eastern trade route between
Amara and Instar. "All within a day's ride of this location." The
finger moved to a point deep in the Steppes of Broken Stone, where a
small annotation in a clerk's careful hand read: "abandoned
fortification, reported."

Aizune turned the nearest map toward himself and passed his fingertips
across the parchment, slowly, left to right. A faint shimmer crossed
his dark gray eyes, there and gone, Minerva's sight brushing the
surface for traces of arcane tampering or residual enchantment. The
maps were clean. Ordinary ink on ordinary paper. Whatever had organized
the orc raids had not touched these documents with magic.

"The goods taken from the first two raids were replaceable. Costly, but
replaceable. The third raid took documents. Contracts, shipping
manifests, correspondence with partners in Instar. Those documents are
not replaceable. They are, in fact, the kind of documents that, in the
wrong hands, could cause me significant difficulty with the Merchant
Guild and, more importantly, with the Baron's tax assessors." He
paused. "I mention this only to explain why the recovery of these
specific items is worth more to me than the general elimination of a
band of orc raiders."

"How much more?" Smiggles asked.

Aldorph named a figure. Smiggles' eyebrows rose. Aizune's did not, but
his ears tilted forward by a fraction that Smiggles, who had learned to
read elven ears the way a sailor reads wind, noted with interest.

"That is generous," Aizune said.

"Finely ground," Smiggles murmured, which from a Zhonhylite gnome was
higher praise than most people would have recognized.

"That is the value of those documents to me, Master Silverwind. Recover
them, and you will find me a grateful and ongoing friend. The Red Raven
Trading Company has resources that extend well beyond gold." He smiled,
and the smile was genuine, but beneath it was the calculation of a man
who had built a trading empire in a city where calculation was survival.
"I also want the orc threat eliminated. The keep cleared. The raiders
dealt with permanently. I cannot have my eastern routes disrupted every
time a shaman decides to play warlord."

"We will need more information about the keep's defenses," Aizune said.
"Your scouts reported an abandoned fortification. How abandoned? How
fortified? How many orcs?"

"This is where it gets interesting." Aldorph reached beneath the desk
and produced a leather folder. "I've had people asking around. Caravan
drivers, independent traders, the occasional bounty hunter. Estimates
range from thirty to fifty orcs in the keep itself, with patrols in the
surrounding Steppes. The fortification is old, built on rubble, crude
but functional. And my people are not the only ones who've been asking
questions." He opened the folder and extracted a single sheet of paper.
"This morning, a message arrived at my front desk. No sender
identified. It reads: 'I have intelligence regarding the orc keep east
of Amara. If this is of interest, I will be at the Ogre's Arse at
midday. Ask for the Sabonaar with the hat.'"

Smiggles took the note. Read it. Passed it to Aizune.

"The Ogre's Arse," Smiggles said flatly.

"The Ogre's Arse," Aldorph confirmed.

"In the Dock District."

"In the Dock District."

"At midday."

"At midday." Aldorph spread his hands. "I make no guarantees about the
quality of the intelligence or the character of the source. But I
thought you might want to hear what this person has to say before you
ride east."

Aizune studied the note. The handwriting was quick and confident, the
ink standard Amaran stock, the paper unremarkable. The phrasing was
direct, practical, and slightly amused. Someone who wanted to be found,
on their own terms, in a place where they felt comfortable.

"We will speak to this person," Aizune said.

"I figured you would." Aldorph gathered his maps and rolled them into a
leather tube, which he offered to Aizune. "Take these. Study them on
the road. And gentlemen, do be careful. The Steppes are unkind to the
unprepared."

"We know," Smiggles said. "We've been there."

Aldorph's smile broadened. "Which is why I hired you."

---

The Ogre's Arse occupied a sagging building at the end of a narrow
alley two streets back from the main wharves, and the sign above its
door depicted the anatomy referenced in its name with a directness that
left nothing to imagination. The Dock District at midday was a
different creature than the Market District at morning: louder, rougher,
fragrant with fish guts and tar and the particular stale-sweet smell of
ale that had been spilled more often than it had been drunk. Warehouses
lined the River Cho, their massive doors open to teams of dwarven
dockworkers hauling crates and barrels. The Bay of Urgot glittered
beyond the harbor mouth, its surface busy with the triangular sails of
Sabonaar trading vessels and the broader, squarer rigs of Nond merchant
ships.

Aizune and Smiggles entered the tavern.

The interior was dark, low-ceilinged, and populated by the kind of
people who chose to drink at midday in a place called the Ogre's Arse:
sailors between voyages, dockworkers on break, a pair of dwarves
playing a complicated dice game in one corner, and a woman whose
profession Aizune chose not to speculate about leaning against the bar
with the patient boredom of someone waiting for the evening shift. The
barkeep, a heavyset Sabonaar man with arms like hawsers, glanced at
them with the practiced assessment of someone who had learned to
evaluate newcomers in the time it took them to cross a threshold.

"We are looking for a Sabonaar with a hat," Aizune said.

The barkeep's gaze moved to a table in the far corner.

The man sitting there looked up from a cup of something amber and
smiled.

He was tall, even sitting down. Broad-shouldered, long-limbed, with the
kind of build that came from years of physical labor conducted at
height and in motion. His skin was dark brown, deepened by sun and
salt. His hair was black and slightly wavy, swept back from his face,
shorter at the sides in the practical fashion of sailors who could not
afford obstruction during a gale. His nose had been broken once and set
well. His jaw was strong, lined with stubble maintained at a precise
and deliberate length. His eyes were dark brown, nearly black, and they
were simultaneously warm and evaluating, the eyes of someone who had
spent years reading people in places exactly like this one.

He wore a fitted leather doublet over a white linen shirt open at the
collar. A crimson sash at his waist. A wide-brimmed hat sat on the
table beside his cup, tilted at an angle that managed to be expressive
even without a head beneath it. A long coat of dark oiled leather hung
over the back of his chair. At his hip, the hilt of a rapier protruded
from beneath the coat's edge, its guard worked with a subtle wave
pattern.

"My frrriends," he said, and the rolled R turned the common greeting
into something that sounded like the opening line of a ballad. There
was a warmth in the word that went beyond diction, a resonance that
settled into the ear and made the listener want to hear what came next.
Aizune noticed it. Smiggles, whose sensitivity to mana in all its
forms was as involuntary as breathing, tilted his head a fraction but
said nothing. Iskar rose from his chair and offered a bow that was
shallow enough to be efficient and theatrical enough to be memorable.
"Iskar. Late of the Cobalt Tide. Please, sit. The ale here is
terrible, but the privacy is excellent."

They sat, and Smiggles ordered brandy. The barkeep informed him that
they did not have brandy. Iskar looked at the barkeep as though the man
had just announced that the sea had dried up overnight. His mouth
opened, then closed. His eyes drifted upward toward the stained ceiling
in what appeared to be a silent consultation with a higher power. Then
his gaze returned, level and mournful, to the barkeep.

"Then I shall sufferr the ale," he said, with the dignified resignation
of a man accepting exile.

Smiggles watched this performance with the expression of someone
cataloguing evidence for future reference. "Slag-water, more like," the
gnome muttered, and ordered the same.

"You sent the message to Broomspatch," Aizune said.

"I did."

"You have intelligence about the orc keep."

"I do." Iskar leaned back in his chair, one arm draped across its back,
his posture the casual sprawl of a man who was either completely
relaxed or very good at appearing so. "I've been tracking the raids for
three months. Not for Broomspatch. For my own reasons. The raids
disrupted supply lines I rely on, contacts I need for other work. I
traced the pattern to the Steppes. Found the keep. Watched it for four
days from a ridge to the northwest."

"And what did you see?"

"Forty to fifty orcs. Mix of warriors and something more organized.
Patrols on a schedule, which is unusual for orcs. A shaman in the
upper level who doesn't come out much. Supply lines from the south,
which means someone in Amara is feeding them, probably hiring them for
the caravan raids. The keep itself is old, crude construction on top of
older rubble, but the walls are solid and there's only one visible
entrance."

"One visible entrance," Smiggles repeated.

Iskar's smile widened. "I said visible. The keep was built on rubble
from something older. Older structures mean older passages. I found
what I believe are ventilation shafts on the eastern face, too narrow
for an orc but wide enough for someone my size. Possibly. And certainly
wide enough for someone yours."

He said this to Smiggles without malice, and the gnome accepted it
without offense, because it was true and because the man had said it
with the easy directness of someone who valued utility over diplomacy.

"Why bring this to us?" Aizune asked. "If you have this intelligence,
you could sell it to Broomspatch directly. Or use it yourself."

"I could. But forty orcs with a shaman is more than one man can handle,
even a man of my considerrable talents." The theatrical emphasis on
"considerable" was accompanied by a slight spreading of his hands that
acknowledged the self-mockery. "I've been looking for capable people to
work with. Your names came up. Aizune Silverwind, priest of Minerva,
divine senses and a reputation for competence. Smiggles von Hoofel,
gnomish mage, arcane firepower and a reputation for creative
problem-solving." He paused. "Also a reputation for being difficult,
but I've been told the two go together."

"They do," Smiggles said.

"I thought so." Iskar picked up his cup, took a drink, and set it down
with the careful precision of someone who did not intend to pick it up
again. "Here is what I propose. I join your expedition. I contribute my
intelligence, my blade, and my knowledge of the approach. In exchange,
I want a share of whatever salvage we find beyond Broomspatch's
contracted goods, and I want first look at any documents or
correspondence that might relate to trade contacts south of Amara."

"Why south of Amara?" Aizune asked.

The warmth in Iskar's eyes cooled by a fraction. Not hostility. A door
closing. "I have reasons. They're my own. They don't conflict with your
job or Broomspatch's interests."

Aizune studied him. The quiet, habitual extension of his priestly
senses reached toward the Sabonaar without conscious decision, the way
a hand reaches toward a flame to test its heat. No malice. No
deception in what the man had chosen to share, though the spaces around
the things he had not shared were deliberate and firm, like doors
closed but not locked. The man was being honest about the fact that he
was withholding information, which was, in its own way, a form of
honesty. His bearing spoke of competence, his intelligence-gathering
spoke of patience and resourcefulness, and his willingness to approach
strangers with a proposition rather than work alone spoke of a man who
understood his own limitations.

Aizune looked at Smiggles. The gnome was twisting one end of his
moustache between his fingers, a gesture that meant he was thinking.
After a moment, he shrugged.

"He's got information we need, he can fight, and he's not an idiot.
I've worked with worse. The numbers are ticking."

"That is not a ringing endorsement," Iskar observed.

"You'll learn that's as good as it gets from him," Aizune said.

Iskar laughed. It was a warm sound, unguarded, the laugh of a man who
had decided he liked these people and saw no reason to conceal it.
"Fair winds, then. When do we leave?"

"Tomorrow. First light. The Steppes are a day's ride east." Aizune
rose. "We will need horses, supplies for a week, and whatever
additional intelligence you can provide about the keep's interior
before we depart. We have route maps from Broomspatch. I would like to
compare them with your observations."

"Done. I have a room at Marn's Respite, down by the wharves. I'll
bring everything I have to your lodgings this evening."

"We are above Eccentricities. In the Market District."

Iskar retrieved his hat from the table and placed it on his head at the
precise angle that transformed it from headwear into a statement. "I
know where it is. I've been watching you for two days."

Smiggles choked on his ale.

"Not in a sinisterr way," Iskar added, with the easy smile of a man
who had expected that reaction and enjoyed it. "In a professional way.
I wanted to know who I was approaching. You'll find that I am
thorough."

"Apparently," Aizune said, and the elf's tone was dry enough to cure
leather.

"Until this evening, then. My frrriend." The rolled R again, warm and
deliberate, accompanied by a shallow bow and a turn toward the door
that managed to make the exit from a dockside tavern look like the
final scene of a play. He was three strides from the door when a
serving woman carrying four mugs of ale on a battered tray stepped into
his path from behind a support beam. Iskar sidestepped her with a
dancer's grace, caught the mug that slid from the tray's edge without
breaking stride, set it back among its companions with a flick of the
wrist, tipped his hat to her startled face, and was through the door
and gone before the woman had finished deciding whether to thank him or
throw something at him.

The door closed behind him.

Smiggles stared at the door for a long moment. Then he took another
drink of his ale, grimaced, and said: "He's going to be exhausting,
isn't he."

"Almost certainly."

"Right." The gnome pushed his cup away and hopped down from his chair.
"Let's go buy supplies. And for Minerva's sake, let's find a place that
sells brandy, because I am not surviving the Steppes of Broken Stone
drinking whatever that was."

---

They departed Amara at first light.

The eastern gate opened onto a packed-earth road that wound through the
last of the city's outlying farms and orchards before climbing into the
dry hills that marked the boundary between civilization and the
borderlands. Behind them, Amara descended toward the bay in a cascade
of tiled rooftops and whitewashed walls, the morning sun catching the
spires of the Temple of Tiovin and the dark stone bulk of Castle Amara
on its hill above the Noble District. The harbor was already busy,
sails unfurling in the dawn breeze, and the sound of the city, that
constant hum of commerce and conversation and competing ambitions,
faded slowly as the road climbed and the air grew drier and the last
orchard gave way to scrub grass and stone.

Aizune rode in silence, his prayer journal balanced on the pommel of
his saddle, his dark gray eyes moving between the road ahead and the
notes he had compiled from Iskar's intelligence the previous evening.
The Sabonaar had been as thorough as promised: hand-drawn maps of the
keep's exterior, patrol schedules observed over four days, notes on orc
numbers and armament, and a detailed description of the ventilation
shafts he had identified on the eastern face. The intelligence was
professional, organized, and presented with the kind of practical
specificity that came from experience rather than natural talent. Iskar
had done this before.

Smiggles rode beside him on a horse that seemed to regard its gnomish
rider with the tolerant resignation of an animal that had accepted an
unusual situation. The gnome's legs stuck out at angles that suggested
the saddle had been designed for someone with considerably longer
limbs, a problem he had solved by stuffing his satchel beneath him as
additional cushioning. His waistcoat, a forest green that complemented
the morning light, was buttoned to the throat. His moustache had been
freshly waxed. His crossbow hung at his hip, and his spellbook rode in
the satchel beneath him, which meant he was sitting on the sum total of
his arcane knowledge, a metaphor he would have appreciated if anyone
had pointed it out.

Iskar rode ahead of them both, his dark oiled coat open to the morning
air, his hat tilted back on his head in the relaxed configuration, his
concertina case strapped behind his saddle alongside a bedroll and a
coil of rope. He had been humming since they left the gate, a Sabonaar
melody with a rising-falling cadence that suggested waves and horizon
lines. He was not aware he was doing it.

The road east crossed gentle hills for the first hour, then flattened
into the broad, featureless expanse that marked the beginning of the
Steppes. The change was gradual but definitive: grass thinned, soil
gave way to hardpan and scattered stone, and the horizon opened into a
vast, empty circle unbroken by tree or structure or any evidence of
human presence. The trade route between Amara and Instar ran through
the northern edge of this desolation, a thin line of packed earth worn
smooth by centuries of caravan wheels. The heroes were not taking the
trade route. They were cutting southeast, directly into the Steppes,
following Iskar's maps toward the keep.

"Charming countryside," Smiggles observed, surveying the wasteland with
the expression of a man who had been promised an adventure and received
a geology lesson.

"The Steppes of Broken Stone," Aizune said. "Named for the exposed
bedrock formations that characterize the region. Geologically, the
result of ancient volcanic activity that..."

"I wasn't asking for a lecture."

"You were not asking for anything. You were complaining."

"I was making an observation. There's a difference."

"The difference is subtle."

"The difference is that an observation invites commiseration, while a
complaint invites a lecture. I wanted commiseration. You gave me rocks."

From ahead, Iskar's humming paused. "Is it always like this with you
two?"

"Yes," they said, simultaneously.

Iskar's laugh carried back to them on the dry wind, warm and genuine,
and he resumed his humming. The horses walked on. The Steppes stretched
ahead, empty and patient, hiding whatever waited at their heart with
the indifference of a landscape that had never cared about the concerns
of the small things that crossed it.

Aizune touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. A habit, not a prayer.
Ahead, the wasteland shimmered in the growing heat, and somewhere
beyond the horizon, in an abandoned keep built on the rubble of
something much older, orcs were waiting with stolen goods and a shaman
who could channel the divine energies of death.

Behind them, Amara was a smudge of white and terracotta against the
bay. The two moons had set hours ago, fading into a morning sky that
held no sign of what was coming. The machine beneath the keep had been
sleeping for five hundred years, and would sleep a little longer, and
then it would not, and everything would change.

But for now, there was only the road, and the sun, and the sound of a
Sabonaar sailor humming a song about the sea in a place where the sea
was already a memory, and the quiet company of three people who did not
yet know that they were riding toward the discovery that would reshape
their lives and the fate of every living thing on Mazariim.

They rode east, into the Steppes, into the silence, into the beginning
of everything.
