# Chapter Eight
## The Wandering Elf

The nightmares began in the season of long light.

Aleron Windstrider was fifty-five years old, which was young for an
elf, barely past the threshold of adulthood, still close enough to
adolescence that the elders of his tribe regarded him with the
indulgent patience reserved for those who had not yet learned that
time was not a thing to be hurried. He lived in the western reaches
of Yaara'sehan, where the forests of the Ya'sehrim homeland ran down
to the coast in great green waves and the air carried the salt of the
ocean and the resin of ancient pines in equal measure. He had trained
with the curved blade. He had studied the prayers of Minerva. He had
meditated in the deep reveries through which elves processed memory
and dream, and the reveries had been, for fifty-five years, quiet
places, pools of silver stillness in which the accumulated experiences
of a young life settled and clarified and became wisdom.

Then the dreams changed.

They came during the deep reverie, the state closest to what humans
called sleep, and they came with a violence that left him gasping at
the surface of consciousness like a man pulled from drowning water. He
saw fire. Not the contained fire of a hearth or a forge but fire that
consumed without purpose, fire that moved across landscapes he could
not identify, burning things he could not name. He saw shapes moving
through the smoke, enormous and purposeful, and he heard sounds that
might have been screams or might have been the tearing of something
far larger than any living throat. The dreams carried a weight that
pressed against the walls of his mind like water against a dam, and
behind that weight was something vast and patient and utterly without
name, something that existed beyond the boundaries of the world he
knew and wanted, with a hunger that was not hunger but something
worse, something more like gravity, to come through.

He woke from these dreams with his hands shaking and his hair damp
with sweat and the taste of ash on his tongue, and the elders could
not explain them, and the priests of Minerva who examined him found
nothing wrong, and the dreams continued, night after night, growing
more vivid, more specific, more certain, until he could feel the heat
of the fire on his skin and smell the smoke and hear the sounds that
might have been screams and knew, with a conviction that had nothing
to do with reason, that what he was feeling was real. Not here. Not
now. But somewhere. Somehow. Real.

He was walking through snow when the world tore open for the first
time.

---

The evening was warm and smelled of lamp oil and cumin and the
particular blend of human conversation and commerce that constituted
Amara's native atmosphere. Aizune sat at a corner table in the common
room of the Ogre's Arse with his back to the wall and his prayer
journal open before him and his mind in three places at once: on the
road to Instar, which they would begin in the morning; on the items
in Iskar's saddlebag, which had not stopped troubling him since the
keep; and on the girl with the yellow eyes, who sat across the room
with a cup of something she had not tasted and an expression of
serene attention directed at everything and nothing.

Smiggles was at the bar, engaged in negotiations with the barkeep
over the quality of the establishment's brandy. The negotiations were
not going well. Smiggles had sampled three options and rejected each
with increasing vehemence, his moustache bristling with professional
affront, his voice carrying the clipped cadence of a man whose
standards had been personally insulted.

"Slag," he said, setting down the third glass with a finality that
made the barkeep's heavy arms fold tighter across his chest. "Absolute
slag. The first one tasted like something died in the barrel. The
second one tasted like whatever killed it. And this one tastes like
they buried both of them and distilled the runoff." He stared at the
barkeep. "Do you have anything, anything at all, that was not produced
by pouring lamp oil through a hat?"

The barkeep, who had the build of a man who settled disputes by
standing very still until the dispute reconsidered its priorities,
said nothing.

"Fine. Ale. The least offensive ale you have. And I want it in a
clean glass, which based on my experience so far is an ambitious
request."

Iskar sat beside Aizune, his hat on the table before him, his rapier
propped against the wall within arm's reach. He was watching the room
with the habitual surveillance of a man who had spent years in places
where inattention was expensive. His eyes moved from face to face,
door to door, cataloguing threats and exits with a speed that was
almost unconscious.

"The bard at the firreside is terrible," he observed. "He's been
playing the same three chords for twenty minutes and I don't think
he knows a fourrth."

"Perhaps he is building toward something."

"He is building towarrd a thrown mug if he doesn't find a melody
soon." Iskar tilted his chair back on two legs, a habit Aizune had
observed correlated with the bard's thinking state. "We leave at dawn
for Instarr?"

"At dawn."

"How many days?"

"Four, if the weather holds. Five if it does not."

"And when we arrive, we hand the spy's gear to the most powerful
collection of mages in the Merchant Nations and say, 'Excuse us,
distinguished wizards, we found this on a dead orc from another
reality, could you take a look?'" He paused. "I'm trying to imagine
their faces."

"Smiggles will handle the presentation. He speaks their language."

"Their language is contempt for anyone who can't cast a cantrip.
I've been to Instarr. The street sweepers look down on you if your
broom isn't enchanted." He let the chair drop forward with a thud.
"But the food is excellent. I'll give them that."

The door opened. Cold air entered the common room, which was unusual,
because the evening was warm, and the cold that came through the door
was not the cold of weather but something else, something localised
and sharp and wrong in the way that the cold in the mirror chamber
beneath the keep had been wrong: a temperature that did not belong to
the air it occupied.

Aizune's hand moved to the hilt of the spirit-touched blade before
his mind completed the thought that had sent it there.

A man stood in the doorway.

He was an elf. Tall, thin, leaning on a quarterstaff of pale ashwood
that bore the smooth wear of years of grip along its length. His robes
were simple, undyed wool and faded brown linen, patched at the elbows
and hem with the resigned pragmatism of someone who had stopped caring
about appearance so long ago that the decision had fossilised into
habit. A deep hood was pushed back from his face, revealing features
that were Ya'sehrim, the angular bone structure and pale skin of
Aizune's own people, but eroded, diminished, as though whatever burned
inside this man had been slowly consuming the substance of his body for
decades. Dark circles sat beneath his eyes like bruises that had never
healed. His skin was pale to the point of translucence, the veins
visible at his temples and the backs of his hands, and the hands
themselves trembled with a faint, constant vibration that spoke of
exhaustion so deep it had become structural.

His hair was long and black, falling past his shoulders in the
Ya'sehrim tradition. And through it, running from both temples through
the full length like veins of quartz through dark stone, broad streaks
of white. Not the silver-white of age. A stark, bleached white, the
colour of something that had been exposed to an energy that leached
pigment from living tissue.

His eyes were pale grey. Almost colourless. And they were not looking
at the same thing.

One eye was focused on Aizune, sharp and present and carrying an
intelligence that cut through the gaunt ruin of the face like a blade
through cloth. The other eye was focused on something beside Aizune's
left shoulder, something that occupied the same space as the common
room's wall but was not, apparently, the wall, and the attention it
gave to this invisible thing was patient and conversational, as though
the man were listening to someone speak.

He crossed the room. His gait was uneven, the quarterstaff bearing
weight that his legs could not sustain alone, each step a negotiation
between will and weakness. As he approached, Aizune felt something
through Minerva's gift that he had no name for: a pressure, a
vibration, a sense of boundaries that were thinner than they should be,
as though the air around this man were stretched taut over something
vast and the fabric of the world was complaining about the strain.

Across the room, Lai set down her untouched drink and stood. The
motion was sudden, involuntary, the reflexive response of a body that
had encountered something it did not like. Her yellow eyes were wide,
her vertical pupils contracted to slits, and her lips had drawn back
from her teeth, which were, in that moment, very visibly too sharp.
She took a step backward. Then another. Then she turned and walked out
of the common room through the rear door without a word, her movements
quick and tight, the sinuous grace replaced by something rigid and
repelled.

Smiggles, returning from the bar with a glass of ale he had not yet
insulted, watched her go. Then he looked at the approaching elf. Then
he looked at Aizune. His expression communicated, in the efficient
shorthand of their partnership, a single word: "What."

The elf reached their table. He stopped. He looked at Aizune with the
eye that was focused on the present, and the other eye drifted from
the invisible presence beside Aizune's shoulder to a point somewhere
above Iskar's head, and then to the wall, and then back, as though
tracking a conversation that moved around the room.

"Ah," he said. His voice was quiet, measured, the voice of a man who
chose each word with care, as though speech cost him something and he
preferred not to waste the currency. "I had... hoped you would be
here. I was... not entirely certain. The echoes said you would, but
the echoes say many things, and not all of them are..." He paused. His
grey eyes flickered to the empty space again. He appeared to listen.
Then he shook his head, a small, weary motion. "No, I am not going to
tell them that. It's rude."

Silence. The common room continued around them, oblivious.

"My name is Aleron Windstrider." He shifted his weight on the
quarterstaff. The trembling in his hands was worse now, visible even in
the dim light. "I need your help."

"Help," Smiggles repeated. The gnome had not sat down. He stood with
his ale in one hand and his other hand resting, with apparent
casualness, near his component pouch. "Help with what, exactly?"

Aleron looked at him. Both eyes focused, briefly, on the same point,
and in that moment of convergence the intelligence behind them was
startling, a depth and clarity that the ruined body could not diminish.
"That is what I am... trying to determine," he said. "I know you are
important. I know I was meant to find you. I do not yet know why."

The silence that followed was comprehensive.

"Right," Smiggles said. "Oy."

---

The forest was white with snow, and the owlbear was dying.

Aleron was fifty-five and had been walking the western coast for three
days, following a hunting trail that wound through old-growth pines
toward a creek where the tribe's fishers set their traps in spring.
The nightmares had been bad the previous night, worse than usual, and
he had left the settlement before dawn because the alternative was to
lie in his reverie-hollow and watch fire consume landscapes that had
no name and feel, again, the terrible pressure of something vast
leaning against the boundaries of the world.

The owlbear erupted from the treeline forty paces ahead. It was
enormous, a mature specimen, its feathered head swivelling toward him
with the flat golden eyes of a predator that had been wounded and was
therefore more dangerous than a healthy one, because pain made all
things desperate and desperation removed the calculations that
separated a territorial display from a killing charge. Blood matted
the feathers along its left flank. Something had hurt it. Something
large.

It charged.

Aleron drew his curved blade, the blade he had purchased during his
youth when he still expected to live a normal elven life, and set his
feet, and felt the fear rise through him like water through a crack in
stone, filling every space it could find, and the fear was enormous
and absolute and it did something.

The world tore.

There was no other word for it. The air in front of him split along a
line that had no colour and no width and no physical presence, and
through the split he saw something that was not the forest: a field of
golden grain stretching to a horizon beneath a sky that was softer and
bluer than any sky he had known, and there were no moons in that sky,
no moons at all, just the soft blue and the grain and a wind that
smelled of warm earth and growing things and nothing else. The split
widened. The forest fell away. The owlbear's roar became distant, then
silent, and Aleron stood in the grain field with his blade in his hand
and the snow melting from his boots into soil that had never known
winter.

A woman descended from the sky.

She came in a whirlwind that flattened the grain in a perfect circle
around her landing, her body broad and powerful, her features elven
but heavier, stronger, built for a world where elves had evolved
differently or been shaped by different pressures. Her arms were thick
with muscle. Her hair was cropped short. She wore armour of a metal he
did not recognise, and the expression on her face as she looked at him
was not fear or hostility but curiosity, the bright, engaged interest
of a scholar encountering a phenomenon she had read about but never
observed.

"Ah," she said, in a dialect of Tarquesta that was close enough to his
own to understand and different enough to feel like hearing his
language spoken by someone who had learned it in a dream. "A visitor
from the world of the living."

Before he could respond, the grain field folded. The sky contracted.
The woman's curious face blurred and stretched and disappeared, and
the forest reassembled around him with a violence that drove the
breath from his lungs and dropped him to his knees beside a creek he
did not remember reaching.

His nose was bleeding. The blood fell onto the snow in drops of vivid
red that steamed in the cold air. His hands were shaking. His blade
lay in the snow beside him, dropped during the transit, and when he
reached for it his fingers would not close properly, as though the
muscles had forgotten their instructions.

He knelt there for a long time. When he finally rose and stumbled back
toward the settlement, the tribe's watchers saw him coming and called
to him, and when he entered the firelight they stopped calling, because
his hair, which had been black that morning, now carried a broad streak
of white running from his left temple through the full length, stark
and bleached, as though something had reached inside him and burned
the colour from the roots.

The streak never faded. The echoes began that same day: faint shapes
at the edges of his vision, voices just below the threshold of
hearing, translucent figures that occupied the same space as the real
world but belonged to somewhere else entirely.

He did not sleep well again for seventy years.

---

"You have been following us," Aizune said.

"Yes." Aleron had accepted the chair Iskar offered with the careful
gratitude of a man whose body protested every transition between
standing and sitting. He sat with the quarterstaff propped against
the table, one hand still resting on it, as though releasing the staff
would mean releasing his grip on the physical world. "Since the keep.
Since you... activated the machine. I felt it." He touched his chest
with his free hand, the gesture vague, indicating something internal.
"Here. A vibration. Like a bell struck in a room you cannot see, but
the sound... the sound comes through the walls."

"You felt it from Fort Hope," Smiggles said. He had sat down. His ale
was untouched. His hazel eyes were fixed on Aleron with the particular
intensity that Smiggles brought to phenomena he could not immediately
explain, which was the intensity of a man who considered unexplained
phenomena a personal affront. "That's hundreds of miles."

"Three hundred and... forty. Approximately. I was in the market
quarter. Buying bread." A pause. The grey eyes drifted to the empty
space above Smiggles' head, held there for a moment, then returned.
"I fell. In the street. The echoes became... very loud. Many voices,
many realities, all speaking at once. It took some time to... to sort
them. To find the one that mattered." Another pause, longer. "I knew.
Deep in my soul, in the place where the gift lives, I knew that
whatever had been activated had something to do with... with why I am
the way I am. With why I was made this way."

"Made this way," Iskar repeated. He was leaning forward, elbows on the
table, his dark eyes moving between Aleron's face and the spaces where
Aleron's attention periodically wandered. The bard's expression was
difficult to read: not hostile, not sympathetic, but something between,
the careful assessment of a man who made his living reading people and
had just encountered a text he could not parse. "Made by whom?"

"Thalaman." The name fell into the conversation with the weight of
something that had been carried for a very long time and set down with
relief. "The entity of chaos. And insanity. And time." Aleron's mouth
curved in a smile that was small and sad and genuinely amused, the
smile of a man who found absurdity comforting because his entire
existence was absurd. "A generous gift, as you can... as you can
imagine. Chaos, insanity, and time travel. The three things most
likely to ruin a life, bundled together and... and handed to an elf
who was walking through a forest minding his own business."

"You're saying a god gave you powers," Smiggles said. His voice was
flat. Not hostile. Flat. The voice of a man who had identified a claim
that required evidence and was waiting for it.

"An entity. Not a god. There are no gods, not... not in the way people
mean when they say the word. There are beings of immense power who
formed from the accumulation of mana in the vastness of space, and
they are... they are remarkable, and they are terrible, and they are
not immortal." He looked at Aizune. "You know this. You serve Minerva.
You feel her presence. But you know she is not omnipotent. You know
she can be... hurt."

The words landed in Aizune's chest with a precision that felt
deliberate. He thought of the machine's vision, the world where elven
lands had been razed completely, where the absence of Minerva had been
a silence so vast it had its own weight. He had not spoken of this to
anyone. He had barely allowed himself to think it.

"I know," he said.

"Thalaman touched me. Seventy years ago. I did not ask for it. I did
not want it. It broke... it broke things inside me that cannot be
repaired. But I believe, I have... I have come to believe, that it
was done for a reason. And I believe that reason is connected to what
you found beneath that keep."

"You saw what we found?" Aizune asked.

"No. I felt it. The machine's activation sent a vibration across...
across dimensional boundaries that I am attuned to in ways I cannot
fully explain. I followed the vibration. I found you. I watched you
for..." He stopped. His eyes tracked something invisible crossing the
room between their table and the bar, and he waited, patiently, for
whatever it was to pass. "For some time. I needed to know what kind
of people you were. Whether you were... capable. And decent. Because
what I am going to ask of you requires both."

"What are you going to ask?" Iskar said.

"I do not know entirely." Both eyes focused. The convergence was brief
but complete, and in it the full weight of the man behind the ruin was
visible: intelligent, compassionate, exhausted, and absolutely
certain. "I have carried this gift for seventy years. I have been
broken by it and remade by it and broken again. I have felt, for all
of those years, that there was a purpose waiting for me, a reason
Thalaman chose me, and I have never known what it was." He leaned
forward. The quarterstaff shifted. "When your machine sent its
vibration across the planes, it was the loudest thing I have ever
felt. The clearest. The most certain. Whatever you found beneath that
keep, whatever you saw through that device, it is connected to my
purpose. I am sure of this in the way I am sure of... of very little
else."

"That doesn't tell us much," Smiggles said.

"No. It does not. But I can offer you something in return for your
patience." He looked at each of them in turn. "Take my hands. All of
you. And I will show you what I am."

---

The years after the forest were a descent.

Aleron wandered Vand'ardhon for two decades, moving from tribe to
tribe, seeking answers from priests and druids and scholars who could
not provide them because the questions he carried had no precedent in
their traditions. The involuntary shifts continued. They came without
warning, triggered by fear, by exhaustion, by emotional disturbance,
by nothing at all: moments when the fabric of reality around him
simply gave way and he was elsewhere, standing in a world that was
not his own, surrounded by landscapes and peoples and skies that bore
no relationship to the one he had left.

He learned to return. That was the mercy of it, the single kindness
in the ruin of his life: whatever force pulled him out always pulled
him back, the transit collapsing after minutes or hours, the world he
belonged to reasserting its claim on him with a violence that left
him bleeding and shaking and grateful. But the return was never clean.
Things followed him back. After one shift, an incorporeal entity, a
thing of cold shadow and malice that had latched onto the tear in
reality his transit had created, slipped through into the village
where he had been staying. It possessed three elves before the
tribe's priest could perform the exorcism, and the priest's face when
he told Aleron to leave carried an expression that Aleron would see
again and again over the following years: fear, pity, and the firm
conviction that whatever Aleron was, he was dangerous, and whatever
he carried, it was contagious.

He left. He always left.

A year later, he was meditating near a river when the shift took him
to a plane of water. He was gone for seconds. The portal he left
behind stayed open for hours, and the water that poured through it
flooded the river valley for miles, destroying crops that would not
grow back for two seasons. He was not there to see the damage. He was
already walking, heading east, toward the mountains, away from anyone
the gift could hurt.

A divination saved him from complete despair. Vocinder, an elderly
priest of Mau who lived in a cave on the eastern slopes of
Vand'ardhon and who was, by reputation, either the wisest oracle on
the continent or the maddest, which in the tradition of Mau were not
mutually exclusive qualities, agreed to examine him. The old priest
sat across from him for three hours, saying nothing, his eyes closed,
his weathered hands resting on his knees. When he finally spoke, his
voice carried the cadence of a man who had spent so long listening to
the silence between words that his own speech had rearranged itself
around it.

"Touched by Thalaman, you are," he said. "The Chaos Walker. The Time
Breaker. The entity that in Limbo exists and across every timeline
simultaneously is fragmented. A piece of its gift you carry, which
also its curse is, which also its purpose is. Remove it, I cannot. No
one on this continent can remove it. Woven into you it is, at a level
that predates your birth and may postdate your death."

"What am I supposed to do with it?" Aleron asked.

Vocinder opened one eye. It was milky with age but sharp beneath the
cloudiness, the eye of a man who had looked at things most people
preferred not to see. "Know this, I do not," he said. "But know this,
I do: given randomly, the gift was not. Nothing randomly does Thalaman
do, despite being the entity of chaos, or perhaps because of it. A
purpose in you there is. Find it you will, or find you it will." The
old priest's mouth curved in a smile that was all gum and no
reassurance. "The latter, I suspect."

"Can you tell me anything about the purpose?" Aleron pressed. "The
dreams, the visions, the fire, the shapes in the smoke. Do they mean
something?"

"Mean something, all things do. But the shape of your purpose, even
Mau does not show me clearly. Only this:" Vocinder raised one
weathered hand and held it flat, palm down, as though pressing against
an invisible surface. "A wall, there is. Between what is and what
should not be. Thin, this wall grows. Thinner still, it will become.
And when it breaks..." He lowered the hand. "There you will stand.
Why, I cannot say. But there, you will be."

Aleron left the cave with more knowledge and no more comfort, which
was, he supposed, the nature of oracles.

Seven years later, the gift killed twelve people.

---

They took his hands.

Smiggles did so with the expression of a man submitting to a medical
procedure he considered probably unnecessary and definitely
uncomfortable. Iskar did so with the expression of a man who had
touched stranger things in stranger ports and survived. Aizune did so
with the expression of a priest whose divine senses were screaming that
something was about to happen and whose faith told him to let it.

Aleron closed his eyes. The trembling in his hands stopped. For the
first time since he had entered the common room, his body was
completely still, the constant low vibration of his damaged frame
subsiding into a quiet that was not peace but concentration, the
gathering of something vast into a point so small it disappeared.

The Ogre's Arse vanished.

The transition was not movement. It was replacement. One moment the
common room surrounded them: the noise, the smoke, the smell of bad
ale and worse cooking, the bard's three chords cycling endlessly by
the fire. The next moment there was nothing but blue.

Blue that had no end. Blue that had no beginning. Blue that stretched
in every direction, above and below and to every horizon, a blue so
vast and so deep and so utterly without boundary that the mind recoiled
from it the way the eye recoils from staring into the sun, not because
it was painful but because it was too large, too complete, too
fundamentally beyond the architecture of mortal comprehension. They
floated in it. They breathed it. The air, if it was air, tasted of
nothing and everything, of distance itself, of the space between
thoughts.

The Astral Sea.

Aizune had read about it. He had studied the cosmological texts that
described the first plane, the plane that Mau had created when the
Oracle's gaze spread across all of time and space. He had understood,
intellectually, that the Astral Sea was the medium in which all other
planes existed, the vast blue emptiness that held the pockets of
reality the way an ocean holds islands.

Understanding it intellectually and floating in it were not the same
experience.

Smiggles made a sound. It was not a word. It was the sound a man makes
when every theoretical framework he has ever constructed meets the
reality it was built to describe and discovers that the reality is
larger, by several orders of magnitude, than the framework can contain.
His hazel eyes were enormous. His moustache was limp. His mouth opened,
closed, opened again.

"Gears and grindstones," he whispered.

Iskar said nothing. He hung in the blue void with his arms
outstretched and his head tilted back and his dark eyes reflecting the
infinite emptiness above him, and on his face was an expression that
Aizune had never seen there before and would not see again for a long
time: wonder, pure and unperformed, the face of a man who had spent
his life on the surface of the ocean and had just been shown its
depth.

A heartbeat. Two. The blue held them, patient and eternal, the silence
of a space that had existed for longer than any consciousness within
it and would exist long after the last consciousness departed.

The Ogre's Arse returned.

The transition was as sudden as the departure. Blue replaced by smoke
and noise and the smell of cooking and the bard's three chords and the
solid, wonderful, impossibly reassuring feeling of a wooden chair
beneath one's body and a floor beneath one's feet. Aizune's hand
released Aleron's. His other hand found the table's edge and gripped
it with the white-knuckled intensity of a man confirming that solid
objects still existed.

Aleron opened his eyes. The grey irises were ringed with a faint
luminescence that faded as Aizune watched, the residual glow of
whatever power he had just channelled draining from his body like
water from a cracked vessel. His hands were shaking again, worse
than before. His skin, already pale, had gone translucent at the
temples and wrists, the veins beneath standing out like ink on
parchment.

"That," he said quietly, "is what I am."

The silence at the table was the silence of three people whose
understanding of the possible had just been comprehensively revised.

Smiggles spoke first. He picked up his ale, drank half of it in one
pull, set it down, and stared at it as though it had personally
betrayed him by not being brandy. "That calibrates," he said. His
voice was hoarse. "That calibrates with everything. The machine, the
vision, the alternate reality. If you can move people between planes,
if you can feel dimensional disturbances across hundreds of miles,
then you are exactly the kind of... of phenomenon that would be
connected to what we found."

"I am not a phenomenon," Aleron said, mildly. "I am a person. A
somewhat... a somewhat damaged person, admittedly. But a person."

"A person who just moved four people to the Astral Plane and back
in the time it takes to blink." Smiggles' voice had found its footing.
The analytical engine behind his eyes was running, processing,
cataloguing. "What's the cost?"

Aleron's smile was thin. "Everything."

"Be specific."

"Each transit drains me. Physically. The... the larger the transit,
the more people I carry, the more it takes. What I just showed you
was a demonstration, brief, controlled. A full transit to another
plane, carrying a group, would leave me... incapacitated for hours.
Possibly days." He paused. His eyes tracked something invisible
crossing the room behind Smiggles' head. He waited for it to pass.
"And there is a risk. If I am damaged during or immediately after a
transit, if my concentration breaks, there is a... a significant
chance of involuntary shifts. Random destinations. Random planes.
Everyone nearby comes with me."

"Everyone nearby," Iskar repeated.

"Yes."

"To a random plane of existence."

"Yes."

"Including, potentially, unpleasant ones."

"Including the Abyss. Including the Negative Energy Plane. Including
places that would kill you in... in seconds." The honesty was
unflinching. "This is what I am. This is the gift. It can take you
where you need to go, and it can kill you if something goes wrong.
I will not pretend otherwise."

Iskar leaned back. He looked at Aizune. He looked at Smiggles. He
looked at Aleron, at the white-streaked hair and the trembling hands
and the grey eyes that saw things no one else could see.

"I've sailed in worrse conditions," he said.

---

He was sitting against a tree when the shift took the twelve.

It was a village in the eastern forests, a small settlement of
Ya'sehrim who had accepted him with the cautious hospitality that his
reputation now preceded: the elf with the white in his hair, the elf
who talked to people who were not there, the elf around whom reality
was thin. He had stayed for a month, the longest he had stayed
anywhere since Vocinder's cave. He had helped with the fishing. He
had played with the children, carefully, always conscious of the
distance he needed to maintain, the invisible perimeter beyond which
his proximity became danger.

He was sitting with his back against an oak, the trunk solid and warm
behind him, the afternoon light falling through the canopy in coins
of gold, and the echoes were quiet for once, the translucent figures
at the edges of his vision faded to whispers, and he was as close to
peace as the gift ever allowed him to come.

The shift came without warning. No fear. No emotional trigger. No
visible cause. The world simply folded, the forest collapsing inward
like a book closing, and the darkness that replaced it was not the
darkness of any world he had visited before.

It was the Abyss.

He knew it immediately. The air was wrong: thick, hot, tasting of ash
and copper and something sweeter that turned his stomach. The ground
beneath him was not ground but something organic, yielding, warm, as
though he were standing on the skin of a living thing. The sky was a
wound: red and black and pulsing, the light coming from everywhere
and nowhere, casting no shadows because there was nothing in this
place clean enough to stop the light.

And he was not alone.

Twelve elves stood around him in the rough circle of the positions
they had occupied in the village: two fishers who had been mending
nets by the stream, a woman who had been hanging laundry, three
children who had been playing in the clearing, six others caught in
the radius of whatever the gift had done. They stood in the Abyss
with the stunned immobility of people who had been in one place and
were now in another and whose minds had not yet begun to process the
distance between the two.

The manes came from the organic ground itself, rising through it like
corpses surfacing from a shallow grave. Pale white creatures, small,
mindless, driven by a hunger that was the only thing they were. Dozens
of them. Then hundreds.

They swarmed the twelve.

Aleron could not move. The shift had drained him, dropped him to his
knees, left his body empty and his mind screaming, and he knelt on
the living ground of the Abyss and watched the pale-white manes climb
over the twelve elves and heard the sounds they made, the sounds that
would never leave him for the rest of his life, that would be waiting
for him every time he closed his eyes and every time the echoes grew
loud and every time the world was quiet enough for memory to speak.

The manes did not touch him. They swarmed around him and over him and
past him as though he were not there, as though the gift that had
brought him here also marked him as untouchable, protected,
preserved, because Thalaman's purpose required him alive and whole
and Thalaman's purpose did not extend to the twelve.

When the shift reversed, when the forest reassembled and the afternoon
light returned and the oak tree pressed against his back with the
solid reliability of a world that had not changed, Aleron was alone.
Twelve elves were dead. He had felt each death through the gift,
twelve small collapses in the fabric of reality, twelve lights
extinguished in a darkness he had carried them into.

He ran. He did not say goodbye to the settlement. He did not explain.
He ran into the forest and he did not stop running until the forest
ended and the mountains began and the mountains ended and the coast
began and the coast ended and there was nothing left to run toward
except the horizon and the knowledge, absolute and unshakable, that
he was a weapon that fired without a trigger and killed without
distinction and would do so again and again until whatever purpose
Thalaman had placed inside him was fulfilled or until he died, and
he did not know which would come first, and he was not certain which
he preferred.

Madness, it would seem, would always be his travelling companion.

"I will help you," Aizune said.

He said it simply, without ceremony, without the qualifying
conditions that a more cautious man would have attached. He said it
because the machine's vision had shown him a world where everything he
loved was destroyed and this man, this ruined, trembling, extraordinary
man, could feel the same vibrations that machine had sent across the
planes, and the divine sense that lived in the place where Minerva's
gift resided told him, with a clarity that burned like silver light,
that Aleron Windstrider was telling the truth.

"I've got questions," Smiggles said. "Many questions. But the
demonstration was..." He searched for the word. "Finely ground. You
are what you say you are. The evidence calibrates. I want to understand
the mechanism, the planar physics, the cost structures, all of it. But
in principle." He looked at Aleron. "In principle, I'm in."

Iskar was quiet for a moment. His dark eyes rested on Aleron's face
with the steady assessment of a man who made decisions about people
the way sailors made decisions about weather: by reading the signs and
trusting the reading. "You said you watched us. That you needed to
know if we were capable and decent."

"Yes."

"And?"

Aleron's smile surfaced again, the small, sad, genuine thing that
appeared when something cut through the echoes and reached the person
underneath. "You brought a dead man home," he said. "A man you had
never met, whose name you did not know, because you believed he
deserved better than the place where he died. That is... that is not
the act of people who are merely capable." He paused. Listened to
something only he could hear. Nodded slightly, as though agreeing with
it. "You are decent. And I am very... very glad I found you."

The common room of the Ogre's Arse continued around them: the bard
found his fourth chord, the barkeep polished a glass with the
meditative patience of a man who had accepted that his evening would
not improve, and in the corner by the rear door, a shadow with yellow
eyes watched the four of them through a crack she had not entirely
closed, her face carrying an expression that was complicated and
private and contained, somewhere in its depths, something that might
have been fear.

They sat together in the noise and the smoke and the light of a tavern
in a city that did not know what was coming, and they began to plan.

Outside, the two moons rose over Amara. Silver and gold. Patient and
whole. Between them, the sky was empty.

But not for long.
