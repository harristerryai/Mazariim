# Chapter One
## The Godslayer's Boredom

The ocean had not changed in eight hundred years.

Rangithyr stood on the veranda of his villa and watched the water do what water had always done: move without purpose, retreat without shame, and return without memory of having left. The waves broke against the pale sand of the crescent beach below the bluff in rhythms that had predated his birth and would outlast his death, if death ever found the courage to come for him, which in eight centuries it had not.

The morning was warm. Salt wind came off the strait in steady currents that bent the scrub grass along the bluff's edge and carried the smell of brine and the faintest trace of volcanic soil, the ancient fertility of an island whose farmers had been dead for longer than most civilisations had existed. The sky was pale blue, cloudless, the three moons having set hours before dawn, and the sun climbed the eastern horizon with the unhurried confidence of a thing that had never been challenged and could not imagine the possibility.

He stood barefoot on the stone. The veranda ran the full length of the villa's seaward face, broad enough for six men to walk abreast, paved in dark slate that held the night's coolness against his soles in a way that he found, after centuries of noticing, faintly pleasant. He wore loose trousers of undyed linen and an open robe that the wind pressed against his frame, and nothing else. No armour. No rank. No insignia. The body beneath the robe needed no advertisement: over eight feet of dense blue skin and heavy musculature, the physiology of an ogre-magi sustained by regeneration that had kept every sinew at its peak for longer than the races he had conquered had kept their histories. The small horns above his brow caught the early light and threw faint shadows down his face, and the deep-set amber eyes beneath them watched the water with an expression that was not quite patience and not quite its opposite.

It was boredom. He had learned to recognise it the way a physician learned to recognise a disease: by its symptoms, by its progression, by the particular quality of discomfort it produced in a body that was otherwise immune to discomfort. Boredom was not pain. Pain he understood. Pain had visited him in the War, when Mordrion's avatar had driven a blessed lance through his shoulder and the divine energy had burned through flesh that was accustomed to healing any wound in seconds and found, for the first and only time, that some wounds resisted. That scar remained. A pale line across blue skin, the only mark on his body that his regeneration had not erased, because the energy that made it was not mortal and did not respect mortal flesh's claim to restoration.

Boredom was not pain. Boredom was absence. The absence of purpose in a being that had been built, from the first cell to the last thought, for purpose. Eight centuries of service to Zuel and the Orceri Empire, eight centuries of war and conquest and the systematic expansion of civilisation across a world and then across the boundaries of reality itself, and now this: an ocean, a villa, a morning that was indistinguishable from the morning before it and the morning before that, stretching backward through decades of identical dawns to the point where memory blurred and the days ceased to be days and became instead a single, undifferentiated experience of waiting.

He was not waiting for anything. That was the problem. To wait implied an expectation, and expectation implied a future that differed from the present, and Rangithyr had not been able to distinguish his future from his present in over a century.

The scimitar waited on its rack inside the villa, in the armoury, in the room where he kept the instruments of a life that the peace had rendered decorative. He would go to it soon. He went to it every morning. The ritual had not varied in a hundred years: rise before dawn, stand on the veranda, watch the ocean fail to change, then go inside and draw the blade from its rack and sit with it across his knees and polish it with slow, meditative strokes, the cloth moving along the curved edge with a rhythm that matched the waves outside, repetitive and pointless and necessary in the way that breathing was necessary, not because it accomplished anything but because the alternative was to stop.

He went inside.

---

The armoury occupied the eastern wing of the villa, a long room with thick stone walls and deep-set windows that admitted the morning light in slanted columns. The windows faced the remnants of the halfling village: geometric patterns in the grass where foundation walls had once supported round doors and thatched roofs and the particular architecture of a people who built for comfort rather than defence and who had discovered, in the final hours of their civilisation, that comfort was not a fortification.

Rangithyr had never ordered the foundations cleared. They did not bother him. They were simply there, the way the sand was there, the way the ocean was there, features of a landscape he had shaped through conquest and now inhabited through habit. If the halflings' ghosts lingered in those geometric patterns, they had the good sense not to manifest. He would not have been troubled if they had. Ghosts were the echoes of beings too weak to survive their own deaths, and weakness, even posthumous weakness, did not interest him.

The weapons lined the walls in racks of dark iron, each one maintained with the same discipline he had applied to them in the field: oiled, sharpened, balanced, arranged by type and by the era of their acquisition. Polearms from the Great War, their hafts replaced twice over the centuries but their heads original, the metal still carrying the faint resonance of the forges where they had been made. Axes from the conquest of the elven continent, heavy-headed and brutal, designed for the work of cutting through barricades and the things that hid behind them. A longbow of composite construction that he had taken from a Shalazari defender whose name he had never learned and whose skill with the weapon had been, he acknowledged, remarkable for a creature half his size. The bow hung on the wall beside the axes, and the acknowledgement lived in the fact of its preservation: Rangithyr kept the weapons of enemies who had earned his respect, and discarded the weapons of enemies who had not, and the distinction was the closest he came to sentiment.

The scimitar occupied the central rack, alone, the space around it empty because nothing else belonged beside it.

He lifted it with one hand. The blade was massive, curved, the edge honed to a geometry that was less sharpness than inevitability, the certainty that anything the edge contacted would part. The metal was dark, almost black, forged from an alloy that the Orceri smiths had developed specifically for this weapon, and within the alloy the harvested mana pulsed: cold bluish light that lived in the steel the way veins lived in flesh, a network of luminescence that intensified when the blade moved through air and dimmed when it rested, as though the energy inside it was aware of the difference between potential and action and preferred the latter.

Mana stripped from so-called gods.

He sat in the heavy chair that faced the window, the scimitar across his knees, and drew the polishing cloth from the table beside him. The cloth was soft, treated with an oil that the blade did not need, because mana-forged steel did not tarnish or dull or corrode, and the oil was therefore not maintenance but ritual, and the ritual was not devotion but remembrance, and the remembrance was the only thing that still connected the being he was now to the being he had been when the blade had mattered.

He drew the cloth along the curve. The steel was warm beneath the fabric. The mana pulsed.

He remembered.

---

The warhammer had been enormous.

Not merely large in the way that dwarven weapons were large, compensating for the stature of their wielders with mass and leverage and the particular stubbornness that defined everything the bearded races built. This weapon had been something beyond craft. It had been the instrument of Mordrion himself, blessed and empowered and given to the one dwarf in the alliance whose arms were strong enough and whose heart was foolish enough to carry a god's wrath into the presence of a god's enemy.

Demious Stoneshaper. The name still tasted of something when Rangithyr spoke it in the silence of his mind. Not respect, precisely. Respect implied equality, and Stoneshaper had not been his equal. The dwarf had been a hero, which was a different thing, a creature elevated beyond its natural capacity by faith and desperation and the particular lunacy that seized mortal beings when they believed their cause was worth more than their lives. Rangithyr did not believe in causes. He believed in objectives. But he recognised the power that belief gave to smaller beings, the way it burned in them like fuel in a furnace, brief and bright and ultimately consumed by the very heat it generated.

Stoneshaper had burned very bright.

The moment came back to him with the clarity of something preserved in amber, every detail intact, every sensation as vivid as the morning he had lived it. The battlefield at Fort Hope. The smoke and the screaming and the ground churned to mud by the feet of a hundred thousand soldiers. Zuel's avatar towering above the carnage, thirty feet of divine fury given flesh, sulphurous eyes blazing, the massive war-axe in his hands already wet with the ichor of two destroyed entities. Quarzel had fallen first, the entity of war overwhelmed by the very force he governed, blue annihilating energy pouring from Zuel's hands and burning through the avatar's defences until there was nothing left, not even the echo of a scream, just a hole in reality where a being of immense power had existed one moment and did not exist the next. Mordrion had followed. Zuel's spiked knee driven through the dwarven entity's chest, divine energies streaking toward the heavens as the avatar collapsed, and Mordrion's scream had been worse than Quarzel's silence, because it carried the particular anguish of a being who had crossed universes to protect a people and was failing them in the hour they needed him most.

And then Stoneshaper.

The dwarf had come through the chaos like a boulder through a river, unstoppable not because he could not be stopped but because stopping him would have required the kind of attention that Zuel's avatar, drunk on the destruction of two entities, had not thought to spare for a single mortal. The warhammer blazed with white-hot runes. The dwarf's face was a mask of grief and fury and the absolute, incandescent certainty that what he was about to do was the only thing in the world that mattered.

The blow would have landed.

Rangithyr knew this with the cold precision of a tactician who had replayed the moment ten thousand times across eight hundred years. The angle was true. The force was sufficient. The divine energy invested in the weapon was calibrated for exactly this purpose: the shattering of an avatar's physical form, the dispersal of the entity's projected consciousness back to its plane of origin. The blow would have landed, and Zuel's avatar would have been destroyed, and the War would have ended, and the alliance would have won, and the world would have been a different place.

The blow did not land.

Rangithyr had been waiting on the Ethereal Plane for six hours. Six hours of hovering in the grey nowhere between realities, watching the battle unfold through the translucent veil that separated the material world from its ghostly reflection, patient in the way that only a being bred for war and sustained by regeneration could be patient, because patience was a weapon and he had learned to wield it before he had learned to wield the scimitar. He had watched Quarzel fall. He had watched Mordrion fall. He had watched the alliance break and reform and break again around the towering nightmare of Zuel's avatar. And he had watched the dwarf.

The dwarf with the blazing hammer. The dwarf whose trajectory through the battlefield was as clear as a compass bearing, aimed at the one point in the chaos where a single blow could end everything.

He had stepped through.

The transition from Ethereal to Material was instantaneous: a flicker of displacement, a breath of cold that was not temperature but the friction of two planes separating as a body forced its way between them, and then he was there, eight feet of blue skin and dense muscle and the scimitar already moving, interposing itself between the dwarf's hammer and Zuel's exposed flank with a precision that was not luck and not instinct but the product of six hours of observation and a lifetime of understanding that the difference between victory and annihilation was measured in inches and fractions of seconds.

The hammer struck the scimitar.

The impact travelled through the blade and into his arms and through his shoulders and into the bones of his spine, and for one moment, one single moment in eight centuries of combat, Rangithyr felt something that might have been surprise, because the force behind that blow was not the force of a dwarf. It was the force of a god channelled through a dwarf, and the difference was the difference between a river and the ocean that fed it. His feet slid backward in the mud. His arms, which had never failed him, which had held against charges and sieges and the physical manifestation of divine wrath, bent at the elbows and trembled under a weight that was not physical but metaphysical, the pressure of a murdered entity's final gift bearing down on the instrument of his killer's protection.

He held.

The hammer rebounded. Stoneshaper staggered. And in the space between the parry and the dwarf's recovery, a space that lasted less than a heartbeat, Zuel's avatar turned, and the war-axe descended, and the hero's head left his shoulders with a sound that Rangithyr heard through the roar of the battle and the screaming of the dying and the thunder of collapsing divine energies, a sound that was small and wet and final.

One parry. The course of the world had pivoted on the edge of his blade, and the world had fallen toward the side he had chosen, and eight hundred years later he was sitting in a villa on an island whose defenders he had killed, polishing the blade that had made the choice, and the ocean outside had not changed and would not change and the morning was the same as every morning and nothing, nothing in the vast and conquered span of reality, required his attention or his strength or the edge that he maintained with such ritual devotion on a weapon that had not been drawn in battle since before most of the civilisations in the tangent realities had learned to smelt iron.

The cloth moved along the curve. The mana pulsed. The memory faded, not because it was weak but because he had revisited it so many times that the edges had worn smooth, and the sharp grief of purpose that it used to provoke had dulled to a warmth that was comfortable and unsatisfying, the warmth of an old fire that had not been fed.

He set down the cloth.

"So long since a good battle, a good war," he said, to the empty room and the weapons on the walls and the morning light falling through the windows onto the geometric patterns in the grass where a civilisation had stood and fallen and been forgotten by everyone except the being who had ended it. "Far too long."

---

The summons had come four hundred and nineteen years ago.

The memory was distinct from the memories of war because it carried a different quality of significance: not the significance of violence, which was immediate and physical, but the significance of revelation, which was slow and vast and changed the shape of everything that followed it.

Ozmakir's laboratory. Deep beneath the capital, in chambers carved from the bedrock that had supported the enemy's fortress before Zuel's victory transformed it into the foundation of empire. The corridors were lit by mana conduits that ran along the walls and ceiling, dark metal pulsing with cold bluish light, the stripped essence of entities who had opposed the Orceri and been consumed, their power repurposed, their divinity reduced to infrastructure. Rangithyr walked those corridors in full armour because Ozmakir's summons had carried the tone of something that demanded formality, and formality, for a soldier, meant the weight of war-plate on his shoulders and the scimitar at his hip and the readiness to act on whatever the wizard had found.

The laboratory door was open. The chamber beyond was vast, the natural cavern expanded and reinforced with Orceri engineering into a space that could have housed a battalion. But it did not house a battalion. It housed one unremarkable orc, and the thing he had built.

Ozmakir stood beside his creation with the posture of a man who had spent four hundred years searching for something and had found it three hours ago and had not yet decided how to feel about the discovery. His dark robes smelled of raspberry incense and sulphur. His hair was neatly trimmed and combed back. His face and hands were clean-shaven, his feet bare on the polished basalt floor, and the only remarkable thing about him was the intelligence in his eyes, which was considerable, and the stillness of his body, which was absolute.

"General," he said. "Thank you for coming."

"I do hope this is important. I am not in a mood to be bothered, wizard."

"It is important." Ozmakir's voice carried no offence. He had known Rangithyr for centuries and had long since learned that the general's impatience was not directed at individuals but at the universe's failure to provide him with adequate stimulation. "I have found something."

"You have been finding things for four centuries. Your laboratory is full of things you have found. I am told you found a new formula for stabilising mana transfer across crystalline lattices last month. The engineering division was beside itself with excitement, which I gather is the engineering division's equivalent of a victory celebration."

"This is not a crystalline lattice." Ozmakir turned toward the curved wall behind him, and for the first time Rangithyr looked at the thing the wizard had built.

Mirrors. Dozens of them. Not glass but liquid-metal surfaces held in frames of dark iron, each one approximately the height and width of a man, arranged in a gentle arc that followed the natural curve of the cavern wall. Most showed nothing: flat silver surfaces reflecting the chamber's mana light in cold blue ripples. But several, clustered near the centre of the arc, showed something else.

Images. Landscapes. Cities and forests and coastlines rendered in shimmering detail that flickered and shifted with the instability of something that was not meant to be seen from this angle, like looking through water at a world that existed below the surface. The images were familiar in their broad composition, recognisable as places that might have existed on this world if this world had evolved differently, and unfamiliar in their specific details, because the cities were not Orceri cities and the figures moving through them were not Orceri.

Humans. Walking upright. Unshackled. Speaking to one another with the casual confidence of beings who had never known subjugation.

Elves. Alive. Their forests intact, green and vast and undamaged, stretching across landscapes that in this reality had been ash and bare earth for eight hundred years.

Dwarves. Free. Building, smithing, trading, their squat bodies moving through cities of their own construction with the proprietary bustle of a race that owned what it occupied.

Rangithyr looked at the mirrors for a long time. His amber eyes moved from image to image with the systematic precision of a commander surveying a battlefield: assessing scale, noting dispositions, cataloguing resources, estimating strength. The expression on his face did not change. His body did not move. Only his eyes were active, and in them the cold blue light of the mirrors reflected alongside something deeper and older, something that the centuries of peace had buried and the mirrors were excavating.

"Other versions of our planet," Ozmakir said. "Alternate realities. Worlds where the history diverged at critical points and produced different outcomes. The theory has existed for centuries, but the proof..." He gestured at the mirrors with one hand, a small, precise motion that managed to encompass the infinite. "The proof is here."

"How many?"

"How many other worlds are there, General?" Ozmakir's unremarkable face produced a smile that was not unremarkable at all. It grew slowly, starting at the corners of his mouth and spreading with the patient inevitability of something that had been waiting a long time to surface. "Infinite, sir. An infinite number."

The word settled into the chamber like a stone into deep water. Rangithyr felt it reach the bottom of his comprehension and send ripples upward through layers of understanding that had not been disturbed in a century. Infinite worlds. Infinite versions of this planet, each one shaped by different choices and different outcomes, each one populated by races that had survived or fallen according to the particular lottery of their history.

Infinite worlds where the alliance had won. Where Stoneshaper's blow had landed. Where the orcs had been broken and scattered and the lesser races had built their pathetic civilisations on the ruins of Zuel's ambition.

Infinite worlds that did not know the Orceri existed.

"Can we reach them?" Rangithyr asked. His voice was quiet. It had been quiet throughout. But the quality of the quiet had changed, the way the quality of air changed before a storm: the same substance, the same temperature, the same pressure, and yet different in a way that the body recognised before the mind could name.

"I believe so. With time. With resources. The barriers between realities are not walls. They are... membranes. Permeable, under the right conditions. The correct frequency, the correct energy, the correct application of force." Ozmakir's smile reached his eyes. "The same principles that govern any siege."

Rangithyr looked at the mirrors. At the cities and the forests and the unshackled humans and the living elves and the free dwarves, all of them existing in blissful ignorance of the reality that bordered their own, the reality where everything they had built and loved and fought for had been torn down and rebuilt in the image of the conqueror.

He looked at the images for a long time.

And then Ozmakir said the word that changed everything.

"Battle."

A single word. Spoken without emphasis, without drama, without the rhetorical inflation that lesser minds applied to significant moments. Ozmakir was not a dramatic man. He was a scientist, and scientists stated facts.

Battle. Not a memory of battle. Not the fading echo of campaigns completed and victories catalogued and purpose fulfilled. Battle: present tense, future tense, the promise of something that the centuries of peace had stolen and the mirrors were returning.

Rangithyr felt it then. Not excitement. Excitement was too small a word, too brief, too easily confused with the shallow stimulations that entertained lesser beings. What he felt was structural, foundational, the sensation of a cornerstone being placed in an architecture that had stood incomplete for so long he had forgotten it was unfinished. Purpose. The particular, irreplaceable certainty that one's existence was aimed at something, that the skills honed over centuries and the strength maintained through millennia of regeneration and the tactical genius that had conquered a world and then conquered the planes beyond it were not decorative, were not relics, were not the artefacts of a life that had served its function and been retired to a villa on an island.

Purpose. He had forgotten what it tasted like. It tasted like the morning air before a charge. It tasted like the weight of the scimitar in his hand when the hand had somewhere to swing. It tasted like the future, which was a flavour the peace had taken from him so gradually that he had not noticed its absence until the mirrors brought it back.

"How many other worlds, my good wizard?" he had asked. And the question had not been academic.

---

Four hundred and nineteen years. Eleven worlds.

The first had been the hardest, not because the resistance was strong but because the mechanisms of interdimensional transit were new, and novelty demanded caution, and caution was a discipline Rangithyr applied with the same rigour he applied to aggression. Ozmakir's scientists had spent a decade refining the crossing technology before the first expeditionary force set foot on an alternate reality, a world whose divergence point was so recent that its geography was almost identical to Ta'Puh and whose inhabitants, a gnomish civilisation of extraordinary mechanical ingenuity, had never encountered an organised military force and had no framework for understanding what was happening to them until it was too late.

The gnomish world fell in three months. Its clockwork designs, its precision instruments, its dirigible technology were absorbed into the Orceri infrastructure with the efficiency of a body absorbing nutrition. The gnomish population was... processed. Rangithyr did not attend to the details of processing. He was a general, not an administrator. His function was to break resistance, and once resistance was broken, the administrators and the biomancers and the engineers who followed in his wake performed their functions as he performed his: competently, thoroughly, without sentiment.

The subsequent worlds fell faster. The psionic elf world, whose population was absorbed into the hivemind that became the sensorium network. The beast world, whose fauna provided the biomantic raw material for war-constructs that the engineers shaped into siege engines and assault platforms and the enormous burrowing organisms that could reduce a fortification to rubble from below. Each conquest added a capability. Each capability made the next conquest more efficient. The Orceri war machine was a self-improving system, and Rangithyr was its architect, and the system functioned with a precision that satisfied the part of him that appreciated craft and the part of him that appreciated results in equal measure.

Eleven worlds. Eleven civilisations identified, assessed, invaded, conquered, and integrated. Each one a campaign with its own challenges and its own satisfactions, each one a problem worthy of the mind that had blocked Stoneshaper's blow and hunted entities across the planes of existence.

And then the campaigns had ended, because the worlds that remained were either too remote to reach efficiently or too barren to justify the resource expenditure, and the great machine of interdimensional conquest had slowed and stopped and Rangithyr had returned to Shalazar and the ocean and the mornings that did not change.

Until now.

---

He stood on the veranda with the scimitar cleaned and racked and the morning settling into its familiar stillness. The ocean moved. The wind bent the grass. The geometric patterns of the halfling foundations were visible in the fields behind the villa, and beyond them the garrison buildings where two thousand Orceri soldiers and administrative staff maintained the island's minimal infrastructure with the quiet efficiency of a machine that had nothing to do and did it well.

An aide appeared at the veranda's edge. Young. Junior officer. The careful posture of a soldier who had been assigned to attend the Godslayer and had received extensive briefing on the protocols this entailed, the most important of which was: do not speak until spoken to, and when spoken to, be brief.

Rangithyr regarded him. The amber eyes performed their assessment in less than a second: rank, posture, nervousness, the communication crystal held in both hands with the particular care of someone carrying an object whose contents were above his security clearance.

"Sir," the aide said. "Priority communication from Zone Command. Relayed through Commander Li'Zhan's office. Marked for your attention, General."

"Leave it."

The aide placed the crystal on the stone railing of the veranda with hands that trembled only slightly, saluted in the Orceri fashion, fist to chest, and withdrew with the controlled haste of a man who had survived an encounter he had been dreading since dawn.

Rangithyr looked at the crystal. It sat on the dark stone, a faceted sphere of deep blue, inert until activated, carrying within its lattice a message that had been transmitted across dimensional boundaries from a monitoring station on the edge of charted reality.

He did not pick it up immediately. Instead he looked at the ocean, one last time, and felt the morning's stillness settle around him like a garment he was about to remove.

Then he took the crystal in one massive hand and activated it with a pulse of mana that was as natural and as unconscious as breathing.

Li'Zhan's voice filled the air, clipped and precise, the voice of a commander delivering intelligence that he knew would travel up a chain of authority that ended at the being now holding the crystal in the salt wind of a conquered island.

"General. Unscheduled contact, tangent designation pending. Zone Kluth Tak monitoring sensoriums detected a dimensional breach at coordinates consistent with a standard prime material plane, intact civilisations, functional entity presence. Contact was brief, accidental in origin, and originated from the target tangent. The breach has been flagged for reconnaissance. Commander Yzrat has been dispatched per standing operational protocols. Full assessment to follow."

The crystal dimmed. The message ended. The ocean continued its indifferent rhythm against the sand.

Rangithyr held the crystal and felt the weight of it, not the physical weight, which was negligible, but the other weight, the one that settled into the architecture of his purpose and found the space that had been waiting for it.

A twelfth world.

Intact civilisations. Functional entity presence. A reality where the lesser races still lived and built and worshipped their so-called gods and believed, with the pathetic confidence of creatures who had never been shown otherwise, that their world was the only one that mattered.

He closed his hand around the crystal. The facets pressed into his palm. The cold bluish light of his mana pulsed in the veins of his skin, responding to the proximity of potential, of purpose, of the thing that the centuries of peace had taken from him and that a monitoring station on the edge of known reality had just returned.

Twelve, he thought. The number had a shape to it. A completeness. Eleven was an architecture with a missing wall. Twelve was finished.

He set the crystal on the railing and went inside to his map room. The broad stone table was bare. It was always bare, because he had not needed it in decades, because maps were for campaigns and campaigns required enemies and enemies required worlds that had not yet been conquered.

He placed both hands flat on the stone and felt its coolness and its solidity and the potential that lived in empty surfaces, the promise that blankness made to the mind that would fill it.

And for the first time in longer than he could precisely recall, the morning felt different.

The silence had a crack in it.

And through the crack, like light through a door that had been closed so long its hinges had rusted shut and then been forced open by a hand that would not be denied, something was entering that Rangithyr recognised the way a man recognised his own heartbeat: not by analysis, not by memory, but by the simple, undeniable fact of its presence.

Purpose.

He did not smile. Smiling was for beings who experienced pleasure as surprise. Rangithyr experienced it as confirmation.

He pulled a chair to the table, sat, and began to think about war.
