# Chapter Eleven
## The Watcher Above

The skymage dressed for war the way other beings dressed for court:
with vanity, with precision, and with the absolute conviction that
appearance was a weapon whose edge should never be allowed to dull.

Yzrat stood before the long mirror in his quarters aboard the command
dirigible and examined himself with the critical attention of a
craftsman inspecting his finest work. The robes came first: bluish
black, the colour of a sky between dusk and true dark, cut to follow
the lines of his body with an exactness that left no fold unintended
and no seam visible. The fabric was Orceri weave, silk-fine and
strong as chainmail, treated with alchemical compounds that rendered
it resistant to fire, acid, and the particular abrasions of high-
altitude flight. He smoothed the front with both hands, palms flat,
fingers spread, feeling the material settle against his torso with
the satisfying precision of something that had been made for him and
for no one else.

The boots next. Mithril riding boots, form-fitted, rising to mid-calf,
their surfaces polished to a mirror sheen that reflected the cold
blue-white light of the cabin's mana lamps in shifting patterns as
he turned. The mithril was not decorative. At altitude, temperature
stripped heat from extremities with a speed that could cost a careless
rider his feet, and mithril conducted the ambient mana of an
enchanted mount upward through the soles, maintaining warmth through
magical circulation rather than insulation. The boots were beautiful
because they were functional, and Yzrat saw no contradiction between
the two qualities. Ugliness was inefficiency. Inefficiency was failure.
Failure was for lesser races.

He raised his left arm and pushed back the sleeve. The mana implant
in his forearm glowed beneath the skin, a thin line of cold bluish
light that pulsed with the rhythm of his circulatory system, each
pulse carrying an infinitesimally small piece of energy that had once
belonged to something that lesser civilisations called a god. The
implant had been installed by Ozmakir's biomancers forty years ago,
during the Orceri conquest of the eighth tangent, and Yzrat remembered
the procedure with the clarity of a man recalling a religious
experience: the pressure, the brief incandescence behind his eyes,
and then the settling of something vast into something small, divine
power compressed into a filament no thicker than a vein, woven into
his flesh, made part of him. A second implant occupied the cartilage
of his left ear, smaller, more delicate, its glow visible only when
he turned his head at certain angles, a blue point of light that
might have been mistaken for a jewel by someone who did not understand
what jewels the Orceri wore.

He touched the forearm implant with one finger. The light intensified
briefly, and the air around his hand shimmered with potential that
was not his own, borrowed divinity responding to the proximity of its
host's attention.

Mana stripped from so-called gods.

The thought pleased him. It always pleased him. The entities of the
prime material planes, the bloated, self-important consciousnesses
that lesser races worshipped as omnipotent creators, reduced to raw
material. Harvested. Processed. Implanted into the flesh of Orceri
soldiers and mages as tools, as power sources, as reminders that
divinity was not a quality but a resource, and resources existed to
be claimed by those strong enough to take them.

The goggles went on last. Smallish lenses of treated crystal set in
frames of dark metal, designed for high-altitude flight where wind
and light and the particular visual distortions of mana-saturated
atmosphere could blind an unprotected rider in seconds. They sat on
his forehead, pushed up, until he needed them. He examined the
completed image in the mirror: robes, boots, implants, goggles, the
smooth manicured skin of his face and hands, the clean-shaven jaw,
the hair cropped close and oiled flat against his skull. His tusks,
smaller than a common orc's and maintained with the same rigour he
applied to every other element of his appearance, caught the cabin
light as he smiled.

The smile was wide. The smile showed teeth. The smile was the
expression of a being who was about to do something interesting after
a long period of doing nothing interesting at all, and who intended
to enjoy every moment of it.

A shadow moved behind him.

It did not move the way shadows were supposed to move, which was to
say it moved with intention, detaching from the dark corner where the
cabin wall met the ceiling with a fluidity that suggested the darkness
itself was alive and had decided to take a more active interest in the
proceedings. The shadow resolved into a shape: slender, powerful,
compact, wearing black leather armour that seemed to drink the light
from the mana lamps so completely that the figure existed as a
silhouette with edges, an absence in the shape of a person.

Two points of cold bluish light marked the mana implants in the
figure's neck and forearm. Twin handaxes hung at its hips, their
edges honed to a thinness that was less a measurement than a
philosophical position.

"BOO!" said Unthil.

Yzrat did not flinch. He did not turn. He adjusted the hang of his
left sleeve with the unhurried precision of a man who had been
subjected to this particular greeting on enough occasions that his
startle reflex had been worn smooth by repetition.

"Must you," he said.

"Must I?" Unthil stepped from the shadow into the light, and the
transition was less a movement than a concession, the darkness
releasing him with visible reluctance. He was grinning. The grin was
wide and bright and contained the particular energy of a being who
found the world endlessly entertaining and intended to share his
entertainment with everyone in it whether they wanted it or not. "I
must. It is how I say hello. It is tradition. If I stopped, you would
worry."

"I would celebrate."

"You would worry. You would think: where is Unthil? Why is he not
leaping from shadows? Is he unwell? Has something happened to his
sense of timing, which is, we both agree, impeccable?" Unthil
bounced on his toes, a motion that was half nervous energy and half
the habitual readiness of a body trained to move from stillness to
lethal action without the intervening step of intention. "You would
miss me."

"I would endure."

"You see? Worry." Unthil leaned against the cabin wall with the
boneless ease of someone whose relationship with solid surfaces was
fundamentally optional. "So. Where are we going?"

"Zone Command confirmed the contact coordinates." Yzrat turned from
the mirror. His amber eyes, set deep beneath a brow that was heavy
without being brutish, assessed Unthil with the automatic evaluation
of a commander who trusted his subordinate's competence and
distrusted his subordinate's impulse control in equal measure. "The
tangent is uncharted. Designation pending. Preliminary scans indicate
a standard prime with intact civilisations, functioning entity
worship, and no awareness of interdimensional transit."

"Primitives."

"Perhaps. Perhaps not. The original contact was brief, accidental, and
originated from their side, which means someone in that reality
possesses either the technology or the talent to breach dimensional
barriers, however clumsily." Yzrat pulled on a pair of thin gloves,
dark leather, fitted, each finger articulated for casting precision.
"Our orders are reconnaissance. Observe. Assess. Do not engage. Do
not reveal. Report."

Unthil's grin did not diminish. "And if they see us?"

"They will not see us."

"But if they do."

Yzrat's smile returned. It was not Unthil's grin, bright and
infectious. It was slower, colder, the expression of a mind that
enjoyed solving problems and did not distinguish between a puzzle and
an enemy. "Then we deny them the means to see us again."

He reached for his hood, drew it up, and settled it over his head.
The goggles came down over his eyes. In the mirror, the figure that
looked back was no longer a vain officer admiring his appearance. It
was something else: streamlined, purposeful, reduced to function, a
weapon in the shape of a man.

"Your mount is ready?" he asked.

"Always."

They left the cabin together, Yzrat walking with the measured stride
of a man for whom haste was a concession to the weakness of others,
Unthil flickering at his side like a shadow that had not yet decided
whether to be cast or kept.

The command dirigible hung at twelve thousand feet above the Orceri
heartland, its massive envelope of treated silk blocking the stars
above and the city grids below, suspended in a darkness that was
absolute and cold and thin. The boarding platform extended from the
gondola's starboard side, a shelf of dark metal over nothing, open to
the wind that howled at this altitude with the constant, mindless fury
of air that resented being displaced.

The dragon waited on the platform.

It was blue, enormous, its scales the colour of deep ocean under
storm light, each one the size of a man's palm and hard as the
adamantine alloy the Orceri forged into their finest weapons. Its
wings were folded along its flanks, membranous expanses of muscle and
cartilage that could, when spread, blot out the sun across half an
acre. Its eyes were pale, almost white, the pupils contracted to
vertical slits against the mana lamps of the boarding platform, and
the intelligence in them was not the intelligence of an animal but
something older and more patient and less forgiving. Biomantic
modifications were visible along its spine and throat: ridges of dark
metal fused to scale, conduits of mana circuitry that glowed with
the same cold bluish light as the implants in Yzrat's flesh,
augmentations that enhanced the creature's natural capabilities and
bound its will, through pain and pleasure in equal measure, to the
rider it had been assigned.

The dragon turned its massive head toward Yzrat as he approached. A
sound came from its throat: not a growl, not a purr, but something
between, a vibration that the platform's metal transmitted through
Yzrat's boots and into his bones.

He placed one hand on the dragon's snout. The scales were warm beneath
his glove. The mana conduits pulsed.

"We fly," he said.

---

They crossed between realities the way a needle passes through cloth:
a brief resistance, a moment of displacement, and then the thread was
on the other side.

The dragon knew the way. The biomantic modifications included planar
navigation systems harvested from the psionic elf tangent, crystalline
structures implanted in the creature's brain that perceived
dimensional boundaries as physical surfaces and could locate the
thinning points where transit required the least expenditure of energy.
Yzrat felt the crossing as a pressure behind his eyes, a flicker of
darkness that lasted less than a heartbeat, and then the stars changed.

Two moons. Not three.

He noted this with the clinical detachment of a man cataloguing
environmental variables. Two moons: one large and silver-white,
full, dominant in the western sky; one smaller, pale gold, a waning
crescent at the eastern edge. The constellations were wrong, shifted
by degrees that indicated not a different hemisphere but a different
sky entirely, the same universe viewed from a reality whose
astronomical history had diverged from the Orceri's own at some point
in the distant past.

The air was different. Thinner. Cleaner. Lacking the particular
density that eight centuries of mana industrialisation had imprinted
on the atmosphere of Ta'Puh, the constant low hum of stripped entity
power that an Orceri citizen breathed from birth and never noticed
until it was absent. This air was quiet. Empty. The mana layer was
present but diffuse, spread thin across the planet's surface, unused,
untapped, a resource so vast and so neglected that Yzrat felt his
implants brighten in response, the harvested divinity within them
reaching toward the ambient mana the way a root reached toward
water.

Primitives. Wasteful primitives, sitting atop an ocean of power and
using it to light candles and heal scratches and throw fireballs at
each other like children throwing stones.

He would have felt contempt if contempt were not itself a waste of
energy.

The dragon climbed. The air thinned further, the temperature plunging,
the mithril boots earning their cost as the ambient cold bit at
everything the enchanted metal did not cover. At twenty thousand feet,
the world below became a map: dark land masses, the silver thread of
a river, the scattered amber points of a city's lights clustered
along a coastline, and to the east, beyond the cultivated lands and
the river valley, the pale expanse of a wasteland that the dragon's
navigational instincts had identified as the source of the original
dimensional contact.

Yzrat activated the invisibility. The working was not a spell in the
conventional sense but a function of the mana implants, a sustained
field that bent light and sound around him and his mount with an
efficiency that no native mage of this reality could detect, let
alone penetrate. He and the dragon ceased to exist for every sense
that the inhabitants of this world possessed.

They descended toward the wasteland.

Unthil had separated at the crossing point, slipping into the Shadow
Plane that bordered this reality as it bordered all realities, moving
through the darkness between dimensions with the ease of a creature
that had been engineered for exactly this purpose. He would approach
from ground level, gather intelligence on the terrain, and rendezvous
at the extraction point. The division of labour was standard. Yzrat
observed from above. Unthil observed from within. Between them,
nothing escaped notice.

The wasteland resolved as they descended. Broken stone. Exposed
bedrock. A desolation of hardpan and scrub that stretched for miles
in every direction, lifeless and featureless except for, at its
approximate centre, a structure.

A keep.

Crude. Small. Stone walls built on older, darker rubble. A watchtower
listing to the east. The kind of fortification that primitive races
threw together from whatever materials were available and defended
until something stronger came along and took it from them.

Yzrat circled at three thousand feet, invisible, patient, cataloguing.
The keep showed signs of recent habitation. Smoke stains above
doorways. Refuse heaps against the outer walls. Animal pens, empty
now. And activity: five figures, small at this altitude but distinct
through the enhanced optics of his goggles, moving around the keep's
entrance with the purposeful coordination of beings who knew the
structure and were returning to it for a specific reason.

He adjusted the goggles. The lenses cycled through enhancement modes:
thermal, mana-signature, structural. In the mana-signature mode, the
five figures blazed.

One burned with cool silver-white light, steady and disciplined, the
signature of divine channelling anchored to an entity whose resonance
Yzrat did not recognise. A priest of some kind. Competent. Nothing
that would trouble a trained Orceri operative.

One burned with warm gold, bright and sharp, the signature of formal
arcane training, a structured mage whose mana output was surprisingly
dense for the size of the figure, which was small, much smaller than
the others. A gnome, perhaps. Or a halfling. Capable enough, and
slightly above the expected baseline for this reality's apparent
development.

One flickered with a subtler resonance, warm and diffuse, woven
through the figure's physical presence rather than projecting from it:
bardic magic, the oldest and most primitive form of arcane expression,
channelled through voice and body rather than formula and gesture.
Unremarkable.

One was wrong.

Yzrat's goggles flickered. The enhancement mode destabilised for a
fraction of a second, the crystal lenses struggling to resolve a mana
signature that did not conform to any pattern in their calibration
database. The fourth figure burned, but the burning was not a colour
or a frequency. It was a distortion: a point in space where the
dimensional boundaries were thinner than they should be, where the
fabric of reality bent inward around the figure like cloth pressed
against a sharp object from the other side. The mana output was
enormous, vast, operating at frequencies that Yzrat's implants
registered as planar rather than material, the signature of a being
whose relationship with dimensional boundaries had been fundamentally
altered.

He had never seen anything like it. In eleven conquered tangents and
four centuries of interdimensional operations, he had never encountered
a mana signature that made his goggles malfunction.

The fifth figure was worse.

Not because its signature was larger or more powerful, but because it
was familiar. It was draconic. The deep, layered resonance of a dragon
in humanoid form, its true mana signature compressed but not concealed,
visible to anyone with the instruments to look. Young. Green. Still
growing, but carrying the particular density of a creature that would
become formidable given time and survival.

A dragon, walking among mortals in disguise. Primitive worlds
produced such oddities.

Yzrat filed the observations with the meticulous precision that was
both his nature and his training. Five figures. Three of standard
capability, unremarkable for an undeveloped tangent. One anomaly of
extraordinary planar resonance. One disguised dragon.

Strange indeed. So weak, well... most of them. But among them were
some with power. Anomalies that bore watching.

He continued to circle, invisible and patient, and watched the five
figures enter the keep.

---

The keep smelled the same.

Smiggles had hoped, irrationally, that the weeks of absence might have
improved the atmosphere. They had not. The orc smell had faded from
the surface level but remained embedded in the stone itself, a
permanent olfactory monument to the years of occupation: rancid fat,
sour musk, and the particular acrid note that orc skin left on any
surface it contacted repeatedly. He breathed through his mouth and
tried not to think about the fact that the particles responsible for
the smell were entering his lungs regardless of which orifice he used
to inhale them.

"Delightful as everr," Iskar said, pulling his collar over his nose.
"I had forgotten how much I missed this place. The ambiance. The
bouquet. The way the walls seem to be actively hostile to the concept
of cleanliness."

"Focus," Aizune said. The priest moved through the ground floor with
the measured efficiency of a man retracing steps he had already
memorised, his hand resting on the hilt of the spirit-touched blade,
his dark grey eyes scanning each room and corridor not for threats,
which had been cleared weeks ago, but for changes: signs that
something had visited in their absence, evidence that the keep's
vacancy had attracted new occupants.

Nothing. The rooms were as they had left them, stripped of anything
valuable, the orc dead long since carried off by scavengers and
weather. The excavation in the northwest corner gaped as before: five
feet across, jagged edges, the crude rope ladder still hanging into
the darkness below.

Aleron waited at the excavation's edge. He had spoken little during
the day's ride from Amara, his grey eyes drifting between the road
and the presences that accompanied him, his staff tapping the earth
with the steady rhythm that was his walking music, the metronome of a
man keeping time with realities that moved at different speeds. Now
he stood at the threshold of the lower level with his head tilted,
listening.

"The vibrations are... still here," he said. "Residual. The machine's
activation left... impressions in the dimensional fabric. Like
footprints in soft earth. They have not faded."

"Can the Orceri detect those impressions?" Smiggles asked.

"I do not... I am not certain. If they have instruments calibrated for
planar harmonics, possibly. The impressions are weak. But they exist."

"Then we do this quickly."

Lai remained on the surface. The lower level was too confined for
comfort, she said, and the mirrors made her uneasy. She did not
explain why mirrors would make a dragon uneasy, and no one asked,
because the list of things they did not ask Lai had grown long enough
to constitute its own document.

They descended.

The lower level received them with the silence of a place that had
been waiting for their return and was not entirely pleased to see
them. The amber glow of Zanthar's permanent flames still burned in
their alcoves, steady and patient, five centuries of continuous
illumination powered by a dead man's final enchantments. The air was
cool. The stone was faintly warm beneath their boots, the residual
mana humming in the walls with a frequency that Smiggles' trained
senses registered as almost musical, the harmonic signature of a
working space that had been saturated with magic for so long that the
magic had become part of the architecture.

They passed through the mirror chamber without stopping. The mirrors
flickered. Versions of Zanthar's face danced in dozens of frames,
living lives that their progenitor had never lived, and in the
peripheral darkness at the edges of the room something moved that was
not a reflection, something that watched their passage with the patient
malice of a thing that had been driven off but not destroyed and that
remembered what had driven it.

Smiggles felt the cold and walked faster.

The machine room opened before them. Forty paces across. Twice the
height of a man. The three great rings in their channels along the
walls, covered in Draconic runes and gemstone powders that caught the
amber light and threw it back in colours: ruby crimson, sapphire blue,
diamond white. The glass globe on its pedestal of fused quartz, the
crack along one meridian visible as a dark line in the amber light.
The two bodies were gone: Remular returned to Blowing Rock, Kuulatch's
corpse presumably dragged away by whatever scavenged in these depths
when the living were not present.

"One activation," Smiggles said. "Brief. We confirm the coordinates,
verify that the tuning fork's frequency matches what the machine shows
us, and we leave. Aizune, the globe responds to your mana. You
activate. Aleron, compare the fork's resonance against the machine's
output and confirm calibration. I monitor the rings for stability.
Iskar, watch the door."

"The blind man watching the rearr," Iskar said. "What a comforrting
arrangement." But he turned and took position at the chamber entrance,
his rapier drawn, his back to the machine and his eyes on the corridor
that led back through the mirrors and the darkness and the things
that lived in both.

Aizune approached the globe. He placed his hands on the cool glass
and felt the machine wake beneath his touch, the copper channels in
the floor flickering from dormancy to attention, the hum rising
through his boots and his bones and the place behind his sternum where
Minerva's gift resided. The innermost ring shuddered. The ruby dust
that coated its surface began to glow, crimson light bleeding through
the Draconic runes, each character illuminating in sequence as the
ring's ancient mechanisms engaged.

The second ring followed. Sapphire blue. The hum deepened, the
harmonic finding its register, the chamber's walls vibrating with
a frequency that was felt in the teeth and the base of the skull.

The third ring. Diamond white. The globe heated beneath his palms.
The crack along its meridian brightened, light leaking through the
flaw like blood through a wound, and the ceiling of the chamber
began to thin, the stone becoming translucent, becoming transparent,
becoming a window onto somewhere else.

Stars. Darkness. A landscape far below, seen as though from a great
height: dark land masses, the silver thread of a river, the scattered
cold blue-white points of a city's lights, mana lights, not fire, the
precise geometric grids of a civilisation that built with rulers and
right angles and the absolute confidence of a people who had conquered
everything worth conquering and organised what remained.

Three moons.

Silver. Gold. And between them, smaller, reddish, occupying the space
where Mazariim's sky was empty, the third moon that marked the world
where everything they loved had been unmade and rebuilt in the image
of the conqueror.

Aleron held the tuning fork aloft. The pale silver metal caught the
polychrome light of the spinning rings and threw it back as a single
clean note, a vibration that married the fork's calibrated frequency
to the machine's output and produced, in the space between them, a
resonance that was confirmation: the frequencies matched. The fork was
true. The key fit the lock.

"Confirmed," Aleron said. Both eyes were focused. Both were bright.
"The fork is... precise. The machine and the fork agree. The tangent
is the same. We can... we can cross."

Aizune released the globe. The rings slowed. The ceiling thickened.
The three moons faded, the reddish light of the smallest lingering
for a moment longer than the others, as though reluctant to release
its grip on their attention, before the stone reasserted itself and
the chamber was once more a room beneath the earth, lit by amber
flames, surrounded by the ghosts of a dead wizard's obsession.

"We have what we came for," Aizune said. "Let us go."

They climbed the rope ladder. They crossed the ground floor. They
emerged into the afternoon light of the Steppes, where the wind blew
across the broken stone with the constant, mindless patience of air
that had nothing to stop it and nowhere to be.

Lai was sitting on a rock outside the keep, eating an apple. She
looked up as they emerged. "Well?"

"Confirmed," Smiggles said. "The fork calibrates. When we cross, we
cross to the right place."

"Good." She bit the apple. The crunch was audible across the
courtyard. "Then let us go home and prepare to become orcs."

They mounted and rode west. The keep diminished behind them, crude
stone on ancient rubble, the watchtower listing against the sky. Five
riders becoming small on the vast pale surface of the Steppes,
trailing dust, carrying knowledge and purpose and the particular
weight of people who have seen what waits for them and have chosen to
walk toward it anyway.

They did not look back.

---

Three thousand feet above, invisible, Yzrat watched them go.

He had observed the entire sequence from altitude: the arrival, the
descent into the keep's lower level, the mana surge that accompanied
the machine's activation, a surge his goggles registered as a bloom
of polychrome light in the sub-visual spectrum, brief and intense,
the unmistakable signature of interdimensional observation equipment
in operation. They had a device. These primitives had a device capable
of viewing across tangent boundaries, crude and unstable and powered
by ambient mana rather than harvested divinity, but functional.

He had also observed the anomaly. The fourth figure, the one whose
mana signature bent dimensional boundaries, had produced a secondary
instrument during the activation sequence: a small object, metallic,
whose vibrational output his goggles recorded as precisely calibrated
to the harmonic frequency of the Orceri home tangent.

A tuning fork. Attuned to Ta'Puh.

The implications arranged themselves in his mind with the clean
efficiency of a filing system. These beings had a viewing device.
They had a tuning fork calibrated to the Orceri reality. They had a
dimensional anomaly capable of planar transit. They were preparing to
cross.

Primitives who were preparing to enter the Orceri home tangent. The
audacity was almost admirable. Almost. In the way that watching a
mouse approach a cat was almost admirable: the courage was real, but
it did not change the outcome.

He could kill them now. Five figures on an open plain, no cover, no
fortification, no awareness that death circled above them on wings
wider than their entire formation. The dragon could descend in seconds.
His own spellwork could end two of them before they understood what
was happening. Unthil, waiting in the shadows of the keep below, could
take a third before the survivors organised a defence.

He considered it. The consideration lasted three seconds.

No.

Killing them was tactically satisfying and strategically wasteful.
Dead enemies provided no intelligence. These beings had already
demonstrated capability beyond the expected baseline of this tangent.
They had activated interdimensional viewing equipment. They had
acquired planar calibration technology. They had allies with the
analytical resources to produce a precisely tuned fork, which meant
institutional support from a centre of arcane learning. Killing them
eliminated five individuals. Observing them might reveal networks,
resources, and the broader defensive capabilities of the tangent
itself.

The Orceri did not conquer eleven realities by killing scouts. They
conquered them by watching the scouts, learning what the scouts knew,
mapping the scouts' supply lines and command structures and
vulnerabilities, and then destroying everything at once, completely,
so thoroughly that the rubble served as the foundation for the next
Orceri city.

But the viewing device was a problem.

A device that could observe across tangent boundaries was, in the
hands of a primitive civilisation, a window. Windows worked in both
directions. Every time they activated it, there was a chance, small
but non-zero, that the observation would be detected by Orceri
monitoring systems. Another unscheduled contact, another alert,
another piece of the puzzle that Command would eventually assemble
into a strategic picture that Yzrat's current orders required remain
incomplete until the reconnaissance was finished.

The device needed to not exist.

He reached into the leather satchel secured to the dragon's saddle
harness and withdrew a small glass vial. Inside the vial, suspended
in a viscous amber fluid that kept it dormant, was a creature no
longer than his smallest finger: a soft, pale, segmented thing that
pulsed with a faint organic rhythm, its skin translucent enough to
show the mana pathways beneath, engineered conduits of biomantic
origin that threaded through its body like veins through a leaf. It
was not natural. It had never been natural. It was a product of the
biomantic arts that the Orceri had refined over four centuries of
cross-tangent conquest, a seed designed for a single purpose.

He unstopped the vial. The creature stirred in the amber fluid,
responding to the change in pressure and the sudden exposure to
ambient mana, which it absorbed through its skin with the desperate
hunger of something that had been starved and was now offered a feast.
It swelled. Its segments thickened. The mana pathways beneath its
skin brightened from dim to vivid, the bluish glow of harvested
divinity coursing through channels that were growing even as he
watched, the organism feeding and building and preparing for the
transformation that was its entire reason for existing.

Yzrat tilted the vial. The creature fell.

It dropped from three thousand feet, a pale speck against the grey
sky, tumbling through the thin air with the helpless trajectory of
something that could not fly and did not need to, because it was not
meant to survive the fall. It was meant to land.

Twelve hours. That was the maturation period. Twelve hours from the
moment the organism touched soil and began to feed on the ambient
mana of the earth itself, drawing power upward through the ground the
way a tree drew water through its roots, swelling, hardening, its
soft segments fusing into armoured plates, its mouth widening into a
bore capable of displacing stone and earth and metal and everything
else that stood between it and the mana-rich deposits that its
biomantic programming drove it to consume.

By tomorrow, the pale worm no longer than a finger would be a
burrowing engine of destruction two hundred feet long and forty feet
in diameter, blindly seeking the densest concentration of mana in its
environment, which would be the machine in the chamber beneath the
keep, the machine whose centuries of operation had saturated the
surrounding stone with a mana signature that would draw the creature
downward through the bedrock like a moth toward a flame.

The keep would be gone. The machine would be gone. The evidence would
be buried beneath a thousand tons of displaced earth and stone, and
when the creature, having consumed the mana source, exhausted its
biomantic programming and died, the resulting collapse would look, to
anyone who came to investigate, like a natural geological event.

Deny the enemy their resources. Standard Orceri doctrine. Rangithyr
himself would have approved.

Yzrat watched the riders diminish into the distance, five small
figures on the vast pale surface of a world that did not know it had
been found. He stroked the dragon's neck. The scales were warm
beneath his glove. The mana conduits pulsed.

"Interesting," he said, to the wind and the empty sky and the two
moons that hung above a world that had two fewer moons than the one
he called home. "Very interesting."

The dragon turned. The wind shifted. At an altitude where the air
was too thin for any native creature to fly and too cold for any
native mage to survive, the invisible rider and his invisible mount
banked toward the dimensional boundary and slipped through it, the
crossing as brief and as silent as a needle passing through cloth.

The sky above the Steppes of Broken Stone was empty.

---

They rode west in the late afternoon light, five travellers with the
weight of confirmation in their saddlebags and the shape of what came
next forming in their minds. The Steppes gave way to cultivated land.
The cultivated land gave way to the outskirts of Amara, where the
road widened and the air thickened with the smell of salt and commerce
and the distant sound of the harbour bell marking the turn of the
watch.

Smiggles rode in silence, which was unusual enough that Iskar noticed
and unusual enough that Iskar chose not to remark on it, which was
itself unusual, and the compound unusualness created a quiet that the
group inhabited without discussion, each of them occupied with the
particular geometry of their own thoughts.

Aizune was thinking about Minerva. About the space where she would
not be when they crossed. About the silence that would fill the place
where her voice lived in him, and whether faith that persisted in
the absence of its object was stronger or weaker than faith that had
never been tested.

Smiggles was thinking about mana saturation as camouflage. About the
specific properties of Orceri atmospheric industrialisation and the
density of ambient interference required to mask individual signatures
from psionic surveillance networks. About whether the theory would
hold in practice and whether practice would give him the chance to
test the theory twice.

Iskar was thinking about Darielo. About a face he had not seen in
seven years. About whether the face had changed. About whether the
man behind the face had changed. About whether rescue was still rescue
if the person you were rescuing had become someone you no longer
recognised, and whether the answer to that question mattered, and
whether the asking of it made him a worse friend or a more honest one.

Aleron was thinking about the mural in the pyramid. About the elf
with dark hair and large grey streaks, energy pouring from his hands
and face. About the destroyed section that Sugnim had chipped away
with his own hands so that Aleron would walk the path without knowing
its end. About ends in general, and whether the people riding beside
him, who had chosen to walk toward a threat that could consume
everything they were, deserved to know the shape of his.

And Lai was thinking about a tower in Instar, and a man with Varkonan
features and deep crimson robes, and the sound of his voice reading
to her from texts she had been too young to understand, and whether
the distance she had placed between herself and that voice was
measured in miles or in something less tangible and more permanent.

They entered Amara as the sun touched the horizon. The harbour turned
to gold. The Market District filled with the sounds of closing
commerce and opening taverns. Somewhere in the Dock District, a
concertina was playing a Sabonaar reel, the melody carrying over the
rooftops in the warm evening air, and Iskar hummed along without
knowing he was doing it, the music living in his voice the way the
sea lived in his walk, inseparable from the man who carried it.

Tomorrow they would become orcs. Tomorrow they would step through the
boundary between their world and the one that wanted to devour it.
Tomorrow the war that had been building since a dead wizard's machine
sent a vibration across the dimensional fabric would move from
observation to confrontation, from theory to ground truth, from the
safety of knowledge to the danger of action.

But that was tomorrow. Tonight they had Amara. They had the harbour
and the lamps and the smell of Yella's cooking drifting through the
Market District. They had each other, which was a thing none of them
would have said aloud and all of them understood.

Smiggles found a brandy that met his standards. Iskar found a song
that needed singing. Aizune found a quiet corner and opened his prayer
journal and wrote, in the precise hand of a priest who believed that
the recording of truth was itself an act of faith, everything they
had learned and everything they planned and the single sentence that
captured what he felt but could not prove: *He knows more than he is
telling us, and I think he is right to keep it.*

Behind them, far to the east, in the darkness of the Steppes where no
one watched and no one would know until it was too late to matter,
something was growing.

The pale worm fed. The mana-rich earth yielded to it. The segments
thickened. The armoured plates hardened. The bore widened. The
biomantic programming drove it downward, toward the densest
concentration of energy in its radius, toward the machine that had
started everything, toward the glass globe and the spinning rings
and the mirrors where a dead wizard's face still danced in a hundred
different lives.

By morning, there would be nothing left.

The two moons rose over Amara. Silver Io'el, full and bright. Pale
gold Randir'el, waning at the edge of its cycle. Between them, the
sky was as it had always been, as it should always be, empty and vast
and familiar.

But the heroes had seen what filled that emptiness in another world.
And the emptiness, they now understood, was not absence.

It was a door. And something was coming through.
