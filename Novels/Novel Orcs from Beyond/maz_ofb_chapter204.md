# Chapter Four
## The World the Orcs Made

The descent took six hours.

They came down from the mountains in silence, four figures
moving through a landscape that did not want them, following a
path that was not a path but the accumulated evidence of water's
long argument with stone: a dry streambed, narrow and loose with
scree, winding between grey shoulders of rock that rose on either
side like the walls of a corridor designed by something that did
not care about the comfort of its occupants. The air thinned as
they climbed down rather than up, which was a confusion of the
body's expectations that Smiggles found personally offensive, and
the wind that had been constant at the cave mouth diminished by
degrees until it was merely cold rather than punishing, and the
sparse conifers that had clung to the upper slopes with the grim
determination of things that had chosen a difficult place to live
and refused to reconsider thickened into actual forest, dark and
dense and smelling of resin and the particular mineral dampness
of soil that had not seen direct sunlight in a very long time.

Aleron walked among them.

He had risen from his reverie three hours after the others had
returned from Giganzalt's chamber, pulling himself upright with
the quarterstaff in a motion that was less standing than
reassembling, the body finding its vertical alignment one joint
at a time, the way a building found its vertical alignment after
an earthquake: still upright, still functional, but with a new
awareness of the forces that could bring it down. His skin had
lost the translucence that had followed the transit, the veins at
his temples no longer visible through the flesh, but the colour
that had replaced it was not health but a lesser pallor, the grey
of a man who had spent something significant and was operating on
what remained. The amulet pulsed green at his throat, steady,
slower than before, the rhythm of a heartbeat that had been asked
to do more than a heartbeat should and was managing, but managing
was not the same as thriving.

He walked with the staff bearing more of his weight than his legs.
He did not complain. He did not speak, except once, early in the
descent, when his head turned toward something the others could
not see and he murmured three words in Tarquesta that might have
been an answer to a question or a refusal of an invitation or
simply the habitual response of a man whose perception included
presences that the world around him did not, and Smiggles, who
walked ahead, did not turn, and Iskar, who walked behind, did not
comment, and Lai, who walked twenty paces to the rear as she
always did when Aleron was present, said nothing, and the three
words dissolved into the mountain air and the descent continued.

The forest ended.

---

The transition was not gradual. They came around a bend in the
streambed where the rocks gave way to earth and the earth gave
way to grass and the grass gave way to something that was not
wilderness and was not civilisation but the border between them,
the place where one became the other, and the becoming was marked
by the first object they encountered that had been made by hands
rather than grown by nature.

A road.

Paved in fitted dark stone, each block cut to precise dimensions
and laid without visible mortar, the surface smooth and even and
extending in both directions with the particular confidence of
infrastructure that had been built by a civilisation that expected
its roads to last and had not been disappointed. The road was
fifteen feet wide. It ran north to south along the base of the
mountain's final slope, following the contour of the terrain with
an efficiency that spoke of surveying instruments and engineering
principles and the systematic application of resources on a scale
that Smiggles, standing at the tree line with his enormous Orceri
hands hanging at his sides, found simultaneously impressive and
appalling.

No one was on the road. But someone had been. The surface was
clean, maintained, the fitted stones free of the weeds and moss
that would have colonised any road in the Merchant Nations within
a season of neglect. A drainage channel ran along each side,
shallow and precise, carrying runoff toward a culvert that
disappeared beneath the road's southern edge. The engineering was
not primitive. It was not approximate. It was the work of a
civilisation that had been building roads for eight hundred years
and had refined the process to the point where refinement was no
longer the correct word and the correct word was something closer
to instinct.

The first mana streetlamp appeared at a crossroads two hundred
yards south.

It stood on a pole of dark metal, ten feet tall, topped with a
glass globe that emitted a steady cold bluish-white light, the
same light that Smiggles had seen in the machine's vision, the
same light that pulsed in Yzrat's mana implants, the same light
that lived in Rangithyr's scimitar: the cold blue radiance of
mana stripped from entities whose names the Orceri no longer
remembered and whose power they had repurposed into the most
mundane applications imaginable. Streetlights. The divine energy
of beings that had shaped planes of existence and commanded the
worship of millions, reduced to a lamp on a pole at a crossroads
in the mountains where two roads met and no one was present to
need the illumination it provided.

The lamp burned in daylight. It would burn at night. It would
burn forever, or until the mana within it was depleted, which
amounted to the same thing on any timescale that mattered to the
beings who walked beneath it.

Smiggles stared at it.

"That's real," he said. His voice was quiet. The Orceri vocal
cords produced the words at a register deeper than his own, the
observation emerging from a chest cavity that did not belong to
him in a body that did not belong to him on a world that did not
belong to anyone he cared about. "That is actually real. I
reasoned it from evidence and believed it from logic and I am
looking at it and it is still not something my brain will
accept."

"Your brain will have to manage," Iskar said. He stood beside
Smiggles at the tree line, and his eyes were not on the lamp.
His eyes were on the road, and the road led south, and south
was where the populated lowlands lay, and the populated
lowlands were where the humans were.

They stepped onto the road and walked south.

---

The first dirigible passed overhead an hour later.

It was distant, a dark shape against the deeper blue of Ta'Puh's
sky, moving from east to west at an altitude that reduced it to
a silhouette: an elongated hull, the gondola beneath it catching
a glint of sunlight, the propulsion system invisible at this range
but the trajectory smooth and constant, the motion of something
that did not depend on wind or current but on the steady
application of forces that wind and current could not match. It
made no sound that reached the road. It simply moved across the
sky with the indifference of a thing that had somewhere to be and
the means to get there and no interest in the four figures walking
a mountain road far below.

Iskar watched it cross the sky and said nothing.

The third moon was visible.

It hung between Io'el and Randir'el, small and reddish, occupying
the space where Mazariim's sky was empty, where Iskar had spent
twenty-nine years looking up and seeing nothing and taking the
nothing for granted the way all beings took the shape of their
sky for granted until someone showed them a sky with a different
shape. The third moon was not large. It was not dramatic. It was
simply there, a fact of this reality that was not a fact of his,
and its presence above the road was the kind of confirmation that
bypassed the intellect and went directly to the part of the brain
where certainty lived, the part that knew things not because it
had been persuaded but because it had been shown.

Three moons. Three moons above a road paved by a civilisation
that had conquered eleven worlds. Iskar looked at the third moon
and looked at the dirigible and looked at the road beneath his
borrowed feet and thought about Darielo Voss, who was imprisoned
in a city called Moamon in a world that had two moons and free
humans and a sky that did not contain the evidence of universal
conquest, and the thought of Darielo was the anchor that kept the
rest of it from overwhelming him, because Darielo was specific
and personal and real in a way that the enormity of three moons
and an empire that spanned realities was not.

The road joined a larger road. The larger road descended into a
valley. The valley was cultivated.

Fields. Ordered, geometric, extending in rows that followed the
contour of the valley floor with the precision of a planting
system that had been optimised over centuries. The crops were
unfamiliar: broad-leafed plants in dark green rows, a grain of
some kind that stood waist-high and golden, root vegetables whose
tops formed a purple carpet across an entire hillside. No weeds
grew between the rows. No birds flew above the fields. No insects
hummed among the grain. The cultivation was total, the biological
messiness of agriculture removed, replaced by something that was
less farming than manufacturing, the systematic production of
food by methods that had no interest in the ecosystem that
agriculture normally sustained.

Figures moved among the rows.

They were distant, but Iskar could see them. Broad-shouldered.
Stooped. Moving with the slow, repetitive rhythm of bodies
performing tasks they had performed a thousand times and would
perform a thousand more, the rhythm of labour that was not chosen
but assigned, not varied but identical, not questioned but
accepted with the dull compliance of things that had been shaped
for acceptance the way the fields had been shaped for yield.

Humans.

Grey tunics. Bare feet. Dull eyes that did not lift from the
soil. Working in the fields of an empire that had bred them for
this, generation after generation, thirty generations of selective
pressure that had produced bodies strong enough to pull and lift
and carry and minds dim enough to never ask why they were pulling
and lifting and carrying or whether there was an alternative or
whether the alternative was worth the risk of imagining it.

Iskar's hands clenched inside his Orceri disguise. The movement
was invisible, the enormous fists tightening within the gloves
that Smiggles had included in the polymorph, but the effort
behind the movement was not invisible to the man who made it. He
remembered Smiggles' words in a common room in Amara, a world
away and a lifetime ago: "You can. Because you're asking the
question." He remembered the question he had asked: whether he
could walk through a world of enslaved humans and maintain his
composure and come back and still be the person who was going to
save Darielo.

He held. It cost him something that he filed away for later
accounting, a debt that the muscles in his jaw and the tendons
in his wrists recorded and that his heart would pay when the
disguise came off and the tusks dissolved and his own face
returned and the performance that had protected him from the
reality of what he was seeing was no longer necessary.

They walked past the fields and into the town.

---

Smiggles had built the Orceri civilisation in his mind before he
saw it.

He had constructed it from evidence: the machine's vision, the
spy's gear, the tuning fork's calibration data, the fragments
that Giganzalt's existence in a cave confirmed, and the logical
architecture that connected these fragments into a coherent
picture of what a civilisation looked like when it had won
everything and had eight centuries to build on the wreckage.
The picture was accurate. The picture was comprehensive. The
picture was, he discovered as the town assembled itself around
him in the late afternoon light of a world with three moons,
entirely insufficient.

The central plaza was the first thing that broke the picture's
sufficiency.

It was clean. Not maintained-clean, not swept-and-tidied-clean,
but clean in a way that went beyond maintenance and entered the
territory of principle, as though cleanliness were not a practice
but a natural law, something the stones and the buildings and the
air itself obeyed without needing to be told. The fitted dark
stone of the paving was spotless. The buildings that ringed the
plaza, two and three stories of the same dark stone and timber
with flat roofs and wide doorways, were uniform, precise, their
surfaces unmarked by the graffiti and weather-staining and
cheerful architectural improvisation that characterised every
city Smiggles had lived in. The glass in the windows was thick,
clear, free of the bubbles and distortions that gnomish glass
still struggled to eliminate. The signage was painted in Orcish
script, precise characters in colours that had not faded, the
paint itself probably mana-stabilised, because of course it was,
because when your civilisation ran on the harvested power of dead
gods you could afford to stabilise your paint.

The statue of Zuel dominated the plaza's centre.

It was not the Zuel that Mazariim's histories recorded: the
savage entity whose army had poured from the mountains and
consumed the world in four years of apocalyptic war. This Zuel
was a dignified figure rendered in dark metal, standing perhaps
fifteen feet tall on a basalt plinth, one hand raised in a
gesture that might have been benediction and might have been
command and was, in the sculptor's ambiguous intent, both. The
other hand rested on an axe, the weapon at ease, the posture of
a warrior whose wars were won. The face was strong, composed,
carrying none of the bestial fury that the temples of Mazariim
associated with the orcish god. This was Zuel as a father of
civilisation. Zuel as a builder. Zuel as the entity whose vision
had lifted his people from barbarism to an empire that spanned
realities.

Orceri passed the statue without looking up. It was background.
Wallpaper. Unremarkable.

Smiggles looked up. He looked at the face of the entity whose
avatar had been shattered at Fort Hope in his reality, whose army
had been broken, whose surviving worshippers had been driven into
the mountains and the wastes. He looked at the dignified metal
features and thought about the version of that entity that existed
in this world, where the avatar had not been shattered, where the
army had not been broken, where the worshippers had not been
driven anywhere but had instead driven everyone else into camps
and chains and extinction.

He looked at the statue and said nothing, because there was
nothing to say that the statue had not already said, and what the
statue said was: we won. We are still winning. We will always
win. And the silence of the plaza around the statue, the clean
stones and the steady lights and the Orceri going about their
lives beneath the gaze of their god, confirmed it.

The commercial ring surrounded the plaza. Wide streets paved in
fitted stone. Shops with wide doorways sized for seven-foot
frames. Mana streetlamps on every corner, their cold blue-white
globes burning with the same eternal steadiness as the lamp at
the mountain crossroads, the same cold divine light, the same
repurposed holiness illuminating the mundane: a clothing shop, a
food vendor, a tool merchant, a purveyor of household goods
whose wares Smiggles could not identify by function but whose
presentation was orderly and whose customers were orderly and
whose transactions were conducted with the quiet efficiency of
people who had never experienced a shortage of anything and could
not imagine the possibility.

Floating carriages. Small platforms of dark metal suspended three
feet above the street by mana repulsion, gliding past in silence,
carrying goods or passengers or both, the propulsion invisible,
the motion smooth, the sound a low hum that was felt in the soles
of the feet rather than heard in the ears. Smiggles watched one
pass and his ham-sized hands did not fidget because he was
gripping them together behind his back with a force that would
have bruised his gnomish fingers and merely strained his Orceri
ones, because the engineer in him wanted to stop the carriage
and dismantle it and understand the mana-repulsion mechanics and
the stabilisation matrix and the power source and the control
interface, and the part of him that was not an engineer wanted to
set fire to every floating carriage in the city and watch them
burn, because they floated on the stolen essence of beings whose
names the Orceri had erased along with their existences.

Above the rooftops, something drifted.

It moved with a slow, purposeful sweep that was neither the path
of a bird nor the trajectory of a dirigible but something between,
a deliberate patrol, the motion of a thing that was looking for
something and intended to look everywhere before it stopped. It
was roughly spherical, the size of a large cart wheel, its surface
a mottled grey-brown that caught the afternoon light and returned
it with the wet gleam of organic tissue. Stalks protruded from
the sphere's upper hemisphere, a dozen or more, severed at varying
lengths, the stumps sealed with metallic caps that pulsed faintly
with the cold blue light of mana conduits. Membranous wings, thin
and dark, stretched from the sphere's equator, beating in slow,
regular strokes that produced no sound audible from the street
below. A single enormous eye dominated the sphere's forward face,
lidless, the iris a deep amber, scanning the street in a sweep
that took in every building, every doorway, every figure on the
pavement with the systematic attention of a device designed to
see and record and report.

A surveillance construct. Beholder-derived, biomantically modified,
the original creature's body repurposed into a platform for the
metallic sensory apparatus that had replaced its natural stalks.
The severed stumps were not wounds but interfaces, connection
points for the mana circuitry that linked the construct to whatever
command network monitored its input. It was alive in the way that
a sensorium was alive: biologically functional, neurologically
directed, and utterly devoid of the independent will that the
original organism had possessed.

A second construct drifted into view from behind a residential
block, following a parallel path at a slightly higher altitude.
The two swept the street in overlapping arcs, their enormous eyes
tracking in synchronised patterns that ensured no section of the
road below went unobserved for more than a few seconds.

The Orceri civilians on the street did not look up. They did not
step aside. They did not acknowledge the constructs' presence any
more than they acknowledged the mana streetlamps or the fitted
stone beneath their feet. The constructs were infrastructure.
Background. Part of the architecture of a city that watched its
inhabitants with the same steady, impersonal attention that its
streetlamps used to illuminate them.

Smiggles watched the constructs pass overhead and filed the
observation in the compartment of his mind that was still
functioning as an analyst rather than as a man who wanted to
scream. Biomantic surveillance. Airborne. Overlapping coverage
patterns. Organic platforms with mana-circuit interfaces. Another
capability absorbed from another conquered world, another tool
in the arsenal of a civilisation that viewed everything, living
or otherwise, as material to be shaped toward function.

"Focus," he told himself. The word was inaudible. The Orceri jaw
produced it as a subvocalisation, the gnomish habit of talking
through problems reduced to a vibration in the throat that only
he could hear. "You are here to observe. Observe."

He observed.

---

The enslaved were everywhere.

They served in the shops. A human woman, broad-shouldered, grey-
tunicked, stood behind a counter in a food vendor's stall, her
hands moving with mechanical precision: measuring grain into bags,
weighing meat on a scale, handing the prepared goods to Orceri
customers who took them without acknowledgement and without eye
contact and without any indication that the hands that had
prepared the goods belonged to a being that was capable of
acknowledgement or eye contact or any form of recognition that
might complicate the transaction.

They carried packages on the streets. A human man, taller than
the woman, heavier, the selective breeding evident in the breadth
of his shoulders and the thickness of his arms and the blankness
of his expression, walked three paces behind an Orceri civilian,
carrying a stack of wrapped parcels that would have strained a
normal human's arms. He did not strain. He carried them the way
a cart carried cargo: without effort, without complaint, without
the possibility of complaint, because the capacity for complaint
had been removed from the lineage that produced him thirty
generations ago and had not returned.

They swept the floors. They cleaned the windows. They stood at
intersections holding parasols over Orceri who did not wish to
walk in direct sunlight. They existed in every space and occupied
no space, furniture that breathed and moved and performed tasks
and registered on the attention of the Orceri who used them no
more than a chair registered on the attention of the person who
sat in it.

Iskar walked through the commercial ring and looked at the
humans and did not speak.

The performance was gone. The warmth, the rolling gait, the easy
looseness that was his resting state in any body and any situation,
the armour of charm and theatre that he wore as naturally as his
coat and his hat: gone. What moved through the streets of the
Orceri town was the body Smiggles had shaped for him, seven feet
of false muscle and false skin and false tusks, and inside it was
a man from Amara whose mother ran a chandlery on the docks and
whose father sailed the trade routes and whose closest friend had
been taken by slavers seven years ago, and the man inside the
body was looking at slavery perfected, slavery completed, slavery
refined across thirty generations until it was not slavery
anymore because slavery implied a condition that could be escaped
or resisted or at least resented, and what he was looking at was
something beyond slavery, something for which the language of
free peoples did not have a word because free peoples had never
imagined it and enslaved peoples had never survived long enough
to name it.

He looked at a human child. The child was perhaps eight years
old, grey-tunicked, carrying a basket of bread that was too
heavy for a child but not too heavy for a child that had been
bred for carrying, and the child's face was the face of every
eight-year-old he had ever seen on the docks of Amara, round and
unformed and waiting to become whatever the world decided it
should become, and in Amara the world offered options, offered
chaos, offered the possibility of becoming a sailor or a thief
or a priest or a bard, and in this world the world offered a
grey tunic and a basket of bread and the certainty that the
child's children would carry the same basket in the same tunic
on the same street until the Orceri decided they no longer needed
bread carried.

His jaw tightened behind his false tusks. The tendons in his
wrists recorded the debt. He walked.

---

Smiggles found the shop.

It was in the commercial ring's eastern quadrant, between a tool
merchant and a vendor of mana-powered cooling devices: a
storefront with a wide glass window displaying an array of mana
engines in graduated sizes, from devices small enough to fit in
a belt pouch to units the size of a barrel that hummed with
visible resonance, their housings of dark metal and crystal
pulsing with the cold blue light that was this civilisation's
defining aesthetic, the colour of stolen divinity.

He entered. The shopkeeper was an Orceri of middle years and
middle build, clean-shaven in the Orceri fashion, wearing a
leather apron over practical clothing, the apron stained with
the particular marks of someone who worked with mana-conductive
materials on a daily basis: faint bluish discolouration at the
fingertips, a shimmer along the forearm where ambient mana had
saturated the skin through repeated contact. A human slave stood
in the corner, motionless, holding a broom, waiting.

"Good afternoon," Smiggles said. The Orcish words came from his
throat with an accent he had practised for three days in Amara,
mimicking the cadence he had heard in the machine's vision and
the phonetic patterns he had extrapolated from Kuulatch's
documents and the educated guesswork of a linguist who was
working from fragments and hoping the fragments were
representative. "I am looking for a portable mana engine.
Civilian grade. Something compact."

The shopkeeper looked at him with the mild professional interest
of a man assessing a customer. "What application?"

"Environmental management. I am posted to a survey team in the
northern reaches." The lie had been prepared. The details were
sparse, because sparse details invited fewer questions than rich
ones, and Smiggles had learned in the Merchant Nations that the
most convincing lies were the ones that sounded too boring to
investigate. "The ambient temperature fluctuations are beyond
comfortable parameters. I need something that can maintain a
thermal envelope in an enclosed space. Compact enough for a
field pack."

The shopkeeper nodded. "Third shelf. The Kren-7 series. Thermal
regulation, ambient noise generation, standard civilian output."
He moved to the shelf and produced a device approximately the
size of a lantern: a cylinder of dark metal with crystal inlays,
its surface warm to the touch, the faint hum of contained mana
vibrating through the housing with a frequency that Smiggles
felt in his teeth.

Smiggles took it. He turned it in his ham-sized hands. The
housing was well-made, the joins precise, the crystal inlays
seated with a craftsmanship that the gnomish precision-
instrument makers of Zhonhylite would have respected and the
Guilded Cog would have envied. The mana output was steady,
consistent, producing a field of ambient energy that extended
approximately ten feet from the device in every direction: a
sphere of mana noise, the thermal regulation its advertised
purpose and the noise its unintended byproduct.

But it was the noise that Smiggles wanted.

The theory had lived in his mind since the rooftop in Amara: if
sensoriums detected individual mana signatures, then a device
that produced ambient mana noise would mask those signatures the
way the roar of a market masked a whispered conversation.
Background radiation. The signal lost in the noise. The
individual frequencies of four non-Orceri mana signatures
dissolved in the general hum of a civilisation that ran on mana
the way Amara ran on gold.

The theory was untested. It was about to be tested.

"How much?" Smiggles asked.

The shopkeeper named a price in currency Smiggles did not have.
The coins in his pouch were Mazariim issue, wrong metal, wrong
weight, wrong everything. He had anticipated this.

"I can trade. Refined crystals. Northern extraction." He produced
three small crystals from his belt pouch, gems he had acquired
in Amara's Market District and selected for this purpose:
unworked mana-conductive stones of a quality that any mana
engineer would recognise as valuable, their internal lattices
clear and well-formed, the kind of raw material that a civilian
mana-device shop would use as components. Universal currency in
a mana-driven economy.

The shopkeeper's eyes sharpened. He took one crystal, held it to
the light, turned it. His professional assessment took four
seconds. "Two of these," he said.

"One," Smiggles said. "And the information that a survey team in
the northern reaches will need supplies again in a month."

The shopkeeper considered. The calculation behind his eyes was
pure commerce, the universal arithmetic of a merchant weighing
immediate gain against potential future business, and it was the
same calculation in every reality, in every civilisation, in
every species that had developed the concept of trade: the greed
of the moment versus the greed of the future.

"Done," the shopkeeper said.

Smiggles took the mana engine. Outside the shop, he crossed the
street to where Aleron leaned against a wall in his hooded
cloak, the quarterstaff propped against the stone beside him,
his gaunt frame blending into the shadow of a doorway with the
practised invisibility of a man who had spent decades making
himself unnoticed. Smiggles opened Aleron's pack and placed the
engine inside, settling it against the elf's spine, the housing's
warmth pressing through the threadbare fabric of the robes.

"You," Smiggles said quietly, "are the biggest anomaly any
sensorium will detect. If the engine masks anything, it needs to
mask you first. The rest of us are noise. You are the signal they
are looking for."

Aleron looked at the device in his pack. One grey eye was focused
on the engine. The other tracked something that was not there.
"Clever," he said. The word was almost steady.

"That is the idea."

The engine hummed against Aleron's back. The noise spread outward,
ten feet, fifteen, a sphere of ambient mana that washed over the
four of them and surrounded their individual signatures with a
wall of sound that was not sound, and Aleron was at its centre,
the planar anomaly whose dimensional signature had made Yzrat's
goggles malfunction wrapped in the background radiation of a
device built to keep a survey tent warm.

The question was whether the wall would hold.

---

Sensoriums.

They appeared at the far end of the commercial street as the
group was moving away from the engine shop, two figures moving
in perfect synchronisation, their strides identical, their pace
identical, their heads turning at the same angle and the same
speed as they scanned the street with eyes that were not eyes in
any sense that mattered.

Smiggles saw them and his body went cold.

They had been elves. The bone structure was still there, beneath
the emaciation and the metal and the transformation that had
taken the most graceful of the mortal races and turned them into
instruments. The height was elven. The proportions were elven.
The ears, rising from beneath the metallic bands that encircled
their skulls, were elven, though they did not move the way elven
ears moved, with the subtle independent articulation that
communicated emotion and attention, because these ears did not
communicate anything to anyone, they received, scanning for mana
signatures the way a compass needle scanned for north, and the
beings attached to them were no more capable of communication
than the needle was capable of conversation.

They were starved. Not merely thin, not merely reduced, but
starved in the systematic, maintained way that spoke of a body
kept at the precise minimum of function that the work required:
enough mass to walk, to scan, to transmit, and nothing more,
because anything more was waste, and the Orceri did not waste.
Metallic chains connected wrist to wrist and ankle to ankle and
wrist to the collar at the throat and the collar to the bands
at the skull, and the chains were not restraints but circuitry,
mana conduits that linked the two figures to each other and to
the network that extended beyond them, the hivemind that
contained however many hundreds or thousands of these beings
existed across the continent, linked, merged, their individual
consciousnesses dissolved into a single distributed awareness
that saw everything and understood nothing and served the empire
that had created it with the perfect, mindless efficiency of a
tool that had been designed for one purpose and had no capacity
for any other.

Their armour was form-fitted, metallic, covering the minimum of
the body required for the maximum of the function. Their eyes
were glazed. Not blind. Not empty. Glazed, the way a window was
glazed: transparent to the input that passed through it and
opaque to everything else, seeing not the street and the
buildings and the Orceri citizens and the human slaves but the
mana field that overlay all of these, the invisible topology of
magical energy that saturated the town and everyone in it.

They walked down the street. The Orceri citizens stepped aside
without comment. A shopkeeper paused mid-transaction. A human
slave pressing flat against a wall did not register in the
sensoriums' perception at all, because a human slave's mana
signature was too faint and too familiar to trigger attention.

The mana engine hummed in Aleron's pack.

The sensoriums approached. Twenty feet. Fifteen. Ten. Five. They
passed the group at arm's length, close enough that Smiggles
could see the individual links in the chains and the thin blue
veins beneath the translucent skin and the glazed eyes that did
not focus on anything in the visual spectrum because they were
focused on something else entirely, something that existed in
the space between the physical world and the mana field that
overlay it.

The engine hummed. The ambient noise it produced surrounded the
group in a sphere of undifferentiated mana, and at the sphere's
centre Aleron stood with his hood drawn forward and his staff in
his hands and his planar signature, the dimensional anomaly that
had defied Yzrat's instruments from a mile away, wrapped in the
noise of a civilian thermal device whose output was designed to
be unremarkable and whose unremarkableness was, in this moment,
the difference between life and discovery.

The sensoriums passed.

They did not pause. They did not turn. They continued down the
street in perfect synchronisation, their starved bodies moving
with the mechanical precision of things that were no longer
bodies in any meaningful sense but components, parts of a system,
the biological hardware of a surveillance network that had been
designed by beings who understood that the most efficient tool
was one that had been stripped of everything except its function.

They turned a corner and were gone.

Aleron exhaled. The sound was small and private and carried a
weight that the sound itself could not contain. His grey eyes
had tracked the sensoriums from the moment they appeared to the
moment they disappeared, and the expression on his gaunt face
was something beyond horror. It was recognition. The bone
structure he had seen beneath the emaciation and the metal was
the bone structure of his people. The ears that scanned without
communicating were the ears that his own ears resembled. The
beings that had passed him at arm's length, their consciousnesses
dissolved into a hivemind of total submission, had been, in some
generation before the Orceri had broken them, Ya'sehrim.

He did not speak. He leaned on his staff and breathed and the
amulet pulsed green at his throat and the echoes of adjacent
realities murmured at the edges of his perception, and he was
grateful for the echoes, because the echoes were his and the
madness was his and the fractures in his consciousness were his,
chosen for him by an entity that had not asked his permission
but had left him his mind, and what had been done to those
elves had not left them their minds, had not left them anything,
had taken the race that had received Minerva's Enlightening and
reduced it to circuitry.

---

The dwarven hulks stood at the town's perimeter.

Smiggles saw the first one from two hundred yards and did not
understand what he was seeing, because what he was seeing did
not correspond to any category his mind maintained for things
that existed in the world. It was too large. Not too large for
a building, or a statue, or a geological formation, but too
large for something that was standing on two legs at an
intersection, because the two legs were each the diameter of
tree trunks, and the body they supported was three times the
height and six times the mass of the dwarves that Smiggles had
grown up beside, had studied with, had drunk with, had argued
with, had trusted with his life and his research and his
reputation in the workshops and laboratories and taverns of
a world where dwarves were people.

Adamantine growths covered the hulk's body. Not armour. Growths.
The metal had been bonded to the skin, or the skin had been
transformed into the metal, or the distinction between skin and
metal had been eliminated by a process that the word biomancy
described without explaining, the way the word murder described
a death without explaining the life that preceded it. Stone
formations erupted from the shoulders and the spine, grey and
dense, integrated into the musculature with a precision that
suggested the integration was not a modification but a redesign,
the original body used as a foundation for something that bore
the same relationship to a dwarf that a siege engine bore to a
cart. The hands were enormous, oversized even for the enormous
body, the fingers fused into shapes that were less hands than
instruments, the fine manipulation that was the hallmark of
dwarven craft, the deft precision that turned raw stone into
architecture and raw metal into beauty, replaced by mass and
force and the ability to reduce a fortification to rubble with
a single blow.

Its eyes were buried. Deep in the transformed face, beneath
ridges of adamantine and stone, two small points of biological
matter remained, dull, unfocused, showing nothing. Not pain.
Not awareness. Not the stubborn, immovable, magnificent defiance
that Smiggles had seen in every dwarf he had ever known, the
quality that made dwarves what they were: unbending, unbreaking,
the people of the stone who outlasted everything because they
were too stubborn to be outlasted. The hulk's eyes showed none
of this. The hulk's eyes showed what remained when stubbornness
had been removed, and what remained was compliance, and
compliance in a dwarf was an obscenity that Smiggles had not
known it was possible to commit.

He went quiet.

Iskar noticed. Lai noticed. Aleron, leaning on his staff with
his hood drawn forward, noticed. All of them noticed because
they had learned, in the weeks and months of working alongside
the gnomish mage, that Smiggles' silence was a signal, a
diagnostic, a warning system more reliable than any arcane
detection, and the silence that fell over him now was not the
brief silence of being impressed or the tactical silence of
assessing a threat but the deep, structural silence of a man
whose understanding of the world had been broken by the thing
he was looking at and who was rebuilding that understanding
from the foundations up, and the rebuilding was not a process
that words could assist.

He stood on the street and looked at what the Orceri had done
to the dwarves.

The hulk did not look back. The hulk did not look at anything.
It stood at the intersection with its massive hands hanging at
its sides, responding to nothing, waiting for nothing, existing
in the particular way that tools existed: present, functional,
and empty of everything that the tool's creators had not
designed it to contain.

Smiggles turned away. He walked. His companions walked with him.
The silence persisted.

---

What was missing.

Smiggles catalogued the absences as they moved through the town's
residential ring toward its perimeter, retracing their path to
the northern road. The mana engine hummed in Aleron's pack. The
polymorph held, the arcane layer and the draconic reinforcement
maintaining the disguise despite the hours that had passed and
the stress that accompanied them, though Iskar reported, once,
quietly, that his left tusk itched.

No wildlife. The streets were empty of the biological noise that
defined every city Smiggles had lived in. No birds on the
rooftops. No stray animals in the alleys. No insects buzzing
around the food vendors' stalls. The absence was total, the
environmental silence of a place where the non-Orceri biological
world had been managed out of existence, and the managing had
been so thorough and so sustained that the absence had become
the norm, the way silence became the norm in a room where no
one had spoken for centuries.

No weeds between the paving stones. No moss on the walls. The
trees planted along the residential streets were identical in
species and size, trimmed to identical shapes, producing neither
fruit nor seed, ornamental in the strictest sense: objects placed
for aesthetic purpose, maintained for aesthetic purpose, serving
no ecological function because the concept of ecology had been
superseded by the concept of control.

No elves. No halflings. These races did not exist here, not in
chains, not in grey tunics, not in any form. The elves had been
twisted into sensoriums. The halflings had been exterminated on
an island that Rangithyr now used as a private retreat. The
absence of these peoples was not a gap in the population but an
erasure from it, the removal of entire threads from the tapestry
of the world, and the tapestry had been rewoven so completely
that the remaining threads showed no sign of the ones that had
been pulled.

No temples. No religious buildings of any kind. The statue of
Zuel in the plaza was cultural, civic, the way a flag was civic:
a symbol of identity rather than an object of worship. The Orceri
did not worship. They had conquered their gods. When you had
stripped the divine energy from the beings the lesser races called
gods and repurposed it as streetlights and floating carriages and
surveillance networks, worship became redundant, a behaviour that
belonged to an earlier stage of civilisational development, a
stage the Orceri had left behind eight hundred years ago.

No poverty. No beggars. No slums. No visible inequality among
Orceri citizens. Every Orceri they passed was fed, clothed,
housed, employed. The empire provided. The streets were clean
because the empire's slaves cleaned them. The food was abundant
because the empire's slaves grew it. The technology functioned
because the empire's slaves and the empire's biomantic
modifications maintained the infrastructure that produced and
distributed the power that ran the devices that made the Orceri's
lives comfortable and productive and entirely, absolutely,
unremarkably pleasant.

The cost of this pleasantness walked three paces behind them in
grey tunics.

---

They departed before the polymorph degraded.

The climb back toward the mountains was faster than the descent,
not because the terrain was easier but because the urgency was
greater, the need to put distance between themselves and the
civilisation they had observed driving their legs up the slope
with a force that exhaustion and altitude could not entirely
negate. Smiggles walked with his hands clenched, the mana
engine humming in Aleron's pack ahead of him. The device
represented the only material gain of the reconnaissance: a
piece of Orceri technology that he could study, analyse,
dismantle, understand. Everything else they had gained was
carried in their minds, and what they carried in their minds
was not intelligence in the military sense but something heavier
and less useful: knowledge. The knowledge of what the Orceri
were. The knowledge of what they had built. The knowledge that
the threat they had come to assess was not a threat in the way
that an army on a border was a threat, a thing that could be met
and fought and possibly defeated, but a threat in the way that
a glacier was a threat, vast and slow and irresistible and
utterly indifferent to the things it consumed.

Iskar walked in silence. The performance had not returned. The
theatrical warmth, the rolled Rs, the easy gait, the charismatic
armour that protected him from the world and the world from the
parts of him that the armour concealed, none of it was present.
What walked up the mountain road was the man beneath the
performance, the man who had seen what human slavery looked like
when it was complete, and the man was quiet, and the quiet was
not the silence of someone who had nothing to say but the silence
of someone who had too much to say and no words in any language
he spoke that could carry the weight of it.

Aleron leaned on his staff and did not speak. His grey eyes were
half-focused on the path and half-focused on something else, the
echoes crowding his perception as fatigue lowered the barriers
that the amulet maintained, the adjacent realities seeping
through the fractures with the patient insistence of water finding
its level. The mana engine hummed against his back, its steady
pulse a second heartbeat beside his own, mechanical and reliable
in a way that his own heartbeat, after seventy years of
Thalaman's gift, was not. He walked because walking was what
remained when everything else had been stripped away, the forward
motion of a body that had been walking toward something for
seventy years and would continue walking until the thing it
walked toward was reached or the body could not walk anymore.

Lai had been quiet throughout.

She had observed the town with the yellow eyes that the Orceri
disguise could not fully conceal, the vertical pupils tracking
the streets and the buildings and the enslaved and the sensoriums
and the dwarven hulks and the surveillance constructs with the
systematic attention of a predator cataloguing an environment,
noting the positions of threat and the avenues of escape and the
resources that the environment contained, the assessment running
beneath the surface of whatever thoughts she did not share and
whatever conclusions she did not offer. She had not spoken in the
commercial ring. She had not spoken at the perimeter. She had not
spoken during the climb.

As they reached the tree line, where the sparse conifers resumed
their grim occupation of the upper slopes and the cultivated
valley fell away below them, she spoke.

"Now you understand," she said, "what I felt pressing against
the borders of our world."

The words were delivered without performance, without provocation,
without the playful veneer that characterised her interactions
with the heroes in every context they had shared. The v/w
substitution was absent. The musical lightness was absent. What
remained was the voice beneath the voices, the voice that belonged
not to Lai the wood elf adventurer or Lai the provocative
companion but to Lyanorra, the young green dragon who had felt
something vast and terrible leaning against the boundary of her
reality and had chosen to walk toward it rather than away from
it, and whose reasons for that choice remained, as they had always
been, her own.

No one answered. The trees closed around them. The third moon
set behind the peaks, and the sky above the mountains held only
two, silver and gold, and the two were the wrong two, because
they were the right two in the wrong world, and the rightness
of the moons was the final cruelty of a reality that looked
almost like home and was not home and could never be home because
home was a place where dwarves were people and humans were free
and elves were not circuitry and the sky had the correct number
of moons.

They climbed. The cave waited above them. Aizune waited within
it, beside a dragon whose history was the history of everything
they had just seen, and the history was eight hundred years long,
and every year of it was worse than the year before, and the
worst part was not the cruelty but the absence of cruelty, the
calm, systematic, utterly sincere efficiency with which a
civilisation had consumed everything that was not itself and
built something clean and orderly and pleasant on the remains.

Smiggles climbed in silence. The mana engine hummed in Aleron's
pack ahead of him. His moustache was gone, hidden beneath the
Orceri disguise, and he missed it with a fierceness that was out
of all proportion to the significance of a piece of facial hair
and was in perfect proportion to the significance of being
something that the Orceri had not shaped, had not bred, had not
optimised, had not stripped of everything that made it what it
was and replaced with something useful.

His moustache was his. The wax was his. The curl was his. The
vanity was his. And in a world where everything that was not
Orceri had been reduced to function, the vanity of a gnomish
mage about his moustache was not vanity at all. It was defiance.

He climbed, and the mana engine hummed, and the mountain
received them back into its cold and empty and blessedly
unconquered silence.
