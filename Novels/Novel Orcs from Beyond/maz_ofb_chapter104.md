# Chapter Four
## Blood on the Steppes

The keep squatted on the horizon like a broken tooth in a dead man's
jaw.

Three days of riding through the Steppes of Broken Stone had stripped
the land of every familiar comfort. The scrub grass had thinned to
nothing by the second morning, replaced by bare hardpan and fields of
shattered rock that stretched in every direction without interruption,
without shade, without the mercy of a single tree or structure to break
the wind that came howling across the flats with nothing to stop it and
nowhere to go. The sky was enormous here, a vast pale dome that pressed
down on the earth with the weight of absolute emptiness, and beneath it
the three riders moved like insects crossing a dinner plate: visible,
exposed, and profoundly aware of both.

"There," Iskar said, reining his horse to a stop on a low ridge of
exposed bedrock. He pointed southeast, where the land dipped into a
shallow depression before rising again toward a cluster of formations
that might have been natural stone and might have been something else.
"The keep. Right where my maps said it would be."

Aizune raised one hand to shade his eyes. The afternoon sun was behind
them, which helped, and at this distance the structure resolved slowly
from the landscape around it: walls of crude stone rising from a base
of older, darker rubble, a squat watchtower at one corner listing
slightly to the east, the whole thing hunched against the Steppes as
though it had been trying to hide and given up halfway through the
attempt. It was not large. Two stories at most, built for function
rather than intimidation, its walls repaired in places with stone that
did not match the original construction. Orc engineering, such as it
was.

"How far?" Smiggles asked. The gnome was sitting on his satchel again,
his spellbook serving as a cushion beneath a cushion, his crossbow
resting across the saddle's pommel. Three days in the Steppes had done
nothing for his temperament. His waistcoat, the forest green one, had
acquired a fine layer of mineral dust that no amount of brushing could
fully remove, and his moustache, while still magnificent, had developed
a slight droop at the right tip that spoke of insufficient access to
quality wax.

"A mile. Perhaps less." Iskar tilted his hat back and studied the keep
with the focused attention of a man counting windows. "We should
dismount here and approach on foot. The ridge gives us cover for
another four hundred paces. After that, we are on open ground."

"Patrols?" Aizune asked.

"Two that I observed during my surveillance. One circles the keep at
roughly two-hour intervals. The other ranges further out, a wider loop,
less predictable. Both consist of four to six orcs." Iskar paused, his
dark eyes still fixed on the distant structure. "The outer patrol is
the danger. If we time our approach between the inner patrol's circuit
and move quickly across the open ground, we reach the eastern wall
without being seen. If the outer patrol returns early, we are three
people standing in the middle of nothing with nowhere to hide."

"Cheerful," Smiggles said.

"I am a realist, my frrriend. Cheerful is for people who haven't
counted the orcs."

"How many?" Aizune asked.

"Forty. Perhaps forty-five. I counted thirty-eight distinct individuals
during my four days of observation, but there were sections of the
interior I could not see from the ridge. Call it forty and assume I am
wrong by five in the direction we don't want."

Aizune touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. Forty orcs. A shaman
who could channel negative energy. A fortified position with a single
visible entrance. He had fought orcs before, in smaller numbers, during
caravan escort work along the Merchant Nations' eastern borders. They
were strong, savage, and fearless in groups. Forty of them, led by a
cleric of death, behind stone walls, was not a fight that three people
won through the front door.

"Tell me about the eastern face," he said.

Iskar's smile was the smile of a man who had been waiting for that
question. He swung down from his horse with the fluid grace of someone
dismounting a ship's rail rather than a saddle, reached into his
saddlebag, and produced a roll of paper that he spread across a flat
stone with the practiced efficiency of a navigator laying out a chart.

"The keep's eastern wall," he said, weighting the paper's corners with
pebbles. The drawing was detailed, clean, annotated in a quick hand.
"Built on rubble from something older. The older construction extends
below ground level, and the joint between old and new stonework is
imperfect. Here, here, and here." His finger tapped three points along
the wall's base. "Ventilation shafts. The original builders, whoever
they were, ran passages through the walls with openings at regular
intervals. The orcs have blocked most of them with loose stone, but the
blocking is crude. An hour's quiet work with a pry bar opens any of
them."

"And inside?"

"The passages run through the walls themselves. Internal. I could not
confirm their full extent from outside, but the pattern of ventilation
openings suggests they connect the keep's major rooms. The walls are
thick enough. Whoever built the original structure wanted to be able to
move through it without using the corridors."

"Spy-holes," Smiggles said, leaning forward on his saddle to study the
drawing. His eyes had sharpened, the irritation of three dusty days
replaced by the focused intensity he brought to any problem worth
solving. "Hidden passages with observation points. That's not military
construction. That's a wizard's paranoia."

"Or a wizard's good sense," Aizune said, "given what happened to
wizards under the Dukhan Empire."

Smiggles glanced at him. "You think this was built by someone hiding
from the Zebulbans?"

"The keep is built on older rubble. The older construction is of a
different style and quality. The Steppes of Broken Stone are within the
historical boundaries of the Dukhan Empire's western territories, which
fell in 213 BW. A wizard fleeing Zebulban persecution would have reason
to build hidden passages in a remote location." He paused. "I am
speculating."

"You're speculating well. That calibrates with everything we're seeing.
Exactly the kind of person who'd build passages through their own
walls." Smiggles' thick fingers traced the drawing's lines. "If the
passages connect to the interior rooms, we can observe the orcs without
being detected. Count them. Identify the shaman's location. Plan the
assault from inside their own walls."

"That," Iskar said, rolling the map with a flourish, "is precisely what
I had in mind."

---

They waited for dusk.

The outer patrol passed their position an hour before sunset: six orcs
in crude leather and chain, carrying axes and javelins, moving in a
loose formation that suggested familiarity with the route rather than
alertness to danger. They passed within three hundred paces of the
ridge where the heroes lay flat against the stone, their horses hobbled
in a shallow depression behind them. Aizune watched the patrol through
narrowed eyes. The orcs were large, heavy-boned, their skin ranging
from grey-green to a muddy brown. They moved with the loping,
ground-eating stride of creatures built for endurance rather than speed.
One of them, the largest, wore a necklace of what appeared to be finger
bones.

The patrol moved on. The sun sank behind the western horizon, painting
the Steppes in shades of rust and amber. The two moons had not yet
risen.

"Now," Iskar said.

They moved fast and low across the open ground, keeping to the
deepening shadows where the rock formations broke the fading light.
Smiggles led the approach, his gnomish darkvision rendering the
twilight landscape in sharp detail that neither elf nor human could
match. Where Aizune's low-light vision gave him an advantage over Iskar
in the dimming world, resolving shapes and distances that the Sabonaar
was already losing to shadow, Smiggles navigated as though it were
midday, picking the surest path between rock formations with the
confidence of eyes built for darkness. Aizune followed his lead, his
ghost touch blade strapped across his back and his bow in his hand.
Iskar came last, his dark coat blending with the stone, his rapier
sheathed, his movements silent with the particular quality of silence
that came from years of moving across ship decks in darkness without
waking a sleeping crew.

They reached the eastern wall without incident. Up close, the keep's
construction was even cruder than it had appeared from the ridge. The
upper walls were rough-stacked stone mortared with a greyish paste that
was already crumbling in places, the work of builders who had
prioritized speed over permanence. But the lower section, the rubble
base that Iskar's maps had identified, was different. Darker stone,
harder, cut with a precision that the crude walls above could not
approach. The joint between old and new construction was visible as a
distinct line, a border between eras.

Iskar found the first ventilation shaft by touch, running his fingers
along the base of the old stonework until he located the gap where the
blocking stones had been wedged. He produced a short pry bar from his
coat, an implement that seemed to appear in his hand with the ease of
long practice, and began working the stones free with slow, careful
movements that made no more sound than a man breathing.

The first stone came loose. Then the second. Behind them, darkness and
the smell of old air: dust, stone, and something fainter beneath it, a
mineral tang that tickled the back of Aizune's throat with a sensation
he could not immediately identify.

Iskar turned to them and grinned. In the last light of the fading sky,
his teeth were very white against his dark skin. "After you, my
frrriends."

"I am not going first into a hole in the ground," Smiggles said.

"You're the smallest."

"And the most valuable. Valuable people don't go first into holes."

"I will go," Aizune said, and went.

The passage was narrow and low, forcing Aizune to crouch and Iskar,
behind him, to bend nearly double. Smiggles, bringing up the rear, fit
comfortably, a fact he did not mention and would deny if asked. The
walls were the same dark stone as the keep's foundation, smooth to the
touch and faintly cool, as though the rock retained the chill of earth
that had not seen sunlight in centuries. The air was close and still,
heavy with the scent of dust and old stone, and beneath it, growing
stronger as they moved deeper into the wall's interior, that same
mineral tang: copper. Ozone. The particular sharp scent that clung to
places where mana had been worked.

The passage turned, angled upward, and opened into a wider space where
the walls fell away on both sides and the ceiling rose high enough for
Iskar to straighten. They stood in the wall's interior, a channel
roughly four feet wide running parallel to what Aizune estimated was
the keep's ground floor. The darkness here was total, the fading
twilight from the shaft entrance lost behind the passage's turn.
Smiggles moved to the front without being asked, his darkvision
rendering the passage in the grey tones of perfect clarity while Aizune
and Iskar stood blind behind him. He surveyed the space for a moment,
grunted in satisfaction, and spoke quietly.

"Passage continues in both directions. Spy-holes on the left wall,
eye height, angled downward, no wider than a coin. Three visible from
here." He paused. "It's safe. We need light for the rest of you."

Aizune whispered a prayer, barely more than a breath, and a soft
silvery light bloomed from his open palm, Minerva's gift, cool and
gentle, illuminating the passage without the harsh glare that might
seep through cracks in the stonework. The passage sprang into view for
all three of them: the spy-holes exactly where Smiggles had described
them, the dark stone walls stretching away in both directions.

"There," Iskar said, pointing to the spy-holes. "Shall we see what
we're dealing with?"

Aizune pressed his eye to the nearest opening.

The room beyond was large, low-ceilinged, and occupied. A dozen orcs
sprawled across the stone floor on bedrolls of animal hide, their
weapons stacked against the walls, the remains of a meal scattered
among them: bones, crusts of hard bread, a clay jug that had been
passed around until its contents were exhausted. The smell that filtered
through the spy-hole was powerful: unwashed bodies, rancid cooking fat,
the particular sour musk of orc that no amount of exposure ever made
tolerable. Several of the orcs were asleep. Three more sat near a
guttering fire in a crude hearth, talking in low grunts.

Aizune moved to the next spy-hole. Another room, smaller, stacked with
crates and barrels. Some of the crates bore markings he recognized: the
Red Raven's crimson bird clutching gold coins. Aldorph's stolen goods.
Beyond the store room, a corridor, and at its end a stairway leading
up.

He moved along the passage, checking each spy-hole in turn. The
passages ran through the keep's walls exactly as Iskar had predicted,
connecting the ground floor rooms in a continuous circuit. Through
successive openings, Aizune counted orcs: twelve in the barracks room,
four in a second sleeping area, six in what appeared to be a guard room
near the main entrance, three more in a small chamber where crude
weapons were being repaired. Twenty-five on the ground floor. The rest
would be above, or on patrol.

He returned to the others and reported in whispers.

"Twenty-five below. The upper level will hold the shaman and his
personal guard. The storeroom with Aldorph's goods is here." He pointed
to his mental map of the layout. "Main entrance is on the western face,
guarded by six."

"Twenty-five plus whatever's upstairs." Smiggles was doing arithmetic.
"Call it thirty-five to forty inside the walls. The patrols account for
the rest. If we hit the ground floor while the patrols are out, we
reduce the numbers before the shaman knows we're here."

"We'd need to block the main entrance," Iskar said. "If the patrols
return while we are still clearing rooms, we're fighting on two fronts."

"I can seal the entrance," Smiggles said. "Webbing across the doorway.
It won't hold forever, but it buys us time."

"How much time?"

"Depends on whether any of them have fire. Orcs with fire, a few
minutes. Orcs without fire, longer."

"Assume they have fire," Aizune said. "They always have fire."

"Then we work fast." Smiggles' voice had shifted, the irritable
traveler replaced by the precise analyst. "Ground floor first. Barracks
room is the largest concentration. Twelve orcs in a confined space. I
open with something that fills the room. Poison fog or fire, depending
on whether we want prisoners."

"We do not want prisoners," Iskar said.

Smiggles looked at Aizune. The elf considered for a moment, then shook
his head. "Kru'ka'ta is the priority. Everything we need to know, his
notes will tell us. The warriors are combatants in a hostile
fortification. We cannot afford to leave enemies at our backs while we
move upward."

"Fire, then." Smiggles said it with the calm of a man ordering
breakfast. "I take the barracks. You two clear the adjacent rooms while
they're still burning. We meet at the stairs and go up together."

"What about the guard room?" Iskar asked. "Six orcs at the main
entrance."

"I seal the entrance with webbing before the fire. The guards are
trapped inside with the webbing between them and the door. By the time
they cut through, we've cleared the ground floor and the patrols can't
get in."

"Bold plan," Iskar said. "I like bold plans. They go wrong in
interresting ways."

"Do you have a better one?"

"No. I have a worse one that involves me going through a ventilation
shaft into a room full of orcs with nothing but charm and good looks."

"Then we go with mine," Smiggles said. "Better fire than good looks."

"You wound me, little mage."

"I haven't started wounding anyone yet. Give me ten minutes."

---

They struck at moonrise.

Smiggles positioned himself at a spy-hole adjacent to the main
entrance, spoke three syllables of Draconic in a rapid, clipped
cadence, and thrust his hand through the narrow opening. Strands of
thick, sticky webbing erupted from his palm and filled the entrance
corridor in an instant, a wall of adhesive filaments that caught the
nearest guard mid-step and pinned him to the floor with his mouth open
and his axe raised in a swing he would never complete. The other five
guards turned, shouting, reaching for weapons, finding themselves
already tangled as the web expanded to fill the corridor from floor to
ceiling and wall to wall.

Smiggles was already moving. Twenty paces down the passage, he reached
the spy-hole overlooking the barracks, and his demeanor changed. The
irritable, moustache-conscious gnome vanished. What remained was a mage
at work: focused, economical, precise. His hands rose. His fingers
traced geometries in the darkness, two-handed, the movements crisp and
sharp, each gesture a component of a formula he had practiced until the
mathematics were as natural as breathing. Six syllables of Draconic,
each one heavier than the last, building like the steps of a man
climbing toward something inevitable. The air around his hands ignited
with warm gold light, the mana responding to his command, shaping
itself to the pattern of his will.

He pressed both palms flat against the stone and spoke the final
syllable.

The blast of fire entered the barracks room through the spy-hole as a
bead of orange-white light no larger than a marble, crossed the room in
the space between heartbeats, and detonated.

The sound was enormous in the confined space, a thunderclap of
superheated air that shook dust from the passage walls and hit Aizune's
chest like a physical blow even through the stone. The spy-hole blazed
with light, amber and orange and the particular terrible white of flesh
catching fire. The screaming started immediately: orc voices, deep and
raw, the involuntary sounds of bodies waking from sleep into an
inferno. The barracks room became a furnace.

"Go," Smiggles said. His voice was flat. His eyes were already moving
to the next spy-hole.

Aizune kicked out the thin stone panel that separated the passage from
the adjacent room, a storage chamber where four orcs had been sleeping
among crates of stolen goods. The panel shattered under his boot. He
came through the opening with his ghost touch blade in his right hand
and Minerva's light blazing from his left, silver-white, filling the
room with a radiance that blinded creatures accustomed to firelight and
darkness. The first orc was still reaching for its weapon when Aizune's
blade took it across the throat.

The second came at him with an axe, bellowing, its eyes streaming from
the sudden light. Aizune sidestepped, let the axe pass, and drove his
blade through the orc's ribs with a precision that came from decades of
Ya'sehrim swordsmanship, the blade training that elves learned
alongside prayer and meditation in the forests of Vand'ardhon. The orc
fell. The third scrambled for the door. Aizune spoke a word of
Draconic, sharp and clear, and the orc's limbs locked rigid, its body
freezing mid-stride, every muscle seizing at once as though the air
itself had become a fist closed around it. The orc's eyes rolled. Its
mouth worked. It did not fall, because falling required the freedom to
move, and that freedom had been taken from it as completely as if it
had been carved from stone. He killed it with a single stroke before
the fourth orc, the largest, slammed into him from the side and drove
him into the wall.

The impact knocked the breath from his lungs. The orc was enormous, a
head taller than Aizune and twice his mass, its hands closing around
his throat with crushing force. His vision darkened at the edges. He
could smell the orc's breath, rotten meat and something acidic, and
feel the heat of its skin, the furnace-temperature that orc physiology
maintained. His blade was pinned between their bodies. He could not
swing it.

He twisted his wrist, angling the edge where the chainmail gaped at the
orc's belly, and pushed. The elven curved blade was forged for exactly
this: the narrow, angled thrust that found the spaces armour could not
protect, the gaps between links, the seams where metal met leather. The
blade slid between the rings and into the creature's chest, and the
ghost touch enchantment did the rest, the steel passing through
chainmail as though the metal were smoke. The orc's grip loosened. Its
eyes went wide. It slid to the floor, and Aizune stepped back,
breathing hard, his robes spattered with blood that was not his own.

From the corridor beyond the storage room, he heard the sound of a
rapier at work.

Iskar had entered the keep through a second passage exit that opened
into the corridor between the guard room and the weapons repair
chamber. He had emerged into a hallway full of smoke and confusion,
three orcs from the repair chamber stumbling through the haze with
weapons drawn, and he had greeted them with the enthusiasm of a man who
had been riding through a wasteland for three days and was profoundly
ready to do something that was not riding through a wasteland.

"Sangre y sal!" The oath rang through the corridor, bright and sharp
above the chaos, and Iskar was among them. His rapier was a silver line
in the smoke-filled air, fast, precise, the blade finding gaps in crude
armour with the educated accuracy of a swordsman trained in the
Sabonaar fencing tradition and refined by years of boarding actions
where the distance between combatants was measured in inches. The first
orc took the blade through the shoulder, staggered, and received a boot
to the chest that sent it crashing backward into its companion. The
second swung a heavy mace in a wide arc that would have crushed Iskar's
skull if it had connected. It did not connect. Iskar ducked beneath it
with the boneless fluidity of a man dodging a swinging boom, came up
inside the orc's guard, and put his rapier through its throat.

The third orc turned to run. Iskar's throwing knife caught it between
the shoulder blades, and it went down face-first with a sound like a
sack of grain hitting a deck.

"Three," Iskar called into the smoke. "How are we doing?"

"Four," Aizune answered from the storage room.

"Twelve," Smiggles said, appearing from the passage entrance with his
crossbow in one hand and the satisfied expression of a man whose
arithmetic had proven correct.

From the main entrance, the sound of orcs hacking at webbing. From
above, the thud of heavy feet on stone. "They know we're here," Aizune
said.

"Let them know," Iskar said, pulling his throwing knife from the dead
orc's back and wiping it on the creature's tunic. "Let them come down
those stairs into a corridor full of smoke with three people who just
killed nineteen of their friends in less time than it takes to finish a
meal. Let them think about that on the way down."

"The shaman won't come down," Smiggles said. "He'll send his guard
first and prepare from above. Something to muffle our approach on the
stairway, maybe. Magical shadow to swallow our light. Something to
blind us before we reach him."

"Then we don't give him time to prepare." Aizune was already moving
toward the stairs. His blade was red to the hilt. Minerva's light still
blazed from his free hand, dimmer now, the divine energy responding to
his fatigue, but steady. "We go up now. Together. Before he sets his
defenses."

"Stow that caution I was expecting, Silvershine," Iskar said with a
grin that was all teeth and no pretense.

"Do not call me that."

"Too late. It's stuck."

They went up.

---

The stairway was narrow, steep, and designed for defenders. Each step
was slightly higher than the last, a construction technique intended to
tire attackers and give the advantage of height to those above. The
smoke from the barracks fire had risen through gaps in the floorboards,
filling the upper level with a grey haze that stank of charred wood and
worse things. The landing at the top was dark, darker than the smoke
alone could account for, and as Aizune climbed the last steps, the
Minerva light in his palm flickered and dimmed.

Magical darkness. A sphere of supernatural shadow that swallowed
natural and magical light alike, filling the landing and the corridor
beyond with an impenetrable black. Smiggles had been right.

"I can't see," Iskar said from behind him, his voice tight.

Smiggles, further back on the stairs, squinted into the blackness. Even
his darkvision, which cut through natural darkness like a blade through
silk, found nothing. The shadow was not an absence of light but a
presence of something else, a magical negation that defeated every form
of sight equally. "Supernatural," the gnome said. "Not natural
darkness. My eyes are as blind as yours in this slag. It's eating the
light."

Aizune concentrated, pushing Minerva's gift against the darkness.
Divine light against divine shadow, his goddess's power against
whatever dark entity fueled Kru'ka'ta's magic. The darkness resisted.
Held. Thinned at the edges but did not break.

"Dispel it," Aizune said.

Smiggles was already casting. Six syllables of precise Draconic, his
voice pitched low and hard, each word a command rather than a request.
The warm gold light of his arcane working flared in the darkness,
visible only as a faint halo around his hands, and then expanded
outward in a pulse that met the supernatural shadow and shattered it
like glass. The darkness collapsed. Light returned, grey and
smoke-thick but light, and in that light they saw the corridor ahead:
short, ending in a heavy wooden door that stood ajar.

Beyond the door, chanting. Low, guttural Draconic spoken in a register
that was not the clean precision of arcane spellwork but something
thicker, heavier, each syllable pulling energy from a source that felt
cold even at this distance.

"He is channelling," Aizune said. He could feel it through Minerva's
gift: the movement of negative energy, the anti-life force that
death-domain clerics drew from the boundary between the living world
and whatever lay beyond it. The sensation was a pressure against his
divine senses, a wrongness that manifested as a chill in his bones and
a faint nausea at the base of his throat.

"Then let's interrrupt him," Iskar said, and the rolled R turned the
urgency into something that sounded, improbably, like an invitation to
a dance.

They hit the door together.

The chamber beyond was the keep's upper room, large enough to serve as
Kru'ka'ta's personal quarters and sanctum. Animal skins covered the
walls. Crude shelves held jars of preserved organs and bundles of dried
herbs that stank of decay. A table was covered in papers, scrolls, and
the remains of alchemical experiments. Candles of black tallow burned
in a circle on the floor, their flames an unnatural violet that cast
shadows in directions the light did not support.

Kru'ka'ta stood at the circle's center.

He was not what Aizune had expected. Orc shamans in his experience were
wild-eyed, frenzied, channelling their magic through rage and religious
ecstasy. Kru'ka'ta was calm. He was also enormous, even for an orc:
nearly seven feet tall, broad as a barrel, his grey-green skin scarred
with ritual markings that ran in parallel lines across his arms and
chest. He wore a chain shirt that had been maintained with more care
than anything else in the keep, and his morningstar, a heavy weapon
with a flanged steel head, rested in his right hand with the ease of
long familiarity. His eyes were flat and dark and utterly devoid of
fear.

Beside him stood his last four guards, warriors in heavy armour, their
weapons drawn, their positions arranged to protect the shaman while he
worked. Between them, on the floor, two shambling figures moved with
the jerking, graceless motion of bodies that had forgotten how to live.
Animated dead. Two of the orcs from the barracks, their flesh blackened
and blistered from the fire, raised by Kru'ka'ta's channelling to
fight again as corpses.

"Oy! He's been busy," Smiggles said.

Kru'ka'ta raised one hand. The violet candlelight pulsed, and a wave
of cold, dark energy rolled outward from his position, washing over the
heroes like a tide of frigid water. Aizune felt it strike his divine
protections and slide off, Minerva's gift shielding him from the worst
of the negative channelling. Beside him, Iskar staggered, his face
going grey, his hand tightening on his rapier hilt as the death energy
sapped the strength from his limbs.

"I'm fine," Iskar said through gritted teeth. He did not look fine. He
looked like a man who had just aged five years in two seconds. "Hit
him."

Smiggles hit him. Three syllables of Draconic, precise as a scalpel,
and three bolts of golden force, unerring missiles that required no aim
and permitted no evasion, streaked across the room and struck Kru'ka'ta
in the chest. The orc staggered. The channelling faltered. And the
guards charged.

The fight that followed was the hardest thing any of them had done.

The four orc guards were not the crude warriors of the ground floor.
They were veterans, armoured in heavy chain and carrying weapons with
the confidence of creatures who had used them often and well. They came
at the heroes in pairs, coordinated, their attacks designed to separate
and overwhelm. The two animated corpses shambled forward between them,
slower but relentless, immune to pain and fear, dead hands grasping.

Aizune met the first pair. His ghost touch blade sang in the
violet-tinged air, the pale luminescence along its edge flaring
brighter as it carved through an orc's guard and opened a gash across
its armoured forearm. The orc bellowed and swung its greatsword in a
horizontal arc that Aizune barely avoided, the blade passing close
enough to tug at his robes. He countered, blade to blade, the impact
jarring his arm to the shoulder. The orc was stronger. He was faster.
He used the speed, stepping inside the greatsword's reach where the
long weapon was a liability, and drove his blade upward through the gap
between chain links at the orc's armpit. The orc fell.

Its companion hit Aizune from the side, a mace blow that caught his
ribs and would have caved them if the mithril shirt beneath his robes
had not turned the worst of the impact. He went down on one knee,
breath exploding from his lungs, and the orc raised its mace for a
killing stroke. Iskar's rapier took it through the eye.

"You're welcome, Silvershine," Iskar said, already turning to meet the
next threat. One of the animated corpses had reached him, its dead
hands closing on his coat. He severed the hands at the wrists with two
quick cuts, kicked the stumbling thing backward, and drove his rapier
through its skull. The corpse collapsed. Iskar looked at the fallen
thing with an expression of profound distaste. "Dead reckoning," he
muttered, and turned to face the second pair of guards.

Smiggles had not been idle. His position at the door gave him the
distance he needed, and he used it with ruthless efficiency. Three
beams of orange-white fire streaked from his outstretched hand and
struck the second animated corpse, reducing it to ash and scattered
bone. A bolt from his crossbow took an orc guard in the throat while
it was distracted by Iskar's blade. His casting was rapid, economical,
each spell chosen for maximum effect with minimum expenditure, the
pragmatic arithmetic of a mage who knew exactly how much magical energy
he had and how much this fight was going to cost.

But Kru'ka'ta was still standing.

The orc shaman had recovered from the force bolt strike and was casting
again, his guttural Draconic filling the chamber with syllables that
seemed to thicken the air. The violet candlelight blazed. A second wave
of negative energy rolled outward, stronger than the first, and this
time Aizune felt it crack against his divine protections like a fist
against a shield. Iskar went to one knee, gasping, his face the colour
of old parchment. Even Smiggles, at the back of the room, flinched as
the death energy washed over him.

"He's channelling again," Aizune said, and his voice was the voice of a
priest, clear and steady and carrying an authority that did not come
from his throat alone. He raised his free hand. Silver-white light
blazed from his palm, brighter than before, brighter than it had any
right to be in a room full of death magic and violet shadow. The light
struck the negative energy and burned through it like the sun through
mist, Minerva's power answering her priest's call with a force that
pushed the darkness back and filled the chamber with a radiance that
was clean and fierce and utterly without mercy.

Kru'ka'ta shielded his eyes. The negative channelling shattered.

Aizune closed the distance. The orc swung his morningstar, a
devastating blow that would have crushed bone and armour alike. Aizune
ducked, felt the flanged head pass over his hair, and came up inside
the orc's reach. His blade found the gap in the chain shirt at the hip.
Kru'ka'ta roared, staggered, and swung again, backhanded, catching
Aizune across the shoulder with the morningstar's haft. Pain exploded
down his arm. He held the blade. He held his footing.

Iskar was there. Sangre y sal. The rapier opened a cut across
Kru'ka'ta's forearm, and the morningstar's grip faltered. Smiggles was
there. Two bolts of golden force struck the orc's chest, driving him
backward into his own circle of black candles, scattering them,
extinguishing the violet flames. The room's unnatural atmosphere
collapsed. The cold receded. The shadows returned to the angles the
remaining light demanded.

Kru'ka'ta fell to his knees. His flat dark eyes moved from Aizune to
Iskar to Smiggles, and something crossed his face that might have been
surprise and might have been recognition of the inevitable. He reached
for his morningstar. Aizune's blade stopped him.

Silence filled the chamber. The smoke from below drifted through the
floorboards. The last of the violet candle-wax guttered and went dark.

"Well," Smiggles said into the quiet. He was breathing hard. His
moustache was singed at one tip. "That was expensive."

"Are you hurt?" Aizune asked.

"My moustache is hurt. The rest of me is furious. I intend to charge
Aldorph extra for the moustache."

Iskar laughed. It was a short, breathless sound, the laugh of a man who
had just survived something he was not entirely sure he would survive.
He was leaning against the wall, one hand pressed to his side where the
negative energy had hit him hardest, his dark skin ashen around the
lips. "That," he said, "was a fight worrth having. Though I could have
done without the dead ones. Walking corpses are poorr opponents. No
style at all."

"Sit down before you fall down," Smiggles told him.

"I am fine."

"You are the colour of old cheese. Sit."

Iskar sat. Aizune knelt beside him and placed a hand on his chest. A
whispered prayer, Minerva's name shaped with intention and need, and
the warm pulse of healing magic flowed from his palm into Iskar's body,
restoring what the negative energy had taken. Colour returned to
Iskar's face. His breathing steadied. He looked up at Aizune with an
expression that was, for once, entirely free of performance.

"Thank you, my frrriend."

"You are welcome."

---

The last orc guard died in the corridor outside Kru'ka'ta's chamber,
cut down by Iskar while trying to flee toward the stairs. The inner
patrol returned twenty minutes later and walked into the web-sealed
entrance, where Smiggles dispatched them with a methodical efficiency
that he did not enjoy and did not pretend to enjoy. The outer patrol,
finding the keep's entrance choked with webbing and the interior filled
with smoke and silence, circled the walls once, howled at the sky, and
fled into the Steppes. Smiggles watched them go from the watchtower and
decided not to spend the magic required to stop them.

"Let them run," he said. "They have nowhere to go and no one to run to.
Not a stripped thread in this plan after all."

The keep was theirs.

They found Aldorph's stolen documents and goods in the ground floor
storeroom, intact and carefully catalogued, which suggested that
whoever had hired Kru'ka'ta valued the documents enough to insist on
their preservation. The contracts, shipping manifests, and
correspondence were bundled in oilcloth and tied with cord. Smiggles
examined them briefly and confirmed they matched Aldorph's description.

"The man will be thrilled," he said. "Thrilled and grateful and
hopefully generous. I intend to charge him extra for the moustache."

It was Aizune who found Kru'ka'ta's notes.

CopyThe orc shaman's table was a chaos of papers, scrolls, and crude
drawings, most of them in Orc script so rough and malformed that it
barely qualified as writing, the letters scratched into the parchment
with the heavy hand of someone who had learned to write late and poorly
and resented the necessity. Marginal annotations appeared in Draconic,
but a Draconic so heavily accented and misspelled that Aizune had to
read each word three times before its meaning surfaced from the
butchered spelling like a fish rising through murky water. He sorted
through the mess with patient hands while Smiggles tended to their
minor wounds and Iskar searched the rest of the upper level.

Most of what he could decipher was unremarkable: supply lists, patrol
schedules, fragments of correspondence with the unknown Amaran merchant
who had hired the tribe, written in a pidgin of Orc and Common that
suggested the merchant communicated through an intermediary. But beneath
the mundane papers, in a separate stack weighted with a river stone,
Aizune found something else.

Kru'ka'ta had been investigating the keep's foundations. His notes,
switching between Orc and his tortured Draconic with increasing
frequency, described an excavation in the ground floor, a place where
his orcs had broken through older stonework into passages below. The
passages led down, deeper than the keep's cellars, into chambers of a
different construction altogether. The notes grew increasingly agitated
as they progressed, the handwriting deteriorating, the Draconic
marginalia giving way entirely to Orc as the shaman's discipline
frayed. Kru'ka'ta had found something down there. Something he could
not explain. He had sent orcs to explore. Two of them had not returned.

The final note was scrawled in Draconic so broken that Aizune had to
piece the meaning together from fragments, the letters carved deep into
the parchment by a hand that trembled: "The machine. The machine is
real. The mirrors show faces. The dead ones scream. Do not go down at
night. Do not go down alone."

Aizune read the note twice. Then he carried it to the others.

"There is something beneath us," he said.

Iskar and Smiggles looked at him. Then they looked at the floor.

"Of course there is," Smiggles said. "Because clearing a keep full of
orcs and a death-channelling shaman wasn't enough for one day. Gears
and grindstones. Of course there's something beneath us."

"A machine," Aizune said. "Mirrors. And two orcs who went down and did
not come back."

A silence followed that was heavier than the one after Kru'ka'ta's
death.

"Well," Iskar said, and his voice was quiet, stripped of its usual
theatrical warmth, carrying instead the tone of a man who understood
that the job they had been hired to do had just become something else
entirely. "I suppose we should go and have a look."

They looked at the floor. The smoke drifted. The two moons rose over
the Steppes of Broken Stone and cast their silver and gold light
through the keep's narrow windows, illuminating a room that smelled of
death and ozone and the faintest trace of copper, a scent that had no
business being in an orc shaman's chamber, a scent that belonged to
something much older and much stranger than anything they had expected
to find.

Aizune touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. Smiggles adjusted his
singed moustache. Iskar reached for his rapier.

They went down.
