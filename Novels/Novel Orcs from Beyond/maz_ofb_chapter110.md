# Chapter Ten
## Preparations

They returned to Amara on the last day of Hinter, riding through the
western gate as the afternoon light painted the harbour in shades of
copper and gold, and the city received them with the indifferent
embrace of a place that had seen ten thousand travellers arrive and
depart and had learned not to attach significance to any of them.

Aizune dismounted in the courtyard of the building above Eccentricities
where they had taken rooms before the journey east, and he stood for a
moment with one hand on his horse's neck, watching the street below
fill with the last commerce of the day. Merchants closing shutters.
A dwarf rolling a barrel of ale toward the Hearthstone with the
precise economy of motion that marked everything the Bryndawi did. Two
children chasing a cat through the legs of a fishmonger who was too
tired to object. The ordinary machinery of a city that did not know
what was coming, turning as it had always turned, patient and blind
and precious in its ignorance.

He touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. The silver was warm from
the day's ride.

"You're doing that thing," Smiggles said, dismounting with the
controlled descent of a man whose horse was too tall and whose dignity
was non-negotiable. "The thing where you look at a city and see
everything it stands to lose. You did it before the keep, too. It's
your pre-mission face."

"I was not aware I had a pre-mission face."

"You do. It involves the clasp and the thousand-yard stare and a
silence that lasts exactly long enough for everyone around you to feel
guilty about not being more contemplative." The gnome landed, adjusted
his waistcoat, and began the ritual verification of his moustache that
followed every dismount. "It's very effective. I hate it."

Iskar swung down from his horse with the loose-limbed ease of a man
who treated dismounting as a minor performance, his coat settling
around him in a way that suggested the garment had been trained. "The
Ogre's Arrse," he said, with the reverence other men reserved for
cathedrals. "A hot meal. A drink that was not prroduced in a
saddlebag. A chair that does not move beneath me." He tilted his hat
to a precise angle that communicated satisfaction and intent in equal
measure. "My frrriends, I have missed this terrible city."

Twenty paces behind the group, Aleron Windstrider leaned on his
quarterstaff and watched something no one else could see cross the
courtyard from the stable door to the far wall. He tracked its
progress with the patient attention of a man observing a bird, waited
for it to pass, and then followed the others inside with the careful
gait of someone whose body had been arguing with his will for the
duration of the ride and whose will had won by the narrowest of
margins.

Twenty paces behind Aleron, Lai stretched her arms above her head and
yawned with the unselfconscious abandon of a creature that experienced
physical satisfaction as a full-body event. The yawn showed teeth. It
always showed teeth.

---

They gathered that evening in the common room of their lodgings, a
space that was modest and clean and smelled of lamp oil and the faint
mineral tang of the wards the shopkeeper below had laid into the
building's foundation, wards so old they had become part of the
architecture, ticking along like a clock no one remembered winding.
Smiggles had secured a table large enough for five and a bottle of
brandy that he pronounced "barely adequate, which by Amaran standards
constitutes a triumph." He poured measures for those who wanted them,
which was everyone except Aleron, who sat at the table's end with his
hands wrapped around a cup of tea and his grey eyes focused on a point
approximately three inches to the left of Smiggles' head.

"Tomorrow," Aleron said. "We should... we should discuss what comes
next."

"We could discuss it tonight," Smiggles said. "Since we're all here
and I have brandy and the alternative is listening to Iskar describe
every meal he intends to eat this week."

"I was not going to describe every meal. Only the ones that matter."
Iskar settled into his chair with the boneless ease that was his
resting state. "Which is all of them."

"Tomorrow," Aleron repeated. His voice carried the particular quality
it took on when both eyes were focused and the echoes had thinned to
a manageable whisper: clear, measured, every word chosen with the
precision of a man who knew that the effort of clarity cost him
something and was willing to pay. "I need one more night. The transit
from Instar took... more than I had hoped. I need to be... fully
present for what I have to explain."

Aizune studied him across the table. The elf's gaunt frame seemed
diminished even from the journey's start, the hollows beneath his eyes
deeper, the white streaks in his black hair more prominent against
skin that had gone translucent at the temples, the veins visible
beneath like ink beneath parchment. The trembling in his hands had not
subsided since they left Instar, a constant low vibration that made
the surface of his tea shiver in its cup.

"Tomorrow," Aizune agreed. "Rest tonight."

Aleron's thin smile surfaced, the small sad expression that appeared
when someone showed him kindness he had not expected. "You remind
me..." he began, then stopped. His eyes drifted to the space beside
Smiggles' head again. He listened. He shook his head slightly. "No,"
he murmured, to someone who was not there. "Different. Similar, but...
different." He returned to the present with the seamless transition
that his companions were learning to recognise as the boundary between
the two worlds he inhabited simultaneously. "Thank you," he said to
Aizune. "Tomorrow."

He rose, took his tea and his quarterstaff, and withdrew to the room
he had been given at the end of the corridor, the room farthest from
Lai's, the distance between them maintained even in the arrangement of
sleeping quarters. Aizune had seen to this without being asked. It had
seemed the obvious thing to do.

From the far side of the common room, Lai watched Aleron go. She sat
cross-legged on a bench beneath the window, a position that should
have looked uncomfortable on a bench that narrow and instead looked
like something a cat would do, all compact economy and perfect balance.
Her yellow eyes followed the elf until he disappeared through the
corridor door, and the expression on her face was the one she wore
when she thought no one was watching: still, assessing, stripped of
the playful performance that was her default, revealing something
underneath that was older and more careful than the girl she pretended
to be.

"Mmmmm," she said, to no one in particular. "He is worse than he
was."

"He's tired," Smiggles said. "The demonstration for the Council, the
gate assessment, the daily drain of whatever's inside him. He needs
rest. Specific, sustained, undisturbed rest."

"He needs more than rest." Lai's voice was quiet. The accent had
thinned, the v-for-w substitution absent, and in its place was the
precise, almost professorial diction that surfaced when she forgot to
be Lai and was simply herself. "The gift is consuming him. Not
quickly, but consistently. Every use takes something that does not
grow back. He is... diminished. More than when I first saw him."

Aizune set down his cup. "You have been watching him since before we
met."

"I have been watching all of you since before you met him." She met
his gaze without evasion, her vertical pupils steady in the lamplight.
"I was in Amara before you returned from the keep. I saw you ride in
with a dead man across your saddle and items that did not belong to
this world in your saddlebags. I followed you to Blowing Rock. I
followed you back." A pause. The accent returned, faintly, at the
edges. "I told you I had been valking for a vhile. I did not say where
I started."

The common room was quiet. The lamp oil crackled. Below them, the
wards in the building's foundation ticked.

"Why?" Aizune asked.

"Because something is wrong with the world." She said it simply,
without drama, as a statement of observed fact. "I felt it. Before
any of you knew. Before the machine was activated, before the monk
died in that chamber, I felt something pressing against the borders
of this reality. A weight. A direction. Something vast and organised
and patient, looking for a door." Her yellow eyes moved from Aizune
to Smiggles to Iskar. "I came to Amara because this is where the
weight was heaviest. I found you because you walked toward it instead
of away."

Smiggles' moustache had gone still, which was its indicator that its
owner was processing information at a speed that left no surplus
attention for habitual grooming. "You felt a planar disturbance.
Before Aleron. Before the machine. Before any of it."

"I am a dragon." She said it without emphasis, the way she might have
said the sky was blue. "We feel things that smaller creatures do not.
The borders of reality are not abstractions to us. They are... walls.
And walls can crack."

"Finely ground," Smiggles said quietly. "Finely ground and deeply
alarming."

---

Tomorrow came with rain.

It fell on Amara in grey curtains that turned the cobblestones to
rivers and filled the gutters of the Market District with rushing
water that carried the day's commerce downstream in miniature: a
cabbage leaf, a scrap of paper, a copper coin that no one would miss
until the rain stopped and the accounting began. Aizune watched the
rain from the window of their common room and thought about water
falling on places that had never seen rain and worlds where rain
carried acids that dissolved stone, and he stopped that line of
thinking because it led to the machine's vision and the machine's
vision led to the place where thought became dread.

Aleron sat at the table with the tuning fork before him.

The fork lay on a square of dark cloth, pale silver against black, its
two tines catching the grey light from the window and throwing it back
as a faint shimmer that was less reflection than resonance, as though
the metal were vibrating at a frequency that light could feel but the
eye could only approximate. Aizune could hear it, or rather he could
sense it through the part of him that Minerva's gift had opened: a low,
constant hum that existed below the threshold of sound, a vibration
that corresponded to no note in any scale he knew and that pulled at
something behind his sternum with the gentle, insistent gravity of a
compass needle finding north.

"This is the key," Aleron said. He looked better this morning. Not
well, not healthy, the hollows and the tremors were permanent features
of a body that had been damaged beyond recovery, but the grey eyes
were focused and present and both of them were pointed at the same
place, which meant the echoes had thinned and his mind was, for the
moment, entirely here. "The fork is attuned to the... the vibrational
signature of the alternate prime you saw through the machine. The
world with three moons. Using the fork and my gift, I can transport
us there."

"All of us," Smiggles said. It was not a question. His hazel eyes
were fixed on the fork with the particular intensity he reserved for
objects that combined scientific elegance with existential threat.

"All of us. The transit will take... a moment. Less than a moment.
You will feel displacement, a sensation of being... unmade and remade.
It is not painful. It is..." Aleron searched for the word. His eyes
did not wander. "Disorienting. Some people are sick afterward. Most
are not."

"Comforting," Iskar said.

"I am not in the business of comfort. I am in the business of...
honesty." Aleron's thin hands framed the fork without touching it.
"There are things you must understand before we cross. The first is
the cost. Every transit drains me. I will need... time to recover
after we arrive. Hours, possibly. During that time I will be...
diminished. Physically. The echoes will be loud. I may not be fully
coherent."

"We protect you during recovery," Aizune said.

"Yes. That is the first requirement." Aleron looked at him with the
gratitude of a man who had spent decades explaining his limitations to
people who treated them as inconveniences rather than realities. "The
second is the risk. If I am damaged during or immediately after
transit, if my concentration breaks at the critical moment, there is
a... a significant probability of involuntary shifts. Random
destinations. Random planes. Everyone within..." He paused. His brow
furrowed in the expression he wore when translating internal knowledge
into external language, a process that seemed to require significant
effort. "Within thirty paces. Approximately."

"Thirty paces," Smiggles repeated. "Which includes everyone in this
room."

"Yes."

"And the destinations are genuinely random. Not weighted toward
survivable planes."

"Genuinely random. The Astral Sea, the Shadow Plane, the Abyss, the
Negative Energy Plane, a water plane, a fire plane. The mathematics
of... of Thalaman's gift do not favour the living." The words were
delivered without self-pity, with the clinical precision of a man
reporting data he had verified through decades of unwilling
experimentation. "This is why the amulet matters."

He reached beneath the collar of his robes and drew out the chain that
hung at his throat. The amulet rested against his chest, and in the
grey light of the rainy morning Aizune saw it clearly for the first
time: silver and gold wire twisted together in a pattern that was not
decorative but structural, each twist and loop a precise geometric
figure that he recognised from Cundo'elda texts on dimensional
containment, surrounding three small rubies set in a triangle. The
rubies glowed. Not with reflected light, not with the stored fire that
gems sometimes held, but with a steady luminescence that was its own
source, faint green, like sunlight seen through deep water, pulsing
with a rhythm that matched nothing Aizune could identify until he
realised, with a small shock of recognition, that it matched Aleron's
heartbeat.

"Sugnim gave this to me," Aleron said, touching the amulet with one
trembling finger. "My teacher. It suppresses the involuntary shifts.
Reduces their frequency to... to something that allows me to exist
near other people without killing them." His voice was steady. His
eyes were steady. The words were the most terrible he had spoken in
Aizune's hearing, and they were delivered as fact. "Without it, I am
a danger to everyone within thirty paces at all times. With it, the
danger is reduced to moments of extreme physical or magical stress.
The amulet does not eliminate the risk. It manages it."

"What happens if the amulet is damaged?" Smiggles asked. His voice
had the flat, quiet quality that meant he was not asking out of
academic curiosity but out of operational necessity.

"Then the shifts resume at their natural frequency. And everyone near
me should... should run."

The rain filled the silence that followed.

"Right," Smiggles said. "Right. So the operational parameters are:
transit costs you, recovery leaves you vulnerable, damage risks
catastrophic misfire, and the amulet is the only thing standing
between controlled transit and uncontrolled disaster." He pulled his
brandy flask from his waistcoat pocket, regarded it for a moment,
and put it back without drinking. "What else?"

---

The rest of the morning was logistics, and Aizune discovered that
logistics, even the logistics of travelling between realities, had
the familiar rhythm of preparation he had known since his first
expeditions into the wilds of Vand'ardhon: the careful enumeration
of what was needed, what was available, and the distance between the
two.

Disguise came first. They could not enter Ta'Puh as themselves. An
elf, a gnome, a human, and whatever Lai was would attract immediate
attention in a world where elves had been exterminated, gnomes were
unknown, and humans existed only as bred servants. They needed to
look like Orceri.

"Polymorph," Smiggles said. He had his spellbook open on the table,
a compact volume bound in dark leather with gnomish-engineered clasps,
its pages dense with Draconic notation in a hand that was precise and
small and occasionally punctuated by marginal notes that appeared to
be arguments with himself. "The working is within my capability.
Duration is limited. I can maintain it for... several hours, with
concentration. After that, the binding degrades and we revert."

"I can extend the duration," Lai said. She had taken a position at the
far end of the table, as far from Aleron as the room allowed, her
legs drawn up beneath her on the chair, her yellow eyes moving between
the speakers with the attentive stillness of something that was
accustomed to watching and was very good at it. "Draconic casting
draws from a different well than arcane. I can layer my working over
yours. The two sources reinforce each other. Combined duration should
be... adequate."

Smiggles looked at her over his spellbook. The expression on his face
was the one he wore when someone demonstrated arcane competence he had
not anticipated: a complicated mixture of professional respect and
personal affront that his monopoly on magical expertise was being
challenged. "You've done this before."

"Mmmmm." The thinking sound, low and considering. "Once or twice."

"Once or twice." He stared at her. She stared back. Her smile showed
the tips of teeth that were too sharp for an elf and exactly right
for a dragon. "Fine. We'll layer the workings. But I lead the initial
shaping. The Orceri physiology needs to be convincing at a level that
requires arcane precision, not just cosmetic alteration. Skin texture.
Bone density. Scent. We cannot walk into an orc city smelling like
an elf and a gnome."

"Agreed." Lai's voice carried the faintest edge of amusement.

"And we will need to practice moving. Orceri are larger than any of
us. The centre of gravity will be different. The stride will be
different. If Iskar walks into an Orceri settlement with a sailor's
rolling gait, every soldier in the district will notice."

"I can walk like anything," Iskar said. "I once spent thrrree weeks
pretending to be a Nond fisherman and the Nond walk like they're
permanently expecting the grrround to pitch forty degrees to
starboard."

"Can you walk like an orc?"

"Can I walk like a crrreature three times my weight with a jaw like
a brick and an opinion about it?" He straightened in his chair,
squared his shoulders, thrust his jaw forward, and adopted an
expression of aggressive self-importance that was so immediately and
recognisably wrong that Smiggles' moustache twitched and Aizune felt
the corner of his mouth pull against his will. "Yes. I believe I can."

"That's not an orc. That's a customs official."

"In my experience, the difference is negligible."

Smiggles closed his eyes. "Gears and grindstones."

---

They spent the afternoon on equipment and contingencies.

Aizune sat with his prayer journal and compiled what they knew of
Orceri society from the machine's vision: the civilised appearance,
the mana technology, the enslaved races, the military hierarchy. The
picture that emerged was incomplete but sufficient to establish the
scale of what they were walking into.

Aizune wrote this in his journal, alongside the notes on polymorph
duration, transit recovery time, and the thirty-pace danger radius.
The page was filling with the precise, interconnected observations
that were his way of imposing order on chaos, each fact connected to
the next by implication if not by ink. He paused, quill lifted, and
considered the entry he had not yet made. The one that lived in the
space between the facts.

Aleron had not spoken of destiny today. He had spoken of logistics,
of risks, of operational realities. He had been clear and honest and
precise. But beneath the precision there was something he was not
saying, something Aizune could feel through the part of him that
Minerva had opened, a weight in the elf's silence that was heavier
than the words he chose to speak. It was not deception. It was
restraint. The restraint of a man who knew more than he was sharing
and who had decided, for reasons of his own, that the knowledge was
his to carry alone.

He closed the journal without writing the observation down. Some
things were better kept where they could not be read.

---

The evening brought a different kind of preparation.

Aizune found Aleron on the roof.

The rain had stopped in the late afternoon, leaving the city washed
and gleaming, the cobblestones reflecting the first lamplight in
scattered constellations that echoed the stars emerging above. The
roof of the building above Eccentricities was flat, edged with a low
wall of dressed stone, and Aleron stood at the western edge with his
quarterstaff planted beside him and his face turned toward the bay,
where the last light of the sun was drowning in the water.

The echoes were quiet. Aizune could tell because the elf's posture
had changed, the constant low tension that lived in his shoulders
easing into something that was not relaxation but its closest
approximation: the stance of a man who was, for the moment, standing
in one world instead of several.

"May I join you?" Aizune asked.

"Please." Aleron did not turn. "The view is... The echoes thin near
water. I do not know why. Perhaps the sound. Perhaps something about
the way light moves on the surface. It quiets them."

Aizune stood beside him. The bay spread before them, dark and vast,
the harbour lights tracing the curve of the waterfront in points of
amber and gold. A ship was moving toward the harbour mouth, its sails
catching the last light, and from somewhere in the Dock District the
sound of music carried: a concertina, playing a Sabonaar reel that
was either Iskar or someone who had learned from the same tradition.

"You serve Minerva," Aleron said. The words came without preamble,
as his words often did, following a thread of thought that had been
running beneath the surface of the conversation before the
conversation began.

"I do."

"Faithfully. With... genuine devotion. Not as duty but as conviction."

"Yes."

Aleron was quiet for a moment. His grey eyes watched the harbour light
move on the water. "When I hold the fork," he said, "when I feel the
vibration of the world we are going to enter, I feel... absences. Gaps
in the fabric. Places where something should exist and does not. Most
of them I cannot identify. The gaps are too large, too alien, shapes I
have no reference for." He paused. The trembling in his hands stilled
against the quarterstaff. "But one of them I recognise. Because I
know what it feels like when Minerva is present, and I can feel, in
the vibration of that world, the space where her presence should be.
And the space is... empty."

The word fell between them like a stone into still water.

"I cannot tell you what happened. I cannot tell you whether she was
destroyed or diminished or driven away. I only know what the fork
tells me, and the fork tells me that where Minerva lives in our
reality, in that reality there is... nothing. A wound in the fabric.
A scar where something vast was removed." His voice was steady. His
hands, wrapped around the quarterstaff, were not. "I tell you this
not to cause you pain but because you must be prepared. When we cross,
you may feel her absence. You may reach for the connection and find
nothing. The place where her gift lives in you may go... quiet."

Aizune stood very still. The bay reflected the lamplight. The
concertina played its reel. The evening air was cool against his face
and carried the salt of the ocean and the particular mineral scent of
a city that had been built and destroyed and rebuilt so many times
that the stones themselves carried the memory of each iteration.

"I felt it," he said. "In the machine's vision. When I looked at the
place where the elven lands should have been and saw nothing. I felt
the absence. I thought it was shock. I thought it was the horror of
seeing... of seeing what they had done." He paused. "It was not shock."

"No."

"It was the space where she should have been. I could not name it
then. But I felt it. An emptiness that had weight." He touched the
clasp at his throat. The silver was cool. The presence behind it, the
vast and patient wisdom that he had felt since adolescence, the warmth
that came when he prayed and the clarity that came when he listened,
was there. It was here. In this world, in this reality, Minerva was
alive and whole and her light shone in Elysium and her gift flowed
through the connection that bound priest to entity across the
distances of dimensional space.

In the world they were about to enter, that light might be gone.

"How do you bear it?" he asked. "Thalaman. The entity who gave you
this gift. Do you feel him the way I feel her?"

"No." Aleron's smile was the thin, sad thing that surfaced when he
spoke of truths that had no comfort in them. "Thalaman is not... he
is not a comforting presence. He is chaos, and insanity, and the
breaking of time. I do not pray to him. I do not feel him as warmth
or wisdom or guidance. I feel him as... as a weight. A direction. A
certainty that I am being used for a purpose I did not choose and
cannot refuse." He paused. "You and I are both priests. But your
faith is a home. Mine is a road."

"And where does the road end?"

Aleron looked at the bay. The last light had gone from the water, and
the surface was dark, and the stars were emerging above Amara in the
slow processional of a sky that had not changed in a thousand years.

"I do not know," he said.

And Aizune, watching him, knew that this was not entirely true.

---

Smiggles and Iskar prepared in the way that pragmatic men prepared for
unreasonable things: by addressing the reasonable parts and leaving the
unreasonable ones for the morning.

They sat in the common room after the others had retired, Smiggles
with his spellbook open and a fresh bottle of brandy that he had
acquired through means he declined to specify, Iskar with a whetstone
and his rapier, drawing the stone along the blade in slow, rhythmic
passes that filled the room with the quiet music of metal being made
sharper.

"The disguise is the first problem," Smiggles said. He had a quill
in one hand and was making annotations in his spellbook with the rapid,
precise strokes of a man whose thoughts moved faster than his hand
could record them. "The polymorph itself is within capability. Orceri
physiology is not dramatically different from standard orc at the
structural level. Larger, cleaner, better fed, but the underlying
architecture is close enough. The challenge is duration and
consistency. If the working fails while we are in a population centre,
we revert to our natural forms in the middle of an orc city."

"How long can you hold it?"

"With Lai's reinforcement, several hours. Possibly longer. But every
additional variable introduces degradation risk. Stress. Proximity to
high-mana environments. Combat, if it comes to that, which given our
history of plans surviving contact with reality, it will."

"Then we plan for failure." Iskar set down the whetstone and examined
the rapier's edge against the lamplight. "Escape routes. Rally points.
A signal for when the disguise starts to slip, so we can withdraw
before it fails completely."

"Already thinking about it." Smiggles turned a page. "Aleron's planar
travel is our primary extraction. If we need to leave in a hurry, he
pulls us out. But that assumes he's conscious and coherent and within
thirty paces and has enough left in whatever reservoir powers his gift
to manage a transit."

"And if he cannot?"

Smiggles looked up from his spellbook. His hazel eyes were steady,
the manic analytical energy that characterised his normal mode replaced
by something quieter and more deliberate. "Then we improvise. Which
is what we always do when plans fail, because plans always fail, and
the people who survive are the ones who had a plan for the failure of
the plan."

"That is either prrrofound or circular."

"It's gnomish."

Iskar laughed. The sound was warm and genuine and filled the common
room with the particular resonance that his voice carried even when
he was not trying to carry anything, the bardic quality that lived in
his speech the way salt lived in the sea. He sheathed the rapier with
a musical note of steel against leather and leaned back in his chair.

"I have a question," he said, "that is not about logistics."

"I was afraid of that."

"The Orcerri world. The one we saw through the machine. The one we
are about to walk into." Iskar's dark eyes were steady in the
lamplight. The performance had thinned, as it did in the moments when
the man beneath the showman surfaced. "In that world, humans are
slaves. Bred for obedience. Thirty generations of... of making them
less than what they were." He paused. "I am going to see that. I am
going to walk among them. People who look like my mother and my father
and my frrriend Darielo, and they will be serving orcs with empty
eyes, and I need to know, before we go, that I can do that without
losing my head."

The name dropped into the conversation with the weight of something
that had been held for a long time. Smiggles set down his quill.

"Darielo."

"My frrriend. From the Cobalt Tide. Captured in Moamon. Seven yearrs
ago." Iskar's voice was even. His hands, resting on the table, were
still. "Every job I take. Every contact I make. Every coin I earrn.
Part of it goes toward finding him. Part of everything I do is about
getting him out."

Smiggles said nothing for a moment. Then: "You've never mentioned the
name before."

"I have never needed to. You knew I had a reason for being in Amara
that was not about the orcs or the keep or the machine. You knew
because you pay attention to the things people do not say, which is
the most annoying and the most valuable thing about you." Iskar's
smile was brief and did not reach his eyes. "His name is Darielo Voss.
He is from Cecastaa. He was the best sailorr I ever knew and the best
frrriend I ever had and he is in Moamon and I cannot reach him and
tomorrrrow I am going to walk into a world where humans are property
and I need to know that I can do that and come back and still be the
person who is going to save him."

Smiggles picked up his brandy glass. He regarded it. He drank. He set
it down.

"You can," he said. "Because you're asking the question. The people
who can't handle it are the ones who don't think to ask." He paused.
"And because you won't be alone. Whatever we see over there, we see
it together. And when we come back, and we will come back, you will
be closer to saving your friend than you were before, because the
people in this room are going to owe each other things that go beyond
money and contracts and professional obligation."

Iskar looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, a small
motion that carried the weight of something understood and accepted.

"Right," Smiggles said. "Now. Back to the question of scent
calibration in polymorphic transformation, which is less emotionally
devastating but equally important."

"You are the worrst person I know at transitions."

"I'm gnomish. We don't do transitions. We do gear changes."

---

The night deepened over Amara. The lamps burned. The harbour reflected
the stars. In the room at the end of the corridor, Aleron Windstrider
sat by his window and spoke to presences that moved through the
darkness of a room that was, to his perception, not entirely dark and
not entirely a room, the walls thinning at the edges of his vision to
show glimpses of other places and other skies and other versions of

The amulet at his throat pulsed green in the darkness. His heartbeat.
The rubies' glow. The same rhythm.

He held the tuning fork in his hands and felt the hum of a world he
was about to enter, a world whose vibration carried absences he could
feel but not explain, gaps in the fabric of reality where vast things
had been removed, a world that resonated with a wrongness so deep it
was almost a sound, almost a voice, almost a warning.

Tomorrow they would cross.

He was not afraid. Fear had burned out of him decades ago, consumed
by the same fire that had consumed his youth and his health and his
peace of mind. What remained was determination, and determination did
not require fear. It required only the willingness to move forward
when every rational calculation argued for retreat.

In the room farthest from his, Lai lay in the darkness and listened
to the city breathe and felt, at the very edge of her perception, the
faint wrongness of his presence, the stretched quality of the air
between their rooms, the coldness that was not temperature but
something else, something that her dragon senses registered as the
proximity of a boundary that should not be as thin as it was.

She did not sleep. Dragons slept lightly, and tonight the thinness
of the world kept her awake.

In the common room, Smiggles von Hoofel fell asleep over his spellbook
with his moustache resting in an empty brandy glass, which Iskar
discovered when he returned from the privy and which he chose, after
a moment of consideration, not to correct. Some images were too
valuable to disturb.

And above them all, the two moons crossed the clearing sky. Silver
Io'el, full and bright. Pale gold Randir'el, waning at the edge of
its long cycle, a sliver of light against the dark.

Between them, the empty space where a third moon did not hang.

Not yet.
