# Chapter Seven
## Blowing Rock

The road south from Amara was kind.

After the Steppes, after the dead stone and the dead air and the dead
men in the chambers below the keep, the kindness of it was almost
difficult to accept. The land greened within an hour of the city gates,
the hardpan giving way to cultivated fields where early wheat stood
knee-high and shivered in a breeze that carried the scent of turned
earth and rain-washed grass and the distant, clean sweetness of
wildflowers whose names Aizune did not know but whose colours he
catalogued with the quiet pleasure of a man who had spent too many days
surrounded by grey. The road was packed dirt, well-maintained, rutted by
cart wheels and softened by the passage of feet and hooves over years of
honest use. Farmsteads dotted the landscape: low stone walls, thatched
roofs, kitchen gardens where beans climbed their poles and chickens
complained about the morning with the passionate indignation that
chickens brought to all things.

It was, by any reasonable measure, a beautiful day. The sky was high and
pale and enormous, the two moons still visible as ghosts above the
western horizon, silver and gold, fading into blue as the sun climbed.
A hawk circled above the wheat fields with the lazy patience of a
predator who had all day and knew it.

Aizune watched the hawk and thought about a world where hawks did not
circle. A world where the fields were tended by creatures that had once
been human, whose eyes were down and whose shoulders were curved inward,
whose obedience was so complete that cruelty was unnecessary. He thought
about the three moons in that alien sky, the third one small and
reddish, hanging where empty space should be. He thought about the army
marching in columns that stretched to the horizon. And then he stopped
thinking about it, deliberately, the way a man sets down a weight he
has been carrying too long, because the morning was kind and the road
was kind and Minerva's light was warm on his face, and there were limits
to what the mind could hold before it began to crack.

Smiggles rode beside him in companionable silence, which was unusual
for Smiggles, whose default state involved commentary on everything
within range of his senses and several things that were not. The gnome
had his satchel on the saddle before him, his spellbook wedged beneath
it as supplementary cushioning, and his eyes were on the road ahead
with the unfocused quality of a mind that was working on something
large and had temporarily suspended all smaller operations. His
moustache, freshly waxed that morning, caught the sunlight at its
upward-curling tips and projected an air of jaunty confidence that the
rest of his expression did not support.

After a mile of silence, he spoke.

"I've been thinking about the wrist device."

"The one from the spy's body," Aizune said.

"The crystal in it. The way it caught the lamplight in the room above
Eccentricities. The reflection was wrong. Too blue. As if the crystal
were filtering light through properties that don't exist in our
spectrum." He paused. One hand rose to adjust a moustache tip, the
motion automatic and meditative. "That doesn't calibrate with any
mineral I know. And I know most of them. Grew up around lens-grinders,
remember. I could identify a flawed quartz by the way it bent a candle
flame before I could read."

"What does it suggest?"

"That wherever they quarried the crystal, or grew it, or manufactured
it, the fundamental properties of the material are different. Not just
the craftsmanship. The substance itself. It behaves as though the laws
that govern mineral formation in our world were written differently in
theirs." He fell quiet again, his hazel eyes narrowing against the
morning light. "I need the Council's equipment. I need someone with a
proper analysis rig and a mana spectrometer and about six hours of
uninterrupted silence. Then I'll know."

"You will have it. After Blowing Rock."

Smiggles glanced at him. The glance contained an entire conversation:
the acknowledgement that returning the monk's body was the right thing
to do, the simultaneous impatience with the delay it imposed, and the
grudging acceptance that Aizune's moral compass, however inconvenient,
was not something the gnome wished to argue with on a morning this
beautiful.

"After Blowing Rock," he agreed.

Behind them, Iskar was singing.

It was not a performance. It was the quiet, half-conscious humming of a
man whose body was on a horse and whose mind was somewhere on the water,
a Sabonaar melody that rose and fell with the rhythm of the road and
carried the salt and distance of harbours the singer had not visited in
years. The song had no words, or rather the words had been worn away by
repetition until only the shape of them remained, vowels and breath and
the ghost of a lyric that might have been about the sea or a woman or
both, because in the Sabonaar tradition these were frequently the same
subject.

Remular's body rode behind him, wrapped in the bedroll, secured with
rope, lighter than silence.

And twenty paces behind Iskar, Lai walked.

She had appeared at the city gates that morning with the inevitability
of weather, standing where the road began its southward curve, eating
another apple, as though she had known precisely when they would depart
and had arranged her breakfast accordingly. Smiggles had said "Oy" with
a weariness that suggested he had expected this and was already
exhausted by it. Aizune had said nothing. Iskar had said "Good
morrning" with a warmth that suggested he had also expected this and
was not exhausted by it at all.

She walked now with the same effortless, ground-eating stride she had
maintained through the Steppes, her wild green-black hair lifting in
the breeze, her yellow eyes moving across the landscape with an
attention that was more than casual. She watched the fields. She watched
the farmsteads. She watched the hawk above the wheat with a particular
stillness, her head tilting at a precise angle, her vertical pupils
narrowing to slits, and for a moment she was so motionless that the
morning air seemed to thicken around her, the way air thickens around
a held breath.

Then she smiled, shook her hair back, and resumed walking, and the
moment passed.

Aizune watched this and filed it beside every other thing about the
young woman that did not fit the shape she claimed to fill. The list
was growing longer. It did not yet have a name.

---

Blowing Rock appeared in the early afternoon, rising from the gentle
country south of Amara like something that had grown there rather than
been built: a scattering of stone houses along a single main road,
kitchen gardens and low walls and a market square no larger than
Aldorph's office, where a woman sold vegetables from a cart and two
men argued about a fence with the leisurely conviction of people who
had been arguing about fences for years and expected to continue. Goats
stood on a hillside and regarded the approaching riders with the blank
philosophical stare that goats directed at all things, sentient and
otherwise, as though everything in the world were equally and mildly
disappointing.

It was a village that existed for no reason other than to exist, and
the simplicity of this was, after everything, quietly magnificent.

Smiggles surveyed the settlement from his saddle with the expression
of a man evaluating a gemstone and finding it small but without visible
flaws. "Ticking along nicely," he said. "Good walls. Clean market.
Nobody looks hungry. Whoever runs this place knows what they're doing,
or at least knows enough to stay out of the way and let the place run
itself."

"The monastery runs it," Aizune said. "Blowing Rock has been under the
protection of the Champions of Pala'dinae for over a century."

"Pala'dinae." Smiggles' nose wrinkled, the involuntary reaction of a
man whose relationship with organised religion was characterised by a
professional respect for divine magic and a personal allergy to divine
authority. "Paladins. Lots of armour and moral certainty, I expect."

"They are good people, Smiggles."

"I didn't say they weren't. I said they'd have lots of armour and moral
certainty. These are observations, not criticisms." He paused. "Though
in my experience, moral certainty and I tend to disagree about most
things within the first five minutes."

Iskar had stopped singing. He brought his horse alongside theirs and
looked at the village with the quick, assessing eyes of a man who
evaluated every new place by its exits, its vantage points, and the
quality of its taverns. "Small," he said. "Quiet. No docks, which is a
shame. But the hill is something."

The hill was indeed something. It rose behind the village to the north,
broad and gentle-shouldered, its slopes covered with grass that was
greener and thicker than the surrounding fields, as though the hill
received more rain or better soil or the simple accumulated blessing of
having been cared for by dedicated hands for longer than the village
below it remembered. At its summit, the monastery: pale stone walls
catching the afternoon sun, a bell tower visible above the treeline,
and the sense, even at this distance, of a place that had been built
with intention and maintained with love.

"We go up," Aizune said. "Lai, you are welcome to wait in the village
if you prefer."

"Mmmmm." She had come to stand beside his horse, her head barely level
with the stirrup, her yellow eyes moving from the village to the
monastery and back with the unhurried assessment of someone cataloguing
a new landscape. "I vould like to see the monastery, if that is
allowed. My teacher spoke of Pala'dinae's halls. He said the gardens
were worth the climb."

"Your teacher knew about a monastery in Blowing Rock?" Smiggles asked,
and the question carried the particular edge of a man adding another
entry to a very long list.

"He knew about many things." She smiled. The smile was warm and
uncomplicated and entirely convincing, and Aizune did not believe it
for a moment.

"You may come," he said. "But this is a place of grief and gratitude.
We are returning a man to his home. I ask that you respect the
solemnity of what we are here to do."

The yellow eyes met his, and for a moment the amusement in them
quieted, replaced by something that he could not name but that felt,
briefly, like understanding. "I vill," she said. "I understand what it
means to bring the dead home."

They rode up the hill.

---

The Valiant Halls of the Dragon were not what Aizune had expected.

He had expected stone and steel, the heavy martial architecture of a
paladin order: barracks, training yards, walls built for defence. He had
expected the fortress-monastery tradition of the Tiovinian crusader
holds, where faith was expressed through fortification and the line
between temple and military installation was drawn with a sword.

What he found was a garden.

The monastery's outer walls were pale limestone, warm in the afternoon
light, their surfaces smooth and unadorned except for a single carved
relief above the main gate: two dragon heads flanking a longsword, the
blade pointed downward, the cross-guard spread like wings. Below the
relief, the gate stood open. Beyond it, the interior courtyard spread
in a design that stopped Aizune at the threshold and held him there for
three full breaths.

It was Ejiin. The courtyard was an Ejiin garden transported whole from
the Lands of Honor and planted in the soil of the Sabon Peninsula, and
the improbability of this was exceeded only by its beauty. A stream ran
through the courtyard's centre, its water clear and cold, channelled
over smooth stones in a course that was too precise to be natural and
too graceful to be entirely artificial. Bridges of pale wood arched
over the stream at intervals, their railings carved with patterns that
suggested flowing water and open sky. Trees had been planted along the
stream's banks: willows and maples and a species Aizune did not
recognise, their branches trained and guided over decades into shapes
that framed the sky above them in arches of green and gold. Gravel
paths wound between beds of moss and stone, each arrangement placed
with a care that transformed the absence of flowers into a statement
rather than a lack. The sound of the stream filled the courtyard with a
music that was constant and clean and required nothing of the listener
except presence.

At the courtyard's far end, where the stream's course curved toward a
stone basin, a statue stood.

It was marble, white as bone, twice the height of a man. Pala'dinae. The
entity was depicted as a powerful human figure in elaborate plate
armour, a longsword held before him with both hands, the blade's point
resting on the ground, the stance of a warrior at rest who had not
forgotten how to fight. His face was strong and calm, the features
those of a man in his middle years, neither young nor old, carrying the
particular quality of authority that comes not from power but from the
willingness to bear its consequences. And from his shoulders, worked
into the armour with a subtlety that revealed itself only as the eye
travelled upward, draconic features emerged: the faintest suggestion of
scales along the gorget, a ridge of bone at the brow that was not quite
human, hands that gripped the longsword with fingers just slightly too
long, too strong, too precisely articulated. On either side of the
statue, carved in the same white marble, dragon heads rose from the
base on long serpentine necks, their jaws closed, their eyes watchful.
Behind the statue, two stone lances crossed in an X, their points
aimed at the sky.

It was beautiful. It was also, in a way that Aizune felt in the deep
place where Minerva's gift resided, true. Not true in the factual sense
of historical accuracy, but true in the way that devotion was true: an
expression of something felt so deeply that the stone itself seemed to
breathe with it.

Behind him, Lai had gone very still.

He turned. She stood in the gateway, one hand resting on the stone
frame, her yellow eyes fixed on the statue with an expression he had
not seen on her face before. The playfulness was gone. The amusement
was gone. She was looking at the marble figure of Pala'dinae with the
draconic features worked into its armour and the dragon heads flanking
its base, and her face held something that was raw and private and not
meant for anyone else to see. It lasted only a moment. Then she blinked,
and the mask resettled, and she was Lai again, the cheerful girl with
the green skin and the easy smile, and she said "Mmmmm, the gardens
are lovely," in a voice that was almost steady.

Aizune said nothing. But he added the moment to his list.

---

The Lord of Justice met them in the courtyard.

He came from the eastern cloister with the stride of a man who had been
told visitors were approaching and had chosen to meet them himself
rather than send a subordinate, and the stride itself told Aizune a
great deal about the kind of leader he was. It was not hurried. It was
not slow. It was the walk of someone who gave each moment its due
weight and did not rush grief or welcome, because both deserved the
dignity of measured steps.

Lionous Harker was a Madronian man, which meant he was paler than the
Sabonaar majority of the surrounding countryside, his skin the colour
of birch bark, his features broader and heavier than the sharp, angular
lines of the Sabonaar. His hair was blonde, a deep gold darkened by sun
and years, pulled back from his face and tied at the nape of his neck
in a simple cord. His eyes were blue. Not the pale, washed blue of
northern skies, but a deep, rich blue, the colour of the ocean at
depth, and they told of two things simultaneously and with equal force:
loss and devotion. He had lost something, or someone, or many someones,
and the loss had not broken him but had instead been absorbed into the
structure of his faith the way a tree absorbs a wound, growing around
it, incorporating it, becoming stronger at the point where it had been
cut. He wore armour, a half-plate of polished steel over a surcoat of
deep blue and white, Pala'dinae's colours, and the armour fit him the
way armour fits a man who wears it every day and has forgotten it is
not his skin. A longsword hung at his hip. He looked younger than his
forty-five years, the lines of his face smoothed by the particular
vitality that sustained men whose faith was not merely believed but
lived.

He stopped before them. He placed his right fist against his left palm
and bowed, a short, precise inclination of the head and shoulders that
was neither casual nor courtly but something between: the greeting of a
man whose order valued respect above formality.

"Welcome to the Valiant Halls," he said. His voice was deep, measured,
carrying the warmth of genuine hospitality and the weight of an
authority he had earned rather than inherited. "I am Lionous Harker,
Lord of Justice of the Champions of Blowing Rock. You honour us with
your visit."

"Lord Harker." Aizune returned the bow, adapting it to the Ya'sehrim
style, his hand touching his chest above the Minerva clasp. "I am
Aizune Silverwind, priest of Minerva, of the Cundo'elda tradition. My
companions: Smiggles von Hoofel, a mage of Zhonhylite. Iskar, a bard
and swordsman of Amara."

Smiggles offered a short nod that managed to convey both respect and
the uncomfortable awareness that he was standing in the headquarters
of an institution whose moral certainty exceeded his own by several
orders of magnitude. Iskar removed his hat, held it against his chest,
and bowed with a depth and sincerity that Aizune had not seen from him
before, the theatrical flourish stripped away, the gesture genuine.

"And Lai," Aizune added. "A companion of the road."

Lai offered a small bow of her own, her yellow eyes lowered, her
manner quiet and contained in a way that was so unlike her usual
performance that Smiggles noticed and his eyebrows rose a fraction.

Lionous acknowledged each of them with a measured look that saw more
than it appraised. Then his gaze moved past them, to the horse standing
at the courtyard's edge, and to the wrapped shape secured across its
back.

The blue eyes changed.

"You carry a body," he said.

"We do." Aizune stepped aside so that Lionous could see the bedroll
clearly. "We found him in a ruin east of Amara, in the Steppes of
Broken Stone. An abandoned keep occupied by orcs. Beneath the keep,
in chambers older than the keep itself, he lay." He paused. The next
words required care, because they carried the weight of a man's life
and death and the manner of his dying, and Aizune would not deliver
them carelessly. "His robes mark him as a monk. His bearing, even in
death, marks him as a man of discipline and faith. We could not
identify him, but the keep is in the region where orc raids have been
striking the eastern trade routes, and a man of Pala'dinae who went to
investigate such raids and did not return seemed likely to belong to a
house of Pala'dinae. We brought him here."

Lionous Harker stood very still. The stillness was not the absence of
movement but the presence of something held back, something enormous
and controlled, the discipline of a man who had learned to meet grief
with the same measured stride he brought to all things.

"Remular," he said.

The name fell into the courtyard's silence like a stone into still
water, and the ripples it sent through the Lord of Justice's composure
were visible to anyone who cared to look. His jaw tightened. His eyes,
that deep rich blue, grew brighter with a moisture he did not attempt
to hide and did not allow to fall. His right hand, resting at his side,
closed into a fist and opened again, slowly, the fingers straightening
one by one, as though he were releasing something he had been holding
for a very long time.

"His name was Remular," he said again, and the second time the name
carried the full weight of what it meant to him: not merely a name but
a life, a boy found and raised and taught and loved and sent into the
world and lost. "He left us four months ago. He had heard reports of
orc raids on the eastern trade routes and believed they concealed
something deeper. He was..." Lionous paused. "He was the kind of man
who walked toward darkness because he believed the light he carried was
meant for the places that needed it most."

He crossed to the horse. His hands, large and calloused and gentle in
the way that strong hands are gentle when they choose to be, untied
the ropes that held the bedroll. He lifted Remular's body, and the
lightness of it, the terrible absence of weight, made his arms stiffen
and his breath catch, because he understood what that lightness meant.
He had seen drained bodies before. He knew what left a man weighing
less than his own clothing.

"How?" he asked. His voice was steady. The steadiness cost him.

Aizune told him. Not everything. Not the machine, not the vision, not
the three moons and the alien sky and the civilised orcs with their
enslaved races and their impossible technology. Those truths were too
large for this moment and too dangerous for a courtyard where grief
was doing its necessary work. He told Lionous about the chambers
beneath the keep, the ancient construction, the presence of an
incorporeal undead that dwelt in the darkness below, and the manner
in which such a creature killed: the draining of life, of warmth, of
substance, until what remained was a husk that weighed nothing and
wore an expression of the last thing its owner had seen.

"His face was peaceful," Aizune said, and this was true, and the truth
of it mattered. "Whatever he saw in his final moments, it was not the
thing that killed him. He died looking at something else. Something
that gave him peace."

Lionous held the body in his arms for a long moment. Then he turned and
carried it toward the eastern cloister, and the three heroes and the
girl with the yellow eyes followed in silence, through a corridor of
pale stone lined with doors that stood open onto small, clean cells
where the paladin-monks of Blowing Rock lived their ordered lives. The
corridor was quiet. Half the cells were empty. Half the monastery's
strength, Aizune remembered, was away on a great crusade to the Ancient
South, and the emptiness of those cells spoke of an institution that
gave its people to the world without reservation and trusted Pala'dinae
to bring them home.

Lionous laid Remular on a bed in a cell at the corridor's end. The
cell was simple: a bed, a desk, a shelf holding a prayer text and a
wooden figure of Pala'dinae no larger than a man's hand. A window looked
out over the courtyard garden, and through it the sound of the stream
entered the room with the gentle persistence of water that did not
know how to stop.

"He grew up in this room," Lionous said. He straightened the robes,
smoothed the dark Sabonaar hair back from the withered face, adjusted
the hands so that they lay folded across the chest. The gestures were
tender and precise, the gestures of a man performing a ritual he had
performed before and hoped never to perform again and knew he would.
"He came to us as a boy. Nine years old. He had been living on the
streets of Amara for two years, stealing food, sleeping in doorways.
Before that, he had been taken by slavers from Moamon."

The word landed in the room with a weight that made Iskar's eyes
change. Moamon. The dark city. The place where Darielo Voss had
disappeared and had not come back. Aizune saw the shift in the bard's
expression, the momentary tightening around his jaw, and filed it as
he filed all things: carefully, for later.

"He escaped the slavers before they could bring him south," Lionous
continued. "A boy of seven, alone in the countryside, walking north
because north was away from the people who had taken him. He walked for
weeks. He arrived here barefoot, starving, and furious. The fury was
the first thing I noticed about him. Not fear. Fury. As though the
world had tried to break him and he had decided, at seven years old,
that the world could go to the Void." A smile crossed Lionous's face,
brief and bright and painful. "He was the angriest child I had ever
met. And the bravest."

"He grew up here?"

"He grew up here. We fed him. We clothed him. We taught him to read,
to fight, to pray. The fury did not leave him. It simply changed shape.
It became purpose. He trained harder than any student I have known. He
became a monk of Pala'dinae, not because someone told him to, but because
he had decided that the world contained slavers and monsters and
darkness, and that someone had to stand against these things, and that
he was that someone." Lionous looked down at the body on the bed.
"He was right."

The silence that followed was the silence of a room that held both
death and devotion and found them, for once, not in opposition but in
communion. The stream murmured through the window. The afternoon light
fell across the cell in bars of gold that moved with the wind.

"Lord Harker," Aizune said. "We are grateful for the chance to return
him to you. It was the least we could do."

Lionous turned to face them. The grief was still in his eyes, would be
in his eyes for days, perhaps longer, but alongside it there was
something else: a gratitude so large and so genuine that it transformed
his bearing, straightened his shoulders, settled into his expression
with the weight of a vow.

"You found him," Lionous said. "You, strangers, with no obligation to
his memory and no knowledge of his name, found him in a dark place
and brought him home. You could have left him. Many would have. You
did not." He placed his fist against his palm and bowed, deeper than
before, the bow of a Lord of Justice acknowledging a debt that rank
and title could not measure. "Pala'dinae teaches that the measure of a
soul is not in the battles it wins but in the kindnesses it offers
when no reward is expected. You have shown me the truth of that
teaching today."

He straightened. "I make you this pledge, and I make it before Pala'dinae
and in the presence of Remular's memory, which is the most sacred
witness I can offer. If any of you, in the course of your journeys,
should find yourselves wounded or weary or in need of sanctuary,
bring yourselves here. Bring them here, to these halls. And I will
call upon Pala'dinae with everything I have and everything I am
to heal what can be healed and shelter what needs sheltering. The
walls of the Valiant Halls have stood for over a century, and no
darkness has breached them. This is not an offer I make lightly.
The protection of Pala'dinae's house is his greatest gift and his
heaviest burden, and I do not extend it to the unworthy." His blue
eyes moved across them: Aizune, Smiggles, Iskar. "You are not
unworthy."

The words settled into the room with the finality of stone laid upon
stone, a foundation, a promise, a weight that would not shift. Aizune
felt them land in the place where Minerva's gift resided, and the
resonance they produced was warm and true.

"Thank you, Lord Harker," he said. "We accept your pledge, and we
honour it."

"Then honour it by staying the night." The warmth returned to Lionous's
voice, the grief receding, not disappearing but finding its place
alongside the duties of hospitality that his order demanded. "You have
ridden half a day. You are tired. We have food, and beds, and a garden
that was designed by a man who believed that beauty was a form of
prayer. Rest here. And in the morning, if you are willing, tell me
what you found beneath that keep. Not all of it, if you are not ready.
But enough for me to understand what killed my son."

"He was your son?" Iskar asked. The question was quiet, stripped of
performance, carrying only the simple need to know.

Lionous looked at the body on the bed. "Not by blood. By choice. Which
is, in my experience, the stronger bond."

---

They stayed the night.

The monastery provided food, plain and good: bread, cheese, roasted
vegetables, a stew of lamb and herbs that Smiggles pronounced "finely
ground, for a military kitchen" and ate two bowls of. Beds were offered
in the guest cells along the western cloister, small and clean and
warmed by stones heated in the kitchen hearth and wrapped in cloth.
Iskar's cell had a window that looked out over the garden, and after
supper he sat on the sill with his concertina on his knee and played
a melody so quiet that it barely reached beyond the stone walls, a
Sabonaar lament for the dead that had no words because the dead did not
need words, only the knowledge that someone remembered them.

The music drifted through the cloister. In his own cell, Aizune heard
it, and set down his prayer journal, and listened.

In the courtyard below, Lai sat on a bench beside the stream.

She had been quiet all evening. She had eaten little, spoken less, and
spent most of the meal watching Lionous with those yellow eyes, her
expression unreadable, her usual commentary absent. When Smiggles had
made a remark about the quality of the monastery's bread, she had
smiled, but the smile had not reached her eyes. When Iskar had told a
brief story about a Sabonaar harbour tradition for honouring the dead,
throwing a lit candle into the outgoing tide, she had listened with
a stillness that was not her usual stillness, the alert predatory
attention of something watching prey, but a different kind entirely.
Quieter. Heavier.

Aizune found her there when he came down to walk the garden before
sleep. She was sitting with her legs drawn up, her arms wrapped around
her knees, her chin resting on her forearms, watching the water move
over the stones with the fixed attention of someone looking at
something that was not the water.

He almost did not speak. The moment felt private, and the woman in it
felt, for the first time since he had met her, genuinely vulnerable.
But he was a priest, and the instinct to offer presence to those who
carried weight was as much a part of him as the Minerva clasp at his
throat.

"The gardens are worth the climb," he said. "Your teacher was right."

She did not look up. "My teacher vas right about many things. Not all
of them. But this one, yes."

"You were affected by the statue."

A pause. The stream murmured. "It is a very fine statue."

"It is. But that is not what I asked."

The yellow eyes found him. In the darkness of the courtyard, lit only
by the monastery's lanterns and the faint silver of the moon through
the willow branches, her eyes caught the light and held it with that
reflective gleam that was not elven, that belonged to something that
saw in spectrums other eyes could not reach. She looked at him for a
long time. Then she looked back at the water.

"The man in the statue is shown with dragon features," she said. "Horns.
Scales. The hands. Did you notice the hands?"

"I did."

"My teacher told me stories about Pala'dinae. He said that Pala'dinae came
from a world where dragons were gods, where the greatest dragon of all
chose to walk among mortals as a man because he believed that strength
meant nothing without compassion." She paused. "To take a smaller
shape because you choose to, not because you must. To walk among
creatures who cannot understand what you are, because you believe they
are worth walking among. That is..." She stopped. Her arms tightened
around her knees. "That is a very specific kind of courage."

Aizune sat down on the bench beside her. The stone was cool beneath
him. The stream's voice filled the silence between them with something
that was not silence but was not speech either, a third thing, patient
and continuous.

"It is," he said.

She said nothing more. After a time, she unfolded herself from the
bench, wished him good night in a voice that had no accent at all, and
disappeared into the guest cloister with a silence that no elf and no
human should have been able to achieve.

Aizune sat alone in the garden and listened to the water and to Iskar's
concertina, distant and fading, and to the night sounds of a village
that slept beneath the protection of men and women who had dedicated
their lives to standing between darkness and the things it wanted to
consume.

He touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. The silver was cool against
his fingers. He closed his eyes.

In his mind, two images sat beside each other with the uncomfortable
intimacy of truths that had not yet been introduced: a world with three
moons where everything he loved had been destroyed, and a garden where
a stream ran over stones and a dead man had been brought home and a
girl with yellow eyes had spoken about dragons and courage with a
sincerity that frightened him more than anything in the chambers below
the keep.

The moon, the silver moon, Io'el the Night Star, hung above the
monastery in its ancient circuit, patient and whole. Beside it, Randir'el
wandered its golden path. Between them, the sky was empty.

Two moons. As it should be.

He went to bed. In the morning, they would ride east, toward Instar
and the answers that waited there, and the world would grow larger and
stranger and more dangerous with every mile. But tonight, in a garden
built by people who believed that beauty was a form of prayer, a dead
man was home, and the living had been promised that death was not the
end, and this was enough.

For one night, this was enough.
