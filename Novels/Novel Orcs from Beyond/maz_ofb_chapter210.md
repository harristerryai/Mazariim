# Chapter Ten
## A Place Already Destroyed

He felt it the way the blind feel weather: not through
any single sense but through the aggregate of senses that
remained when the obvious ones had been stripped away, the
way seventy years of fracture had taught him to perceive
the shape of reality not as a surface to be observed but
as a fabric to be read with the fingers of a consciousness
that had been torn open and sewn shut and torn open again
until the stitching itself became a kind of sight, and what
Aleron Windstrider felt in the days following Winderbourgh,
lying in the room above Eccentricities with his quarterstaff
across his chest and the amulet pulsing green at his throat
and the echoes murmuring their ceaseless overlay of adjacent
worlds, was a wound in the east that was growing.

Not quickly. Not the sharp engineered violence of the
Orceri crossing at Winderbourgh, the forty individual
boundary punctures that had woken him like a blade to the
sternum. This was slower. Deeper. The sensation of something
vast being forced through something that had already been
damaged, the way water forces itself through a crack in a
dam: patient, relentless, the pressure constant and the
crack widening by increments too small to measure and too
large to ignore.

He had been feeling it since Ta'Puh. Since the return
crossing, since the transit that had cost him everything
and left him draped across a saddle with his consciousness
reduced to courtesy. The wound in the east had been there
before the crossing, before Winderbourgh, before the
heroes had ever heard the word Orceri. It had been there
for fifty years, waiting.

Gaal'Tarak.

The name arrived in his mind with the particular clarity
that Thalaman's gift reserved for the things it most
wanted him to know: not whispered, not shouted, but simply
present, the way his own name was present, the way the
colour of the sky was present, a fact so fundamental that
questioning it would require questioning the architecture
of perception itself. He had felt it as a compass needle
in his chest since Fort Hope. He had followed it to the
keep, to Amara, to the heroes. He had followed it across
the dimensional boundary to Ta'Puh and back. And now the
needle was pointing east with a precision that stripped
away every remaining ambiguity and left only the
destination and the knowledge that the destination had
been waiting for him since before he was born.

He told them on a Starday evening when the harbour bell
rang the eighth hour and the smell of Yella's cooking
rose through the floorboards and the common room above
Eccentricities held the golden light of oil lamps and
the particular warmth of a space that had become, through
the accumulation of shared meals and shared silences and
the slow building of trust that was the real architecture
of their fellowship, something that none of them would
have called home and all of them would have recognised
as its nearest equivalent.

Aizune sat by the window with his prayer journal open.
Smiggles occupied the largest chair, his boots on the
table, the mana engine on the surface beside him trailing
a disassembled casing plate and three copper filaments
that he had extracted with the focused delicacy of a
surgeon and the muttered commentary of a man who found
the engineering simultaneously impressive and personally
offensive. Iskar sat with his concertina on his knee,
not playing, his fingers resting on the bellows in the
posture of a musician whose hands sought the instrument
the way other men's hands sought a glass or a coin: for
comfort, for the familiar weight of a thing that
connected the present to the self. Lai was absent. She
had been absent frequently since the return, her
movements unexplained, her yellow eyes appearing in
doorways and disappearing from rooftops with the
unpredictable rhythm of a creature whose relationship
with human schedules was more performance than practice.

Aleron stood.

The standing was deliberate. It was the planned sequence
his companions had seen before: staff planted, weight
transferred, body ascending one joint at a time, the
careful engineering of a man whose physical resources
required management rather than assumption. He gained
his feet. He steadied. The tremor in his hands was
constant now, not the intermittent flutter of fatigue
but the permanent vibration of a body whose internal
calibration had been reset by the Winderbourgh transit
to a frequency that would not quiet, and the not-
quieting was data, and the data said that the baseline
was lower again, and the lowering was not recovery but
trajectory.

Both eyes were focused. The grey irises held the room.
The echoes were present, murmuring at the margins of
his perception, translucent figures drifting through the
walls in overlapping layers that he had long since
learned to navigate by ignoring, but the central
channel was clear, the lucid window open, and what he
said through it carried the authority of a man who had
been carrying a secret for weeks and had decided that
the weight of the secret exceeded the weight of the
fear that had kept it contained.

"I know where they will come."

The room changed. Not the physical room. The quality of
attention. Smiggles' fingers stopped moving on the
copper filament. Iskar's hands stilled on the
concertina. Aizune looked up from the journal.

"Fifty years ago," Aleron said, "something destroyed a
dwarven citadel in the Kirak Mountains. The citadel was
called Gaal'Tarak. It was a major hold, well-engineered,
well-defended. And in a single day it was destroyed.
Not besieged. Not conquered. Destroyed. The dwarves who
survived described the sky tearing open and fire pouring
through and the mountain itself cracking as though the
stone had been struck by something that did not obey the
laws that stone was built to withstand." He paused. The
pause was not for effect. It was for the echo that
drifted across his perception, a fragment of a voice
from a reality where the citadel still stood, where the
sky had not torn, where the dwarves were sleeping behind
walls that would never fall, and the fragment was
beautiful and he let it pass. "Thousands of dwarven
refugees fled south into Moamon. Two years later, the
tent city they had built was destroyed by fire. The
Zebulbans were suspected. The citadel was never rebuilt.
The cause of the original disaster was never explained."

"I know of Gaal'Tarak," Aizune said. "The refugees are
referenced in the Liberators' records. A humanitarian
crisis that the Merchant Nations failed to address. But
the cause..."

"The cause is a wound," Aleron said. "A wound in the
boundary between this reality and every other. The
dimensional fabric there is not merely thin. It is
damaged. Scarred. As though something tore through it
with catastrophic force and the tearing left a weakness
that has never healed." He held the tuning fork. The
pale silver caught the lamplight. The two tines vibrated
with a frequency that was felt in the teeth rather than
heard, the habitual hum that had been Aleron's constant
companion since Instar's mages had calibrated it to
Ta'Puh's vibrational signature. "I can feel it through
this. The scar is... resonant. It responds to the fork
the way a wound responds to pressure. And the Orceri
can feel it too."

Smiggles set down the copper filament. The gesture was
precise, the filament placed parallel to the casing
plate with the unconscious orderliness of a man whose
hands organised things while his mind processed other
things entirely. "They are using it."

"They are building a portal on the other side." Aleron's
voice was steady. The steadiness was not calm but
discipline, the particular quality of a man who had
decided that his companions deserved the truth delivered
without the distortion of his fear. "The weakness in the
boundary provides the foundation. They do not need to
force a breach through intact dimensional fabric. They
only need to widen a crack that already exists. And the
crack is widening. I can feel it accelerating. The
boundary at Gaal'Tarak is thinner today than it was
yesterday, and tomorrow it will be thinner still."

"How long," Smiggles said. The words were not a question.
They were a demand for the number that would determine
everything that followed.

"Weeks. Perhaps less. The acceleration is not constant.
It surges. Each surge thins the boundary further. When
the thinning reaches a critical threshold, the Orceri
will punch through. Not forty soldiers. Not a probe."
He looked at them. The grey eyes carried the weight of
Giganzalt's testimony, of eleven conquered worlds, of
sky citadels and leviathans and armies that had reduced
civilisations to resources. "An invasion force. The kind
that has never been defeated."

The room held the information the way a vessel holds
water that exceeds its capacity: with visible strain at
every edge.

Iskar spoke first. "Then we go to Gaal'Tarak."

"Yes."

"And do what, precisely?" Smiggles' voice was clipped.
The gnomish craft idiom surfaced, each consonant struck
clean, the analytical engine behind the moustache running
at the speed that his companions recognised as full
engagement: not panic but the particular intensity of a
mind that processed threat by disassembling it into
components and evaluating each component for
vulnerability. "We cannot hold a dwarven citadel against
an invasion force. We cannot close a dimensional wound
with mid-tier spellcraft. We cannot out-fight a
civilisation that has conquered eleven worlds. What can
we do at Gaal'Tarak that we cannot do from Amara with
better supply lines and thicker walls?"

"I can seal it."

The words were simple. Three words. Aleron delivered
them with the directness that his companions had learned
to associate with his most capable state, the lucid
clarity that emerged when both eyes focused and the
echoes quieted and the man behind the fractures spoke
without interference.

"The gift Thalaman gave me is not only transit. It is
not only perception. It is the ability to interact with
the dimensional boundary itself, to feel its shape and
its strength and its weakness, and at Gaal'Tarak, where
the boundary is already wounded, I can pour the gift
into the wound and seal it." He paused. One eye drifted
toward something that was not there. Came back. "I
have been preparing for this since the pyramid. Since
Sugnim taught me what the gift was for. Not travel. Not
escape. Repair. The boundary was torn. It must be
mended. And Thalaman's gift is the thread."

Smiggles studied him. The hazel eyes were sharp and
the sharpness was not scepticism but assessment, the
precise evaluation of a man who respected evidence and
who was reading, in the tremor of Aleron's hands and
the translucence of his temples and the careful
economy of his words, the evidence that the elf was
not telling them everything and that the everything
he was withholding was the cost.

"What does sealing it require?" Smiggles asked. The
question was quiet. The quietness was deliberate.

"It requires me to reach the portal before it is
complete and to channel the gift into the breach at
sufficient intensity to collapse the opening and
cauterise the wound." Aleron held the fork. The hum
was audible now, a thin singing note that the room
could hear, the vibration responding to the
intensity of its wielder's focus. "The Orceri will
be defending the site. Their forces will be
concentrated. Getting to the portal will require
fighting through whatever they have deployed. That
is why I need you."

"And after?" Aizune's voice was measured. The Minerva
clasp was warm at his throat. His dark grey eyes were
on Aleron's face with the attention that was not
curiosity but the priestly perception that the
Cundo'elda had trained into him, the sight that read
the truth beneath the surface of what was spoken, and
what the sight perceived in Aleron Windstrider's
careful omissions was a shape that Aizune had been
sensing since the rooftop conversation months ago,
since the words "your faith is a home, mine is a
road," and the shape was not ambiguous. It was the
shape of a road that ended. "After you seal the
breach. What happens to you?"

The question hung in the golden light of the common
room. The harbour bell rang the ninth hour. The
distance between them was the width of a table and
the width of everything that Aleron could not say
and that Aizune had already heard in the silence
between the words.

"I will be significantly weakened," Aleron said.

The answer was true. It was not complete. Aizune
heard both the truth and the incompleteness and
the incompleteness was the louder sound, and his
hand moved to the clasp at his throat and the silver
was warm and the warmth was Minerva's presence and
Minerva's gift was wisdom, and wisdom was not the
accumulation of knowledge but the willingness to
understand what the knowledge required, and what
Aizune understood in this moment, with the certainty
that the clasp's warmth confirmed and that his
prayer journal would record and that his voice would
not speak because speaking it would make it real in
a way that the room could not bear, was that Aleron
Windstrider was walking toward something that he
did not expect to walk back from.

"Then we go to Gaal'Tarak," Aizune said. His voice
was steady. The steadiness was the gift he could give
his companions. "And we get you to the portal."

---

The preparations began at dawn.

Smiggles buried himself in research with the
systematic ferocity that was his response to every
threat he could not fireball. The mana engine sat on
the table beside him, half-disassembled, its
components arranged in the precise grid that the
gnomish engineering tradition used for diagnostic
evaluation, each piece labelled in Draconic shorthand
in the margins of his spellbook. He was not studying
the engine for its own sake. He was studying it for
the principle: the physics of dimensional boundaries,
the theory of planar sealing, the mathematics of
energy required to collapse a forced breach in the
fabric between realities. The engine's mana-field
generation provided a baseline for understanding how
the Orceri manipulated dimensional frequencies, and
the understanding, however incomplete, was the
foundation upon which he would build whatever
countermeasures were possible in the weeks they had.

"The boundary at Gaal'Tarak is not a wall," he said,
speaking to no one and everyone, the self-directed
commentary that his companions had learned was not
conversation but the audible surface of an internal
process that ran faster than silence could contain.
"It is more like a membrane. A living membrane that
has been punctured. The puncture wants to heal. The
Orceri are preventing the healing by applying sustained
pressure from the other side, widening the puncture
faster than the membrane's natural elasticity can
close it." He tapped the engine casing with one
ink-stained finger. "The system is overwound. They
are forcing more energy through the breach than the
breach was designed to handle, and the membrane is
degrading under the strain rather than tearing
cleanly, which means the failure point is
predictable, which means it can be intercepted."
He sat back. The moustache twitched. "Aleron's gift
is not a weapon. It is a catalyst. He accelerates
the healing. He gives the membrane the energy it
needs to close despite the pressure." He paused.
The hazel eyes moved to the copper filaments, to the
crystal inlays, to the dark metal casing that hummed
with the ambient noise of a civilisation built on
stolen divinity, and the eyes saw what the hands had
disassembled and the mind had mapped and the
conclusion was there, waiting, in the mathematics
that he could not refute and would not speak. "The
energy has to come from somewhere."

He did not finish the thought. He did not need to.
The somewhere was Aleron, and the question of whether
the somewhere would survive the somewhere's depletion
was the question that Smiggles' analytical engine had
already answered and that the man behind the engine
was refusing to process because processing it would
require him to sit with a conclusion that his
friendship could not accept and his intellect could
not refute.

He returned to the engine. The copper filaments
gleamed in the morning light. His hands were steady.
His moustache was freshly waxed. The brandy flask sat
on the table, untouched.

---

Iskar wrote a letter.

He wrote it at the small desk in his room above
Eccentricities, in the hours before dawn when the
harbour was quiet and the only sound was the distant
slap of water against pilings and the nearer sound
of his own breathing, and the breathing was controlled
because the letter required control and control was
what the Sabonaar maritime tradition taught you to
maintain when the sea was rising and the heading was
set and the thing you were sailing toward was the
thing you could not sail around.

The letter was addressed to Darielo Voss, care of the
Liberators at the Shrine to the Wanderer.

He had not written to Darielo in seven years. Not
because he had forgotten. Not because the mission
had changed. Because every word he might have written
would have been a promise, and promises required a
future in which to keep them, and until now Iskar had
always assumed the future was his to spend. The
assumption was a luxury. The assumption had been
stripped at Winderbourgh, not by the Orceri blade that
had cut his shoulder but by the knowledge that the
blade was a sample, that the army behind the blade was
building a door in a mountain, and that the door opened
onto a civilisation that bred humans into furniture
and considered the breeding a kindness.

*Darielo*, he wrote.

*I should have written sooner. I should have written
a hundred times. I did not write because I was afraid
that writing would make the distance real, and I have
spent seven years pretending that the distance is
temporary, that the next job will be the one that
provides the contact, the coin, the opportunity, and
that I will come for you and we will sail home. I am
still pretending. But the pretence is different now.
The world is different now. I have seen things that I
cannot explain in a letter and that I will explain
when I see you, because I will see you, because that
is the one promise I will not release regardless of
what the world requires of me.*

*There is a place in the mountains where something
terrible is happening. I am going there with people
I trust, which is a sentence I could not have written
a year ago, because a year ago I did not trust anyone
enough to walk beside them toward something that might
not have a return journey. I trust them now. The elf
prays and the gnome argues and the other elf talks to
people who are not there and the woman who is not a
woman watches everything with yellow eyes and I would
follow any of them into the dark, which is, I think,
what the word "crew" means when you strip away the
nautical terminology and find the thing underneath.*

*If I do not come back from the mountains, the
Liberators will know what to do. Finkdonklonk has
my operational notes. The contacts in Moamon are
current as of last season. The harbour master at
the eastern dock knows your face from the drawing
I gave him. The chain is intact. Someone will pull
it.*

*But I intend to pull it myself.*

*Sangre y sal, my friend. I am coming.*

He folded the letter. He sealed it with candle wax,
pressing the base of his compass into the warm pool
because he did not own a signet ring and because the
compass rose was the closest thing he had to a family
crest and because the gesture amused him in the grim
way that the sea amused sailors who had learned that
laughing at the wave was the only response the wave
respected. The wax set. The compass rose held. He
regarded it with the critical eye of a man who had
sealed a hundred letters and found this one, the one
that mattered, marked with an instrument of
navigation rather than identity, and the substitution
was, he decided, appropriate. He had always been
better at finding his way than at explaining who he
was.

He took the letter to the Ogre's Arse before sunrise.
The barkeep, a Sabonaar woman whose discretion was
purchased by years of reliable custom rather than coin,
accepted it without comment. The instructions were
simple: if Iskar did not return within two months,
deliver the letter to the Shrine to the Wanderer.

He walked back through the Dock District as the
eastern sky lightened. The ships at anchor rocked
on the morning tide. A gull cried. The air smelled
of salt and tar and the cold clean scent of a harbour
before the day's commerce filled it with humanity.
He passed the chandlery where his mother had sold
rope and canvas and storm lanterns to sailors who
never came back and sailors who always did, and the
distinction between the two was not courage or skill
but the particular grace that the sea extended to some
and withheld from others, and the grace was random,
and the randomness was the thing that every sailor
carried and no sailor discussed.

He did not look at the chandlery. He looked at the
harbour. The two moons were fading in the brightening
sky, silver and gold, and between them the space was
empty.

He adjusted his hat. He walked back to Eccentricities.
There was work to do.

---

Aizune found Aleron on the roof.

The elf sat at the edge where the flat stone met the
low parapet, his legs folded, the quarterstaff across
his knees, the hood pushed back. The white streaks in
his black hair caught the moonlight and held it with
the cold luminescence of something that was not natural
pigmentation but the visible record of a life lived in
proximity to forces that marked the body the way rivers
marked stone: slowly, permanently, without malice. His
face was turned toward the east. The Kirak Mountains
were not visible from Amara, not at night, not at this
distance. But his grey eyes looked in their direction
with the fixed attention of someone watching something
that was not visible and was not invisible but was
present in a register that his fractured perception
could access and that the mountains could not conceal.

"The boundary thinned again today," Aleron said. He
did not turn. He had known Aizune was there. The
priest's divine signature was a warmth that Aleron
could feel through the fractures, a steady silver-
white presence in the overlapping layers of his
perception, and the warmth was Minerva's, and Minerva
was alive in this reality, and the alive-ness was
a comfort that Aleron did not take for granted because
he had stood in a world where the alive-ness was
absent and the absence was a wound that the fabric of
reality itself had never healed.

Aizune sat beside him. The Minerva clasp was warm
at his throat. The prayer journal was in his lap.
The harbour stretched below them, the water holding
the doubled light of two moons, and the city was
quiet with the particular quiet of a place that had
not yet learned to be afraid and that the quiet was
protecting.

"You are not going to survive this," Aizune said.

The words were not a question. They were spoken in
the measured cadence that was Aizune's natural
register, the formal diction that was not stiff but
precise, every word chosen and placed with the care
of a man who understood that some sentences, once
spoken, rearranged the world they were spoken into,
and the rearrangement was irreversible, and the
irreversibility was the reason the sentence had to
be spoken rather than merely thought.

Aleron was quiet for a long time. The echoes murmured.
A translucent figure drifted across the rooftop, a
woman from a reality where Amara was a ruin and the
harbour was dry and the moons, he noticed, were three
instead of two. She passed through him. He let her go.

"I do not know," he said. The words were careful.
"Sugnim destroyed the section of the mural that
showed what comes after. I have spent thirty-seven
years trying to reconstruct it from the fragments I
can feel through the fractures, from the echoes of
realities where the mural was not destroyed, where
the path ended differently. But the echoes
contradict each other. In some, the figure in the
mural is standing after the rift closes. In others,
the figure is not." He looked at his hands. The
tremor was constant. "I suspect. I have suspected
for a very long time. But I do not know."

"You suspect," Aizune said.

"Yes."

"And you are going anyway."

"The gift does not ask my permission. It never has.
Thalaman gave it to me without consent and it has
consumed my life without apology and it has brought
me here, to this roof, beside a priest of Minerva
in a city that does not know what is coming, and
the gift is pointing east with the same certainty
that drew me across the desert to a pyramid where a
fox-faced priest was waiting with a truth he would
not fully share." The ghost of a smile. The thin,
sad, genuine expression that appeared when the
echoes were quiet and the man behind them was
present and the man was tired in a way that sleep
could not address and rest could not remedy and only
completion could resolve. "I am not brave, Aizune.
I am not noble. I am simply out of road. The path
goes east. The path ends at Gaal'Tarak. What the
ending looks like is the question that Sugnim would
not answer, and I have decided that the question is
less important than the walking."

Aizune's hand moved to the clasp. The silver was
warm. The warmth was the entity's presence, and the
presence was wisdom, and the wisdom was this: that
some truths could not be spoken and some
companionships could not be expressed and some
farewells were conducted in the register of silence
rather than words, and the silence between two
Ya'sehrim priests on a rooftop in Amara, one who
served Minerva with devotion and one who served
Thalaman with resignation, was the deepest
conversation the trilogy would hold.

"I will get you to the portal," Aizune said. "I
will stand beside you until the standing is
finished. Whatever comes after, you will not walk
the last of the road alone."

Aleron looked at him. Both eyes focused. The grey
irises were bright in the moonlight and the
brightness was not the luminescence that sometimes
followed planar use but the brightness of a man
who had been carrying a weight for seventy years
and who had just been told that the carrying was
shared.

"Thank you," he said. The words were thin. The
words were enough.

They sat together on the roof. Below them, the
harbour. Above them, two moons, silver and gold.
The east was dark. In the darkness, in the
mountains, in the ruins of a citadel that had been
destroyed fifty years ago by forces that no one
could explain, the boundary between realities
thinned and thinned and thinned, and the thinning
was patient, and the patience was running out.

---

Lai found him after Aizune went inside.

Aleron heard her approach. Not the footsteps, which
were silent in the way that dragons were silent when
they chose to be. The dimensional signature. The
draconic mana-field that his fractured perception
registered as a pressure against the boundary of
his awareness, the particular wrongness that her
lawful nature and his chaotic gift produced when
they occupied the same proximity: a dissonance, a
friction, the sensation of two frequencies that
could not harmonise attempting to occupy the same
space and finding the space insufficient for both.

She stopped at the edge of the buffer. Twenty paces.
The invisible line that their incompatible natures
had drawn and that they honoured by instinct rather
than agreement.

"You told them," she said.

"I told them about Gaal'Tarak."

"You did not tell them about the cost."

He did not answer. The echo of Aizune's words was
still warm in the air beside him, the promise to
stand, the promise to walk the road together, and
the promise was a kindness and the kindness was a
weight because kindness from someone who did not
know the full truth was a gift received under false
terms, and Aleron was tired of receiving gifts under
false terms, and the tiredness was the tiredness of
a man who had been given a gift by an entity without
his consent and was now required to give the gift
back without anyone else's.

Lai crossed the buffer.

She did not walk. She did not stride. She moved
through the twenty paces with a deliberation that
was visible in the set of her shoulders and the
angle of her jaw and the way her yellow eyes narrowed
against something that was not visible but was felt,
the pressure of his chaotic field against her
draconic nature intensifying with each step, and the
intensification was discomfort and the discomfort was
not enough to stop her because stopping was a
concession to instinct and concessions to instinct
were, in her assessment, for creatures who had not
spent twenty years learning to override every instinct
they possessed in order to move through a world that
was not designed for them.

She sat beside him. The contact was not physical.
The proximity was enough. The echoes in Aleron's
perception roared, the overlapping realities
multiplying in response to the draconic field's
interference, translucent figures crowding the
rooftop in layers that were thick and bright and
wrong, and he gripped the quarterstaff and focused
on the weight of the ashwood and the texture of the
worn grain and the temperature of the night air on
his face, the anchors that kept him in this reality
when the others pressed in.

She did not touch him. The last time she had touched
him, in the cave, the contact had produced a spark
that had roared through both of them and she had said
three words and none of them had been gentle. She did
not repeat the experiment. She sat beside him and
the sitting was the gesture and the gesture was, in
its way, more significant than the words that other
companions might have offered, because Lyanorra did
not offer comfort and did not seek proximity and did
not sit beside people whose presence caused her
physical distress unless the sitting meant something
that her lawful evil draconic nature could not
articulate and that her Instar education could not
classify and that the green-eyed creature beneath
all the masks was choosing to express through the
only medium available to her, which was presence.

"You know what it will cost," she said.

The words were the same she had spoken in the cave,
months ago, before the crossing. The accent was
absent. The musical lightness was absent. The voice
was the one that belonged to something older and
larger than the small green-tinted elf who sat on
a rooftop in Amara with her leather armour and her
yellow eyes and her secrets.

Aleron looked east. The mountains were invisible.
The wound was not.

"I know what I suspect," he said. "The distinction
is the only mercy Sugnim left me."

She nodded. Once. The nod carried the weight of a
being whose species measured time in centuries and
whose understanding of sacrifice was not moral but
structural: things were built and things fell and
the falling was not tragedy but architecture, and
the architecture required certain elements to bear
weight that other elements could not, and the
bearing was not fair and was not unfair and was
simply the way that buildings stood.

She rose. The buffer reasserted itself as she walked
away, the pressure in Aleron's perception easing
with each step, the echoes thinning, the rooftop
settling back into singular reality. At the edge of
the distance, she paused.

"I will be at Gaal'Tarak," she said. She did not
explain why. She did not need to. The explanation
was the same as Aizune's promise and Smiggles'
research and Iskar's letter: the accumulation of
choices that preceded the choice that mattered, the
individual decisions to stand that, taken together,

She went inside. The rooftop was empty.

Aleron sat alone with the echoes and the two moons
and the wound in the east that was growing, and the
section of the mural that Sugnim had destroyed was
present in its absence the way all destroyed things
are present: not as image but as question, not as
answer but as the space where the answer should have
been, and the space was large enough to hold
everything he feared and everything he hoped and the
everything was the same, because what he feared was
that the road ended at Gaal'Tarak, and what he hoped
was that the ending meant something, and the meaning
was the only thing that Thalaman's gift had never
guaranteed.

The harbour bell rang midnight.

The east was dark.

The boundary thinned.
