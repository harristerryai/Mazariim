# Chapter Six
## Unscheduled Contact

They rode west with a dead man and too many questions.

The monk's body, wrapped in a bedroll and secured across the back of
Iskar's horse, was the simplest of their burdens. It weighed almost
nothing. The Whisper had drained it so thoroughly that the remains were
barely heavier than the cloth that contained them, and the horse carried
the additional load without complaint. The other burdens were less
visible and considerably heavier: a saddlebag full of impossible
objects, a memory of three moons in an alien sky, and the growing,
unspoken certainty that what they had found beneath the keep was not a
discovery but a warning.

They had been riding for half a day when they found the girl.

She was sitting on a rock at the edge of the trade road, roughly eight
miles west of the keep, in a stretch of the Steppes where the hardpan
gave way to scattered patches of scrub grass and the first tentative
signs of life beyond the wasteland's dead heart. She was eating an
apple, and she looked up as they approached and smiled, and the smile
did something to the air between them that Aizune could not immediately
explain.

She was beautiful. Not beautiful in the way that court ladies or temple
maidens were beautiful, with careful arrangement and cultivated grace.
Beautiful in the way that a flame was beautiful, or a blade, or a
sudden break in storm clouds that revealed the sun: immediate,
arresting, and faintly dangerous. She was small, slender, fine-boned, a
young woman of elven appearance sitting on a rock in the middle of
nowhere with the relaxed confidence of someone who had chosen to be
there and could choose to be elsewhere whenever she pleased. Her skin
carried a distinct green tint, vivid and strange, as though sunlight
filtered through forest canopy had permanently stained her complexion.
Her hair was a wild cascade of green and black and brown, the colours
shifting as she moved, untamed and unapologetic. Her ears were pointed,
longer and sharper than a wood elf's typically were. Her leather armour
was dark brown, close-fitted, and covered precisely enough of her body
to qualify as armour while leaving enough of that green-tinted skin
visible to make the qualification generous. Very generous.

But it was her eyes that stopped him. They were yellow. Vivid, bright,
predatory yellow, the colour of lantern-light through amber glass, with
vertical pupils that widened as she looked at them and then narrowed
with an intelligence that was not merely observant but assessing. She
blinked less often than she should have. The eyes caught the afternoon
sun and threw it back with a faint, reflective gleam that belonged to
something other than an elf, something that hunted in darkness and saw
in spectrums that elven eyes could not reach.

"Hello," she said. Her voice was light, musical, carrying an undertone
of amusement that suggested everything she encountered was slightly
funny and she was the only one who had noticed. There was something
else in it too, a faint softening of certain consonants, a warmth on
the vowels that did not belong to any elven dialect Aizune could place.
"You look like you've had an interesting day."

Behind Aizune, Iskar made a sound. It was not quite a word. It was the
involuntary intake of breath that happened when a man who considered
himself worldly, experienced, and resistant to surprise encountered
something that bypassed his defences entirely and arrived directly at a
part of his brain that had nothing to do with worldliness or experience.
Aizune did not need to look back to know that the Sabonaar's dark eyes
had gone wide and that his mouth had opened slightly and that the
carefully maintained stubble along his jaw was framing an expression of
unguarded and thoroughly unprofessional appreciation.

Smiggles, who was immune to most forms of distraction but not blind,
glanced at Iskar, glanced at the girl, and then looked at Aizune with
an expression that communicated, in the eloquent shorthand of a year's
partnership, a complete sentence: "This is going to be a problem."

"Good afternoon," Aizune said, because someone had to speak and neither
of his companions appeared ready to contribute. "You are alone out
here."

"I am." She took another bite of the apple. The motion was casual,
unhurried, performed with the full awareness that three armed strangers
on horseback were looking at her and the complete absence of concern
about this fact. "I've been valking east for two days. I heard there
were adventures to be had in the Steppes." She gestured vaguely toward
the wasteland behind them. "Ruins. Monsters. Treasure. The usual."

"The usual," Smiggles repeated. His voice carried the flat scepticism
of a man who had identified something wrong and was in the process of
determining what. "You've been walking through the Steppes of Broken
Stone alone, on foot, for two days, looking for adventures."

"Yes."

"In that armour."

She looked down at herself. "Vhat is wrong with my armour?"

"It doesn't cover anything."

"It covers the important things." The smile widened. The yellow eyes
found Iskar, who had recovered enough composure to close his mouth but
not enough to stop staring, and something in her expression sharpened
with a delight that was entirely deliberate. "Your friend seems to
approve."

"My friend is having a moment," Smiggles said. "He'll recover. Who are
you?"

"Lai." She hopped down from the rock with a fluid, sinuous grace that
belonged to something larger wearing a smaller body. The movement was
beautiful and wrong in equal measure: too smooth, too controlled, every
muscle coordinating with a precision that spoke not of training but of
nature, of a body that had been designed rather than developed. She was
shorter than Aizune had estimated from a distance, barely over five
feet, and the disparity between her small frame and the coiled vitality
she projected was striking. "I'm from the south. Naarlam, originally.
I've been travelling for a vhile now."

"Naarlam." Aizune's ears shifted forward by a fraction that Smiggles
noted. "Which house?"

"No house. I vas raised by a scholar. He taught me things and I left to
see if any of them were true." She finished the apple and tossed the
core over her shoulder with a casual accuracy that put it precisely in
the centre of the rock she had been sitting on. "You're coming from the
east. From the keep. I saw the smoke yesterday." Her yellow eyes moved
to the wrapped bundle on Iskar's horse, and something in her expression
changed, a brief flicker of something that was not amusement but
awareness. "You're carrying a body."

"We are," Aizune said.

"And you look like you've seen something that frightened you. All three
of you. Even the little one, and he doesn't look like he frightens
easily."

"I don't," Smiggles confirmed.

"So." She clasped her hands behind her back, a gesture that was girlish
and knowing simultaneously, and looked up at them with an expression of
earnest enthusiasm underlaid by something sharper than earnestness could
account for. "It sounds like you've found exactly the kind of adventure
I've been looking for. May I come with you?"

"No," Smiggles said.

"Absolutely not," Aizune said.

"I would be delighted," Iskar said, and then caught the looks from his
companions and cleared his throat. "That is. I mean. We should discuss
it."

"We should not discuss it," Smiggles said. "We should say no and ride
on. We don't know this person. She appeared from nowhere in the middle
of a wasteland, she's alone, she's unarmed, and she's..." He paused,
searching for the word that would encompass what was bothering him
without verbalising the specific quality that was bothering him, which
was that the young woman standing in front of them radiated a physical
magnetism so potent it was interfering with the cognitive function of at
least one member of the party and the peripheral awareness of the other
two. "She's suspicious," he finished.

"I'm not suspicious." Lai's lower lip extended in a pout that was
calculated with the precision of a siege weapon. "I'm friendly. There's
a difference."

"In my experience, the friendlier someone is in the Steppes, the more
suspicious they should be."

"Mmmmm." The sound was low, drawn out, and carried the quality of
something tasting the air rather than considering a point. "Your
experience sounds exhausting."

"It is. It's kept me alive. And none of this calibrates. A girl alone
in the Steppes, no gear, no supplies, no weapon, eating an apple like
she's on holiday."

"I like apples. My teacher used to say that the snake who rests in the
sun is not lazy, only patient." She smiled. "I am very patient."

Smiggles stared at her. "What does that even mean?"

"Mmmmm. I am not entirely sure. He said many things like that."

Aizune studied her. His divine senses, still heightened from the
encounters below the keep, reached toward her with the quiet, habitual
extension of a priest whose gift was woven into every act of perception.
What he felt was confusing. There was no evil emanating from her, not in
the sharp, immediate sense that his training had taught him to recognise.
But there was something. A density. A weight of presence that did not
match the small frame and the playful smile, as though the body he was
looking at contained more than a body should, packed tight, compressed,
a larger thing folded into a smaller space. The sensation was subtle
enough to be imagination and persistent enough to be real, and he could
not resolve it into certainty either way.

"We are returning to Amara," he said. "The road is dangerous. If you
wish to walk in our company as far as the city, you may."

"Aizune," Smiggles said.

"She is alone in the Steppes. We cannot leave her here."

"We can. It's easy. We ride west. She stays here."

"That is not something I am prepared to do."

Smiggles stared at him. Then he stared at Lai, who smiled at him with
the radiant innocence of a creature that had never done anything wrong
in its life and could not imagine why anyone would think otherwise. Then
he stared at Iskar, who was very carefully not looking at anyone and
adjusting his hat with unnecessary concentration.

"Fine," Smiggles said. "Fine. She walks with us to Amara. She does not
touch our gear. She does not read our documents. She does not ask
questions about what we found in the keep. And if she turns out to be
something other than what she appears to be, I am going to say 'I told
you so' with such force and frequency that you will hear it in your
reverie for the next century."

"Noted," Aizune said.

"Vonderful," Lai said, and fell into step beside Iskar's horse with the
easy, ground-eating stride of someone who was very accustomed to
covering distance on foot and not at all inconvenienced by the horses'
pace. She looked up at Iskar and her yellow eyes caught the light. "I
like your hat."

Iskar opened his mouth. What should have emerged was one of the
effortless compliments that the Sabonaar produced the way other men
produced breathing: naturally, constantly, and without apparent thought.
What actually emerged was a pause so complete that Aizune could hear the
wind moving through the scrub grass on both sides of the road. Iskar's
mouth worked. His eyes, which had been performing the complex operation
of looking at Lai's face without looking at the rest of Lai, lost this
battle comprehensively. He blinked. He appeared to forget, briefly,
which language he spoke.

"Thank you," he managed. His voice had recovered most of its usual
warmth, though a slight roughness at the edges suggested the recovery
was structural rather than complete. "It is my second-favourrite
possession."

"What's the first?"

"My rapierr. But the hat is more verrsa..." He stopped. The word
"versatile" had apparently lodged somewhere between his brain and his
tongue and was refusing to complete the journey. He took a breath, reset
his expression to something approaching his usual composure, and tried
again. "More versatile."

She laughed. The sound was bright, musical, and entirely genuine, and it
did something to Iskar's expression that made Smiggles mutter "Oy"
darkly into his moustache and kick his horse to a faster walk.

---

Twelve thousand feet above a city that had no name in any language the
heroes spoke, Commander Li'Zhan stood on the observation deck of Zone
Kluth Tak Airborne Monitoring Station and watched the world turn
beneath him.

The dirigible was enormous. Three hundred feet from bow to stern, its
gas envelope a sleek ovoid of treated silk and alchemical membrane, its
gondola a structure of dark metal and reinforced glass that hung beneath
the envelope on cables of braided mithril. The deck on which Li'Zhan
stood extended from the gondola's forward section like the prow of a
ship, open to the sky, ringed by a railing of polished steel. The wind
at this altitude was constant and cold, pulling at the hem of Li'Zhan's
uniform and carrying with it the faint, acrid scent of the mana engines
that kept the station aloft: ozone and hot metal and something sweeter
beneath both, the particular fragrance of energy that had been stripped
from beings whose names the orceri no longer bothered to remember.

Li'Zhan was an elegant orceri. Tall, lean for his race, with the smooth
green skin and careful grooming that marked the officer class of Zuel's
military hierarchy. His uniform was dark, close-fitted, bearing the
rank insignia of a monitoring station commander at the collar and cuffs.
His hair was cropped short and oiled. His tusks, smaller than a common
orc's and meticulously maintained, protruded just past his lower lip,
giving his face a quality that was more thoughtful than threatening. He
smoked a small pipe with a magical flame built into its bowl, a
convenience that allowed him to light it with a thought and extinguish
it with another, and the thin curl of aromatic smoke that rose from the
pipe was the only thing about Commander Li'Zhan that moved without
precision or purpose.

Below him, the city spread to every horizon. Pale stone buildings
arranged in orderly grids. Broad avenues lit by the steady blue-white
glow of mana streetlamps. Green parks. Commercial districts. Military
installations, their perimeters marked by the faint shimmer of
ward-barriers. And moving through the streets, above the streets,
around the streets, the citizens of the Orceri Empire went about the
business of a civilisation that had conquered twelve worlds and saw no
reason to stop.

Twelve. Li'Zhan corrected himself. Eleven. The twelfth was still under
evaluation. The twelfth was why he was awake at this hour, why the pipe
was in his mouth, and why the deck beneath his boots felt less steady
than the engineering that supported it should have allowed.

The sensoriums reported from their stations throughout the gondola.
Their voice, when it came, was not a single voice but many: a
polyphonic harmonic of dozens of individual consciousnesses merged into
a unified instrument of perception. The elven sensoriums were, by any
reasonable assessment, the most disturbing of the Empire's biomantic
achievements. They had been elves once. They retained the pointed ears,
the fine bone structure, the large eyes. But the eyes were blank, pupils
dilated to the edges of their irises, and the bodies were thin, twisted,
wrapped in metallic chains and armour that served not as protection but
as conduit, linking each sensorium to its neighbours and to the
detection arrays mounted along the dirigible's hull. They did not speak
as individuals. They did not think as individuals. They were a network,
a living instrument, their merged awareness sweeping the dimensional
frequencies with a sensitivity that no mechanical device could match.

"Commander."

Li'Zhan turned from the railing. The sensorium that addressed him stood
in the doorway of the observation deck, its body swaying slightly, its
blank eyes oriented in his general direction without truly focusing. Its
mouth moved, but the sound that emerged came from everywhere and
nowhere, resonating through the metallic links of its armour and the
armour of every other sensorium in the gondola, a chorus of one voice
spoken by many throats.

"Report," Li'Zhan said.

"Unscheduled contact. South by southeast, fourteen miles. Alternate
prime, designation unknown. Planar boundary disruption consistent with
observation-class device activation. Duration: four minutes, eleven
seconds. Harmonic signature matches no catalogued tangent."

Li'Zhan's pipe stopped halfway to his mouth.

"Repeat."

The sensorium repeated. The polyphonic voice was patient, mechanical,
devoid of the urgency that the words demanded. South by southeast.
Fourteen miles. Alternate prime. Unknown.

Li'Zhan lowered the pipe. His hand, he noticed with professional
displeasure, was not entirely steady.

An unknown alternate prime. An observation device, activated, peering
across the dimensional boundary into orceri space. Not one of the
eleven conquered tangents. Not a known research frequency. Something
new. Something that had looked at the Empire, however briefly, from a
direction the sensoriums had never been asked to monitor because no
contact had ever come from that direction before.

"Duration of contact?" he asked, though the sensorium had already
provided this. He needed to hear it again. He needed the number to be
real.

"Four minutes, eleven seconds. Contact terminated. No subsequent
activity detected."

"Source location?"

"Indeterminate. Harmonic analysis suggests origin point within the
alternate prime itself. The device was operated from the other side of
the boundary."

Someone, in an undiscovered reality, had built a machine capable of
looking into the Orceri Empire. Someone had activated it. Someone had
looked.

Li'Zhan placed his pipe carefully on the railing, straightened his
uniform, and walked to the communication station at the rear of the
observation deck. The station was a panel of dark metal and crystal,
similar in construction to the device they had found on the dead scout's
wrist, though considerably larger and more powerful. He placed his hand
flat against the panel's surface. The crystal warmed. A connection
opened.

"This is Commander Li'Zhan, Zone Kluth Tak Airborne Monitoring
Station." His voice was calm. His voice was always calm. Calm was what
the Empire required of its officers, and Li'Zhan had spent a career
providing what the Empire required. "Priority alert. I have need of
your services. And the need for secrecy is absolute. Report to my
station immediately. Do not use standard channels."

He closed the connection. He picked up his pipe. He looked south by
southeast, toward a point fourteen miles distant where the dimensional
boundary had been, for four minutes and eleven seconds, thinner than it
should have been.

The smoke from his pipe curled into the cold air and dispersed.

---

They reached Amara on the evening of the fourth day.

The Free City appeared as a smudge of white and terracotta against the
darkening bay, its harbour lights kindling one by one as the sun settled
into the water, and the sight of it, after the barren emptiness of the
Steppes and the horrors beneath the keep, hit Aizune with an unexpected
force that he recognised, after a moment, as relief. Amara was noise
and commerce and the smell of Yella's bread on the evening wind. Amara
was ordinary. After what they had seen, ordinary was precious.

Lai had kept pace with the horses for four days without visible effort
or complaint. She had been cheerful, energetic, and relentlessly curious
about everything except the subjects Smiggles had forbidden her to ask
about, a prohibition she obeyed with a good-natured compliance that the
gnome found more suspicious than defiance would have been. She had
volunteered for every task: gathering firewood, scouting the road ahead,
tending the horses. She had returned with firewood in half the time it
should have taken, scouted further than any of them could have covered
on foot in the time she was gone, and handled the horses with a
confidence that made the animals simultaneously calm and uneasy, as
though they could not decide whether the small woman with the yellow
eyes was a friend or something they should be running from.

On the second night, she had sat by the fire and told stories. Not the
polished performances of a trained bard like Iskar, but rambling,
enthusiastic tales of places she claimed to have visited and things she
claimed to have seen, delivered with a breathless energy and a liberal
attitude toward plausibility that made Iskar laugh and Smiggles squint.
She spoke of Instar's towers as though she had walked among them. She
described the Thaumaturgic Council's public debates with a specificity
that suggested either excellent research or firsthand experience. She
named obscure arcane theorists whose work Smiggles recognised and whose
arguments she summarised with a casual accuracy that made the gnome
forget, for several minutes, that he was supposed to be suspicious.

Between stories, she had a habit of pausing and making a low, drawn-out
sound, "Mmmmm," as though she were tasting the night air or rolling a
thought around in her mouth before deciding whether to swallow it or
share it. The sound was oddly compelling. It preceded some of her most
interesting observations and some of her most transparent deflections,
and Smiggles had not yet determined which category was more frequent.

She peppered her tales with phrases that did not belong to a young wood
elf from Naarlam. "The river does not fight the stone; it simply
remembers the way around it," she said at one point, quoting something
or someone with the absent confidence of a person repeating words heard
so often they had become their own. "My teacher used to say that.
He also said that the hawk who hunts at dawn sees the mouse that the
wolf who hunts at dusk has forgotten." Smiggles had pointed out that
this made no practical sense. Lai had smiled and said, "Mmmmm, it does
if you are the hawk," which made no more sense but was delivered with
such serene certainty that the gnome had temporarily lost the thread of
his objection.

On the third evening, she had asked Iskar about the stars above the
Bay of Urgot, and when he began to describe the constellations that
Sabonaar sailors navigated by, she had tilted her head at the precise
angle that reduced him to incoherence and said, "In my teacher's land,
they say the stars are the scattered embers of a fire the gods built
and forgot. But in the east, the sages say each star is a lesson that
no one has yet learned." Iskar had responded, eventually, that in the
Sabonaar tradition the stars were the eyes of drowned sailors watching
over the living. "How beautiful," she had said, and the way she said
"beautiful" softened the b and lingered on the vowels in a manner that
had nothing to do with any elven accent and everything to do with
something older.

She had also, without apparent effort, learned the precise angle at
which to tilt her head that caused Iskar to lose his train of thought.
She deployed this weapon with the remorseless efficiency of a siege
engine, and the Sabonaar, to his credit, recognised what she was doing
and fell for it every single time.

Aizune watched all of this with the patient attention of a priest whose
training had taught him that the most important details were often the
ones that hid in plain sight. Something about Lai was wrong. Not evil.
Not threatening, exactly. But wrong in the way that a note played
slightly out of tune was wrong: recognisable as music, close enough to
correct to pass casual inspection, but carrying a dissonance that
nagged at the ear of anyone trained to listen. Her accent shifted. Her
vocabulary shifted. The girl who said "vhat" and "vas" with the casual
imprecision of someone whose first language shaped different sounds
was the same girl who quoted scholars from traditions separated by
thousands of miles of ocean. The dissonance was not accidental.

He could not identify it. He filed it away and watched.

They delivered Lai to the gates of Amara with Smiggles' brusque
recommendation that she find an inn and stay out of trouble, a
suggestion she received with the cheerful agreement of someone who had
no intention of following it. She waved as they rode into the city, her
yellow eyes bright in the torchlight, her smile carrying the particular
quality of a person who knew they would be meeting again soon and was
looking forward to it.

"She's going to follow us," Smiggles said.

"Almost certainly," Aizune agreed.

"And you're fine with that."

"I did not say I was fine with it. I said I could not leave her in the
Steppes."

"She was fine in the Steppes. She was finer than we were. She walked
forty miles in four days without breaking stride, she carried firewood
like it weighed nothing, and the horses are terrified of her."

"Horses are often nervous around strangers."

"Horses are nervous around predators, Aizune. That doesn't calibrate
with a wood elf girl out for a stroll. And she quotes proverbs from the
Ancient South and the Lands of Honor like she grew up in a library. A
library in two countries at once."

The elf did not respond. They rode on through the Market District toward
Eccentricities, and the noise of Amara closed around them like a
familiar blanket, and the questions that the Steppes had given them did
not diminish but merely settled, temporarily, beneath the weight of more
immediate concerns.

---

Aldorph Broomspatch's reaction to the return of his stolen documents
was everything Smiggles had predicted and more.

"Gentlemen!" The merchant came around his desk like a ship under full
sail, arms spread, face blazing with the incandescent delight of a man
whose tax problems had just been solved. "Gentlemen, you have done it,
you magnificent, extraordinary, heroic gentlemen, you have done it!" He
clasped Aizune's hand, pumped it, moved to Smiggles, pumped his, moved
to Iskar, pumped his, and then returned to the desk to seize the
oilcloth bundle of documents and press it to his chest with the
tenderness of a father reuniting with a lost child.

"The contracts," he said, riffling through the papers with expert
fingers. "The manifests. The Instar correspondence. It's all here.
Every page. Blessed Tiovin, every page." He looked up at them with eyes
that were genuinely, almost alarmingly, moist. "You will find me a
grateful man, my friends. A very grateful man."

He paid them. The sum was generous. Then he added a bonus, which was
more generous. Then he produced three bottles of wine from a cabinet
behind his desk and insisted they drink to the success of the venture,
which they did, because refusing Aldorph Broomspatch in a mood of
celebration would have required a degree of resolve that none of them
possessed after four days in the Steppes.

"The keep is cleared?" Aldorph asked, refilling glasses with the
practised hand of a man who understood that wine was a lubricant for
negotiation and celebration alike.

"Cleared," Aizune confirmed. "The shaman is dead. His warriors are dead
or fled. The keep itself is empty."

"Excellent. Excellent. And the keep's defences? The location? I ask
because, as I mentioned, the eastern trade routes are vital to my
operations, and a cleared fortification in the Steppes could serve as a
waypoint for future caravans if properly maintained."

"I would not recommend occupying the keep," Aizune said.

Something in his tone made Aldorph pause with his wine glass halfway to
his lips. The merchant's eyes, behind his monocle, sharpened with the
instinct of a man who had built a trading empire by knowing when a
conversation had shifted from pleasantry to substance. "Oh?"

Aizune looked at Smiggles. The gnome gave a fractional nod. They had
discussed this on the road: what to tell Aldorph, how much to share,
where to draw the line between honesty and prudence. Aldorph was an
ally, a client, a man of resources and connections. He was also a
merchant, and merchants traded in information as readily as they traded
in goods.

"There are structures beneath the keep," Aizune said. "Older. More
complex. We found things there that require further investigation before
the site can be considered safe."

"Things," Aldorph repeated.

"Things that go beyond the scope of the job you hired us for. We will
provide a full accounting when we have a better understanding of what we
found. For now, I would advise keeping your caravans away from the keep
and its immediate surroundings."

Aldorph studied him. The celebration had not left his face, but a layer
of calculation had settled over it, the merchant's mind working behind
the bonhomie. He nodded slowly. "I trust your judgement, Master
Silverwind. The Red Raven Trading Company has resources that extend
beyond gold, as I said. When you are ready to share more, I am ready to
listen." He raised his glass. "To successful ventures. And to the ones
that follow."

They drank. They departed. The night air of Amara's Market District was
warm and smelled of lamp oil and the distant salt of the bay, and the
two moons were rising over the harbour, silver and gold, whole and
familiar, and between them the sky was empty.

Two moons. As it should be.

---

The discussion happened that night, in the rented room above
Eccentricities, with the door locked and the windows shuttered and
Smiggles' voice pitched low enough that the shopkeeper below could not
hear.

"Right," the gnome said. He was sitting cross-legged on the bed, the
orc spy's possessions arranged before him in a precise grid: the
adamantine blade, the strange coins, the wrist device, a small pouch of
items they had not yet identified. His brandy flask was at his elbow.
His moustache had been rewaxed. His eyes were sharp. "Let's reason
through this."

He picked up the adamantine blade and turned it in the lamplight. The
metal gleamed with a lustre that was different from any adamantine
Aizune had seen: finer-grained, lighter, carrying an almost
imperceptible blue sheen that suggested an alloy rather than a pure
metal. Smiggles had spent an hour examining it on the road and had not
been able to identify the secondary component.

"We know what we saw. An orcish civilisation. Cities. Technology.
Enslaved races. A professional army. Three moons." He set down the
blade and picked up one of the coins. "We know this isn't from here.
The metallurgy is wrong. The craftsmanship exceeds anything available in
the Merchant Nations, the Inner Kingdoms, or the dwarven realms. Iskar,
you've handled goods from every major port on the western coast. Have
you ever seen work like this?"

"Never." Iskar was leaning against the wall by the window, his arms
folded, his hat hanging on a hook beside him. His expression was
serious, the performance stripped away, the man beneath it present and
focused. "The blade alone would be worth a fortune in any market I've
traded in. The balance is perfect. The edge is beyond anything Underhill
could produce, and Underhill is the best dwarven smith in the Merchant
Nations. Whoever forged this had access to techniques and materials that
don't exist here."

"And the orc it belonged to was clean, groomed, and well-equipped,"
Smiggles continued. "Not a tribal warrior. Not a raider. A professional.
A scout or soldier from an organised military force." He set down the
coin and looked at them both. "So. What are we looking at? Option one:
the past. A lost civilisation. Orcs who built an empire and fell, and
this is a relic. Problem: the machine showed a living, functioning
civilisation with technology beyond our current capability. Lost
civilisations don't have better technology than the present. And three
moons. We've had two moons for as long as anyone's kept records. That's
a stripped thread right from the start."

"Not the past," Aizune agreed.

"Option two: the future. Problem: same as option one, only worse. Three
moons means the astronomy is different, not just the history. A third
moon doesn't appear because orcs built cities. A third moon appears
because the fundamental structure of the world is different. That's not
a temporal change. That's a..." He searched for the word. "A dimensional
one. A branching. A version of the world where something went
differently at a very deep level."

"An alternate reality," Iskar said.

"An alternate material plane." Smiggles picked up the wrist device and
held it toward the lamplight. The crystal embedded in its surface caught
the flame and threw back a reflection that was slightly wrong, slightly
too blue, as though the crystal were filtering the light through
properties that did not exist in the lamp's spectrum. "A parallel
version of our world. Same geography, same coastlines, same rivers.
Different history. Different outcome. In their world, the orcs won. They
conquered everything. They enslaved the other races. They built a
civilisation on top of the wreckage."

The silence that followed was the silence of a conclusion that all three
of them had been circling for four days, finally spoken aloud, finally
real.

"And they sent a scout to ours," Iskar said.

"Or the machine opened a window and the scout came through it. Either
way, they had the capability to cross the boundary between their world
and ours. And if the scout found the machine, others know about it."
Smiggles set down the device. "We need confirmation. I can theorise all
night, but I need someone who can analyse these materials with proper
equipment and tell me, definitively, whether they originate from another
plane of existence. I need the Thaumaturgic Council."

"Instar," Aizune said.

"Instar. But not yet." Smiggles looked at Aizune with an expression
that was, for once, entirely without sarcasm or mockery. "You want to
take the monk home first."

"I do."

"That's south. Instar is east."

"I know."

"And you want to go south first, to return the body of a man we never
met to a monastery we've never visited, before we go east to warn the
most powerful concentration of magical talent in the Merchant Nations
that an alternate reality full of civilised orcs may have just noticed
we exist."

"Yes."

Smiggles studied him. Then he nodded. "Fine. We go south. But we go
fast, and we don't stay long. Because if I'm right about what we found,
and I am usually right, then speed matters. And every day we spend on
courtesy is a day the other side has to figure out what they saw when
their scout didn't come home."

"Agreed."

Iskar pushed himself off the wall. "Then we leave at dawn?"

"At dawn." Aizune paused. "The young woman. Lai. She may attempt to
follow us."

"She will definitely attempt to follow us," Smiggles said. "She has
been following us since we left the Steppes, despite my very clear
instructions. I saw her twice in the Market District this evening."

"You saw her?" Iskar looked surprised.

"I am observant, unlike some people who are distracted by yellow eyes
and insufficient armour."

"I was not distracted."

"You forgot your own name when she tilted her head at you."

"That is a grotesque exaggeration."

"Is it? Because I distinctly recall a moment on the second day when she
asked you about the Cobalt Tide and you said, and I quote, 'It was a
ship. A very good ship. With sails.' And then you stopped talking and
stared at her until she changed the subject."

"I was being concise."

"You were being lobotomised."

Aizune raised a hand. The gesture was small, but both of them fell
silent, because when Aizune raised a hand in conversation it meant he
had decided something and the debate was over. "If she follows, she
follows. We watch her. We learn what we can about who she is and why she
is here. Smiggles is right that something about her does not fit. But
she has done nothing threatening, she has been helpful, and I am not in
the habit of turning away allies simply because they make me uneasy."

"She doesn't make you uneasy," Smiggles said. "She makes you confused.
There's a difference."

"The difference is subtle."

"The difference is that confusion is what she wants. And she's very good
at it. Her and her snake proverbs and her star lessons."

The lamp guttered. The room fell into shadow, and in the shadow, the orc
spy's possessions gleamed with a faint, alien lustre that had no
business existing in a rented room above a magical components shop in
the Free City of Amara.

Aizune picked up the adamantine blade. Held it. Felt its weight, its
balance, the perfection of its edge.

Somewhere, in a world with three moons and no elves and cities built on
the bones of enslaved races, the smiths who forged this blade were still
working. The army they equipped was still marching. The civilisation
they served was still growing, still conquering, still looking outward
toward horizons that included, perhaps, the one that Aizune stood on.

He set the blade down. Touched the Minerva clasp at his throat.

"Dawn," he said. "South to Blowing Rock. Then east to Instar. Then we
will see."

The lamp burned low. The two moons shone through the shuttered window.
The night settled over Amara with the patient weight of a city that did
not know what was coming, and in the rooms above Eccentricities, three
people who did know sat with their thoughts and their questions and the
beginning of a fear that none of them had yet found words for.

In the alley below, a shadow with yellow eyes watched their window,
smiled to itself, and disappeared into the warm dark with a silence that
no elf and no human and no gnome could have matched.

Only something older. Only something other.

Only something that had chosen, for reasons it did not share, to follow
three strangers into a story that was much larger than any of them yet
understood.
