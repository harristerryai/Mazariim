# Chapter Six
## Homecoming

The polymorph was failing when they reached the cave.

Iskar felt it first: the left tusk, which had itched for hours,
softened against his lower lip with the unsettling sensation of
bone becoming flesh, the structural certainty of the Orceri jaw
dissolving into something that was neither orc nor human but the
intermediate state between them, the architectural confusion of
a body that had been told to be one thing and was remembering
that it was another. The skin of his hands rippled at the
knuckles, the grey-green darkening and then lightening in patches
as the Sabonaar bronze beneath fought its way through the
polymorph like a swimmer surfacing through murky water.

"Move," Smiggles said. He could feel his own disguise degrading:
the enormous Orceri frame compressing, the bones of his hands
shortening, the ham-sized fingers thinning toward the precise
gnomish digits that had shaped them in the first place. The
draconic layer that Lai had placed over his arcane working was
holding longer than the arcane working beneath it, which created
the peculiar effect of an Orceri exterior that was structurally
sound and an interior that was collapsing, as though the shell
of a building had survived an earthquake that had destroyed every
wall within.

They reached the passage. The mountain's cold enveloped them.
The mana engine hummed in Aleron's pack, steady and oblivious,
its civilian function unaware that it had spent the afternoon
preventing the detection and probable death of four beings from
another reality. Aleron walked ahead, his quarterstaff tapping
the stone with a rhythm that had grown slower over the descent,
the intervals between taps lengthening as his body consumed the
last of whatever reserve the morning's reverie had provided,
and the tapping was a countdown, the sound of a mechanism running
down, and Smiggles watched the gaunt figure and calculated the
margin between functional and collapse and found it
uncomfortable.

The passage turned twice and opened into the chamber, and the
chamber was as they had left it: vast, dim, warm with the
geothermal breath of deep stone, and filled by the dragon who
had not moved in the hours since the reconnaissance party had
departed and who gave no indication of having moved in the
decades before that.

Aizune sat where they had left him.

He was cross-legged on the cavern floor beside Giganzalt's
forelimb, his prayer journal closed on his knees, his hands
resting on the leather cover, and his face carried an expression
that Smiggles had not seen before and that Iskar had not seen
before and that Lai, watching from the passage entrance with
yellow eyes that the Orceri disguise was releasing back to their
natural vertical intensity, had not seen before: not fear, not
despair, but the particular stillness of a man whose
understanding of the world had been rebuilt from the foundations
up. The rebuilding was visible not in what his face showed but
in what it did not. The composure that Aizune wore as naturally
as his robes, the measured calm that the Cundo'elda had trained
into him across decades of instruction, was present, but it was
present in the way that a wall was present after it had been
taken down and put back up with the same stones in the same
order: identical in appearance, different in the knowledge of
what it had cost to reassemble.

The Minerva clasp was cold at his throat. It had been cold for
hours. It would remain cold until they left this world.

"You are back," he said. The words were quiet and carried the
weight of someone who had spent hours in the company of a being
whose testimony had reshaped the interior architecture of his
faith and who was now looking at the faces of the people he
loved and seeing them, for the first time, against the backdrop
of what those faces would look like if the Orceri reached their
world.

Smiggles' disguise failed.

The collapse was not dramatic. It was simply complete: one
moment the Orceri frame, the next the gnome, standing three
feet tall in robes that had been sized for a body seven feet
tall and now pooled around his boots in folds of fabric that
would have been comic in any other context and were, in this
context, merely the physical evidence of a spell that had done
its work and was finished. His moustache was back. The
magnificent Salvador Dali curves, the precisely waxed points
that no amount of interdimensional travel or polymorph magic
or existential horror could dislodge from their positions of
glory, emerged from the dissolving Orceri features like a
flag being raised over a recaptured fortress.

He did not touch it. He did not adjust it. He simply stood in
the cave in his own body and felt the moustache on his face
and the moustache was his and the face was his and the body was
his and for one moment the fact of being himself was enough,
was sufficient, was the only victory that mattered.

Then the moment passed, and the analytical engine that lived
behind the moustache resumed its function, and the function
was to process what they had seen and communicate it to the
elf who sat beside the dragon and had spent the afternoon
learning the history of the thing they had observed.

"Sit down," Aizune said. "All of you. We have much to share."

---

They debriefed in the dragon's shadow.

The reconnaissance party spoke first, because the reconnaissance
was immediate and visceral and required no context that the
listeners did not already possess. Smiggles led, his voice
precise, the gnomish craft idiom surfacing as his analytical mind
sorted the observations into categories: infrastructure,
population, technology, surveillance, military presence,
biological modification.

"The town is a mid-sized regional centre," he said. "Clean.
Orderly. Deeply, fundamentally wrong. They have mana streetlamps
on every corner. Floating carriages. Dirigibles overhead.
Surveillance constructs in the air, beholder-derived, biomantically
modified, overlapping patrol patterns. Sensorium pairs on the
streets. Dwarven hulks at the perimeter." He paused. The pause
was the Smiggles silence, the diagnostic, the signal that what
followed had passed through the analytical framework and emerged
on the other side carrying a weight that analysis could not
reduce. "They have enslaved humans everywhere. In every shop. On
every street. Carrying, cleaning, serving. Thirty generations of
breeding. The humans do not resist because the capacity for
resistance has been removed from them. It is not oppression. It
is architecture. They built it into the species."

Aizune listened. His face was still. The journal was open again,
the quill moving, recording Smiggles' observations in the
Elda'lam script that had absorbed Giganzalt's history hours
before.

Iskar spoke next. His voice was flat, the Sabonaar warmth absent,
the rolled Rs reduced to their functional minimum, the performance
so completely withdrawn that the man who remained was someone his
companions had not met before: the Iskar beneath the Iskar, the
sailor from the docks whose capacity for joy was matched by a
capacity for fury that the joy normally concealed.

"I saw a child," he said. "Eight years old. Carrying bread. Grey
tunic. The face of every child I have ever seen on the docks of
Amara, except this child's eyes were empty. Not afraid. Not sad.
Empty. The way a tool is empty. The way a broom is empty." He
stopped. The muscles of his jaw worked. "In Moamon, they take
slaves. They chain them. The chains are visible. The cruelty is
visible. You can see it and name it and fight it. This..." He
gestured at the passage, at the mountain, at the world beyond
the mountain. "This is not slavery. This is something that does
not have a name because free people never imagined it."

Aizune wrote.

Lai spoke last. "The sensoriums are elven," she said. The words
were delivered without inflection, without performance, in the
voice that the tree line had revealed: stripped, precise, the
voice of a being that was not pretending to be smaller than it
was. "Twisted. Starved. Chained together with mana conduits.
They scan for signatures. They passed us at arm's length." She
looked at Aleron, who sat against the cavern wall with his hood
drawn forward and his staff across his knees and the mana engine
humming softly in the pack beside him. "The engine masked us.
Smiggles' theory held. But the engine is one device, and the
sensoriums are a network that covers the continent, and the
margin between detection and concealment was not comfortable."

"Smiggles," Aizune said. "The mana engine. You are certain it
was the engine that prevented detection?"

"Certain is a word I use when I have controlled variables and
repeated trials," Smiggles said. "I have one trial and zero
controlled variables. What I have is a sensorium pair that
walked past four non-Orceri mana signatures at arm's length
and did not react. The engine was the only difference between
our signatures and the ambient environment. The correlation is
strong. The causation is inferred. But the inference is the
best I have."

"It will do," Aizune said. He closed the journal.

---

Then Aizune shared what Giganzalt had told him.

He did it methodically, the way the Cundo'elda had trained him
to report: facts first, assessments second, implications third.
The War. Stoneshaper's blocked blow. The extermination that
followed: elven lands razed, halflings exterminated, humans
herded into camps. The War of the Heavens: entities destroyed,
Minerva among them, her plane collapsed, the dimensional scar
permanent. Biomancy. Sensoriums. Eleven conquered worlds, each
one adding a capability, each capability making the next
conquest more efficient. The Akovarian world, seventy years ago,
providing the mana mastery that enabled sky citadels and the
current apex of Orceri military technology.

And Rangithyr.

"Giganzalt's assessment," Aizune said, and his voice did not
change, because his voice had been steady throughout and would
remain steady because steadiness was the gift he could give his
companions when the content of what he was saying would have
justified any amount of unsteadiness, "is that Rangithyr is the
most dangerous mortal being in existence. He personally
destroyed multiple entities during the War of the Heavens. He
blocked Stoneshaper's blow, which is the single action that
created this reality. And he has commanded every major military
operation the Orceri have conducted for eight centuries." He
paused. "Giganzalt believes that if Rangithyr enters our world
personally, the discussion of defence becomes academic."

The word academic fell into the cave and lay there.

Smiggles processed through analysis. The analytical engine was
running at full capacity, the gears turning behind hazel eyes
that were sharp and focused and showed none of the horror that
the mind behind them was systematically filing into compartments
labelled later, later, later, because the horror was real but
the analysis was necessary and the analysis required the horror
to wait its turn.

"They have four hundred years of interdimensional conquest
experience," he said. His voice was clipped, precise, the
gnomish craft idiom surfacing in the way he struck each
consonant. "They have technology from eleven absorbed
civilisations. They have biomantic weapons, mana-stripped
divine energy, sky citadels, and a military commanded by a
being who personally destroyed multiple entities." He paused.
The pause was not the deep structural silence of the dwarven
hulks. It was the brief operational silence of a mind that had
reached a conclusion and was choosing how to articulate it.
"We have..." Another pause. "We have us."

Iskar processed through action. The flatness was gone from his
voice, replaced by something harder, something that had been
forged in the hours since the town and that was not yet fully
cooled. He was already planning: "Who do we warn first? Bastien.
He has the intelligence network and the pragmatism. Instar. They
have the magical resources and the evidence we brought them.
Blowing Rock. Lionous will commit without hesitation." The words
came in clipped sequences, the sailor planning a course through
hostile waters, the instinct for navigation applied to the
problem of organising a defence against an enemy that had
conquered eleven worlds. The performance had returned, but it
was thinner now, the theatrical warmth underlaid by something
that had not been there before: the hardness of a man who had
seen what human slavery looked like when it was complete and who
had decided, in the quiet part of himself that the performance
did not reach, that he would die before he allowed it to happen
in his world.

Lai was quiet. She sat apart from the others, her yellow eyes
moving between the speakers with the systematic attention that
was her resting state, cataloguing, assessing, filing conclusions
she did not share. When the debriefing reached its natural end,
when the facts had been stated and the assessments had been
offered and the implications had been allowed to settle into the
cave's silence like sediment into deep water, she said nothing.
She offered no connections. She provided no strategic insight.
She could have. A dragon with ties to Instar's Thaumaturgic
Council could have opened doors that remained closed to the
heroes, could have provided introductions and leveraged
relationships and deployed the considerable influence that her
upbringing among the most powerful mages in the Merchant Nations
had given her. She did not. Whether this was strategic patience,
personal reluctance to reveal her Instar connections, or
something else entirely was unclear, and the lack of clarity was,
for those who noticed it, its own kind of information.

---

Giganzalt watched the debriefing with the patience of a being
for whom patience was not a virtue but a biological state, the
default condition of a consciousness that had spent eight
centuries in a cave and had developed no urgency about anything
because urgency implied that time was limited, and time, for a
dragon whose age was measured in eons, was not limited. It was
merely slow.

When the debriefing ended, he spoke.

"The bargain," he said. The sibilants were measured, formal,
the register of negotiation rather than testimony. "I have
provided what I promissed. The hissstory. The military
sstructure. The capabilitiess. The weaknesssess, ssuch ass
they are." The blue eyes moved to Aizune. "You have what you
came for, noble elf. The quesstion now iss whether you can
carry what you came for, and me, acrrosss the boundary and
back to the world where the ssky hass the correct number of
moonss."

All eyes moved to Aleron.

The elf sat against the cavern wall. The mana engine had been
removed from his pack and sat on the stone beside him, humming
its steady civilian hum, and without it against his spine his
posture had changed, the slight forward lean that the device's
weight had imposed replaced by the natural slump of a body that
was conserving every reserve it possessed for the effort that
was about to be demanded of it. His hands trembled on the
quarterstaff. His skin was pale, not the dangerous translucence
that had followed the crossing but the settled pallor of a man
whose baseline had shifted downward and would not shift back.
The amulet pulsed green at his throat, steady, patient, doing
its work.

Both eyes were focused. Both grey irises were fixed on the
middle distance with the startling clarity that his companions
had learned to recognise as his most dangerous and most capable
state: the lucid window, the interval between echoes when the
fractures in his perception aligned and the intelligence behind
them was available, fully and sharply, to the present moment.

"I can carry you," he said. He addressed Giganzalt directly,
and the directness was itself a measure of his clarity, the
echoes for once not intervening between intention and speech.
"The transit will be the most demanding I have attempted. Six
people and a dragon across dimensional boundaries." He paused.
His eyes held the dragon's, grey meeting blue across the
distance of the cavern, a man who weighed perhaps ten stone
addressing a being that weighed perhaps a hundred thousand, and
the disparity of scale was irrelevant because the ability to
cross between worlds was not a function of mass but of will and
gift, and Aleron had both. "You will need to compress your form.
As small as you can manage. The transit does not move bodies. It
replaces realities. The less volume the replacement must account
for, the less the cost."

"I can compresss," Giganzalt said. "Ancient dragonss posssesss
the ability. I have not usssed it in centuriess." A slow
movement, the first significant motion of his body since the
heroes had arrived: his head turned, massive, the dust cascading
from his skull in pale streams, and the blue eyes found the
cavern walls and the ceiling and the floor that had been his
world for eight hundred years. "I do not imagine I will misss
thiss place."

"The cost," Aizune said. He was looking at Aleron. He had been
looking at Aleron since the elf began speaking, and the looking
carried the weight of everything the rooftop conversation in
Amara had established and everything the hours with Giganzalt
had deepened: the awareness that what Aleron was about to do
would cost him, and the cost would be paid in the currency that
his body could not replenish, and each payment brought him
closer to the ledger's end.

"The cost is mine," Aleron said. He said it without self-pity,
without drama, with the matter-of-fact tone of a man stating
the terms of a transaction he had agreed to long ago. "It has
always been mine. That is the nature of the gift."

"What do you need from us?" Smiggles asked. The question was
practical, precise, the question of a man who could not carry
the burden but could carry the things around the burden, clearing
the path so the burden-bearer could walk.

"Stay close. Touch me if you can. Speak my name if I..." The
lucidity flickered. One eye drifted. Came back. "If I am not
entirely present when the transit ends. The echoes will be...
considerable."

"I will be there," Aizune said.

---

Giganzalt compressed.

The process was not shapechanging in the way that Lyanorra's
transformation was shapechanging: instantaneous, fluid, the
body exchanging one form for another with the ease of a hand
opening and closing. This was something older and more difficult,
the forced reduction of a physical form that had grown across
eons of accretion, the reversal of a body's deepest imperative,
which was to become larger, to occupy more space, to define more
of the world by the fact of one's presence within it. Ancient
dragons could do it. They could not do it quickly or comfortably,
and the discomfort was visible in the way Giganzalt's body
shuddered as it began, the scales rippling along his flanks like
water over stone, the vast musculature tensing and compressing,
the wings folding in upon themselves with a sound like sails
being reefed in a gale.

The dust fell from him in curtains. Centuries of accumulated
stillness cascaded to the cavern floor, revealing the full
coloration of his scales: deep ocean blue, brilliant, the blue
of water at depth where sunlight fails, each scale catching the
dim geothermal glow and returning it as cold fire. The scars
along his left flank were visible now, silver-white against the
blue, and the missing horn-tip on his right side was a smooth
truncation that caught the light differently from the intact
horn beside it, the break so old that it had become a feature
rather than a wound.

He compressed. The body that filled the cavern reduced, the
massive frame folding inward with a controlled violence that
was the opposite of grace, the physical expression of a being
forcing itself into a space it was not designed to occupy. The
process took minutes. When it was finished, Giganzalt was the
size of a large horse: still enormous by any standard except
the one he had occupied moments before, still radiating the
subsonic resonance that was felt in the teeth and the sternum,
still carrying in his compressed form the weight of something
vastly larger that was being contained rather than reduced.

His eyes had not changed. Deep electric blue, ancient, carrying
the intelligence that eight centuries of isolation had sharpened
to a cutting edge. The eyes of an ancient dragon did not
compress. They were the one feature that the compression could
not diminish, because the eyes were not physical in the way that
scales and muscle and bone were physical. The eyes were the
visible expression of the consciousness behind them, and the
consciousness was ageless and irreducible and it looked out
from the compressed body with the same patient, devastating
attention that it had wielded from the full one.

"Thiss," Giganzalt said, and the voice was different, smaller,
the subsonic vibration reduced but not eliminated, the sibilants
carrying the same coiling quality in a register that was an
octave higher and profoundly wrong, the voice of a mountain
compressed into a boulder, "iss not comfortable."

"I know," Aleron said. "I am sorry."

"Do not be ssorry. Be quick."

---

Aleron stood.

The motion was deliberate: staff planted, weight transferred,
body ascending from the wall in a sequence that was planned
rather than natural, each joint considered, each muscle
directed. His companions watched. They had seen him stand a
hundred times and each time the standing was a negotiation
between the body's willingness and the will's insistence, and
the will always won, and the winning always cost something.

He took the tuning fork from his pouch. The pale silver tines
caught the cave's dim light and held it, and the hum that
lived below the threshold of hearing intensified, the fork's
bond with the distant reality of Mazariim responding to his
touch, the way a compass needle responded to the proximity of
north. He held the fork in his left hand. His right hand
found the cavern wall. The stone was warm.

"Close," he said. "Everyone close. Touch me. Touch each other.
Stay in contact."

They gathered. Smiggles placed a hand on Aleron's arm, the
gnomish fingers small and precise against the threadbare fabric
of the robe. Iskar placed a hand on Smiggles' shoulder, the
Sabonaar's long reach spanning the distance with ease. Lai
stood at the edge of the group, twenty paces back, and the
twenty paces were the buffer that her draconic nature and
Aleron's planar energies had established between them, the zone
of mutual repulsion that neither had ever breached.

She crossed it.

She walked forward through the twenty paces with the deliberate
stride of someone performing an act of will against an instinct
that was screaming at her to stop, and the screaming was not
metaphorical. The air between them thickened. Aleron's echoes
exploded. The carefully suppressed multiversal bleed that the
amulet contained surged against its bonds, the fractures in his
perception widening as her draconic mana-field collided with
his planar signature, two incompatible frequencies grinding
against each other like tectonic plates, and the grinding
produced heat and light and noise that only Aleron could perceive,
the cacophony of adjacent realities flooding through fractures
that the collision had forced wider.

His hands shook violently. His jaw clenched. One eye tracked
something that was not there. The other found her face, close
now, closer than she had ever been, the yellow vertical pupils
enormous and bright and carrying an expression that he had not
seen from her before: discomfort, yes, the physical wrongness
that his proximity caused her, but beneath the discomfort
something harder and more deliberate, the will of a dragon who
had decided that the buffer would not prevent her from doing
what was necessary.

She placed her hand on his shoulder. The contact was a spark.
The echoes roared.

"Go," she said. "Now."

Giganzalt, compressed and enormous and deeply uncomfortable,
pressed his flank against Iskar's back. The subsonic resonance
of his body vibrated through the chain of contact: dragon to
bard to gnome to elf to fork to wall to stone to the frequency
that lived between realities.

Aleron closed his eyes. The fork sang. The note was not sound
but displacement, the sensation of reality bending around a
fixed point, the dimensions of the cave stretching and
compressing simultaneously as the fork's calibrated frequency
found the seam between this world and the world they had left.
The air distorted. The dim light of the cave shifted from warm
to cold, the geothermal glow replaced by something thinner,
sharper, the light of a transition that was about to occur.

The crossing took everything he had.

Not most of what he had. Not the majority, the significant
portion, the amount that left something in reserve for the
recovery that would follow. Everything. The transit of six
people had cost him. The transit of six people and a dragon
whose compressed mass still registered in the dimensional
fabric as something equivalent to a small mountain cost him
everything, the complete expenditure of whatever vitality the
gift drew upon, the final coin from a purse that had been
emptying for seventy years.

The cave vanished. The stone vanished. The warmth vanished.
The three moons of Ta'Puh vanished.

Cold. Stone. Different stone. The smell of pine and granite
and the thin sharp air of altitude, but a different altitude,
a different mountain, a mountain that belonged to a world
where the sky had two moons and the entities were alive and
the elves were free and the dwarves were people and the humans
chose their own lives and the silence at Aizune's throat was
about to end.

Mazariim.

---

Aleron collapsed.

The word was inadequate. Collapse implied a structure that
retained its components while losing its arrangement, a
building that fell but whose bricks remained. What happened
to Aleron was closer to dissolution: his legs ceased to
support him and his staff clattered to the stone and his
body folded in upon itself with the boneless descent of
something whose internal architecture had been withdrawn, and
he went down on the rock of a mountain east of Amara in the
world he had been born in with a sound that was not impact
but exhalation, the final breath of a body that had given
everything the crossing demanded and had nothing left.

His skin went translucent. The veins at his temples and wrists
stood out like ink through parchment, blue-dark against the
grey-white of flesh that had surrendered its opacity. His hands
shook with a vibration that was not trembling but seizing, the
fine motor control that the quarterstaff had anchored for
decades overwhelmed by the dimensional aftermath, the echoes
not merely present but dominant, the fractures in his
perception blown wide open by the effort of carrying seven
beings across the boundary between realities.

He murmured. The words were fragments, pieces of conversations
that belonged to realities adjacent to this one, responses to
questions that had been asked in worlds where the questions
made sense and the answers mattered and the person answering
was not lying on a mountainside with his mind distributed across
a dozen overlapping planes of existence. "No... the door is not
... I told you, the current is... please, I cannot hear you
when you all... all speak at..."

Aizune knelt beside him. His hand found Aleron's. His other
hand found the elf's face, turning it, seeking the grey eyes
that were open and seeing everything and nothing and the
distinction between the two had collapsed along with the body.

"Aleron. You are here. You are in Mazariim. The stone is real.
The cold is real. I am real."

The clasp flared.

The cold that had lived at his throat for hours, the absence
of Minerva's presence that had been the constant ache of every
moment in Ta'Puh, ignited. The silver went from ice to warmth
in the space between one heartbeat and the next, and the warmth
was not temperature but connection, the thread that ran from
priest to entity across the dimensional architecture of Elysium
finding its other end, finding the living entity, finding the
vast patient luminous presence that had been silent for hours
and was not silent now.

Minerva.

The sensation flooded through him with a force that made him
gasp: warmth and light and the particular quality of awareness
that Minerva's sight conferred, the divine lens through which
the world revealed its deeper structure, the hidden connections,
the spiritual currents, the meaning beneath the surface. It
poured into the space that the silence had hollowed out, and
the space was larger than it had been before the crossing
because the silence had expanded it, the absence had stretched
the boundaries of what the presence could fill, and the filling
was overwhelming, and the overwhelming was joy, and the joy
was the first thing Aizune had felt that was not analysis or
discipline or the stubborn refusal to despair since the cave
in the mountains of a conquered world.

He pressed his hand against Aleron's forehead and the warmth
flowed through the contact, Minerva's healing channelled
through a priest whose connection had been restored, and the
silver-white light bloomed from his palm for the first time
in hours, clean and fierce and gentle, the light of an entity
who was alive in this world and whose gift moved through the
hands of a priest who had carried her teaching into a reality
where she was dead and had brought it back, and the bringing
back was its own kind of prayer.

Aleron's trembling eased. The translucence receded. The
murmuring subsided, the fragments of adjacent conversations
fading as the amulet reasserted its containment and the
fractures narrowed and the echoes withdrew from dominance to
their usual murmur, the constant background noise of a life
that had never been quiet and would not be quiet but was, in
this moment, quieter, steadied by the warmth that flowed from
a priest's hand and the presence of an entity whose light
reached across the dimensional architecture of the world and
found, in a mountainside east of Amara, a servant who had
earned its comfort.

"Rest," Aizune said. His voice was thick. "Rest."

---

Giganzalt unfolded.

The compression released with a violence that was the inverse
of the discipline that had imposed it: scales expanding, wings
extending, the massive body reclaiming the space it had been
denied with the explosive force of a thing returning to its
natural state after a prolonged and unnatural confinement. The
mountain ledge where they had arrived was broad enough to
accommodate the group but not broad enough to accommodate a
dragon, and Giganzalt's unfolding redefined the ledge's
boundaries, his body extending over the edges, his wings
spreading beyond the rock into the open air of a Mazariim
mountainside.

The wings.

They spread. Two hundred feet of membrane and bone and the
deep ocean blue of scales that had not seen open sky in eight
centuries, extending from his body in a motion that was not a
stretch but a reclamation, the physical expression of a being
recovering something that had been taken from it so long ago
that the recovery felt like a first experience rather than a
return. The wings caught the wind, the mountain wind of
Mazariim that carried pine and granite and the distant salt of
the sea and the warmth of a world whose entities were alive
and whose sky held two moons, silver and gold, and between
them nothing, nothing but open air, and the open air was the
most beautiful thing Giganzalt had seen in eight hundred years.

The sound he made was not a roar.

It was quieter than a roar and more devastating. It was the
exhalation of a being who had been holding his breath for
eight hundred years, and the breath had been held not in his
lungs but in his spirit, the compression of a consciousness
that had been confined to a cave and a mountain and a world
that was not his and that had reduced him from a being that
flew to a being that hid, and the exhalation released all of
it, the centuries of patience that were not patience but
endurance, the decades of stillness that were not stillness
but imprisonment, the years of watching the same stone walls
and breathing the same stale air and feeling the same
unrelenting pressure of a world that would kill him if it
found him, released into the wind of a world that would not.

The sound carried across the mountains. Birds, startled from
pines hundreds of feet below, scattered into the sky. The
echoes returned from distant peaks, diminished and multiplied,
and the sound of a dragon's relief bouncing from mountain to
mountain was, for those who heard it, a thing that would live
in memory long after the reason for it was forgotten.

Giganzalt did not fly. Not yet. The group needed to decide
where he would go. He was too large and too powerful to enter
a city, too visible to move in daylight without drawing
attention that would complicate the heroes' already complicated
situation.

"The mountains," Smiggles said. "He stays in the mountains
until we have a plan."

Lai, who had been quiet, who had been quiet in the Orceri town
and quiet during the debriefing and quiet during the transit
and quiet during Aleron's collapse and quiet during Giganzalt's
unfolding, spoke.

"I know a place," she said. She did not elaborate. The yellow
eyes held no explanation and offered none. The words were
simple and carried the particular weight of someone who
possessed information they had not been asked for and were
not prepared to justify, and the offer was the offer, and the
alternative was the mountains, and the mountains were cold
and exposed and offered a dragon nothing that a dragon needed
except the sky.

Giganzalt regarded her. The blue eyes that had not changed
during the compression, the ancient intelligence that
occupied a body of any size with the same devastating attention,
found the young green dragon who stood in her wood elf form on
a mountain ledge in a world she had never left and met her
gaze with the particular focus of a being assessing an offer
from a being it did not yet understand.

"Lead, then, hatchling," he said. The sibilants were gentle.

---

The ride back to Amara took the remainder of the day.

They acquired horses at a waystation in the foothills, the
transaction unremarkable, the coins Mazariim-minted and the
currency correct and the act of buying horses in one's own
world with one's own money in one's own body a normality that
felt, after the hours in Ta'Puh, like a benediction. Aleron
rode rather than walked, his body draped across the saddle with
the precarious stability of a man whose consciousness was
present but whose body was making it clear that consciousness
was a courtesy it was extending temporarily and could revoke
without notice. Aizune rode beside him, one hand on the reins
and the other intermittently reaching across to steady the elf
when the echoes made him list.

The road to Amara descended from the foothills through the
farmland east of the city, and the farmland was the first thing
that looked different.

Not physically different. The fields were the same fields they
had ridden past on the journey out: wheat and barley and the
winter vegetables that the Sabonaar farmers planted in the
coastal soil and harvested in early spring. The stone walls
that divided the fields were the same stone walls, built by the
same hands, maintained by the same families whose names were
known at the market stalls where their produce was sold. The
road was the same road, rutted by cart traffic, bordered by
wildflowers that grew in the verge because no one had decided
to prevent them from growing.

The difference was in the seeing.

Iskar looked at the fields and saw humans working them. Free
humans. Sabonaar farmers with calloused hands and sunburned
faces and the particular posture of people who were tired from
labour they had chosen, labour that fed their families and
filled their stalls and gave them the dignity of effort applied
to purpose. They looked up as the riders passed. They nodded.
One woman, straightening from a row of winter onions with a
hand pressed to her lower back, raised a hand in greeting, and
the greeting was ordinary and meaningless and was the most
important gesture Iskar had seen in a day that had included the
technological achievements of a civilisation that spanned
realities.

She chose to wave. The choice was hers. The hand was hers. The
field was hers.

Iskar raised his hand in return, and the Sabonaar sailor's
courtesy that he extended to every woman he met, "my lady"
offered with warmth that was equal parts performance and
genuine kindness, was not spoken, because his throat was tight,
and the tightness was not grief but its opposite, the
constriction that occurred when something one loved was seen
clearly for the first time, seen against the backdrop of its
absence, and the seeing confirmed its value in a way that
familiarity had never permitted.

Amara appeared at the road's end: the harbour, the Market
District, the spires of the Blessed Halls of Eternal Light
catching the late afternoon sun, the whole city rendered in
the golden light that came from the bay and the river
conspiring to illuminate everything they touched. The city
received them with its usual indifference. Carts rumbled.
Merchants called. The harbour bell rang the afternoon watch.
Gulls circled above the fishing boats. The Ogre's Arse
sagged in its alley. Yella's cooking drifted from the
narrow storefront between the cobbler and the candle-maker.

The ordinary machinery of a city that did not know what was
coming, turning as it had always turned.

Every street. Every market stall. Every child chasing a cat
through the legs of a fishmonger.

Precious.

The word arrived in Aizune's mind with the force of
revelation, which was strange because it was not a new thought
but an old one seen from a new angle, the way a familiar
landscape looked different when the light changed. The city
was precious. The people in it were precious. The fishmonger
and the child and the cat and the woman selling oranges from
a basket and the dwarf sharpening a blade on a whetstone in
the doorway of Underhill's and the halfling sweeping the step
of the Hearthstone temple and the sound of a concertina
drifting from somewhere in the Dock District, all of it,
every unremarkable detail of a city that had never been
remarkable and had never needed to be, because the
unremarkable was the thing that the Orceri consumed, the
ordinary was the thing that the Orceri replaced with the
clean streets and the steady lights and the grey tunics, and
the ordinary was worth more than anything the Orceri had
built on the ruins of it.

Aizune watched the harbour lights. The sun was lowering. The
water held the gold and the gold held the light and the light
was warm, and the warmth was Minerva's, and Minerva was alive,
and the clasp at his throat was warm.

He thought about Elysium burning.

He thought about the ash blowing out to sea.

He thought about the sound a world makes when the thing that
holds reality together flinches, as though the universe itself
felt pain.

And he thought about the city below him, golden in the late
light, ordinary and precious and unaware, and the thought
settled into him beside the knowledge that Giganzalt had given
him and the faith that Minerva sustained in him and the
certainty, quiet and immovable, that what he had learned in
the cave and what he had seen in the town and what he carried
in his journal would be the foundation on which he and his
companions built whatever defence their world required.

The riders descended toward the harbour. The city opened to
receive them. The harbour bell rang.

And the sky above Amara held two moons, silver and gold, and
between them the space was empty, and the emptiness was not
absence but promise, the promise of a sky that had not yet
been violated, a world that had not yet been conquered, a
future that had not yet been written.

The promise held.

For now.
