# Chapter Five
## The Room of Mirrors

The excavation was a wound in the keep's floor.

Someone had taken a hammer and chisel to the flagstones in the
northwest corner of the ground level, breaking through the crude
surface construction into the older stonework beneath, and the
difference between the two was immediately and unsettlingly apparent.
The keep's floor was rough-cut granite, poorly fitted, mortar
crumbling in the joints. What lay beneath was something else entirely:
dark stone, smooth and dense, veined with channels of tarnished metal
that caught Minerva's light and threw it back in faint geometric
patterns. The excavation hole was roughly five feet across, its edges
jagged where the orcs had broken through, and a crude rope ladder
dangled from an iron spike driven into the floor above. The air that
rose from the opening was cold, still, and heavy with the scent of
copper and old stone and something sharper beneath both: ozone,
metallic and electric, the residue of magic that had been worked in a
confined space and never fully dissipated.

Smiggles lay flat at the edge and looked down, his darkvision
resolving what the others could not see. The rope ladder descended
into darkness that was absolute for Aizune and Iskar but merely dim
for the gnome. His hazel eyes traced the walls of the shaft the orcs
had opened: rough at the top where they had broken through,
transitioning within a few feet to smooth, dark stone lined with
channels of copper and silver running in precise geometric lines that
were too regular, too deliberate, to be natural mineral veining.
Someone had placed them there. Someone had carved channels into stone
and filled them with metal and inscribed them with patterns whose
craftsmanship suggested a level of skill that the orcs above could not
have imagined, let alone achieved.

"Draconic," he said.

He adjusted his angle, squinted deeper into the shaft where the
inscriptions grew denser. His voice had shifted registers, the crude
bluntness replaced by the precise, clipped diction of formal arcane
analysis. "The inscriptions in the channels. Draconic script. Thousands
of characters. I can read fragments from here." He paused, tilted his
head. "Resonance formulae. Planar harmonic calculations. Transmutation
matrices for converting raw stone into a stable crystalline structure."
Another pause, longer. "Whoever built this was working at a level of
theoretical sophistication I have seen in textbooks but never in
practice. This is not a basement. This is a laboratory."

Aizune crouched beside him. From his position he could see the first
few feet of the shaft where Minerva's light reached, the beginning of
the dark stone, the glint of metal channels. Beyond that, only shadow.
"How old?" he asked.

"The copper has oxidised but the silver hasn't, which means the
channels were treated with a stabilising agent before the metal was
poured. That kind of preparation adds decades to metal life in a
sealed environment. The stonework itself shows no structural
degradation. Given the depth, the climate, and the quality of the
transmutation work on the walls..." He trailed off, calculating. "Five
hundred years. Minimum. Possibly significantly more."

"Pre-War," Aizune said quietly.

"Pre-War. Pre-Dukhan collapse. This was built when the Zebulban Empire
was still standing." Smiggles pulled back from the edge and sat up,
brushing dust from his waistcoat with absent, mechanical motions. His
expression was the expression of a man whose professional curiosity had
just collided with his professional caution, and the collision had not
yet produced a clear winner. "Aizune. Whoever built this was a mage of
extraordinary ability working in secret, probably hiding from the
Zebulbans. The passages through the walls, the hidden construction, the
location in the middle of nowhere. This is exactly what we speculated:
a fugitive wizard's sanctuary. But the work down there is beyond
anything a fugitive should have been able to accomplish alone. The
resonance formulae alone would have taken years to develop. The
transmutation of the walls, years more. This was someone's life's
work."

"And the machine," Aizune said.

"And the machine." Smiggles met his eyes. "Kru'ka'ta's notes mentioned
a machine. I want to see it. I also want to be very clear that we are
about to descend into an unknown magical environment built by a dead
wizard of exceptional power, in which two orcs have already died, and
that my professional recommendation is that we proceed with extreme
caution and a healthy appreciation for the fact that dead wizards'
laboratories are, historically, among the most dangerous places in the
world."

"Noted," Aizune said.

From behind them, Iskar spoke. He had been standing at the edge of the
excavation with his arms folded and his hat pushed back, studying the
darkness below with the expression of a man evaluating the depth of
unfamiliar waters. "I have a question."

"Go ahead."

"The two orcs who went down and didn't come back. What killed them?"

A silence followed in which no one offered a reassuring answer.

"Right," Iskar said. He drew his rapier with a sound that was almost
musical, the blade singing faintly against the scabbard's throat. "I'll
go first this time."

"You will not," Aizune said.

"I went last into the passages. Fair is fair."

"Fair has nothing to do with it. My senses are attuned to magical
threats, and I have the blade for what we might find down there." His
hand moved to the hilt of the elven curved blade at his hip, the sword
the armoursmiths called spirit-touched, its edge forged to bite where
ordinary steel passed through empty air. Against incorporeal creatures,
shadows and wraiths and the bodiless dead, a conventional weapon was
little more than a gesture. This blade was something else. "I go
first."

"And if whatever killed those orcs is waiting at the bottom?"

"Then I will be the first to know. And I will be armed for it."

Iskar considered this. Then he smiled, a real smile, not the
performance he wore for strangers but the smaller, warmer expression
that surfaced when something genuinely amused him. "Fair winds, then,
Silvershine. Lead the way."

"I said do not call me that."

"And I said it's stuck."

"Smiggles, you go second," Aizune said. "Your darkvision gives us eyes
if my light fails. Iskar, rear guard."

"The blind man watching the rearr," Iskar said. "What a comforrting
arrangement."

Aizune descended the rope ladder into the dark.

---

The shaft opened into a corridor that should not have existed.

The transition from the excavation's rough edges to the finished
stonework below was abrupt: one moment Aizune's boots were finding
purchase on broken stone and crude iron rungs, the next they were
touching a floor that was perfectly smooth, perfectly level, and faintly
warm beneath his soles. He whispered a prayer, barely more than a
breath, and a soft silvery light bloomed from his open palm, Minerva's
gift, cool and gentle, illuminating the passage without the harsh glare
that might seep through cracks in the stonework above. The corridor
extended in both directions, curving gently, its walls lined with the
same dark transmuted stone he had seen from above, the copper and
silver channels tracing geometric patterns that covered every surface
in dense, precise script. The ceiling was low, perhaps eight feet, and
the air was utterly still, sealed from the wind and weather of the
Steppes above by hundreds of years of undisturbed stone.

And there was light.

Not his light. Not Minerva's silver glow, which still burned in his
palm. Another light, fainter, steadier, coming from somewhere ahead and
to the left: a warm amber radiance that pulsed with the quality of a
held breath, patient and constant.

Smiggles dropped from the ladder behind him, landing with barely a
sound, his darkvision already mapping the corridor beyond the reach of
Aizune's divine light. "The amber glow continues around the curve," he
said quietly. "Passage is clear for at least forty paces. The
inscriptions get denser ahead." He paused, and when he spoke again the
formal register had taken his voice entirely. "Permanent flame. Tied to
a stable mana source within the stone itself. The copper channels are
probably serving double duty as both inscription medium and power
conduit." His voice was hushed, not with fear but with the particular
reverence that a craftsman brings to the contemplation of superior
work. "Whoever built this invested the time and power to create
illumination that would outlast their own life by centuries. That
doesn't just calibrate with a fugitive. That's someone who intended
this place to endure."

Iskar descended last, his long frame requiring him to navigate the
narrow shaft with the creative contortion of a man accustomed to
fitting through spaces designed for smaller people. They stood together
in the corridor, three abreast, and looked at what five hundred years
of silence had preserved.

"Minerva willing," Aizune breathed.

The Draconic script on the walls was not merely inscription. It was
language. Thousands upon thousands of characters, each one no larger
than a fingernail, carved with a jeweller's precision and filled with
metal that still gleamed after centuries. Aizune could read Draconic,
as could any educated priest whose divine tradition required it, and
the fragments he could parse in passing were staggering: equations
describing the vibrational frequencies of material planes, calculations
for the conversion of consciousness into directed energy, theoretical
frameworks for the observation of parallel realities. He was reading
the marginalia of a mind that had grasped the architecture of the
universe and tried to build a window into it.

"This way," he said, moving toward the amber light.

The corridor curved, opened, and delivered them to a doorway.

Beyond the doorway, the mirrors waited.

---

The room was large. Aizune estimated thirty paces across, roughly
circular, with a ceiling that rose into shadow beyond the reach of both
his divine light and the amber glow of the permanent flames that burned
in three alcoves along the walls. The flames were spheres of warm amber
light, hovering where wicks had once been, casting a steady
illumination that painted the room in shades of gold and deep shadow.

And every shadow contained a face.

Mirrors filled the chamber. Dozens of them, mounted on walls and
freestanding frames and propped against stone surfaces in arrangements
that had long since lost any pretence of order. Some were tall,
full-length mirrors in frames of dark metal. Others were small, no
larger than a man's hand, set into brackets on the walls. A few lay
flat on the floor, their surfaces facing the ceiling like pools of
still water. They were of different sizes, different shapes, different
ages, but they shared one quality that Aizune recognised the moment his
eyes adjusted to the strange doubled light of flame and reflection:
they were all enchanted. The air in the room hummed with it, a low,
constant vibration that he felt in his teeth and the base of his skull,
the residual mana of enchantments that had been built to last and had
kept their promise across five centuries of solitude.

In every mirror, something moved.

Not reflections. Aizune could see his own reflection in none of them.
Instead, each mirror contained what he could only describe as a window:
a view into somewhere else, somewhere that was not this room and not
this world. In the nearest mirror, a tall, full-length glass in a frame
of tarnished bronze, a man worked a farm. Dark-haired, olive-skinned,
his sleeves rolled to the elbow, he swung a scythe through golden wheat
with the easy rhythm of someone who had done this every day of his
life. In the mirror beside it, the same face, older, stood at a podium
before a crowd, gesturing with the confidence of an orator, his hair
silver but his back straight, his robes white and unmarked. In a third,
the face was scarred and hard beneath a heavy helm, armour scored with
the marks of battles fought and survived.

The same man. The same face. Living different lives in different
mirrors, unaware of each other, unaware of being watched, unaware of
the dark room where their images danced for an audience that had not
existed for five hundred years.

"It's the same person," Iskar said. His voice was barely above a
whisper, and the theatrical warmth was gone from it entirely. He stood
before a mirror that showed the man as a merchant, counting coins in a
sunlit room, and his dark eyes moved from one mirror to the next with
the expression of someone whose understanding of the world was being
quietly and irrevocably expanded. "Every mirror. The same man, living a
different life."

"Alternate realities," Smiggles said.

The words fell into the room like stones into still water. Aizune and
Iskar both turned to look at him. The gnome was standing before a small
mirror mounted on the far wall, his hazel eyes reflecting the amber
light, his face stripped of its usual sardonic animation. He was not
joking. He was not performing. He was thinking, rapidly and deeply, and
the conclusions he was reaching were visible in the progressive
tightening of his jaw.

"Alternate material planes," he continued, his voice dropping into the
register of formal arcane analysis that signalled he was speaking from
training rather than speculation. "The resonance formulae on the walls
describe a method for tuning observation to specific planar frequencies.
Each mirror is calibrated to a different frequency. Each frequency
corresponds to a different version of the material plane. A different
reality." He turned from the mirror and looked at them both. "The
wizard who built this place discovered how to look into parallel worlds.
And he spent years, decades, building mirrors to watch one man's life
play out across infinite variations."

"His own life," Aizune said.

"His own life. That's his face in every mirror. He was watching himself.
Every version of himself he could find." Smiggles paused. Something
moved across his expression that was not analytical, something that
understood the human cost of what they were looking at: a man, alone
underground, watching the lives he might have lived and could not reach.
"He was looking for something. Or someone."

The room was quiet except for the hum of the enchantments and the
faint, ghostly sounds that leaked from the mirrors: the distant whisper
of wheat in wind, the murmur of a crowd, the ring of steel on steel.
Ambient noise from other worlds, filtering through glass that should
not have been transparent to anything at all.

Aizune felt the cold before he understood it.

It came from behind them, from the corridor they had entered through, a
sudden drop in temperature that was sharp and localised and wrong. The
ambient chill of the underground gave way to something deeper,
something that bit at exposed skin and turned breath to vapour. The
amber flames in their alcoves guttered. Just once. Just for a moment.

The shadows in the mirrors changed.

In the nearest glass, the farmer stopped mid-swing and turned toward
the surface of the mirror, his expression blank, his eyes unfocused. In
the mirror beside it, the orator paused mid-gesture and turned. In a
third, the soldier lowered his sword and stared outward with the empty
attention of a man hearing a sound too faint to identify. One by one,
across the room, the images of the wizard's alternate selves paused in
their endless routines and oriented toward the glass, toward the room,
toward the three people standing among them.

"That is not supposed to happen," Smiggles said.

The cold deepened. Aizune's divine senses screamed. Something was in
the room with them, something incorporeal, something that existed in
the spaces between the mirrors' reflections, moving through shadow with
the patience of centuries and the hunger of an open wound. He could not
see it. He could feel it: a presence, vast and diffuse, pressing
against the boundaries of the physical world from a place that was
adjacent to it but not quite part of it. The permanent flames guttered
again. The nearest mirror's surface rippled, its image distorting, the
farmer's face stretching and blurring as though something behind the
glass were pushing forward.

"The dead orcs," Iskar said. His rapier was in his hand, his stance
low, his eyes sweeping the room with the quick, calculating movements
of a man assessing threats he could not see. "The ones Kru'ka'ta wrote
about. Frozen. Screaming. Emaciated."

"Strength drain," Smiggles said. His crossbow was up, but his eyes were
on the mirrors, tracking the movement of shadows that did not
correspond to anything in the room. "Incorporeal undead. Shadow-class
or wraith-class, feeding on life force. The mirrors are its habitat. It
lives in the reflections."

"Can you hit it?"

"If it manifests, I can hit it with force effects. Bolts of pure force.
Nothing else touches incorporeal creatures reliably." He glanced at
Aizune's hip. "Except that blade of his."

Aizune's hand was already on the hilt of the spirit-touched blade. The
pale luminescence along its edge had brightened without his bidding, the
enchantment responding to the presence of something that existed between
the planes, the precise kind of enemy the blade had been forged to
fight. In the dim amber light of the chamber, the sword's edge glowed
with a cold, steady gleam that had nothing to do with reflected flame,
a light that came from the steel itself, from the magic woven into its
forging, the enchantment that allowed it to bite into things that had
no flesh and draw blood from things that had no blood.

"If it comes at us," Aizune said, drawing the blade, "stay behind me."

The cold closed like a fist.

Iskar gasped, his back arching, his free hand clutching at his chest.
The thing had come from behind, from the space between two mirrors
where the shadows overlapped, and it struck with a speed that gave no
warning and permitted no defense. For a moment, less than a heartbeat,
Aizune saw it: a shape in the shadow behind Iskar, darker than the
darkness, a silhouette that was almost human and was not, its outline
flickering with the same amber shimmer that the mirrors carried at
their edges. It had no face. It had no features. It was an absence, a
hole in the air shaped like a man, and where it touched Iskar's shadow,
the bard's body shuddered and weakened.

Aizune moved.

The spirit-touched blade carved through the space where the silhouette
writhed against Iskar's back, and the steel found something. Not flesh.
Not bone. Something colder and thinner, like cutting through a current
of freezing water that resisted the edge for a fraction of a second
before parting. The blade's pale glow flared white as it connected, the
enchantment blazing with a sudden fierce light, and the silhouette
shrieked. The sound was not audible in the ordinary sense. It was felt:
a vibration in the teeth, in the spine, in the marrow, the voiceless
scream of something that had no throat and no lungs and had been cut
by a weapon that should not have been able to touch it.

The thing tore free of Iskar and recoiled, fragments of its darkness
scattering like ink dropped in water. Aizune pressed forward, the
spirit-touched blade tracing silver arcs in the amber light, driving
the shadow back toward the mirrors. Each stroke that connected produced
the same blazing flare, the same voiceless shriek, the same sensation
of cutting through something that was real and was not, that occupied
space and did not, that fled from the enchanted edge with the
desperate speed of a predator that had suddenly become prey.

Silver-white light blazed from his free hand, Minerva's radiance
joining the blade's glow, and he drove the shadow into the mirrors
with the focused intensity of a priest whose faith was not abstract but
immediate, a weapon forged from conviction and wielded without
hesitation. The light struck the silhouette and it fragmented further,
dissolving into the mirrors like smoke pulled through a dozen keyholes,
the amber-edged darkness retreating into the glass surfaces and the
reflections beyond them, fleeing from the twin assault of divine light
and spirit-touched steel.

The cold retreated. The amber flames steadied. The images in the
mirrors returned to their routines, the alternate selves resuming their
lives as though they had never been interrupted.

Iskar's knees buckled. He caught himself on a mirror's frame, his
rapier hand trembling, his face drained of colour. "Something," he said
through clenched teeth, "just reached inside me and took a piece."

"Sit." Aizune was at his side, one hand on his shoulder, the
spirit-touched blade still in his other hand, its glow fading slowly
from the fierce white of combat to the steady pale luminescence of
readiness. Healing magic flowed before the prayer was fully formed.
The warmth of Minerva's gift pushed back the chill in Iskar's body,
restoring the vitality that had been stolen. Not all of it. The
restoration was partial. Whatever the shadow had taken, it had taken
deep.

"I am all right," Iskar said. His voice was steady but his eyes were
not. He looked at the mirrors with the expression of a man who had just
been robbed by something he could not fight, and for the first time
since Aizune had met him, the Sabonaar's composure showed cracks. "What
in the name of every salt-cursed sea was that?"

"The Whisper," Smiggles said. He had not moved from his position, his
crossbow still raised, his eyes still tracking the mirrors. His voice
was flat and calm in the way that Smiggles' voice became flat and calm
when a situation had exceeded the threshold of what sarcasm could
address. "That's what killed Kru'ka'ta's orcs. It feeds on life force.
It lives in the mirrors. And it will come back."

"Then let it come," Aizune said. The spirit-touched blade hummed faintly
in his grip, the enchantment still alert, still hungry, the steel
remembering the thing it had cut and ready to cut it again. "It learned
something just now. It learned that it can be hurt."

"It can be hurt," Smiggles agreed. "But can it be killed? That blade
wounded it. Drove it off. I don't know if it destroyed it. Incorporeal
undead bound to an anchor, the mirrors, the enchantments, five hundred
years of accumulated mana, that's a deep well to drain."

"Then we move," Aizune said. "Quickly. And we stay close."

They moved. Past the mirrors, through the chamber, toward the amber
light that burned steadier and brighter in the passage beyond. The dead
orcs were where Kru'ka'ta's notes had predicted: two bodies on the
floor near the far wall, frozen in postures of absolute horror, their
mouths open, their eyes wide, their bodies emaciated beyond anything
starvation could produce. The Whisper had drained them completely.
Aizune paused long enough to confirm they were dead, not undead, and
moved on. The spirit-touched blade stayed in his hand, its edge
glowing.

The corridor beyond the mirror chamber descended in a gentle slope,
curving left, the copper channels in the walls growing denser, the
Draconic inscriptions more elaborate, as though they were approaching
the heart of whatever this place had been built to contain. The ozone
scent grew stronger. The hum in the air intensified, a vibration that
passed through the stone and into the bones of Aizune's feet and up
through his legs, resonating in the chambers of his chest with a
frequency that was almost musical.

The passage opened into the machine room.

---

Aizune had seen temples. He had stood in the Blessed Halls of Eternal
Light in Amara, where the ceiling rose sixty feet above polished marble
and the morning sun struck the western windows in bars of gold and
crimson. He had visited the Halls of Iron, where dwarven engineering
married stone and steel into spaces that made the air itself feel
heavier, more purposeful, weighted with the accumulated devotion of
centuries.

This room was not a temple. But it shared with temples the quality of
having been built for something larger than the person who built it,
designed to contain an act of profound ambition or profound faith, and
the distinction between the two was not always clear.

The chamber was roughly forty paces across and twice the height of a
man. The walls were the same smooth dark stone as the corridors,
transmuted from the native sandstone into something harder, denser,
veined with copper and silver channels that traced geometric patterns of
extraordinary complexity across every surface. Three permanent flames
burned in alcoves spaced evenly around the room, their amber light
steady and warm, casting the chamber in gold and deep shadow. The
Draconic script that covered the walls was denser here than anywhere
else in the complex, thousands of characters arranged in interlocking
sequences that Aizune could not read at speed but whose structure
suggested mathematical formulae of staggering scope.

And in the centre of the room, the machine.

Three great rings of dark metal stood along the room's borders, each
one twice the height of Aizune, each inscribed on every surface with
Draconic runes so fine they seemed etched by light rather than tool.
The channels between the runes were filled with powder that caught the
amber light and threw it back in muted colour: deep red in the
innermost ring, blue in the second, clear white in the third. The rings
were mounted on mechanisms of dark bronze that Smiggles, who had
stopped breathing the moment he entered the room, identified in a
whisper as "transmuted alloy, self-lubricating, precision-engineered
beyond anything I've seen outside a gnomish workshop, and I grew up in
a gnomish workshop." Each ring was mounted on a different axis: one
vertical, one horizontal, one diagonal, their paths intersecting at a
single point in the chamber's heart.

There, on a pedestal of fused quartz, sat a glass globe no larger than
a man's head. Its surface was so smooth, so perfectly formed, that it
seemed less like glass and more like a captured bubble of still water. A
single crack ran along one meridian, thin as a hair, the only
imperfection in an object that otherwise defied the limitations of its
material.

Two bodies lay on the floor near the pedestal.

The first was human: a man in simple robes, lying on his side with his
arms drawn up to his chest and his face turned toward the globe. He had
been dead for some time. His flesh was withered, dried, pulled tight
against the bones of his face in a manner that spoke not of
decomposition but of the same draining that had killed the orcs in the
mirror room. His robes were plain, unadorned, the robes of a monk or an
ascetic. His hair, dark with Sabonaar colouring, was pulled back from
his face. Despite the desiccation, his expression was oddly peaceful,
the face of a man who had seen something in his final moments that had,
perhaps, been worth the cost.

The second body was something else entirely.

It was an orc. But it was not like the orcs they had fought in the keep
above, not like any orc Aizune had ever seen or read about or been
taught to recognise. This orc was clean. Its skin, a lighter green than
the tribal warriors upstairs, was smooth and well-maintained. Its hair
was neatly trimmed and combed back from its face. Its hands, visible
where they lay at its sides, were manicured, the nails trimmed short
and clean. It wore armour of a dark metal that Aizune did not recognise,
fitted with a precision that suggested measurement and craftsmanship
rather than the crude shaping that characterised orcish equipment. A
weapon lay beside it: a short, heavy blade with an edge that still
gleamed despite whatever time had passed since its wielder's death. The
blade's metal was wrong. It was too fine, too light, too perfectly
balanced. It was made of something that should not exist in an orc's
hand.

"Sangre y sal," Iskar whispered. He crouched beside the body, his dark
eyes moving over the details with the practised assessment of a man who
had spent years evaluating people and their possessions. "Look at this.
Look at the craftsmanship. This armour is better than anything I've
seen in Amara. Better than anything in the Wayward Warrior or
Underhill's." He lifted the weapon, tested its balance, and his
eyebrows rose. "This blade is adamantine. I'd stake my hat on it.
Adamantine, and forged with a skill that..." He trailed off, turning
the weapon in the amber light. "I don't know who made this. But they
weren't orcs. Not our orcs."

"Oy," Smiggles said softly, which was unusual, because Smiggles said
most things loudly. He was already examining the body's other
possessions. A pouch at its belt contained coins: round, heavy, stamped
with symbols that were not Amaran, not Instari, not from any mint in
the Merchant Nations. The metal of the coins was unfamiliar. A small
device on its wrist, resembling a bracelet of dark metal and crystal,
that Smiggles turned over in his hands with the expression of a man
holding a puzzle whose pieces did not belong to any puzzle he had ever
encountered.

"This isn't from here," Smiggles said. His voice was quiet, the formal
register, all trace of humour burned away. "None of this is from here.
The alloy in the armour, the composition of the coins, the crystalline
structure in this device. I can identify every metal and mineral
available in the Merchant Nations and most of what's available in the
Inner Kingdoms and the dwarven realms. None of it calibrates. This is
something else. This is from somewhere else entirely."

"Another world," Aizune said.

They looked at each other. They looked at the mirrors in the room
behind them, the dead farmer and orator and soldier still living their
parallel lives in parallel glass. They looked at the machine, the three
great rings and the cracked globe and the copper channels humming with
five centuries of patient energy. They looked at the orc, well-groomed
and finely equipped, lying dead on the floor of a wizard's laboratory
in a place no orc from Mazariim would ever have found, wearing gear no
smith in Mazariim could have forged.

"The machine," Aizune said. "It shows other worlds. The mirrors are
windows. And this orc came from one of them."

"How?" Iskar asked. "The mirrors show images. You can't walk through an
image."

"I don't know," Aizune said. "But the machine might."

He sheathed the spirit-touched blade at his hip, where its glow dimmed
to its resting luminescence, and approached the pedestal. The glass
globe sat in its cradle of fused quartz, dark and cold, the crack along
its meridian visible now as a line of faintly discoloured glass. The
three rings loomed around him, their runes dark, their mechanisms
still. Up close, the workmanship was even more remarkable than it had
appeared from the doorway: every rune was perfect, every channel
precise, every surface finished to a standard that spoke of years, not
months, of devoted labour.

He placed his hands on the globe.

"Aizune," Smiggles said. There was a warning in his voice.

"I know."

"You're touching an unknown magical device built by a dead wizard, in a
room where an incorporeal undead has already attacked us, next to two
corpses."

"I know."

"Just making sure the situation is ticking along as planned."

The globe was cold beneath his palms. Smooth, impossibly smooth, the
glass curving against his skin with a perfection that made his
fingertips feel coarse by comparison. He closed his eyes. Reached
inward, toward the quiet place where Minerva's gift resided, the
channel that decades of priestly training had carved open in his
consciousness. Drew a breath. Let the divine energy flow.

The globe woke.

Warmth bloomed beneath his hands, sudden and startling, the glass
responding to the mana flowing through him with the eagerness of a
mechanism that had been waiting for exactly this. The copper channels in
the walls flickered. A hum rose through the floor, through his boots,
through the bones of his legs, the same vibration he had felt in the
corridor but stronger now, focused, directed. The geometric patterns on
the walls began to glow, copper brightening to amber, silver
brightening to white, the entire chamber awakening like a vast
instrument being tuned by the touch of a hand on a single string.

The innermost ring unlocked.

The sound was enormous in the confined space: a deep, resonant clang,
like a sword drawn from a stone sheath, metal releasing metal with a
precision that sent harmonics racing through the chamber walls. Ruby
dust in the ring's channels blazed to life, crimson light flaring along
every rune, and the ring began to turn. Slowly. The vertical rotation
sent reflections of red light spinning across the ceiling.

The second ring followed. The horizontal axis engaged with a harmonic
that layered over the first, sapphire dust igniting in channels of blue
light that crossed the crimson and created patterns of shifting purple
where the two frequencies intersected. The hum deepened. The walls
sang.

The third ring. The diagonal axis. Diamond dust, white and brilliant,
catching and amplifying every other light in the room until the chamber
blazed with colour: red, blue, white, purple, amber, and the particular
pale gold that was the machine's own signature, the echo of the mage
who had built it centuries ago.

The rings spun faster. The harmonic rose through frequencies that Aizune
felt rather than heard, a vibration that passed beyond sound and became
something physical, a pressure in the air, in the stone, in the
architecture of reality itself. The globe beneath his palms grew hot.
Then hotter. His hands wanted to pull away. He held them.

The dark stone above them became a window, a vast circular aperture
that should not have existed forty paces underground, and through it
the machine painted a world. Below their feet the stone remained solid,
felt but no longer seen, and on every side the chamber opened outward
into a landscape that stretched to a horizon that had no right to exist
in a room of transmuted rock. Smiggles and Iskar stood in what appeared
to be empty air, surrounded on all sides by a vision that moved when
Aizune's attention moved, the machine responding to his will as though
his consciousness were the hand that pointed an enormous eye.

---

The Steppes. Their Steppes, the barren expanse of broken stone they had
crossed to reach the keep, stretching in every direction under a sky
that was familiar and was not. The perspective shifted, rising, as
though the three of them were being lifted from the ground and carried
into the air above the wasteland. The Steppes blurred. The coast
appeared, the glittering line of the Bay of Urgot, the harbour that
Iskar had sailed from as a younger man, the harbour where Smiggles had
bought contaminated moonstone and complained about the brandy.

A city where the coast met the river. Almost familiar. Almost Amara.

The harbour was wrong. The buildings were arranged differently, clustered
where open water should be, absent where warehouses had stood for
generations. The architecture was not Sabonaar: low, sprawling
structures of pale stone in styles that none of them recognised. Where
the Grand Market should have been, a broad avenue lined with structures
that pulsed with a faint, cold light. Where the Temple of Tiovin should
have stood, a tower of dark metal and glass that rose above the
surrounding buildings with the functional ugliness of something built
for purpose rather than worship.

And the people.

They were orcs.

Not the crude, tribal warriors of the keep above. Not the bestial
raiders that terrorised the borderlands of the Merchant Nations. These
were orcs as none of them had ever imagined orcs could be: clean,
clothed, walking through streets with the unhurried purpose of citizens
going about their daily lives. They wore fitted garments. They carried
themselves with a bearing that suggested education, occupation, routine.
Some wore what appeared to be uniforms. Others carried items that
Smiggles, staring at the world that surrounded them with his mouth
slightly open, identified in a strangled whisper as "magical devices.
Integrated mana technology. That's not enchantment, that's
engineering."

The city drifted around them as Aizune turned his attention eastward. A
market square where orc vendors sold goods from stalls that were clean
and orderly. A school, or something like a school, where young orcs sat
in rows and listened to an instructor. A park, green and maintained,
where orc families walked among trees that had been planted and tended
with deliberate care.

And among the orcs, other figures. Smaller. Moving differently. Moving
with the hunched, shuffling gait of creatures that had learned to make
themselves small.

Humans.

Aizune felt the cold before he understood what he was seeing. The humans
in the vision were servants. Slaves. They moved through the orcish city
with their eyes down and their shoulders curved inward, carrying loads,
cleaning surfaces, performing the thousand small tasks of service with
the mechanical obedience of beings that had been broken so thoroughly
the breaking no longer showed. They were dressed in simple garments.
They were clean and fed. They were not beaten, not chained, not visibly
mistreated. They were simply owned, and the absence of cruelty made it
worse, because it meant the ownership was so complete, so fundamental,
so normalised, that cruelty was unnecessary.

And there were others. Larger. Broader. Shapes that moved through the
streets with a heavy, lumbering gait that shook the ground. Dwarves, or
what had once been dwarves: massive figures three times the size they
should have been, their bodies grotesquely enlarged, stone and metal
growths bonded to their skin, their faces slack and empty. They carried
loads that no unaltered body could have borne. They did not look up.
They did not look at anything. Their eyes were open and saw nothing.

"Void take it," Smiggles breathed. "Void take everything about this."

The world shifted again as Aizune willed the perspective upward, and he
felt something in his chest that was not the machine's vibration but
the physical weight of what he was seeing pressing against his ribs.
The city fell away beneath their feet, the streets and buildings
shrinking, and a broader landscape appeared: fields, roads, settlements,
all bearing the mark of orcish civilisation, organised, maintained,
populated by orcs who lived comfortable lives above the labour of
enslaved races. And beyond the settlements, at the edge of the vision's
reach, something that made all three of them fall silent.

An army. Vast, dark, disciplined, stretching across the landscape in
columns that extended to the horizon. Not a horde. Not the screaming
mass that orcish armies had been in the stories and histories of
Mazariim. A professional military force, uniformed and equipped,
marching in formation toward a destination that the vision could not
resolve, carrying weapons of the same alien craftsmanship that the dead
orc spy wore on his body twenty feet below them.

The world climbed higher still around them, the landscape spreading to
every horizon, and the sky opened above the orcish world, vast and
close, and Aizune looked up into a heaven that was almost his own and
was not.

Three moons hung in the sky.

Two he recognised. The silver moon and the pale gold moon that had
risen over the Steppes every night of their journey, the same two moons
that had circled Mazariim since before recorded history. They were
there, in their familiar positions, their light falling on a world that
was and was not the world they knew. The third moon was small, reddish,
occupying a position between the others that should have been empty
sky. It had no right to be there. Nothing in Aizune's training, nothing
in Smiggles' arcane education, nothing in Iskar's experience of the
night sky above a hundred different harbours, could account for a third
moon. There were two moons. There had always been two moons.

The globe went dark beneath his palms. The rings slowed, their harmonic
descending through frequencies that rattled loose dust from the chamber
walls. Ruby and sapphire and diamond dust settled into their channels.
The copper channels dimmed. The other world collapsed inward, the
horizon contracting, the sky folding, the orcish city shrinking to a
point of light that flickered once and went out, and the dark stone
walls of the machine room reassembled around them like the closing of a
fist, solid and real and very, very small.

Aizune released the globe and stepped back from the pedestal. His hands
were shaking. His eyes were wet.

The silence that filled the machine room was the silence of three people
whose understanding of the world had just been broken and rearranged
into a shape that none of them had the framework to comprehend. It
lasted a long time.

Iskar broke it.

"That was Amara." His voice was rough. He was leaning against the
nearest ring, one hand gripping the dark metal as though it were a
ship's rail in heavy seas. "The coastline. The river. The bay. That was
Amara, or it was a place where Amara should be. And it was full of
orcs." He paused. Swallowed. "Civilised orcs. Orcs with cities and
armies and enslaved humans who didn't even flinch when they walked
past."

"Not just humans," Smiggles said. The gnome had sunk to the floor, his
back against the pedestal, his legs drawn up, his hands resting on his
knees. His moustache was limp. The fact that he had not noticed or
corrected this was more alarming than anything else. "The large ones.
The ones carrying the loads. Those were dwarves. Transmuted.
Biomantically altered, if that word means anything in this context.
Someone took living dwarves and rebuilt them into beasts of burden."

"And there were no elves," Aizune said quietly. "I looked. In the
entire vision. Not a single elf."

The silence returned. It was heavier this time.

"Another world," Smiggles said finally. "Not the past, because the orcs
won and they haven't won here. Not the future, because three moons. Not
a pocket dimension or a demiplane, because the geography matches, the
coastline matches, the rivers match. A parallel reality. An alternate
material plane. A version of our world where everything went differently
and the orcs rule and we..." He stopped. Looked at the dead orc on the
floor, its manicured hands and its adamantine blade and its neatly
combed hair. "And they sent a scout. To us. Through this machine or
something like it. They know we exist."

"They may not," Aizune said. "The scout is dead. Killed by the orcs
upstairs, from the evidence. He may not have reported back."

"He may not have needed to. If they have the capability to send a scout,
they have the capability to detect the machine's activation. The monk
activated it before us. The scout came after. Now we've activated it
again." Smiggles met Aizune's eyes. "If they are watching, they know
someone just turned it on."

The implications of this settled over them like snowfall.

Aizune looked at the dead monk. The peaceful face. The withered body.
The simple robes of a man who had come to investigate orc raids and
found something beyond the scope of any investigation, and died for it.

"We need to take him with us," Aizune said.

"What?" Iskar looked at the body.

"He was a monk. Look at his robes, his bearing, the way his hair is
kept. This was a man of discipline and faith. He deserves better than a
tomb in a dead wizard's cellar." Aizune knelt beside the body. It was
light, desiccated, barely heavier than the robes that covered it. "We
will find where he came from and return him."

Iskar stared at him. Then he nodded, slowly, the gesture carrying a
weight that acknowledged what Aizune was really saying: that in the
face of what they had just witnessed, an enslaved world and a civilised
horror and three moons in a sky that should hold two, the elf's first
instinct was to ensure that a dead stranger received the dignity he was
owed. It was an act of faith. In the hollow silence of the machine
room, with the shadows gathering again at the edges of the mirrors'
light, faith was the only currency that still held its value.

"We take the spy's gear as well," Smiggles said, standing, brushing off
his trousers with automatic, habitual motions. "The weapons, the coins,
the device. Everything that proves what we saw is real. Because when we
tell this story, and we will have to tell this story, no one will
believe us without evidence."

They worked quickly. The cold had begun to creep back, and in the
mirror chamber beyond, the shadows were moving again, the Whisper
gathering its patience for a second approach. Aizune lifted the monk's
body. Iskar collected the orc spy's possessions with efficient hands,
wrapping the adamantine blade in cloth, pocketing the strange coins,
removing the wrist device with careful fingers. Smiggles took one last,
long look at the machine, his hazel eyes moving across the rings, the
globe, the channels, the runes, committing every detail to the memory
that served him as reliably as his spellbook.

"We'll need to come back," he said. "Or we'll need someone who can tell
us what this machine really is and what it's capable of."

"Agreed," Aizune said.

"But not tonight."

"No. Not tonight."

They climbed the rope ladder in silence, Iskar going first with the
spy's gear, Aizune following with the monk's body strapped across his
back, Smiggles last, his darkvision watching the darkness below long
after the others had lost sight of it, his crossbow loaded, his eyes
tracking the mirror chamber's amber glow until the shaft's rough edges
closed around him and the light disappeared beneath his boots.

---

They emerged into the keep's ground floor and the cold, clean air of
the Steppes night. The two moons had risen high, silver and gold, their
light falling through the keep's broken windows onto a scene of recent
violence that seemed, in the aftermath of what they had witnessed below,
almost quaint. Dead orcs. Scattered weapons. The smell of smoke and
blood, ordinary and mortal and comprehensible.

Aizune carried the monk's body to the keep's entrance and laid it
gently on a bedroll they had brought from their supplies. Iskar
arranged the orc spy's possessions in his saddlebag with the meticulous
care of a man who understood that what he was carrying might be the most
important collection of objects in the Merchant Nations. Smiggles sat on
a broken wall, his legs dangling, his brandy flask in his hand. He had
found brandy somewhere, or had been carrying an emergency supply he had
not previously disclosed. He drank, wiped his moustache, and stared at
the sky with an expression that combined exhaustion, awe, and the
particular grim satisfaction of a man who had been told the universe was
stranger than he thought and had responded by immediately starting to
calculate how much stranger.

"Three moons," he said.

"Three moons," Aizune confirmed.

"An orcish civilisation with technology we can't match. Enslaved humans
and dwarves. No elves. A professional army. And a dead spy in our
basement wearing gear that shouldn't exist." He took another drink. "We
need to go to Instar."

"Why Instar?" Iskar asked.

"Because the Thaumaturgic Council has the resources to confirm what the
spy's gear is made of, to analyse the machine's resonance frequencies,
and to tell us whether what we just saw is what I think we just saw." He
paused. "And because if I'm right, if that was an alternate material
plane where the orcs conquered the world and built a civilisation on the
bones of everyone else, then this is bigger than Aldorph's stolen
documents. This is bigger than anything. And we are going to need help
from people who are smarter than us. Which is a very short list, but
Instar is on it."

Aizune looked south, toward Amara, invisible beyond the dark horizon.
Then east, toward the far lights of Instar. Then up, at the sky, where
two moons hung in their ancient, familiar positions and the space
between them was empty, as it had always been, as it should always be.

"First we return to Amara," he said. "We report to Aldorph. We deliver
his documents. We collect our payment." He looked at the monk's body on
the bedroll, the peaceful face, the withered hands. "And we find where
this man belongs and bring him home."

"And then?" Iskar asked.

Aizune touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. The silver was cool
against his fingers. In the darkness of the Steppes, the spirit-touched
blade at his hip shimmered with pale light, and the permanent flames in
the chambers below burned on with the faithfulness of an enchantment
that had outlasted its creator by five hundred years.

"And then we find out what we are dealing with."

They left the keep at dawn. Behind them, the crude stone walls stood
empty above the older, stranger foundations, and below those foundations
the machine waited in its circle of dark metal and gemstone dust, and
the mirrors watched with faces that were not their own, and in the
silence between the reflections, the Whisper stirred and settled and
stirred again, patient as centuries, hungry as grief.

The two moons set. The sun rose over the Steppes of Broken Stone. Three
riders and one dead man began the long road back to Amara, carrying
with them the knowledge that the world was larger, stranger, and more
terribly endangered than any of them had imagined, and that what they
had seen in the chamber beneath the keep was not an ending but a
beginning.

The beginning of everything.
