# Chapter Three
## The Crossing

The morning began with tusks.

"Hold still," Smiggles said.

"I am holding still."

"You are not holding still. You are fidgeting. Your face is
fidgeting. How is it possible for a face to fidget? Stop it."

Iskar stood in the centre of the common room above Eccentricities
with his arms extended and his eyes closed and his jaw clenched in
the particular expression of a man submitting to a process he did
not enjoy and could not control. Smiggles stood before him on a
chair, which brought the gnome's hands to the level of the human's
face, and the hands were moving in precise geometries, the fingers
tracing patterns in the air that left trails of warm gold light
hanging for fractions of a second before dissolving. Six syllables
of Draconic, each consonant struck with the clipped precision of a
mage whose craft permitted no imprecision and whose temperament
permitted no patience for those who made the craft harder than it
needed to be.

The transformation began at the jaw.

Bone shifted beneath skin. The sound was not audible to anyone
except the man experiencing it, a deep, grinding pressure that
travelled through the skull and settled behind the eyes. Iskar's
jawline widened, thickened, the fine Sabonaar bone structure
broadening into something heavier, more prominent, the mandible
extending forward to accommodate the tusks that were forming in
the lower gumline with a slow, organic insistence that was more
disturbing than pain. The tusks emerged: two curved points of
dense enamel rising from the jaw, pushing through tissue that the
polymorph reshaped as it went, the flesh accommodating the growth
with the resigned cooperation of a body that had been informed it
was changing and had no say in the matter.

"Oy," Smiggles muttered, adjusting a gesture. "The left one is
crooked. Hold on." Two more syllables. The left tusk straightened.
"Better."

Iskar's skin darkened, the rich bronze of Sabonaar sun-exposure
deepening through shades of olive and grey-green until it reached
the particular hue of Orceri flesh: not the crude grey-green of a
Mazariim orc but something cleaner, healthier, the complexion of
a race that had won its war and spent eight centuries eating well
and breeding selectively. His height increased. Shoulders broadened.
The lean, rope-muscled build of a sailor compressed and thickened
into the heavy frame of a being whose physiology prioritised mass
and endurance over agility.

"Open your eyes," Smiggles said.

Iskar opened his eyes. They were amber now, set deeper beneath a
brow that was heavier than the one he had woken with. He looked
down at his hands. The hands were enormous. The fingers that had
held a rapier with the delicacy of a surgeon now looked as though
they had been designed for the primary purpose of breaking things
and the secondary purpose of holding the things that remained.

"Sangre y sal," he said. The Sabonaar oath sounded profoundly
wrong coming from a mouth full of tusks. "I look like something
that picks fights with buildings."

"You look like an Orceri citizen of average build and unremarkable
appearance, which is the point." Smiggles descended from the chair,
regarded his work with the critical squint of a craftsman who saw
every flaw before anyone else could find them, and nodded once.
"Skin texture is correct. Bone density is plausible. Scent will
pass a casual encounter but not a sustained one, so do not embrace
anyone."

"I was not planning to embrace anyone."

"With you, one never knows."

Aizune stood by the window, already transformed. His polymorph had
been completed first, the process smoother for the elf than for
the human because the anatomical distance between Ya'sehrim and
Orceri was, paradoxically, smaller: both tall, both angular, the
bone structure requiring adjustment rather than reconstruction.
He looked strange. The forest green robes hung on a body that was
broader and heavier than the one they had been fitted for, the
Minerva clasp at his throat incongruous against Orceri skin, a
symbol worn by a face that belonged to the race that had killed
the entity it honoured. He had not commented on this. He had
touched the clasp once, felt its warmth, and said nothing, and
the nothing had been louder than anything he might have said.

Smiggles turned to himself. This was the part he had been dreading.

The polymorph was self-directed, which required a precision that
bordered on the philosophical: reshaping one's own body while
maintaining the consciousness that directed the reshaping, a
process that was simultaneously the sculptor and the clay. Six
syllables. The warm gold light enveloped him. He felt his bones
lengthen, his mass increase, his proportions distort from gnomish
compactness to Orceri bulk. The world shifted around him as his
eye level rose by three feet. His hands swelled to the dimensions
that had offended him on Iskar.

"I hate everything about this," he said. His voice was deeper,
the resonance of a larger chest cavity altering the acoustic
properties of every word. "The proportions are wrong. My hands are
the size of hams. How does anyone do fine work with hands like
hams?" He flexed the enormous fingers. "My moustache is gone. My
moustache is gone and I can feel where it was and where it was is
a tusk. A tusk, where my moustache should be. This is a personal
violation of the highest order."

"You look verrry handsome," Iskar said. "Distinguished, even. The
tusks bring out your eyes."

"Shut up."

"They do. They complement the jaw. You have a commanding jaw now.
Before, you had a jaw that gave orrders to moustache wax. Now you
have a jaw that could headbutt a forrtification."

"I said shut up."

"I'm just saying. If the Orrceri had moustaches, yours would be
magnificent."

Smiggles closed his eyes. When he opened them, his expression,
translated through Orceri features that he had not yet learned to
control, communicated a depth of suffering that transcended species.

Lai stepped forward from the corner where she had been watching.
She had not been transformed. She did not need Smiggles' arcane
reshaping, because draconic shapechanging was not polymorph but
something more fundamental, a rearrangement of the self at a level
that arcane magic could approximate but not achieve. She closed her
eyes. The air around her shivered, the faintest displacement, and
when it settled she was taller, broader, green-skinned and amber-
eyed, the Orceri form as convincing as the wood elf form had been,
which was to say perfectly convincing in every respect except the
eyes, which remained yellow and vertical and carried the particular
quality of a predator wearing a herbivore's skin.

"The eyes," Smiggles said.

"The eyes are always the tell." She shrugged, the gesture strange
on an Orceri frame. "Draconic heritage. It cannot be fully
suppressed. I will keep the hood up."

She moved to each of them in turn, placing her hands on their
shoulders, and Aizune felt a second working settle over the first:
different in texture, different in origin, the draconic casting
layering onto Smiggles' arcane foundation the way lacquer layered
onto wood, sealing it, reinforcing it, extending the duration of
the disguise through the combination of two distinct magical
traditions drawing from two distinct wells of power.

"That will hold," she said. "Longer than the arcane working alone.
Do not ask me to be precise about duration. We will know when it
begins to degrade."

"How will we know?" Aizune asked.

"Your tusks will itch."

Aleron had not been transformed. He stood by the door in his
threadbare robes with his quarterstaff in his trembling hands and
his hood pulled forward, the gaunt Ya'sehrim features hidden in
shadow, the amulet pulsing green beneath the fabric at his throat.
His planar signature would corrupt any polymorph layered over it,
the dimensional energies of his gift interfering with the arcane
restructuring the way a strong current interfered with a careful
knot. He would travel as himself, hooded and cloaked, and the
others would keep him hidden.

"Ready?" he asked. The word was quiet. Both eyes were focused.

Aizune looked at the common room. The modest furniture. The lamp.
The window that looked out over the Market District, where the
morning commerce was beginning its daily cycle, merchants opening
shutters, carts rumbling over cobblestones, the harbour bell
marking the first watch. The ordinary world, turning as it always
turned.

"Ready," he said.

---

They gathered in the courtyard below, five figures that no longer
matched the bodies they had been born in and one that matched his
all too well. The morning air was cool, carrying the salt of the
harbour and the distant sound of gulls and the particular quality
of Amaran light that came from the sun reflecting off the bay,
golden and diffuse, the light of a city built where river met
ocean and both conspired to illuminate everything they touched.

Aleron positioned them. Close. Within arm's reach. He held the
tuning fork in his left hand and placed his right hand flat against
the courtyard wall, the fingers spread, the trembling stilled by
contact with solid stone. The fork began to vibrate.

The vibration was not sound. Aizune felt it through Minerva's gift
as a displacement, a sensation of reality bending around a fixed
point, the dimensions of the courtyard stretching and compressing
simultaneously as the fork's calibrated frequency reached into the
fabric of the world and found the seam it had been designed to
find. The air thickened. The morning light distorted, the golden
quality shifting toward something colder, bluer, the spectrum
adjusting as the boundary between realities thinned.

"Stay close," Aleron said. His voice was steady. His eyes were
closed. The amulet flared green, the steady pulse accelerating to
match the fork's frequency, the containment working harder than it
had in months, the dimensional energies of the transit pressing
against the suppression field with the force of a river pressing
against a dam. "Do not let go of each other. Do not move. Do not
think about where you are. Think about where you are going."

The fork sang. The note was below hearing and above sensation,
occupying the narrow band that Aleron's gift lived in, the
frequency that existed between what the body could feel and what
the mind could name. The courtyard walls became translucent.
Through them, briefly, Aizune saw Amara as though from a great
distance: the harbour, the Market District, the spires of the
Blessed Halls of Eternal Light catching the morning sun, the whole
city rendered in miniature, precious and fragile, a thing of
light and stone and living people that was simultaneously
everything he was leaving and everything he was fighting to
protect.

Then the courtyard was gone.

The transition was not movement. It was replacement. Amara did not
recede. It ceased. One reality ended and another began, the
boundary between them crossed in a duration that was not time but
its absence, a gap in the continuity of experience that the mind
registered as a flicker of darkness, a pressure behind the eyes,
and then the irrevocable fact of being somewhere else.

Cold. Stone. The smell of rock and altitude and the thin, sharp
air of a place that was very high and very far from anything warm.
Darkness, not absolute but deep, the dim luminescence of
geothermal heat radiating from stone that had been warming in the
earth's embrace for millennia. Aizune's boots found purchase on a
surface that was smooth and worn and slightly warm, the floor of
a natural cave at altitude.

The Minerva clasp went cold.

Not cool. Not the gradual loss of warmth that metal surrendered to
air. Cold. Instant. Complete. The silver at his throat became ice
against his skin, and the sensation that accompanied it was not
temperature but amputation: the vast, patient, luminous presence
that had lived behind his sternum since adolescence, the warmth
that came when he prayed and the clarity that came when he listened,
the connection that ran from priest to entity across the
dimensional architecture of Elysium, severed. Not attenuated. Not
diminished. Gone. The place where Minerva's gift resided was empty.
The thread that had connected him to something vaster than himself,
the thread he had felt every waking moment of his adult life, was
not stretched thin or weakened. It was absent. As though the other
end had been cut, or as though the other end had never existed, as
though the entity whose wisdom and light and presence had been the
foundation of his faith and his identity and his understanding of
his place in the world simply was not.

He stood in the darkness of a cave in another reality and felt the
silence where she should have been.

It was not shock. He had been warned. Aleron had told him on the
rooftop in Amara, the words precise and honest and terrible: "The
space where her presence should be is empty. A wound in the fabric.
A scar where something vast was removed." He had prepared for it.
He had meditated. He had prayed to Minerva in the hours before the
crossing and felt her response and held it in his memory the way a
man held his breath before diving into water, storing the last
inhalation against the depth to come.

The preparation was insufficient. Nothing could have prepared him
for the silence. It was not the absence of sound. It was the
absence of meaning. The absence of the framework through which he
had interpreted every experience, every decision, every moment of
beauty and every moment of terror, for a hundred and forty-one
years. The world was the same. The cave was the same. His body was
the same. But the lens through which he had seen the world, the
divine sight that had given depth and colour and significance to
everything he observed, was gone. He was seeing with mortal eyes
for the first time since Minerva had touched him, and the world
seen through mortal eyes was flat, and grey, and silent, and the
silence was the worst thing he had ever experienced because it was
not the absence of noise but the absence of her.

He touched the clasp. The silver was cold. The symbol of the open
eye overlaid with the oak leaf was cold. Everything was cold.

"Aizune." Smiggles' voice. Distant. "Aizune. Look at me."

He looked. The gnome's Orceri face was close, the hazel eyes
beneath the amber disguise sharp with concern. Behind him, the
cave mouth admitted a blade of grey light and the bitter cold of
high altitude.

"She is alive," Smiggles said. The words were measured, deliberate,
chosen by a man who did not do comfort naturally but who understood
that this moment required it and was unwilling to fail the
requirement. "In our world. In Mazariim. She is alive and whole
and this is temporary. We go back and the connection returns. This
is not permanent. Do you hear me?"

"I hear you." His voice was steady. He did not know how his voice
was steady when the silence inside him was so vast. "I hear you."

"Good." Smiggles held his gaze for a moment longer. Then he
stepped back, and his expression shifted from concern to the
operational mode that was his armour against things that could not
be solved by analysis, and he turned toward the cave mouth and the
grey light beyond.

Three moons.

They were visible through the opening, hanging in a sky that was
the wrong shade of blue, deeper and colder than the sky he had
grown up under. Silver Io'el, full. Pale gold Randir'el, waning.
And between them, small, reddish, occupying the space where
Mazariim's sky was empty, the third moon: confirmation, undeniable,
felt in the gut before the mind could process it, that they had
crossed.

They were in Ta'Puh. And Minerva was not.

Aleron fell.

The collapse was total. His legs gave. His quarterstaff clattered
against the stone. He went down on his side with the boneless
descent of a man whose body had been running on credit for the
duration of the transit and had presented the bill the instant
the transit ended. His skin, already pale, went translucent at
the temples and wrists, the veins beneath standing out like ink,
and the trembling in his hands accelerated to a vibration that
shook his entire frame, the amulet at his throat flaring bright
green before settling to a rapid, unsteady pulse that matched
the arrhythmic stutter of a heartbeat under duress.

His eyes were open. They were not focused on the cave.

"No," he murmured, to something that was not there. "No, I... I
cannot, not now. Please." His head turned, tracking an invisible
presence that moved across the cave with purpose. "I said not now.
I am here. I am in the... in the cave. The stone. The cold. I am
here." The words were anchors, thrown outward toward the physical
world, the technique Sugnim had taught him: grip the real, name
the real, refuse the bleed. "Stone. Cold. The staff. Where is...
where is the..."

Aizune knelt beside him. The divine healing that would have
steadied the elf was gone. The warm pulse from his palm that had
mended wounds and soothed pain since his earliest training was
gone. His hands were only hands. He took Aleron's, which was
ice-cold and shaking, and placed it on the quarterstaff that had
rolled two feet from the elf's body. The trembling fingers closed
on the ashwood with a grip that was less holding than clinging,
the drowning man's grip on the only solid thing in a world that
had liquefied.

"You are here," Aizune said. His voice was steady. He anchored
Aleron to the present because Sugnim had taught the elf to need
this, and because it was the one thing he could do in a reality
where the entity that gave his abilities meaning did not exist.
"Aleron. You are in the cave. The stone is real. The cold is
real. I am real. You are here."

"Here," Aleron repeated. His eyes drifted toward focus. Drifted
away. Drifted back. "Here. Yes. The... the crossing was..."

"Rest."

"...expensive."

"Rest."

Aleron's eyes closed. His breathing slowed. The amulet's pulse
steadied, the green glow dimming from urgent brightness to its
normal rhythm, the containment reasserting itself over whatever
the transit had disrupted. He did not sleep. Elves did not sleep.
But he withdrew into something adjacent to sleep, a deep reverie
that shut out the echoes and the cave and the cold and the three
moons visible through the entrance and left only the staff in his
hands and the stone beneath his body and the green pulse at his
throat, counting his heartbeats, holding the boundary, doing the
work it had been built to do.

"Hours," Smiggles said quietly, looking down at the fallen elf.
"He said hours."

"Then we have hours." Aizune rose. The emptiness where Minerva's
presence should have been was vast, but he was standing, and he
was functional, and faith that persisted in the absence of its
object was, he decided in that moment, the only faith that
deserved the name. "And we use them."

---

The cave was larger than it appeared.

While Aleron rested, the others explored. The entrance opened onto
a ledge approximately eight thousand feet above sea level, the
mountain falling away in grey scree and sparse conifer below the
treeline, the slopes barren and wind-scored above it. The view
was enormous. Mountains stretched in every direction, high and
cold and empty, their peaks snow-covered, their ridgelines sharp
against the deeper blue of Ta'Puh's altered sky. No roads. No
structures. No mana streetlamps, no dirigibles, no surveillance
constructs drifting above the peaks. The northernmost mountains
of Ta'Puh, chosen because Aleron had felt them through the fork
as the one place on this world where the Orceri's civilisational
footprint did not reach.

The wind was constant and cutting. Aizune stood on the ledge and
felt it press against his Orceri disguise and felt nothing else,
no divine sense extending outward to read the wind's intention, no
subtle awareness of the spiritual currents that Minerva's gift
normally wove through his perception of the natural world. Just
wind. Just cold. Just the flat, grey, mortal experience of
standing on a mountain in a world where his goddess had been
murdered.

Behind the ledge, the cave descended. A natural passage, wide
enough for two abreast, its floor worn smooth by something that
had passed this way many times over a very long period. The air
temperature rose as they descended, geothermal warmth radiating
from deep rock, the cold of the mountaintop giving way to
something approaching comfort. The passage turned twice and opened.

Aizune felt the space before his eyes could process it. The
sudden expansion of the air. The way sound changed, the compressed
acoustics of the passage releasing into something vaster, the
echoes of their footsteps scattering outward and returning from
distances that the dim light could not reach.

Smiggles raised his hand. Warm gold light bloomed from his palm,
the cantrip reflexive, unconscious, and the light reached outward
into the darkness and found the boundaries of a chamber that was
not a chamber but a cavern, roughly circular, two hundred feet
across, the ceiling lost in darkness above, the floor worn smooth
in grooves that curved and overlapped in patterns too large and
too regular to be geological.

Something filled the cavern.

It did not move. It did not breathe in any way that was visible.
It existed in the space the way a mountain existed in a landscape:
not occupying it but defining it, the walls and ceiling and floor
existing in relation to it rather than the reverse. Smiggles'
light reached the nearest surface and returned the deep shimmer
of scales, blue, the blue of deep ocean under storm light, each
one the size of a man's palm, layered in patterns that caught
the faint illumination and sent it back as cold fire. Dust lay
across the scales in inches, undisturbed, the uniform accumulation
of years, perhaps decades, of absolute stillness.

Aizune's hand found the hilt of the spirit-touched blade. Beside
him, Iskar's hand found his rapier, the weapon absurd in the
enormous Orceri fingers that now held it, but the instinct behind
the grip was unchanged. Behind them both, Lai had gone very still,
a stillness that was different from the stillness of the others,
deeper, more fundamental, the stillness of a creature whose body
had recognised something that her mind was still processing.

Eyes opened.

Two points of light in the darkness, deep electric blue, ancient
beyond the capacity of the word ancient to contain, carrying an
intelligence that had been sharpened against the whetstone of
centuries of isolation until it could cut through any pretence and
any disguise and any comfortable assumption about the nature of
the world. The eyes did not blink. They did not need to. They
simply looked, and the looking was an event, a force, a weight
that pressed against the four figures in the passage entrance with
the gentle, absolute authority of a gaze that had been aimed at
things most mortal minds could not conceive.

"Ssso." The word emerged from the darkness on a current of air
that smelled of ozone and deep stone and something older than both,
the exhalation of a body that had been breathing the same air for
centuries and had left its chemical signature in every molecule.
The sibilant stretched, lingered, tasted itself. "I ssmell elf."

The voice filled the cavern the way the body filled the cavern:
completely, without effort, the deep resonance of a sound produced
by a throat that could have swallowed a horse and lungs that could
have powered a bellows large enough to forge a city gate. The
subsonic vibration that accompanied the words was felt in the
sternum and the teeth and the bones of the inner ear, a physical
pressure that registered as sound only after the body had already
responded to it as force. But the voice was not merely large. It
was layered. Harmonics lived beneath the surface of each word,
the acoustic signature of a being that had spoken language since
before the races it addressed had possessed language, and the
harmonics carried meaning that the words themselves did not: age,
patience, the particular quality of attention that developed in
a consciousness that had observed the rise and fall of
civilisations and found both processes instructive and neither
permanent.

"And gnome. And human." The blue eyes moved, tracking from figure
to figure with the slow, deliberate precision of a being that
measured intervals in geological strata and found mortal haste
faintly amusing. "Wearing orc sskins." A pause that lasted
exactly long enough for the silence to become uncomfortable, the
pause of a predator that enjoyed the moment before the pounce
not for cruelty but for craft. "Badly."

Smiggles opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. No sound
emerged.

The blue eyes continued their survey and stopped on the fourth
figure, the one who had not reached for a weapon and had not
spoken and stood at the rear of the group in a stillness that was
not fear but recognition, the stillness of a young predator in
the presence of something so far beyond its own capacity that the
instinct to compete or flee had been replaced by something older
and simpler: acknowledgement.

"And ssomething elssse." The sibilants coiled around the words,
tasting them, the Draconic phonemes bleeding through the Common
speech the way heat bled through stone: gradually, inevitably,
altering everything they touched. "Ssomething that is none of
thossse things. Ssomething that wearsss its ssmall sshape the way
a river wearsss a channell, becaussse the true sshape would not
fit in thiss passage." The eyes narrowed. The vertical pupils
contracted. "Young. Green. Ssstill growing. I can tassste your
lineage on the air, hatchling. You carry the sscent of forests
that no longer exisst."

Lai said nothing. Her yellow eyes, the only part of her that the
draconic disguise could not fully alter, met the ancient blue ones
across two hundred feet of dusty stone, and the meeting was a
conversation conducted in frequencies that the others could not
hear, the deep harmonic of draconic recognition that bypassed
language and went directly to the part of the brain where species
knew its own.

The dragon's mouth moved. In a human, the movement might have been
a smile. In a creature whose jaws could accommodate the word
cavernous as a literal description, the movement was closer to a
geological event, the slow rearrangement of a landscape of scale
and tooth and the particular architecture of amusement that
ancient dragons wore when something surprised them, which was,
after centuries, almost nothing.

"You are either very brave or very foolissh," Giganzalt said.
"In my experience, the dissstinction iss academic. You may
approach."

---

They approached.

Giganzalt watched them come with the patience of a being that had
watched the ice retreat from mountain valleys and had not found the
process rushed. The full scale of him became apparent as they
crossed the cavern floor, the dim light of Smiggles' cantrip
revealing more with each step: the vast expanse of wing folded
along his flanks, membranous, the membrane scarred in one section
where it had healed translucent. The horns sweeping back from his
skull, one tip missing on the right side, the break ancient and
weathered. Scars along his left flank where something terrible
had scored the scales in parallel lines. Old wounds. He had not
fought in eight centuries.

"You came from the other sside," he said. It was not a question.
"Through the boundary. I felt your transssit. A dissturbance in
the dimensional fabric, ssmall but precissse. Too precissse for
accident." The blue eyes settled on Aizune, and the gaze was
heavier than the gaze he had given the others, denser, as though
the ancient intelligence behind it had found something in the
elf's face that required closer examination. "You. I tassste
grief on you, priessst-of-the-sssilent-one. The grief of a child
who has called for its mother and heard nothing. Your goddessss
iss dead in thiss world. You feel it now, yess? The emptinesss
where her voice sshould be."

The words landed in Aizune's chest with the precision of a blade.
He had not spoken of the silence. He had not needed to. The dragon
could taste it on the air.

"Remove the dissguise from your face, if you would," Giganzalt
continued. "I find it difficult to take a conversssation
sseriously when I am sspeaking to a massk."

Aizune hesitated. Smiggles caught his eye and gave the smallest
nod: a dragon of this magnitude could smell through the polymorph
regardless. The disguise was irrelevant here. Aizune touched his
jaw. The Orceri features softened, blurred, and resettled into the
angular Ya'sehrim face beneath, the pale skin and dark grey eyes
emerging from the heavier flesh like a sculpture from unworked
stone. The Minerva clasp caught Smiggles' mana-light and threw
back a brief flash of silver that was beautiful and empty, a
symbol without the presence it symbolised, jewellery rather than
devotion.

"Better," Giganzalt said. "Now. You are from the world that
ssharess our geography but not our hissstory. The world where the
War ended differently." The ancient eyes moved to a distance that
was not the cavern wall but something beyond it, something
measured in centuries rather than feet. "I felt that day. The
shockwave when Zuel'ss avatar sshattered. Even here, even in
thessse mountains, the fabric of reality sshuddered when the
dwarf'ss blow landed and the entity'ss projection dissssolved."
A slow breath, the dust cascading from his scales in pale streams.
"I have wondered, ssince that day. Wondered what kind of world
grew in the ssoil that blow turned. What kind of creaturess took
root in the peace the dwarf'ss hammer purchassed." The blue eyes
returned to Aizune. "You are, I assssume, the ansswer."

"We are travellers," Aizune said, choosing the words with the
deliberate precision of a man who understood that the being before
him could end the conversation and everyone in it without
inconvenience and who wished to ensure that every word earned its
place in the air between them. "From the alternate reality you
describe. We have crossed to gather intelligence about the
civilisation that occupies this world. We mean no harm to you."

"Harm." The word coiled in the air between them, the sibilant
stretching into something that was almost a laugh and almost a sigh
and was, in its ancient complexity, both and neither. "Noble elf.
I have spent eight hundred yearss hiding in a cave. The Orceri
conquered my world, desssstroyed or ensslaved every ssentient

weight that Common could not fully bear, the sibilant deepening
into a register that vibrated in the bones. "The thingss that
could harm me have been dead for centuriess. You could not harm me
if you devoted your remaining lifesspasss to the attempt." The
blue eyes softened, fractionally, the vast intelligence behind
them finding something in Aizune's face that warranted a reduction
in the display of power. "But courtessy iss a rare sscent in
thessse mountainss. I appreciate it."

"You have been hiding," Smiggles said. His voice had recovered.
The analytical mind behind the Orceri disguise was running,
processing, cataloguing, and the questions that the mind generated
were pushing past the awe that the dragon's presence inspired. "For
eight hundred years. In a cave. Why not fight?"

"Fight." The word was not a question but a taste, the dragon
rolling it across his tongue the way a connoisseur rolled wine,
