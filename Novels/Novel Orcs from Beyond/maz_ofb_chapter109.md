# Chapter Nine
## The Confirmation

They left Amara on a morning that tasted of salt and possibility, five
travellers and a weight of evidence that no salt could dissolve, riding
east toward a city where knowledge was the only currency that mattered.

The party had changed shape overnight. Aleron Windstrider walked among
them now, his pale ashwood quarterstaff tapping the packed earth of the
eastern road with the steady rhythm of a man who had been walking for
a very long time and expected to walk for a very long time more. He
kept pace with the horses despite his gaunt frame and trembling hands,
and Smiggles, who had spent the first hour of the journey watching him
with the analytical scrutiny of a mage evaluating an unfamiliar
mechanism, concluded that the elf's endurance was sustained by
something other than physical strength. The gift, whatever it was,
whatever it cost him, kept him upright the way a mainspring kept a
clock ticking: not through abundance of energy but through the precise
and relentless application of what little remained.

He talked to himself. Or rather, he talked to things that were not
there, pausing mid-stride to listen to a voice no one else could hear,
tilting his head at an angle that suggested the voice was coming from
somewhere specific, a point in the air beside his left shoulder or
above his head or, on one occasion, from directly beneath his feet.
He would nod, murmur a response, occasionally shake his head with the
weary patience of a man declining an invitation he had declined many
times before, and then resume walking as though nothing had happened.
The first time he did this, Iskar's hand drifted to his rapier. The
second time, the hand relaxed. By the third, the bard had accepted
the behaviour with the pragmatic adaptability of a man who had shared
close quarters with eccentric sailors for eight years and had learned
that unusual habits were survivable as long as the person exhibiting
them could be relied upon when it mattered.

Lai walked at the rear of the group, which was new.

Since joining them on the road from the keep, she had ranged freely,
sometimes beside Iskar's horse, sometimes ahead of the group, once
disappearing for an hour and returning with wild berries and a smile
that defied interrogation. Now she walked at the back, and the
distance she maintained from Aleron was not casual. It was precise.
Twenty paces, minimum, a gap she preserved with the unconscious
vigilance of a body responding to a stimulus it could not name. Her
yellow eyes returned to the elf with the white-streaked hair at
intervals that were too regular to be curiosity and too frequent to
be indifference, and when Aleron's attention drifted in her direction,
which it did rarely and briefly, she looked away with a speed that
bordered on flinching.

"She doesn't like him," Smiggles observed to Aizune, keeping his
voice low. "Or she's afraid of him. Which doesn't calibrate with
anything else about her, because I have not seen that girl afraid of
a single thing since we found her eating an apple in the middle of
nowhere."

"She is uncomfortable," Aizune said. "So is he. Watch his echoes when
she moves closer."

Smiggles watched. When Lai, adjusting her position to avoid a muddy
stretch of road, drifted within fifteen paces of Aleron, the elf's
head snapped to the side, his grey eyes unfocusing, his lips moving in
rapid, silent conversation with presences that multiplied around him
like reflections in facing mirrors. The effect lasted until she moved
away, at which point the echoes subsided and Aleron's gaze cleared and
he resumed his measured stride as though a storm had passed.

"Oy," Smiggles said softly. "That's not social discomfort. That's a
reaction. A physical one. Whatever she is, whatever he is, they
interfere with each other."

"Like magnets of opposed polarity," Aizune said.

"Exactly like that. Which raises the question of what kind of magnet
our green friend is hiding under that insufficient armour."

The question answered itself on the third day.

---

The bandits were not the problem.

They emerged from a copse of scrub oak where the road narrowed between
two rocky outcrops, a dozen men in mismatched armour carrying weapons
that ranged from serviceable short swords to farming implements that
had been optimistically repurposed. Their leader was a heavyset man
with a scar that bisected his left eyebrow and continued down his
cheek, giving his face the appearance of a page that had been torn and
imperfectly repaired. He stepped into the road with the confident
swagger of a man who had done this many times and expected this time to
proceed like all the others.

"Purses and weapons on the ground," he said. "Horses too. Walk away
alive. Good deal."

Smiggles, who was riding at the front and whose patience for
interruptions had been thinning steadily over three days of road dust
and inadequate brandy, looked at the bandit leader with the expression
he reserved for contaminated moonstone: mild disgust overlaying deep
professional offence.

"No," he said.

The bandit leader blinked. "No?"

"No. And I want you to think very carefully about what happens next,
because I am a mage, the elf behind me is a priest with a sword that
cuts through things swords shouldn't cut through, and the tall one
with the hat has been waiting for someone to fight since we left Amara.
So. Think."

The bandit leader thought. He looked at Smiggles, who was three feet
tall and sitting on a horse. He looked at the dozen armed men behind
him. He made his decision.

"Take them."

The dozen charged. Smiggles sighed, raised one hand, and spoke three
syllables of Draconic in the rapid, clipped cadence that preceded
something unpleasant for everyone standing in front of him. Thick
strands of sticky webbing erupted from his palm and filled the gap
between the outcrops from ground to the height of a man's head,
catching the first four bandits in mid-stride and pinning them to
each other and to the rocks in attitudes of comical immobility. The
remaining eight split around the obstruction, four to each side.

Aizune dismounted with the fluid efficiency of someone who had
transitioned from horseback to combat before. The spirit-touched blade
sang from its sheath, its pale edge brightening in the afternoon
light. Iskar was already on the ground, rapier drawn, his hat somehow
remaining on his head at its precise angle despite the speed of his
dismount. "Gentlemen!" he called to the approaching bandits with
genuine warmth. "You have the singularr misforrtune of facing Iskar of
the Cobalt Tide, and I would offerr you mercy, but I left it in my
other coat!"

The bandits were not the problem.

The problem came from the sky.

It descended with a sound like a sail tearing in a gale, a rush of
displaced air and the heavy percussion of wings too large for any bird,
and the shadow it cast across the road was the wrong shape: elongated,
angular, the shadow of something with a wingspan wider than the road
itself. Smiggles looked up. His moustache went flat.

A wyvern. Forty feet from wingtip to wingtip, its scales the dull
brown of dried mud, its tail ending in the venomous barb that made the
species one of the most dangerous predators in the Merchant Nations.
It had been roosting on the ridge above the outcrops, drawn by the
noise, or perhaps it had been there all along, the real reason this
stretch of road saw so many disappearances that bandits could operate
freely. It screamed, a sound that was less a vocalisation and more a
physical assault, a frequency that rattled the teeth and turned the
bones to water, and it dove toward the group with the focused intent
of a creature that saw horses and humans as the same thing: food.

Smiggles' hands moved. Six syllables of Draconic, each heavier than
the last, building toward the bead of orange-white light that would
detonate on contact with the wyvern's hide and end the encounter in
fire and thunder.

He never finished the incantation.

Something passed over his head. Something fast, something low,
something that displaced the air with a force that flattened his
moustache against his face and filled his nostrils with a scent he
knew but could not, in that first fractured second, place: the sharp,
acrid bite of something caustic, something that dissolved organic
material on contact, something green.

Lai was gone. The space where she had been standing, twenty paces
behind the group, was empty. In her place, rising from the road with
a speed that defied the geometry of the body that had occupied that
space a heartbeat earlier, was a dragon.

She was not enormous. Not yet. She was young, compact, sleek, her
body designed for speed rather than mass, every line speaking of a
predator that had not yet reached its full size and did not need to.
Her scales were deep forest green, darkening to near-black along her
spine and the leading edges of wings that spread wider than the road
and beat the air with a power that sent dust and gravel spiralling
outward in a circle that stung exposed skin. Lighter emerald undertones
gleamed along her throat and belly. Her horns swept back from her
skull in elegant curves. Her tail, long and muscular and ridged at its
point, lashed behind her as she climbed. And her eyes, those eyes,
vivid yellow with vertical slit pupils, the same eyes that had watched
them from a small elven face for days, burned in the dragon's skull
with an intelligence and a fury that the humanoid form had contained
but never concealed, not really, not to anyone who had been paying
attention.

She hit the wyvern like a thunderbolt.

The impact was mid-air, thirty feet above the road, and the sound it
produced was the sound of two large bodies colliding at speed, a
concussion of scale against scale and claw against hide that sent a
shockwave rippling through the afternoon air. The wyvern screamed
again, this time in pain rather than aggression, because the dragon's
claws had found purchase along its flank and the dragon's jaws had
closed on the joint where its right wing met its body, and the bite
was not the instinctive snap of a startled predator but the calculated
strike of a killer that knew exactly where the structure was weakest.

The wyvern's barbed tail swung. The dragon released, twisted in the
air with a fluid grace that her humanoid form had only hinted at, and
opened her mouth.

The acid came in a cone of caustic green vapour that engulfed the
wyvern's head and upper body with a hiss that Smiggles heard from the
ground, forty feet below, over the sound of his own heartbeat. Where
the vapour touched, scales bubbled. Membrane dissolved. The wyvern's
second scream was shorter than the first and ended in a wet, choking
sound that was the sound of something essential being unmade. It
folded. It fell. It struck the rocky outcrop with an impact that shook
the ground and lay still, its body steaming, its right wing hanging
by threads of sinew, its head a ruin.

The dragon landed on the road.

The impact of her weight cracked the packed earth beneath her claws.
She stood in the afternoon light, wings half-furled, tail coiled, her
green scales catching the sun and throwing it back in patterns that
moved as she breathed, and she looked at them with those yellow eyes,
the same yellow eyes, and the expression in them was not apologetic.

The bandits, those who were not trapped in webbing, had fled. The
bandit leader was visible as a diminishing figure on the road behind
them, running with the committed speed of a man who had fundamentally
reassessed his career choices.

Smiggles stood in the road. His unfinished incantation hung in the
air like a sentence abandoned mid-word. His moustache was in disarray.
His mouth was open.

"I told you so," he said.

---

The silence that followed lasted longer than any silence had a right
to last among five people standing on a road next to a dead wyvern
and a living dragon.

The dragon shifted. The process was not gradual. One moment the compact
green form filled the road, wings and scales and the dense coiled power
of a predator at rest. The next, Lai stood where the dragon had been,
small and green-skinned and yellow-eyed, her leather armour intact, her
wild hair lifting in a breeze that might have been wind and might have
been the residual displacement of a body that had just compressed
itself from something very large into something very small. She brushed
a strand of hair from her face with the casual gesture of someone who
had just done something unremarkable and was mildly surprised by the
attention.

"Mmmmm," she said. "That vas exciting."

"You're a dragon," Smiggles said.

"Yes."

"A green dragon."

"Yes."

"You have been walking with us for days, eating our food, telling us
stories about your teacher and his proverbs, tilting your head at
Iskar until his brain stopped functioning, and you are a dragon."

"Also yes." She smiled. The smile showed teeth that were, as they had
always been, slightly too sharp. "I vas going to tell you. Eventually."

"When, exactly? When you set fire to something? Do green dragons
breathe fire? No, acid. When you dissolved something? When the
situation arose organically?"

"Something like that."

Smiggles looked at Aizune. Aizune looked at Smiggles. The exchange
that passed between them was the exchange of two men who had each, in
their own way, known that something about this woman was wrong, and
who now understood what that wrongness was, and who were processing the
understanding in very different registers: Aizune with the quiet
recalibration of a priest whose divine senses had been telling him the
truth all along, and Smiggles with the volcanic indignation of a man
whose analytical pride had been personally affronted.

"I knew," Smiggles said. "I knew something didn't calibrate. The
horses. The speed. The way she carried firewood like it weighed
nothing. The way she navigated terrain in the dark. The eyes, for
Rivoleen's sake, the eyes." He turned to Lai. "Green dragon. Lawful
evil by nature, if the texts are correct. Acid breath. Shapechanging.
Casting with nothing but her voice, no gestures, no components, and
from any tradition she pleases, arcane or divine or druidic, it
doesn't matter, she simply speaks and the magic answers. Silence
workings slide off her like water off scale. And she can unravel
another mage's spell with a word while she's casting her own." He
ticked each item on his fingers with the precise fury of a man
reciting evidence at a tribunal. "What else? What else are you
hiding?"

"Many things." The amusement had not left her voice, but beneath it
there was something steadier, something that the playful mask had been
covering and that the mask's removal had exposed: a watchfulness, a
weight, the particular gravity of a creature that was older than it
looked and more dangerous than it admitted. "But I am not hiding my
intentions. I am here because I chose to be. I could leave. I could
have left at any time. I stayed because..." She paused. The "Mmmmm"
sound, low and drawn out, the sound of something tasting the air. "I
stayed because what you found beneath that keep matters. And because
the world I live in is the world those orcs want to destroy."

"Self-interest," Smiggles said.

"Self-interest aligned with your survival. Is that not sufficient?"

Twenty paces behind her, Aleron had pressed himself against a tree at
the road's edge. His grey eyes were wide and unfocused, the echoes
crowding his perception, translucent figures multiplying around him
like reflections in shattered glass. His lips moved in rapid,
soundless conversation. His hands gripped the quarterstaff so tightly
that the knuckles had gone white, and a sheen of sweat stood on his
translucent skin despite the cool of the afternoon.

Lai did not look at him. The deliberateness of this was visible.

"He cannot... he cannot be near her," Aleron said, and it took Aizune
a moment to realise that the elf was speaking about himself in the
third person, or was relaying the words of something else that was
speaking about him, the distinction unclear and perhaps irrelevant.
"The echoes. They... when she is close, they scream. Her nature and
mine, they... they do not agree. Like oil on water. Like..." He
pressed one hand to his temple. "Like two songs in different keys
played at the same time. It hurts."

"Does he feel the same to you?" Aizune asked Lai.

Her yellow eyes narrowed. For the first time, the amusement was
entirely absent. "He feels wrong," she said. "Like standing near a
fire that burns cold. Like the air around him is... stretched. Thin.
As though reality itself is uncomfortable with his presence." She
paused. "I do not enjoy it."

"Then we maintain distance," Aizune said. "Between the two of you,
at all times. Can you work together at that range?"

"I can work with anyone," Lai said.

"I can... endure," Aleron said. His eyes had refocused, the echoes
subsiding as Lai shifted her weight subtly backward, increasing the
gap between them by another few paces. "I have endured worse."

Smiggles had been watching this exchange with his arms folded and his
moustache still bristling. "Fine," he said. "Fine. The dragon stays.
The mad elf stays. We maintain a twenty-pace buffer between the two
of them at all times, like managing seating at a dinner party where
the guests might accidentally destroy reality." He turned on his heel
and walked back toward his horse. "Gears and grindstones. I need a
drink."

Iskar had not moved from the spot where he had drawn his rapier at the
bandits' charge. The rapier was still in his hand, though the hand had
lowered to his side and the blade's point rested in the dirt. He was
looking at Lai with an expression that Smiggles, who prided himself on
reading situations, could not immediately categorise. It was not shock.
It was not fear. It was not the simple attraction that had characterised
his previous responses to her. It was something more complex: the face
of a man who had known, who had sensed from the beginning that the mask
was a mask, and who was now seeing the face beneath it for the first
time and discovering that it was more beautiful and more terrifying than
he had imagined.

"A dragon," he said.

"A dragon," she agreed.

"You dissolved that wyvern's face."

"It was trying to eat you."

"I appreciate that. Trrruly." He paused. The rapier came up, was
sheathed with the musical sound of steel against leather that was as
natural to him as breathing. "I have one question."

"Ask."

"The head tilt. The one that makes me lose my trrain of thought.
Is that a dragon thing, or is that a you thing?"

She laughed. The sound was bright and musical and carried, beneath the
amusement, the deeper resonance of something much larger speaking
through a much smaller throat. "Mmmmm. That is entirely a me thing."

"Good," Iskar said. "I would hate to think it was generic."

---

Instar announced itself from a distance of ten miles, which was
appropriate for a city that considered understatement a form of
intellectual cowardice.

The Jinook Valley opened before them on the afternoon of the fourth
day, a broad river valley of cultivated fields and terraced hillsides
that channelled the eye toward its eastern end, where the city rose
against the foothills like a declaration of intent. Towers. Dozens of
them, scores of them, rising from the city's heart in every
architectural tradition the known world could produce and several it
could not: slender spires of white stone, squat cylindrical keeps of
dark granite, spiralling constructs of glass and metal that caught
the afternoon sun and broke it into spectrums that painted the
surrounding buildings in shifting colour. Between the towers, the city
sprawled in terraces and plazas and broad avenues, and every surface,
every wall, every archway and dome and bridge, carried the mark of
magic in the form of permanent lights that were already glowing in the
afternoon sun, blue-white and steady, anticipating the dusk that was
still hours away.

"Gears and grindstones," Smiggles breathed.

He had been to Instar once before, years ago, passing through on his
way south from Zhonhylite, and the memory of that visit had lodged in
his mind as a splinter lodges in skin: small, persistent, impossible
to ignore. But memory, he was discovering, was a poor architect. It
had built a model of Instar that was accurate in its details and
utterly inadequate in its scale. The city was larger than he remembered.
More complex. More alive with the particular energy that mages produced
when they were concentrated in sufficient numbers: a hum in the air
that was not sound but presence, the accumulated mana of hundreds of
spellcasters living and working in proximity, each one a small sun in
the invisible spectrum, and together they burned with a brightness that
made Smiggles' trained senses itch in a way that was half pleasure and
half headache.

"It's bigger than I expected," Iskar said, which was generous, because
the expression on his face suggested that what he had expected and what
he was seeing occupied different categories of experience entirely.

"It's a magocracy," Smiggles said. "The entire city is governed by
the most powerful mages in the Merchant Nations. Social status is
determined by arcane ability. The more powerful you are, the higher
your standing. If you can't cast, you're..." He searched for a
diplomatic way to phrase it and failed. "Furniture."

"Charming."

"It's not charming. It's efficient. And arrogant. And brilliant. And
infuriating." He kicked his horse to a faster walk. "I love it."

They entered through the western gate, where the guards wore robes
instead of armour and the gate tax was assessed not in coin but in a
brief examination of each traveller's mana signature, a process that
involved a guard pressing two fingers to the traveller's wrist and
concentrating for three seconds. Aizune's divine mana registered as a
warm pulse that made the guard nod with professional respect. Smiggles'
arcane mana produced a sharper reading that earned a second glance and
a slight widening of the eyes. Iskar's bardic resonance was apparently
less impressive: the guard's expression remained polite but
uninterested, the look of a man assessing a candle in a city of
bonfires.

"I have been weighed and found wanting," Iskar said, "by a man at a
gate. My prride may never recoverr."

Aleron's reading made the guard step back. The man's face went pale,
his fingers jerking from the elf's wrist as though he had touched a
hot surface, and he waved them through without meeting Aleron's eyes.

Lai was not examined. She had stepped past the guard with a smile and
a murmured word that Smiggles could not hear but that made the guard's
expression go blank and pleasant in the way that expressions went
blank and pleasant when someone was being very good at magic that you
did not want people to notice. Smiggles noticed. He added it to the
list, which was now long enough to fill a small book.

---

The Thaumaturgic Council received them in a chamber of pale stone and
permanent light, three days after their arrival.

The intervening days had been spent in the elaborate social
choreography that Instar required of anyone seeking an audience with
its ruling body: letters of introduction, submitted through
intermediaries, reviewed by functionaries whose arcane credentials
exceeded those of most city rulers, and finally acknowledged by a
clerk whose desk occupied an antechamber so magnificently appointed
that Iskar asked, in genuine confusion, whether they had already
reached the Council hall.

It was Aleron who secured the audience. Not through social navigation,
at which the wandering elf was profoundly untalented, but through a
demonstration that he offered to the clerk with the weary pragmatism
of a man who had learned that words convinced academics far less
effectively than evidence. He placed his hand flat on the clerk's desk,
closed his eyes, and the desk disappeared for two seconds, occupying a
point in the Astral Plane, before returning to its original position
with all papers undisturbed.

The clerk had them before the Council the following morning.

The chamber was vast, circular, its walls lined with shelves that held
not books but crystalline spheres of varying size, each one containing
a preserved magical effect: spells trapped in amber, enchantments
frozen mid-execution, the collected research of centuries stored in
glass and light. A long curved table dominated the centre, at which
sat seven members of the Thaumaturgic Council, chosen by their
colleagues to evaluate the visitors' claim.

Smiggles recognised Yelinda Featherstep immediately. The Ya'sehrim
enchantress who co-ruled Instar was smaller than he had imagined from
her reputation, fine-boned and silver-haired, her dark eyes carrying
the accumulated weight of over a century of governance and the
particular sharpness of an elf who had been patient with fools for
longer than most fools had been alive. Beside her sat Myth'raal
Dragonsbane, the Yav'daerim evoker, whose force magic specialty made
him the Council's foremost expert on the physical properties of
extraplanar materials. A gnome sat at the table's end, small and
bright-eyed, his hands stained with alchemical residue, and Smiggles'
stomach performed a slow, cold revolution as he recognised the family
resemblance in the sharp nose and the quick, assessing gaze.

Snarf Copperthumb. Gnome alchemist. Cousin of Mastersmith Pendrik
Copperthumb, who led the Guilded Cog, who maintained a standing
bounty on a certain mage who had embarrassed the consortium by
publishing evidence of defective gemscrolls in the Zhonhylite market
quarter.

Smiggles adjusted his moustache, adopted an expression of scholarly
neutrality, and made a private decision to speak as little as
possible and let Aizune handle the introductions.

Aizune handled the introductions. His Ya'sehrim heritage and
Cundo'elda credentials granted him standing that the Council
respected, and he presented their case with the measured precision of
a priest accustomed to delivering complex truths to sceptical
audiences. The machine. The vision. The alternate reality. The Orceri
spy's body. He spoke for twenty minutes, and the Council listened
without interruption, which Smiggles recognised as the highest
compliment Instar could pay.

Then Smiggles placed the items on the table.

The adamantine blade. The strange coins. The wrist device with its
crystal that caught the light and threw it back too blue. He arranged
them in a precise line, said nothing, and stepped back. The items
spoke for themselves.

Myth'raal Dragonsbane picked up the blade. His hands, fine-fingered
and scarred with the occupational marks of a career spent working with
force effects, turned it in the light. A shimmer of warm gold, the
force mage's diagnostic cantrip, played across the metal. His
expression changed.

"The molecular structure is wrong," he said. "The adamantine lattice
is consistent with known properties, but the secondary alloy is
nothing I have encountered. The binding frequencies between the metals
are..." He trailed off, turned the blade again. "They are tuned to a
vibrational signature that does not correspond to any frequency
catalogued in the Jinook Repository. This was not forged on our
material plane. It was not forged on any known plane."

"An alternate prime," Yelinda Featherstep said. The words were quiet,
precise, delivered with the composure of a woman who had governed a
city of mages for over a century and had learned to absorb extraordinary
information without flinching. "You are claiming that these items
originate from an alternate prime material plane."

"We are not claiming," Aizune said. "We are presenting evidence and
asking for your assessment."

"My assessment," Myth'raal said, still holding the blade, "is that
your evidence is genuine. These items were manufactured in a reality
whose fundamental vibrational properties differ from ours. The
craftsmanship is extraordinary. The technology implied by this device,"
he indicated the wrist mechanism, "is beyond anything in our current
capability."

A silence followed. The crystalline spheres on the walls seemed to
pulse with it, the preserved magic within them resonating with the
implications of what had just been confirmed.

"We need a planar tuning fork," Smiggles said. He had intended to let
Aizune speak, but the words came out before his intention could stop
them, pulled from his mouth by the specific urgency of a problem that
had a specific solution. "Attuned to the frequency of the alternate
prime these items originated from. Using the items themselves as
calibration material. With that fork, and with Aleron's abilities, we
can transit to the other reality and conduct reconnaissance."

Yelinda Featherstep studied him. Her dark eyes lingered on his face,
moved to his moustache, moved to his hands, returned to his eyes. The
scrutiny lasted three seconds and felt like a year.

"The process would require approximately one week," she said. "The
calibration is delicate. The items would need to remain with our
artificers during the tuning."

"Agreed."

"In exchange, the Council requires full disclosure of all intelligence
gathered during any crossing into the alternate prime. Reports,
observations, material samples if obtainable. Instar will serve as
the primary analytical resource for any findings. This is not a
request."

"Understood," Aizune said.

"Then we are agreed." Yelinda stood. The other Council members stood
with her, a synchronised motion that was not choreographed but simply
the natural response of powerful people to a signal from the most
powerful person in the room. "One week. You will be provided lodgings
in the scholars' quarter. I suggest you use the time productively."

Snarf Copperthumb had been examining the coins throughout the
discussion, turning them in his small hands with the practised speed
of a man who could assess a metal's composition by weight and texture
alone. As the Council rose, his bright eyes found Smiggles across the
table, and they held for a moment, and in that moment Smiggles saw
the calculation happening behind them: the family resemblance working
in reverse, the gnomish features being compared against a description
that had circulated through Guilded Cog channels for years.

Smiggles met the gaze with the steady, expressionless calm of a man
who had been assessed before and had survived the assessment. After a
moment, Snarf looked away, returning his attention to the coins. The
exchange was brief enough that no one else noticed.

Smiggles noticed. He made a private decision to avoid the alchemical
wing of the city for the duration of their stay.

---

The week in Instar was the most productive seven days of Smiggles'
life, and he resented every moment of its brevity.

He haunted the arcane libraries with the single-minded hunger of a man
who had been denied access to proper scholarly resources for years and
intended to consume as much as possible before the door closed again.
The Jinook Repository alone contained texts he had only read
references to in other texts: planar cosmology treatises written by
mages who had conducted actual interdimensional research, metallurgical
analyses of extraplanar materials, theoretical frameworks for the
observation of alternate realities that predated Zanthar's machine by
centuries. He read for twelve hours a day, took notes in a hand that
grew increasingly illegible as the days progressed, and emerged each
evening with the wild-eyed intensity of a man in the grip of an
intellectual fever that sleep could not cure.

Iskar worked the taverns. Instar's social establishments were not the
rough dockside haunts he preferred, but they served the same function:
places where information flowed with the drink and a man with the
right questions and the right manner could learn things that official
channels would never disclose. He mapped Instar's informal power
structures in three days, identified the taverns where junior Council
functionaries relaxed their discretion, and built a network of
contacts that would serve the group if they needed to return. He also
found a tavern that served Sabonaar food, which he declared "acceptable,
though the cook has neverr seen the ocean and it shows in the fish."

Aizune visited the temples. Instar's divine community was small but
distinguished, centred on the shrines of Alagalothor and Minerva that
occupied a quiet plaza in the city's southern quarter. He spent an
afternoon in conversation with Ariana Ignescreo, the ifrit cleric of
Mau who sat on the Council.

She received him in a garden behind the shrine of Mau, a space of
white gravel and carefully placed stones that she maintained herself,
and his first thought upon seeing her was that the word "ifrit" had
prepared him for fire but not for this. Ariana Ignescreo was fire made
flesh and the flesh had been made generous. Her skin was the colour of
cooling embers, a deep burnished red that darkened to black at her
fingertips and the points of her ears and lightened to molten copper
at her throat and the hollows of her collarbones. Her eyes flickered
with an inner flame that was literal rather than metaphorical, twin
points of orange-gold that moved with a liquid heat behind irises that
were not truly irises at all but something closer to windows into a
furnace. Her hair was a cascade of deep crimson and black that fell
past her shoulders and across her chest, where it served, alongside
a draping of sheer Varkonan silk so thin it concealed nothing and
merely suggested that concealment had been considered and rejected, as
the only barrier between her skin and the afternoon air. She was
broad-hipped and full-bodied in the way of fire: generous, consuming,
unapologetic. Every movement she made produced a faint shimmer of
heat distortion in the air around her, as though her body existed at
a temperature that the surrounding atmosphere found difficult to
accommodate. She sat cross-legged on the white gravel with the
unselfconscious ease of a being for whom clothing was a concession to
mortal custom rather than a personal requirement, and the warmth that
radiated from her skin was detectable from six feet away, a gentle,
persistent heat that smelled of woodsmoke and cinnamon and something
sweeter that Aizune could not name and suspected was the scent of
divine fire translated into a fragrance the mortal nose could process
without igniting.

She smiled when he approached, and the smile was warm in every sense
the word possessed.

"A priest of Minerva," she said. Her voice carried the warmth of fire
and the cool precision of someone who served a deity of secrets and
had learned to measure every word against the weight of what it
revealed. "In Instar. That is unusual enough to be interesting. Sit
with me."

He sat. The gravel was warm beneath him, heated by her proximity or by
the sun or by both, and the conversation that followed was one of the
most unsettling and illuminating of his life, not because of what she
said but because of the way she said it: with the intimate directness
of a being whose relationship with truth was physical rather than
philosophical, her flame-flickering eyes holding his without evasion,
her body shifting on the gravel with a sinuous ease that drew the
sheer silk across her skin in ways that were, he suspected, not
entirely accidental.

"You ask whether entities can be destroyed across alternate realities,"
Ariana said. She leaned forward as she spoke, and the cascade of
crimson-black hair shifted across her chest, and the silk shifted with
it, and Aizune fixed his gaze on the white gravel between them with
the disciplined focus of a priest whose training had prepared him for
many trials but not, specifically, for this one. "The answer is yes.
An entity's consciousness is not distributed across alternates. Each
alternate reality contains its own version of each entity, formed
independently. Destroy the entity in one reality, and the others
continue. But the destruction creates a... wound. A scar in the
dimensional fabric at the point where that entity's plane existed."

"And if the entity is Minerva?" Aizune asked. "If, in another reality,
Minerva was destroyed completely, her consciousness obliterated, what
would that mean for the elves of that world?"

"It would mean exactly what you fear." The ifrit's flame-flickering
eyes held Aizune's without evasion. She reached out and touched his
wrist, briefly, and where her fingers met his skin the warmth was
startling, not burning but intimate, the temperature of a body that
ran hotter than any mortal's and did not apologise for it. "An
entity's bond with its chosen people is reciprocal. Destroy the
entity, and the bond severs. The people do not die. But they lose
something that cannot be replaced: the connection to the divine that
gives their culture its foundation, their magic its source, their
identity its root. They become... unmoored. Vulnerable in ways that
physical destruction cannot achieve."

Aizune touched the Minerva clasp at his throat. The silver was cool
against his fingers, a counterpoint to the lingering warmth where
Ariana's hand had rested on his wrist. The ifrit watched him do it
and said nothing more, because there was nothing more to say.

Aleron spent the week in their lodgings, resting. The transit
demonstration at the gate and the Council audience had cost him, and
the cost was visible in the deepening hollows beneath his eyes and
the increased frequency of his conversations with the unseen.
Smiggles, passing through the common room at odd hours, would find him
sitting by the window, talking to someone in the empty chair beside
him, his grey eyes focused on a presence that the firelight could not
illuminate.

"Are they real?" Smiggles asked him once. "The things you see. The
people you talk to."

Aleron considered the question with the careful attention he gave to
everything. "Some of them... I think so. Echoes. Versions of people
from other realities, bleeding through the... the fractures that the
gift created. Others are..." He paused. Listened. "I don't know.
And I have... I have stopped trying to determine which is which. The
distinction matters less than you would think."

"It matters if they're giving you bad information."

"They give me all kinds of information. The trick is not to... to sort
the true from the false. The trick is to act on what you can verify
and endure what you cannot." He smiled, the small, sad expression
that surfaced during his lucid moments. "You do the same thing. You
just do it with physical evidence instead of... invisible
conversationalists."

"I prefer my method."

"So would I, if the choice were... were mine."

And Lai moved through Instar with the ease of a creature returning to
familiar water.

This was the detail that Smiggles could not reconcile, the piece that
would not fit the puzzle he had constructed around the young dragon's
identity. She navigated the city without hesitation, turning down
streets before landmarks appeared, choosing the shorter route through
the scholars' quarter without consulting the maps that the lodgings
provided, pausing before specific buildings with the proprietary
attention of someone recognising places she had known in a previous
life. She knew which vendors in the market plaza sold the best
pastries. She knew which bridges over the canal were faster at
different times of day. She knew, and this was the detail that settled
in Smiggles' mind with the immovable weight of a conclusion he could
not yet prove, the name of a bookshop in the eastern quarter that was
not on any map and whose owner greeted her with the practised
familiarity of someone welcoming a regular customer.

On the fifth day, Smiggles was crossing the scholars' quarter plaza
with Aizune when the crowd parted, as crowds in Instar invariably did,
for two figures whose combined presence commanded the particular
deference that only profound magical power could produce.

The first was a man of Varkonan descent, tall and dark-skinned, his
features carrying the ancient geometry of the desert people,
his robes of deep crimson embroidered with gold thread in patterns
that shifted when the light struck them at different angles. His
bearing was that of a man who had been powerful for so long that power
had become invisible to him, the way air is invisible to those who
breathe it. Salrutis. Varkonan sorcerer. Member of the Thaumaturgic
Council. The name surfaced in Smiggles' memory from the Council
audience, where the sorcerer had been absent.

Beside him walked a man who was smaller and older and carried himself
with the particular stillness of deep water: Yigba, the Ejiin wu-jen,
co-ruler of Instar, whose power was reputed to be the greatest on the
Council and whose manner was the most reserved. His face was lined but
ageless in the way of those whose inner discipline arrested the
external marks of time. His dark eyes moved across the plaza with the
unhurried attention of someone who saw everything and commented on
nothing.

They passed within ten feet of where Smiggles and Aizune stood.
Salrutis's gaze swept over them without pausing. Yigba did not look
at them at all.

Smiggles turned to make a remark to Lai, who had been walking three
paces behind them.

She was gone.

He scanned the plaza. The crowd moved in its usual patterns, mages
and scholars and functionaries flowing through the space with the
purposeful rhythm of people who had places to be and spells to cast.
No green-skinned woman. No yellow eyes. No wild cascade of shifting
hair. She had vanished with a completeness that was entirely
consistent with a creature that could reshape itself with a thought
and bend light around her body with a murmured word.

"She saw them," he said to Aizune.

Aizune's dark grey eyes tracked the retreating figures of Salrutis
and Yigba as they crossed the plaza and disappeared into the portico
of the Council hall. "She saw them," he agreed. "And she did not want
them to see her."

"A dragon who knows Instar intimately, who is comfortable here, who
navigates the city like she grew up in it, and who hides from two
members of the Thaumaturgic Council." Smiggles' moustache twitched.
"That's not a stripped thread. That's a whole new clock."

"One we cannot assemble yet."

"No. But I'm keeping every gear."

Lai reappeared twenty minutes later, emerging from a side street with
an apple in her hand and an expression of serene unconcern. She
offered no explanation. They did not ask for one, though Smiggles'
silence on the subject was the loudest thing about him for the rest
of the afternoon.

The tuning fork was ready on the seventh day.

It was smaller than Smiggles had expected: a slender instrument of
pale silver metal, no longer than his hand, its two tines vibrating
at a frequency that was felt rather than heard, a deep, constant hum
that resonated in the bones of his fingers when he held it and
produced, at the back of his skull, a sensation that was not pain and
not pleasure but recognition, the vibrational signature of a reality
he had glimpsed through a dead wizard's machine and would soon walk
upon with his own feet.

"The calibration is precise to within a margin that we consider
acceptable for exploratory transit," Myth'raal Dragonsbane said,
handing the fork to Aleron, whose grey eyes brightened when his
fingers closed around it, the way a compass needle brightens when it
finds north. "The frequency is locked to the alternate prime from
which the items originated. It will not drift. It will not degrade.
It is, within the limits of our current understanding, a key to a
door that has never been opened from this side."

"It has been opened," Aleron said quietly. "Once. By a scout who
came through and did not... did not return."

"Then you will be the second. And the first to come back." Myth'raal's
expression was severe and professional and contained, beneath its
surface, a curiosity so intense it was almost hunger. "The Council
expects full intelligence reports. Observations, samples, analyses.
Whatever you find, we want to know."

"You will know," Aizune said.

They departed Instar that afternoon, riding west into the Jinook
Valley with the sun behind them and the tuning fork in Aleron's leg
pouch, resting against the other tuning forks he carried, the
tarnished keys to planes he had visited and survived. This new fork
was brighter than the others. Newer. Its hum was different: deeper,
steadier, carrying the particular resonance of a reality that had
been locked onto by the finest instruments in the Merchant Nations
and confirmed as real.

Smiggles rode in silence, his mind full of everything he had read in
the Jinook Repository and everything he had seen in the scholars'
quarter and the image, persistent and unresolved, of a green-skinned
woman disappearing into a crowd at the sight of two Council members
whose combined power could reshape the landscape of the valley they
were riding through.

Behind him, Lai walked at her usual distance from Aleron, her yellow
eyes on the road ahead, her expression unreadable, her secrets intact.

The road stretched west toward Amara, toward preparation, toward the
crossing that would take them from the world they knew into the world
they had glimpsed through a dead wizard's glass. The tuning fork hummed
in its pouch. The two moons rose, silver and gold, above the darkening
valley, and between them the sky was empty.

For now.
