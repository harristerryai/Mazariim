# Chapter One
## The Wizard's Grief

The stones remembered what the world above had forgotten.

Deep beneath the Steppes of Broken Stone, in chambers carved by hand and magic and the slow patient work of years, a man stood before a thing that should not exist. The air was close and still, heavy with the mineral tang of freshly cut rock and the sharper scent of ozone that clung to places where mana had been shaped too often and too aggressively. Three lamps of permanent flame, their wicks long since burned away and replaced by steady spheres of amber light, illuminated a chamber roughly forty paces across and twice the height of the man who had built it. The walls were smooth, transmuted from raw sandstone into something harder and darker, veined with channels of copper and silver that traced geometric patterns too precise for any chisel. Draconic script covered every surface, thousands of characters inscribed with a jeweler's patience, each one a fragment of a formula that, taken together, described the architecture of reality itself.

And in the center of the chamber, the machine waited.

Three great rings of dark metal stood along the room's borders, each one twice the height of the man, each inscribed on every surface with Draconic runes so fine they seemed etched by light rather than tool. Gemstone powders filled the channels between the runes: ruby dust in the innermost ring, sapphire in the second, diamond in the third. The rings were mounted on mechanisms of transmuted bronze that allowed each to rotate independently, one on a vertical axis, one horizontal, one diagonal, their paths intersecting at a single point in the chamber's heart. There, on a pedestal of fused quartz, sat a glass globe no larger than a man's head, its surface so perfectly smooth it seemed less like glass and more like a captured bubble of still water.

The man who had built all of this stood before the pedestal with his hands at his sides and his heart hammering against his ribs.

Zanthar was not an old man, though grief had carved years into his face that time had not yet earned. His hair was dark and thick, worn long in the Sabonaar fashion and tied back with a leather cord. His skin was olive, weathered from years of coastal sun before he had buried himself beneath these stones, and his hands were calloused in ways that no mage's hands should be: from hauling rock, from mixingite morite, from the raw physical labor of building a sanctuary in a place where no one would think to look. His robes had been fine once, Dukhan-made, the deep blue of a licensed arcanist in the Empire's service. They were patched now, stained with mineral dust and ink and the residue of a hundred alchemical preparations. He wore them anyway. They were the last thing she had mended for him, the night before they came for her.

He closed his eyes. Drew a breath that tasted of copper and cold stone. Opened them.

"Begin," he said, to no one but himself and the silence.

His hands rose. Four syllables of Draconic left his mouth, precise and clipped, each consonant struck like a hammer on an anvil. The air around his fingers shimmered with a pale amber luminescence, the color of old parchment held before a candle. A conjurer's light, pulling energy from somewhere else, bending it to arrive here, now, in this shape. The geometric patterns on the walls responded. Copper channels flickered with sympathetic resonance, a faint humming that rose through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his legs.

He spoke again. Six syllables now, longer, more demanding, the sustained invocation of a working that required the full architecture of his training. The pale amber light at his hands deepened to gold, and he felt the familiar pressure behind his eyes, the sensation of mana flowing through channels in his mind that formal instruction had carved open decades ago in the lecture halls of Dukhan. His fingers traced interlocking geometries in the air, two-handed, precise, the somatic components of a working that existed in no spellbook because he had invented it. Seven years of theory. Three years of construction. Everything he had been, everything he had lost, distilled into this single act.

He placed his hands on the glass globe.

The world shifted.

The innermost ring unlocked with a sound like a sword drawn from a stone sheath, and it began to turn. Slowly at first. Ruby dust in its channels caught the light and threw crimson reflections across the ceiling. The second ring followed, rotating on its horizontal axis, sapphire dust blazing blue. The third ring engaged with a deep harmonic that Zanthar felt in his teeth and the base of his skull, diamond powder igniting into white brilliance as the diagonal rotation began.

The three rings spun faster. The chamber filled with light and sound, a rising harmonic that passed through audible frequencies and became something felt rather than heard, a vibration in the stone, in the air, in the architecture of the space between moments. The geometric patterns on the walls blazed copper-bright. The globe beneath his palms grew warm, then hot, then incandescent, and Zanthar gripped it and did not let go, because letting go now meant death or madness or both, and he had not spent seven years in a hole in the ground to flinch at the moment of truth.

The light became everything. The sound became silence. His body ceased to matter.

His consciousness poured outward like water through a shattered dam.

He saw the Steppes. His Steppes, the barren expanse of broken stone above his hidden chamber, stretching in every direction under a sky he had not seen in months. But he was not seeing through eyes. He was seeing through the machine, his awareness projected upward and outward at a speed that stole the breath from a body he could no longer feel. The Steppes blurred. The coast appeared, the glittering line of the Bay of Urgot, the harbor he had sailed from as a younger man when the world was whole and she was alive and the Empire had not yet taken everything from him.

A city where the coast met the river. Familiar. Almost familiar. The harbor was wrong. The buildings were arranged differently, clustered where open water should be, absent where warehouses had stood for generations. The great ziggurat of Zebulba that dominated every Dukhan city, the black stepped pyramid that had shadowed his childhood and consumed his wife, was not there. In its place stood structures he did not recognize: low, sprawling, built of pale stone in styles no Dukhan architect had ever conceived.

He looked up.

Three moons hung in the sky.

Two he knew. Io'el, the silver-white constant, the Night Star whose
twenty-eight-day cycle had governed calendars since before written
history. Randir'el, the pale gold wanderer, whose irregular orbit
made it a symbol of change and fortune. He had charted their
movements himself, using them to calibrate the machine's resonance
frequencies. But the third, smaller, reddish, occupying a position
between the others that should have been empty sky, was impossible.
There were two moons. There had always been two moons.

There were three.

The machine tore him back. The globe went dark beneath his palms. The rings slowed, their harmonic descending through frequencies that rattled loose stones from the chamber walls. Ruby and sapphire and diamond dust settled into their channels like exhausted soldiers collapsing into trenches.

Zanthar stood in the sudden dimness, his permanent flames guttering from the displaced air, his hands still gripping the cooling glass, and trembled.

He had seen another world.

Not a distant continent, not a hidden kingdom, not a pocket dimension folded into the spaces between planes. Another version of the world itself. The same coastline, the same bay, the same river meeting the same sea, but different. Changed. A world where the ziggurat had never been built, or had been torn down, or had never needed to exist because the Zebulban priests who demanded its construction had never risen to power.

A world where three moons crossed the sky instead of two.

Alternate realities were real.

He released the globe. His hands were shaking. He sank to the chamber floor, his back against the pedestal, his patched robes pooling around him. The copper channels in the walls still hummed faintly, residual mana bleeding away into the stone. He could taste it at the back of his throat: metallic, electric, the aftertaste of a working that had exceeded his most ambitious calculations.

He pressed the heels of his palms against his eyes and laughed. It was not a sane sound. It was the laughter of a man who had just proved that the universe was larger than anyone had ever imagined, and who understood, with the clarity that only grief and genius combined can produce, exactly what he was going to do with that knowledge.

If there were other worlds, there were other versions of everything.

Other versions of everyone.

Somewhere, in the infinite lattice of realities that his machine could reach, there was a world where Tatyana was alive.

---

Twenty-three years passed.

The chamber had not changed. The machine waited in its circle of dark metal and gemstone dust, patient as a held breath. The copper channels still traced their geometric prayers across the walls. The permanent flames still burned.

The man had changed beyond recognition.

Zanthar's hair was grey and wild, uncut and unwashed, falling past his shoulders in matted tangles that he neither noticed nor cared about. His face was gaunt, the olive skin pulled tight over cheekbones that jutted like the stones of his adopted wasteland. His eyes, which had once been dark and sharp with the focused intelligence of a trained arcanist, were sunken and restless, moving constantly, tracking things that were not in the chamber. His robes had been patched beyond patching. His hands, still calloused, now trembled with a persistent tremor that worsened when he was tired, which was always.

He was not alone. He had not been alone for years.

Dozens of mirrors filled the adjacent chamber, mounted on walls and stands and propped against stone surfaces in haphazard arrangements that had long since consumed any pretense of organization. Each mirror was a masterwork of transmutation and conjuration: glass transmuted to resonate at specific planar frequencies, frames enchanted with stabilization matrices that held the resonance constant, surfaces that looked like ordinary silvered glass until the light caught them at certain angles and revealed the faintest shimmer of amber, Zanthar's amber, the conjurer's light that was his signature.

In every mirror, a face moved.

His face. His face, repeated and refracted across infinite possibilities. In one mirror, a version of him worked a farm, brown-armed and sun-dark, swinging a scythe through golden wheat with the easy rhythm of a man who had never learned a word of Draconic. In another, he stood at a podium before a crowd, gesturing with the confidence of an orator, his hair silver but his back straight, his robes white and unmarked. In a third, he wore armor, heavy plate scored with the marks of battle, a sword at his hip, his face harder and older and marked with scars that the Zanthar of this reality had never earned.

Farmer. Orator. Soldier. Scholar. Merchant. Beggar. King. In one mirror, he was dead, a stone marker in a graveyard overgrown with moss. In another, he was a child, running through streets that might have been Dukhan before the Empire grew teeth. In another, he sat beside a fire with a woman whose back was turned, and Zanthar had spent eleven months staring at that mirror, waiting for her to turn around, and she never did, and he could not be certain, could never be certain, whether the dark hair that fell across her shoulders was the right dark hair, whether the curve of her neck belonged to the woman he was searching for or to a stranger wearing a shape that grief had taught him to see everywhere.

He had not found her. Twenty-three years. Hundreds of mirrors. Thousands of realities glimpsed and cataloged and discarded. He had mapped the resonance frequencies of forty-seven alternate material planes, charted the vibrational relationships between parallel worlds with a rigor that would have earned him the highest honors of any arcane academy in existence, and he had not found a single reality in which Tatyana lived.

He understood, on the level of mathematical certainty, that this meant nothing. The realities were infinite. His machine could access only a fraction of that infinity, constrained by the resonance range of his gemstone powders and the limitations of his own failing concentration. She was out there. She had to be out there. In a universe of infinite possibilities, the possibility of her was a certainty. He simply had not looked far enough.

He understood, on another level entirely, that he had stopped believing this some time ago.

He stood before the machine. The mirrors watched from their chamber, his infinite selves living their infinite lives, none of them searching, none of them ruined, none of them standing in a hole beneath a wasteland with shaking hands and a mind that had been coming apart for years. The globe waited on its pedestal. The rings waited in their cradles. The chamber waited, patient as a tomb.

He thought of her.

Not the abstraction of her, the theoretical Tatyana who existed in an unvisited frequency, but the real woman. The weight of her hand in his. The sound of her laughter in their small house in the shadow of the ziggurat, laughter she kept quiet because the priests had ears everywhere and joy was suspicious, joy meant you had something worth taking. Her hair, dark and heavy, falling across the pillow when she slept. The way she argued with merchants, hands on her hips, chin raised, magnificent in her refusal to be cheated. The night she told him about Tiovin, about the prayers she whispered when he was not home, about the faith she had kept hidden like a coal in her chest for years, and the look in her eyes when she asked if he would keep her secret, and the steadiness of his voice when he said yes. The morning the Zebulban priests came for her, six of them in their grey robes with their dead eyes, and the sound the door made when they broke it, and the sound she made when they took her, and the silence after.

The way they killed her. In the square. Before the ziggurat. While the city watched. While he watched, held by two guards who did not need to hold him hard because he could not move, could not breathe, could not do anything but watch the woman he loved die for the crime of believing in something good.

He placed his hands on the globe. His fingers were trembling. His eyes were wet.

"Tatyana," he said.

The machine responded. The rings unlocked. Ruby dust blazed crimson. Sapphire ignited. Diamond flared white. The harmonic rose through the chamber and the copper channels sang and the globe grew hot beneath his shaking hands and his consciousness poured outward, outward, reaching for a frequency he could feel but not define, a resonance that was not mathematical but emotional, not calculated but desperate, the machine responding not to precision but to need, and need was not enough, had never been enough, and the frequency shifted and wobbled and his concentration, his poor shattered concentration that had held him together for twenty-three years through grief and obsession and the slow erosion of everything that made a man a man, faltered.

The harmonic screamed.

Light filled the chamber. Not amber. Not gold. White. Pure white, the color of mana stripped of intention, raw force without shape or direction. The globe erupted. The rings spun beyond their tolerances, their mechanisms shrieking, gemstone powder flaring in colors that had no names. Zanthar felt his hands burning and could not release them. He felt his body lifting from the floor. He felt, for one instant, a presence vast and patient and unutterably ancient, as though the machine in its death throes had brushed against something that existed between all realities, something that watched and remembered and did not intervene.

In the last moment, he saw her face.

Not in a mirror. Not through the machine. Simply there, in the white light, as clear and present as the day he married her. Her dark hair. Her brown eyes, warm and fierce and full of the stubborn compassion that had killed her. She was smiling. He was certain she was smiling.

The light took him.

The shockwave erupted from the machine like a living thing, a wall of compressed force that blew outward through the chamber door, howled through the corridor, screamed up through the tower's hidden stairways and burst from the disguised boulders into the empty sky above the Steppes of Broken Stone. For a moment the wasteland was illuminated: a pillar of white light and displaced air punching upward into the darkness, visible for miles to anyone who might have been watching, though no one was, because no one knew this place existed and no one was looking.

The shockwave reached its apex. Hung in the sky, silent, trembling.

Fell back.

It came down like a hammer. The disguised boulders shattered. The tower walls cracked and buckled inward. Stone collapsed upon stone in a cascading roar that echoed across the empty Steppes, and when the dust settled and the echoes faded and the silence returned, the surface was nothing but rubble. A mound of broken stone indistinguishable from the thousand other mounds of broken stone that gave the Steppes their name.

Below, the lower chambers held. The walls Zanthar had transmuted from sandstone into something harder and darker absorbed the shockwave's return and did not yield. The machine room was intact. The globe sat dark and cold on its pedestal, cracked along one meridian but whole. The rings had stopped. The gemstone dust had settled. The copper channels were dark.

Of Zanthar, nothing remained. Not ash. Not bone. Not a scorch mark on the stone floor. He had been unmade, his substance converted to the energy that had nearly torn his creation apart, and that energy had spent itself against the sky and the stone and the silence of a wasteland that did not care.

The permanent flames still burned. They would burn for centuries, faithful to the enchantment of a man who no longer existed, casting their steady amber light across an empty room.

---

Centuries passed.

Sand blew through cracks in the rubble above. Rain seeped down through layers of collapsed stone, leaving mineral traces on the walls of the mirror chamber like the tracks of slow tears. The permanent flames burned on, their light unchanged, illuminating mirrors that no one watched.

But the mirrors watched.

The images still moved, though they had long since ceased to show anything real. The mirrors had lost their connection to the living worlds beyond in the same instant Zanthar lost his life, the resonance that sustained them severed when the machine screamed and the man who fed it was unmade. What remained were echoes: captured fragments, moments of observation seared into the enchanted glass like light burned into the eye after staring at the sun. The farmer still swung his scythe, the same scythe, the same stroke, the same arc of golden wheat falling and falling and falling again in an endless repetition that no longer corresponded to any living arm or any standing field. The orator still addressed his crowds, the same gesture, the same silent word shaped by the same silver-haired mouth, a loop of frozen eloquence playing to an audience that had aged and died and been forgotten centuries ago. The soldier still bore his scars. They moved in their silvered frames with the mechanical persistence of enchantments that had been built to last, and they did last, through decades and centuries, through the slow accumulation of dust and silence and the patient work of time, faithfully replaying moments that belonged to a world that had long since moved beyond them.

Something changed.

It did not happen quickly. It happened the way rot happens, the way rust happens, the way a crack in a foundation spreads: invisibly, inevitably, one small surrender at a time. The images in the mirrors began to flicker. Not with the steady shimmer of Zanthar's enchantments but with something else, a quality of movement that was wrong in ways that would have been difficult to articulate even if anyone had been present to observe it. Shadows fell at angles the chamber's lights did not support. Reflections lingered a fraction too long after their sources moved. In one mirror, and then in several, the images of Zanthar's alternate selves paused in their endless routines and turned, slowly, toward the surface of the glass, as though something on the other side had caught their attention.

The permanent flames guttered. Just once. Just for a moment.

In the silence of the mirror chamber, something that was not Zanthar and was not alive and was not entirely dead began, with the patience of centuries and the hunger of an open wound, to move.
