# Chapter Seven
## The Skymage's Assessment

The wind at forty thousand feet carried no sound.

Yzrat had learned this in his first year of flight training,
when the instructors at the Orceri Aerial Command had strapped
cadets to the observation rails of training dirigibles and sent
them above the weather ceiling and told them to listen. The
cadets had listened. They had heard nothing. The instructors
had explained: at sufficient altitude, the atmosphere thinned
past the point where sound could propagate with any efficiency,
and what remained was not silence but the absence of the medium
through which silence was defined, a negation so complete that
the ears, deprived of input, began to manufacture their own,
producing phantom whistles and hums and the faint rhythmic
pulse of one's own blood moving through the vessels of the
inner ear. The instructors had called this the Voice of
Altitude. Most cadets found it unsettling. Yzrat had found it
restful.

Forty years later, perched in the command saddle of his
biomantically enhanced blue dragon mount at an altitude that
would have killed an unprotected being in minutes, Yzrat found
it restful still.

The mount banked in a slow, patient arc, its wings extended to
their full span, the ridges of dark metal fused to scale
catching the starlight and returning it as the cold shimmer of
mana circuitry that kept the creature's blood warm and its
muscles functional in air that was thin enough to see the
curvature of the world through. The night was clear. Both moons
were up: silver Io'el full and bright, pale gold Randir'el in
its waning quarter, and between them the empty space where the
third moon would have hung if this had been Ta'Puh, and the
empty space was the first thing Yzrat checked every time he
looked at the sky of this world, because the absence confirmed
his location in the way that a compass confirmed heading, and
a skymage who lost track of which reality he occupied was a
skymage who would not occupy any reality for long.

Below him, the world called Mazariim spread to every horizon.

He adjusted his goggles. The lenses were not glass but
crystalline mana-lattice, grown in Ozmakir's laboratory from
materials harvested during the seventh tangent conquest, and
they translated the visual spectrum into a layered display that
overlaid the physical landscape with its mana topology: the
thin, diffuse wash of ambient magical energy that covered the
world like dew, punctuated by brighter concentrations where
spellcasters worked or wards persisted or natural mana wells
bubbled up from the geological substrate. The display was
colour-coded by tradition and intensity. Arcane signatures
registered in warm gold. Divine signatures in silver-white.
Draconic signatures in deep layered green. And at the edges
of the display, where the goggles' sensitivity approached its
limits, the faint distortions that dimensional anomalies
produced: not colour but the absence of colour, holes in the
mana field where something that did not belong to this reality
pressed against the fabric that separated this from elsewhere.

He had been watching this world for weeks.

The initial deployment had been straightforward. Li'Zhan's
priority alert had come through the command structure with the
particular urgency that unscheduled interdimensional contact
warranted: a tangent not yet catalogued, a viewing device of
unknown origin, a brief but unmistakable resonance that the
sensorium network at Zone Kluth Tak had detected and classified
and reported with the polyphonic efficiency that justified the
network's considerable construction costs. Yzrat had been
assigned the reconnaissance. He had crossed to the new tangent
on his mount, established an observation pattern at extreme
altitude, deployed invisibility protocols that bent light and
sound around his position with a thoroughness that native mages
could not penetrate, and begun the systematic process of
intelligence gathering that the Orceri Aerial Command had
refined across eleven previous operations.

The process was methodical. It was patient. It was, Yzrat
reflected as the mount completed another arc and the goggles
painted the landscape below in the jewelled colours of magical
taxonomy, the most professionally satisfying work he had done
in decades.

The natives were interesting.

---

The five figures he had been tracking since the initial contact
had proven more capable than their individual power levels
suggested. This was the assessment that had delayed his report
to Rangithyr, the assessment that had kept him circling above
this world's coastal city of Amara and its surrounding
territories for weeks rather than the days that a standard
tangent reconnaissance required: the natives were individually
weak by Orceri standards, mid-tier spellcasters and a bardic
fighter whose combined output would not have troubled a single
Orceri combat squad, but they had demonstrated a capacity for
coordination and adaptation that their raw capabilities did not
predict.

He reviewed the data.

The planar anomaly remained the primary variable. The elf whose
dimensional signature had caused Yzrat's goggles to malfunction
during the first observation, whose mana profile defied every
classification system the Orceri had developed across four
centuries of interdimensional reconnaissance, was a factor that
no previous tangent had produced. Eleven worlds conquered, and
in none of them had Yzrat or any skymage in the Aerial Command
encountered a being whose relationship with the dimensional
fabric was so fundamental that standard instruments could not
resolve it. The anomaly was not powerful in the conventional
sense. His physical signature was diminished, fragile, the
output of a body that had been consuming itself for decades.
But the dimensional component was without precedent, a
distortion in the fabric of reality that surrounded the elf
like an atmosphere, and the distortion had implications that
Yzrat's training required him to flag and that his professional
instinct required him to treat with the particular caution
reserved for variables whose behaviour could not be predicted
from existing models.

The disguised dragon was the secondary concern. The draconic
signature that Yzrat had detected during the initial
observation, the deep layered green that his goggles rendered
as a compressed resonance far exceeding the being's apparent
size, was a young green wyrm travelling in humanoid form. Young
dragons were not, by themselves, militarily significant. Young
dragons accompanied by anomalies and mid-tier spellcasters who
had demonstrated the ability to penetrate Orceri territory
undetected were a different calculation.

The three standard operatives, the elven priest, the gnomish
mage, and the human bard, were the connective tissue. Their
individual capabilities were unremarkable. Their collective
function was not. The priest provided divine support and social
navigation. The mage provided arcane firepower and analytical
problem-solving. The bard provided infiltration, morale, and
the kind of adaptive tactical thinking that formal military
training often suppressed. Together, they formed an organism
whose capabilities exceeded the sum of its components, and
organisms of this type were, in Yzrat's experience, the most
dangerous adversaries a reconnaissance operation could
encounter, because their behaviour was emergent rather than
prescribed, which meant it could not be anticipated from the
analysis of individual elements.

He had observed their departure for Ta'Puh.

The dimensional disturbance had registered on his goggles as
a sharp, localised disruption in the mana field above the
coastal city: a point of intense compression followed by an
instantaneous absence, the signature of a planar transit
executed with a precision that was impressive by any standard
and alarming by the standard of a world that should not have
possessed the capability. The anomaly had carried them. The
tuning fork that the mages' council in the inland city had
calibrated, the fork whose creation Yzrat had observed through
long-range mana-spectrum analysis, had provided the targeting.
The transit itself was the anomaly's gift, the supernatural
ability that no instrument could fully classify and no model
could fully predict.

He had observed their return.

The return disturbance had been larger. Significantly larger.
The compression-absence signature of the transit had been
accompanied by an additional resonance that Yzrat's goggles
rendered as a massive, deep-spectrum displacement: something
enormous had crossed with them. The signature was draconic.
Not the compressed young-green resonance of the disguised
companion. Something vastly older, vastly more powerful, a
mana profile that registered on the goggles as a sustained
harmonic so deep that the instrument's display flickered at
the edges, struggling to render the full depth of the
reading.

They had brought back a dragon. An ancient dragon. A dragon
that had been hiding in the mountains of Ta'Puh for eight
centuries, whose mana signature Yzrat had never detected
because the being had been motionless and shielded by
kilometres of stone, and whose presence in this world
represented a strategic development that elevated the entire
reconnaissance from routine to urgent.

He touched the mana communicator at his wrist. The dark metal
and crystal device pulsed with cold blue light, the
connection to the Orceri command frequency stable despite the
altitude and the dimensional boundary that separated him from
the relay network.

"Report compilation complete," he said. His voice was quiet,
precise, the words shaped for the communicator's transcription
protocols rather than for any listener's comfort. "Priority:
high. Classification: strategic. Recipient: General Rangithyr,
personal attention."

The communicator acknowledged. The message would travel through
the relay network, cross the dimensional boundary through one
of Ozmakir's stabilised conduits, and arrive at the
communication crystal in the villa on Shalazar within hours.
Yzrat had delayed the full report until he was certain the
intelligence package was comprehensive. A skymage did not
bring partial data to the Godslayer. Partial data invited
questions. Questions invited the particular quality of
attention that Rangithyr directed at subordinates whose work
was incomplete, and that attention, while never intemperate,
carried a weight that lingered.

"BOO!"

The voice came from immediately behind his left ear, which
was impossible at forty thousand feet, which was the point.

Yzrat did not flinch. The mount did not flinch. Neither of
them had flinched at Unthil's arrivals since the third month
of their operational partnership, which had been thirty-seven
years ago, and the absence of flinching had become, over
those decades, a form of communication in itself: Yzrat's
refusal to react was his acknowledgement, and Unthil's
continued attempts were his greeting, and between the two a
relationship functioned that was, beneath the surface
irritation, one of considerable trust.

"Must you," Yzrat said.

"Must I?" Unthil's voice carried the playful energy that was
his resting state and his operational disguise and possibly,
beneath both, his actual personality, the three layers so
thoroughly integrated that even Yzrat, who had worked with
the assassin for nearly four decades, could not reliably
distinguish between them. "I must. It is tradition. Tradition
must be honoured. The alternative is chaos, and chaos is for
the primitives."

He materialised from the shadow of the mount's wing: slender,
powerful, compact, the black leather armour absorbing the
starlight, the mana implants at his neck and forearm pulsing
with the cold blue light that was the Orceri's universal
signature, the twin handaxes at his hips catching the
moonlight with the dull gleam of blades that had been used
recently and cleaned thoroughly and would be used again when
the opportunity presented itself, which Unthil would ensure
it did.

He settled onto the mount's secondary saddle with the ease
of someone for whom forty thousand feet of altitude was merely
a different floor of the same building. "I have things to
report."

"You always have things to report. The question is whether
the things are worth the altitude."

"Commander." Unthil placed a hand over his chest in mock
offence, the gesture theatrical and precise and delivered
with the timing of someone who had been performing for an
audience of one for thirty-seven years and had refined the
performance to the point where the refinement was itself
part of the joke. "When have my things ever been unworthy
of the altitude?"

"The incident with the fishing boat."

"That was intelligence."

"That was a man catching eels."

"He was catching eels suspiciously." Unthil grinned, the
tusks smaller and sharper than a standard Orceri's, the
grin carrying the particular quality of a being who found
the world endlessly entertaining and intended to remain
entertained regardless of the world's cooperation. "But
fine. Today's things are worthy. I have been on the ground.
I have been in their city. I have been, Commander, inside
their drinking establishments, and I have learned things
that your goggles cannot see from this admittedly impressive
but socially limited altitude."

Yzrat waited. The waiting was permission.

Unthil's manner shifted. The playful energy remained, because
the playful energy always remained, but beneath it the
operational clarity surfaced, the competence that Rangithyr
valued and that Yzrat relied upon and that Unthil concealed
beneath his performance the way a blade was concealed beneath
a coat: present, functional, and more dangerous for being
underestimated.

"Their political structure is fragmented," he said. "Dozens
of independent city-states. No unified command. No central
military authority. No equivalent of a General Staff or an
Aerial Command or even a coordinated border-watch system.
Each city governs itself, defends itself, and regards its
neighbours with a suspicion that ranges from mild distrust
to active hostility." He held up a finger. "The coastal
city, Amara. Ruled by a merchant-lord. Competent. Pragmatic.
Maintains a small professional guard and an intelligence
network that is, by local standards, impressive and by our
standards, quaint. He would be the first to respond to a
credible threat, because responding to threats is how his
family has maintained power for three generations."

A second finger. "The inland mage-city. Instar. Ruled by a
council of spellcasters. The most significant magical
concentration on the peninsula. They have the anomaly's
tuning fork. They have the physical evidence from the scout
we lost." He meant Kuulatch. The scout's death at the hands
of the primitive orcs in the keep had been an operational
embarrassment that the subsequent destruction of the keep
had only partially remedied. "Their analytical capability
is adequate. They confirmed the extradimensional origin of
Kuulatch's equipment within days. If anyone in this world
can develop countermeasures to our technology, it is this
council."

A third finger. "The monastery. South of Amara. A martial
religious order dedicated to one of their surviving entities.
Small. Committed. The kind of force that is militarily
insignificant and strategically inconvenient, because
committed forces fight beyond their capability and die in
positions that complicate an advance." He lowered his hand.
"There are others. A druid circle in the hills. A network
of operatives who help enslaved humans escape from a city
to the south. Minor assets. Nothing that alters the
strategic picture."

"And the anomalies?" Yzrat asked. The word was plural
because the five figures, taken together, constituted an
anomaly in the operational sense: a cluster of capability
that deviated from the baseline of the world they inhabited.

"Active. Coordinated. Increasingly connected to the local
power structures. The priest has credibility with the
mage-council. The bard has contacts in the merchant-lord's
intelligence network. The gnome has scholarly credentials
that the mage-council respects despite what appears to be
a personal dispute with one of its members." Unthil paused.
The pause was brief and carried the particular weight of
information that the speaker considered important. "And
they have the ancient dragon. Somewhere in the mountains
east of the city. I could not locate it precisely. The
signature is..." He searched for the word. "Enormous. And
hidden. Someone is concealing it, and concealing it well."

"The disguised dragon," Yzrat said. "The young green."

"Almost certainly. She has knowledge of the local terrain
that exceeds what a being of her apparent age should
possess. She has connections she has not revealed to her
companions. She is, Commander, considerably more than she
appears, and what she appears to be is already more than
the average native of this world can claim."

Yzrat filed this. The young green was a secondary concern
elevated by association: her individual capability was
manageable, but her role as concealer of an ancient dragon
and coordinator of a group whose collective capability
exceeded its individual components made her a node in a
network, and nodes were targeted not for their own value
but for the connections they sustained.

He looked down at the world below. The coastal city was a
cluster of warm light against the dark coastline, the mana
signatures of its inhabitants a diffuse golden glow that
his goggles rendered as a field of fireflies, thousands of
individual sparks of varying brightness representing the
magical output of a population that went about its business
unaware that it was being observed from an altitude that
its most powerful mages could not reach and through
instruments that its most advanced scholars could not
conceive.

"Strategic assessment," Yzrat said. The words were formal,
the register shifting from operational discussion to the
structured format that Orceri intelligence protocols
required for reports destined for command authority.

Unthil straightened. The playful energy did not disappear
but it rearranged itself, the performer yielding to the
professional, the shift so practised that it occurred
without visible transition. "Proceed, Commander."

"Fragmented command is both weakness and strength." Yzrat
spoke the assessment as he had composed it, the sentences
shaped by weeks of observation and refined by the
analytical framework that the Aerial Command had drilled
into him alongside flight mechanics and combat casting
and the particular art of remaining invisible at altitudes
where invisibility was a matter of survival rather than
convenience. "There is no single throat to cut. No capital
whose fall would collapse resistance. No unified military
whose destruction would leave the population defenceless.
Each city-state maintains its own defences, its own
intelligence apparatus, its own leadership. Decapitation
strikes, which have been effective in nine of eleven
previous tangent operations, will not produce the cascade
failure that a centralised command structure permits."

He adjusted the goggles. The display shifted, the mana
topology of the coastal region rendering in layered detail:
the bright cluster of Amara, the dimmer constellations of
smaller settlements, the steady glow of the inland
mage-city visible at the edge of the display's range, the
scattered individual signatures of travellers and farmers
and the ambient noise of a world that was alive with the
low-level magical activity that characterised all tangents
in the pre-conquest phase.

"However," he continued, "the fragmentation also prevents
coordinated defence. No unified command means no coordinated
response. No coordinated response means each city-state
faces the invasion individually, with whatever forces it
can muster from its own resources. The anomalies are the
variable. They have demonstrated the ability to move between
the fragments, to connect the disparate authorities, to
function as the connective tissue that the political
structure lacks. If they succeed in building an alliance,
the fragmented command becomes a distributed network, and
distributed networks are harder to destroy than centralised
ones."

"Recommendation?" Unthil asked.

"Accelerate the timeline. The anomalies are building
connections. Every day we delay, the connections strengthen.
The mage-council has evidence. The merchant-lord has
intelligence. The monastery has commitment. The ancient
dragon has eight centuries of knowledge about our operations.
If these elements coordinate, the invasion's opening phase
becomes significantly more costly."

He touched the communicator again. "Appending tactical
recommendation to the strategic report. Full intelligence
package transmitting to General Rangithyr's personal
frequency. Acknowledge."

The communicator pulsed. Acknowledged.

Yzrat looked at the sky. Two moons. The empty space between
them.

"We should withdraw to observation distance," he said. "The
report is sent. The General will respond in his own time."

"And if his response is what I think it will be?" Unthil
asked. The grin had returned, but it was sharper now, the
playful edge honed by the anticipation of something that
the assassin's nature craved and the decades of routine
reconnaissance had denied him.

"Then we prepare for the next phase," Yzrat said. "And the
next phase, Unthil, will not be observation."

The mount banked. The wind at forty thousand feet carried no
sound, and the silence was restful, and the world below
turned in its ignorance, and the report travelled through
conduits that spanned the space between realities, carrying
the comprehensive assessment of a world that did not know
it had been assessed.

---

The report arrived at the villa on Shalazar at dawn.

The communication crystal sat on the broad stone table in
the map room, a faceted sphere of deep blue that caught the
first light through the seaward window and held it in its
interior the way a deep pool held sunlight: absorbed,
transformed, returned as something colder and more precise.
The crystal pulsed once, the activation signal, and the
pulse was the only announcement it made, because Orceri
communication technology did not announce itself with
fanfare or urgency. It simply functioned. The message was
present. The recipient would attend to it when the
recipient chose to attend.

Rangithyr chose to attend immediately.

He had been on the veranda. The scimitar ritual was
complete, the blade returned to its rack in the armoury,
the morning's meditation finished, the memories catalogued
and filed and the present moment reasserted over the past
with the discipline of a being who had been managing the
relationship between memory and purpose for eight centuries.
He had been watching the ocean. The ocean was the same as
it had been yesterday and the day before and the century
before that: grey-blue, restless, indifferent, the waves
breaking on the pale sand of the crescent beach with the
rhythm that was the only music the villa permitted, because
Rangithyr did not listen to music. Music was a human
invention. He found it unnecessary.

The crystal's pulse reached him through the open doorway
of the map room, a subtle shift in the ambient mana of
the villa that he registered the way a predator registered
movement at the edge of its territory: instantly,
unconsciously, the awareness translating into motion before
the conscious mind had classified the stimulus.

He entered the map room. Bare feet on polished basalt. The
loose trousers and open robe of his domestic hours, the
deep blue skin exposed at chest and forearms, the small
horns catching the dawn light as he crossed to the table
and placed his hand on the crystal and activated the
playback with a pulse of mana that was less than a thought
and more than a gesture.

Yzrat's voice filled the room.

The report was comprehensive. It was, Rangithyr noted with
the particular approval he reserved for subordinates whose
work met his standards without exceeding the boundaries of
their authority, exactly what he had requested: a complete
intelligence package, assembled with patience, delivered
with precision, containing no speculation that was not
clearly identified as such and no conclusion that was not
supported by the evidence that preceded it.

The world's political geography rendered itself in
Rangithyr's mind as Yzrat described it: the fragmented
city-states, the absence of central authority, the
competing interests and mutual suspicions that prevented
the kind of coordinated response that had, in one previous
tangent, delayed the conquest by six months. The military
dispositions: primitive by Orceri standards, local militias
and small professional guards and a martial religious order
whose commitment was noted and whose numbers were not. The
magical capabilities: moderate, concentrated in the inland
mage-city, uncoordinated between population centres. The
key personalities: the merchant-lord, pragmatic; the
mage-council, analytical; the religious order, committed;
and the anomalies.

The anomalies.

Rangithyr listened to Yzrat's assessment of the five
figures with the attention he gave to all intelligence
regarding adversaries, which was the same attention he
gave to the positioning of forces before a battle: total,
undivided, the full weight of eight centuries of strategic
experience focused on the question of what the information
meant and what it required.

Three mid-tier operatives whose collective function
exceeded their individual capability. A disguised young
dragon functioning as concealer and coordinator. And the
anomaly. The planar being whose dimensional signature
defied classification and whose demonstrated ability to
transport groups across the boundary between realities
had, in a single act, introduced an ancient dragon into
the strategic equation.

The intelligence package concluded with Yzrat's tactical
recommendation. Accelerate. The anomalies were connecting
the fragments. Every day strengthened the connections.

Rangithyr deactivated the crystal.

The room was quiet. The dawn light had moved across the
floor while the report played, the angular shadow of the
window frame advancing toward the table with the steady
progression of something that had been advancing for as
long as the window had existed and would continue advancing
for as long as the light endured. Rangithyr stood at the
table and the shadow touched his bare feet and he did not
notice, because his attention was on the bare stone surface
of the table and on the campaign that was taking shape on
it, the campaign that existed for now only in the space
between intelligence and decision, the space where strategy
lived before it became orders and orders became action and
action became history.

He took a stylus from the table's single drawer. He took
a blank slate sheet from the stack beside the drawer. The
slate was smooth, dark, the surface prepared for the
precise notation that Orceri military planning required:
grid coordinates, force dispositions, temporal markers, the
graphic language of conquest that Rangithyr had been writing
and reading for eight hundred years and that he wrote now
with the fluid economy of a master calligrapher producing
a text he had composed in his mind before the stylus
touched the surface.

Phase one. Probe. Assess response time, force composition,
magical capability. Standard doctrine. A company-strength
force deployed through a temporary breach at a point of
minimal strategic value. Clean data. Minimal risk. The
natives' response, or lack of response, would determine
the parameters for phase two.

Phase two. Portal construction. Ozmakir had identified a
location in the target reality where the dimensional
boundary had been damaged by an event that predated Orceri
contact: a collapsed dwarven citadel in the eastern
mountains, where the fabric of reality had been scarred
by energies whose origin Ozmakir's instruments could not
determine but whose effect was unmistakable. The scar
tissue was ideal for forced breach. The boundary at that
point was not merely thinner than normal. It was
compromised, the dimensional integrity weakened in a way
that reduced the energy requirements for a sustained
portal by an order of magnitude. Ozmakir's engineers could
construct a one-way portal at that location capable of
sustaining a full invasion force.

Phase three. Assault. Overwhelming force through the portal
before the natives could organise. The fragmented command
structure meant that each city-state would respond
independently. The anomalies might connect some of them,
but connection was not coordination, and coordination was
not unified command, and without unified command the response
would be sequential rather than simultaneous, and sequential
responses were defeated in detail. Standard Orceri doctrine.
Applied to eleven previous tangents. Effective in all eleven.

He set the stylus down. He placed his hands flat on the
table, the enormous scarred fingers spread across the slate,
and the hands were still, and the stillness was not rest
but decision, the particular quality of motionlessness that
preceded action in a being whose actions were never
impulsive and never uncertain and never, in eight hundred
years of service, wrong.

The probe would tell him what he needed to know. Whether
this world would fall in weeks, as most tangents did, or
whether the anomalies and their ancient dragon and their
fragmented but adapting network of alliances would produce
something that required a more considered approach. The
answer did not concern him. Both outcomes led to conquest.
One simply offered more resistance along the way.

He stood at the table as the dawn light advanced across
the floor and reached the far wall and began to climb it,
the morning establishing itself in the room with the
patient certainty of a process that had been repeating for
longer than the room had existed. The ocean was visible
through the window. The waves broke on the beach. The pale
sand held the foam for a moment before the water withdrew,
and the withdrawal left patterns in the sand that were
unique and transient and would be erased by the next wave,
and the next wave was already forming, and the pattern was
eternal even though each individual expression of it was
not.

Rangithyr looked at the campaign plan on the slate. Three
phases. Clean. Economical. The work of a mind that had
conducted eleven invasions and learned from each one and
refined the process until the process was not a plan but a
principle, the principle that conquest was not an event but
a sequence, and the sequence was not improvised but
designed, and the design was not inspired but tested, tested
across eleven worlds and four centuries and the lives of
billions of beings who had not understood what was happening
to them until the understanding was academic.

He left the map room. He walked to the armoury. The mana
scimitar waited on its rack, the cold blue light pulsing
in the steel like a heartbeat, steady and patient and ready.

He did not touch it. Not yet. The blade would know when it
was time. The blade always knew.

He stood before it and the light of the scimitar met the
light of the dawn and the two lights were different, the
dawn warm and golden and the blade cold and blue, and
between them the figure of the Godslayer stood in his bare
feet and his open robe, his amber eyes on the weapon that
had blocked a god's killing blow eight centuries ago, and
the weapon glowed, and the morning was quiet, and the quiet
was no longer empty.

Something was beginning.
