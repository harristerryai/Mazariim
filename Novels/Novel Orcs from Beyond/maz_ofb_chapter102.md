# Chapter Two
## The Battle That Made Two Worlds

Three moons hung above Fort Hope on the night the world was decided.

Two of them were the same moons that had circled this world since
before the first elf opened her eyes beneath the canopy of
Vand'ardhon, before the first dwarf struck stone in the mountains of
the north, before the first human looked up from the savannah and
wondered what the lights in the sky meant. Io'el, the silver
constant, and Randir'el, the pale gold wanderer, ancient beyond
memory, moving through their eternal dance above a world that had
never known any other sky. On this night they were full, both of
them, a conjunction that occurred once in a generation, and the elves
called such nights Arathalion, the Watching, because the old songs
said that when both moons opened their eyes together, the fate of
the world hung in the balance. Between them, smaller and ruddier,
Zhur'el held its place as it always had, casting a faint rust-colored
light that tinged the edges of the silver and gold with something
that might have been warmth, or might have been warning.

The old songs, it would seem, were right.

Fort Hope occupied a low ridge above the confluence of two rivers,
its walls of quarried granite rising forty feet above a killing ground
that the alliance's engineers had spent three years preparing.
Trenches radiated outward from the walls in concentric arcs, their
floors studded with sharpened stakes. Earthen ramparts channeled any
approach into narrow corridors where archers and spellcasters could
concentrate fire. Beyond the ramparts, the plains stretched flat and
featureless to the horizon, and on this night those plains were not
empty. They had not been empty for three days.

Zuel's army covered the earth like a stain.

From the walls of Fort Hope, the torches of the orcish host stretched
to the limits of vision in every direction, a carpet of guttering
orange light that blurred the line between ground and sky. The sound
of them was a constant, physical pressure: the rhythmic stamp of
hundreds of thousands of feet, the guttural chanting of war-priests
consecrating the ground in Zuel's name, the deep brass moan of
war-horns that had been sounding since dusk. The smell reached the
walls hours before the army itself, carried on a wind that tasted of
smoke and iron and something fouler beneath, the musk of a million
bodies pressed together with the single-minded purpose of
annihilation. Scouts had attempted to count the host. They had given
up. The numbers exceeded the capacity of military language to express.
The words that found their way into the reports of the alliance
commanders were not numbers but impressions: "endless," "without
limit," "the plains themselves are moving."

Against this, the alliance held Fort Hope with everything it had left.

Three years of war had reduced the forces of the free peoples to a
fraction of their original strength. The dwarven shield-walls that
had once numbered in the tens of thousands were thinned to desperate
companies, their ranks filled with apprentice smiths and
silver-bearded elders who had taken up arms for the first time in
living memory. Human cavalry from a dozen nations formed ragged
squadrons along the flanks, their horses gaunt and their lances
notched from a hundred engagements. Gnomish engineers manned siege
weapons of clockwork and steam along the walls, their faces grim
behind welding goggles, their ammunition running low. Halfling
auxiliaries, who had never been warriors and had become warriors
because the alternative was extinction, held the supply lines and
field hospitals with a quiet ferocity that shamed soldiers twice
their size.

And the elves. What remained of the elves.

Of the thirty thousand Ya'sehrim warriors who had crossed the sea
from Vand'ardhon three years ago, fewer than eighteen thousand still
drew breath. The elven navy that had carried them was a memory,
seventy-five percent of its ships dragged beneath the waves by the
daemon-things that Zuel had called from the deep places between
planes. The survivors held the eastern wall of Fort Hope, their
silver armor catching the moonlight, their faces composed with the
stillness of a people who understood, with the patience of immortal
lives, that this was the night that determined whether those lives
would continue.

Among them stood the Artharans.

Not all seven. Three had fallen in the campaigns of the previous
year, their borrowed fragments of Minerva's essence returning to the
goddess like sparks rising from a dying fire. Four remained, and they
burned. There was no other word for it. They stood at intervals along
the eastern wall, and the air around each of them shimmered with
contained radiance, a cool white light that was not fire and not
starlight but something older and more deliberate, the visible
manifestation of a goddess who had divided herself among her chosen
so that her people might survive the night. The Artharans did not
speak. They did not need to. The psychic command network that
Minerva's sacrifice had created linked them to every elven warrior on
the field, a web of shared awareness that turned eighteen thousand
individual soldiers into a single, coordinated instrument. When an
Artharan turned her gaze toward a section of the field, the elves
there shifted formation before the order was given. When an Artharan
raised his blade, a thousand bows drew in unison.

It was not enough. Everyone knew it was not enough. But it was what
they had.

The war-horns changed pitch. Lower. Longer. A sound like the earth
groaning.

On the walls of Fort Hope, ten thousand defenders drew breath and
held it.

The orcish host moved.

---

It began as all battles begin, with a sound that was not yet violence
but contained the promise of violence in every note. The rhythmic
stamp of the orcish advance shook the ground, a vibration that
climbed through the stone of the walls and into the bones of the
defenders, so that every soldier felt the enemy's approach as a
tremor in his own skeleton before his eyes confirmed what his body
already knew. The chanting rose. The war-horns blared. And then the
first wave struck the outer trenches, and the night tore open.

Arrows fell like rain. Elven longbows sang from the eastern wall,
each shaft aimed with the preternatural precision of warriors linked
by a goddess's will. Dwarven crossbow bolts punched through orcish
shields in disciplined volleys, the mechanical thunk of their release
as regular as a heartbeat. Gnomish siege engines hurled canisters of
alchemist's fire that burst among the advancing ranks in blooms of
orange and white, scattering burning figures across the killing
ground. The outer trenches became a charnel corridor. Orcs fell by
the hundred, by the thousand, their bodies piling in the narrow
channels that the alliance engineers had designed for exactly this
purpose.

It did not matter. The host did not slow. The host could not slow.
There were too many. The dead became bridges for the living, and the
living poured over them in an unending tide, climbing ramparts,
filling trenches, absorbing losses that would have shattered any
mortal army because this was not a mortal army in any meaningful
sense. This was a god's instrument, a million lives wielded as a
single weapon by the will of an entity who had spent decades forging
them for this moment.

The first orcs reached the walls. Scaling ladders rose. Grappling
hooks bit stone. And above it all, above the screaming and the dying
and the desperate courage of defenders who knew they were outnumbered
beyond hope, the sky changed.

Three shapes descended from the darkness, and the battle became
something else entirely.

---

The avatars of entities were not creatures in the way that mortals
understood the word. They were presences given form, fragments of
consciousness so vast that the bodies containing them strained at the
boundaries of physical law. They were fifteen feet tall, each of
them, and the air around their forms bent and shimmered with the
pressure of power barely contained within flesh.

Mordrion came first.

The Dwarffather's avatar struck the earth before the western wall and
the ground cracked beneath his feet. He was carved from the living
idea of what a dwarf should be: broad as a fortress gate, armored in
plates of mithril and adamantine that had been forged in the heart of
his own plane, his beard a cascade of iron-grey that fell to his
waist and seemed to move with a will of its own. His warhammer,
Grund, was a slab of star-metal the size of a man's torso, its head
inscribed with runes that blazed white-hot with every swing. Where he
walked, the stone rose to meet him. Where he struck, the enemy ceased
to exist, not died but was unmade, scattered into component matter by
the force of a blow that carried the weight of a god's will behind
it.

The orcish ranks parted around him like water around a boulder. Not
in retreat. In recognition. They knew what he was. They had been
told. And they knew that their own god was coming.

Quarzel arrived in fire.

The God of War's avatar descended on wings of burning bronze, his
body a sculpture of martial perfection wrapped in armor that wept
liquid flame. His sword was a blade of condensed fury, twelve feet of
white-hot metal that screamed when it moved through air. Where
Mordrion was stone and endurance, Quarzel was speed and violence, his
fighting style a continuous explosion of movement that scattered
orcish bodies in arcs of fire and shattered steel. He landed on the
northern rampart and cut a swath through the scaling troops, his
wings spreading and folding with each strike, every motion designed
not just to kill but to terrify. An aura of dread radiated from him
in palpable waves, and orcs who came within thirty paces of his
position dropped their weapons and fled, trampling their own ranks,
because the God of War did not merely fight. He imposed his nature on
the battlefield, and his nature was fear.

For a moment, hope lived.

Two avatars, fighting in concert, their combined power sweeping the
walls clean of attackers. Mordrion anchored the western approach.
Quarzel burned the northern scaling parties to ash. The defenders
roared, because for the first time in three years the tide was
turning, and the elves on the eastern wall raised their blades in
salute, and the dwarves beat their axes against their shields in a
thunder of defiance.

Then the earth split open, and Zuel rose from the wound.

The Devourer's avatar was not beautiful. Where Mordrion embodied
craft and Quarzel embodied war, Zuel embodied hunger. He was taller
than the other two, a towering figure of grey-green flesh and black
iron, his body thick with muscle that moved beneath his skin like
restless serpents. His armor was spiked, each spike a weapon, each
plate designed not for protection but for the infliction of damage on
anything that came near. His face was a mask of brutal intelligence:
heavy-browed, tusked, his eyes burning with a sulphurous yellow light
that cast shadows in impossible directions. In his hands he carried
no weapon. His hands were the weapons, massive, clawed, each finger
capable of crushing stone.

He radiated nothing so simple as fear. What Zuel projected was
inevitability. The sensation that spread across the battlefield when
his avatar rose from the broken earth was not terror but despair, the
cold mathematical certainty that resistance was futile, that every
blow struck against this enemy was a gesture made in defiance of a
conclusion already determined. The defenders closest to his point of
emergence simply stopped fighting. Not from cowardice. From the
sudden, bone-deep understanding that they were in the presence of
something that regarded their entire civilization as an impediment.

Quarzel struck first.

The God of War screamed his challenge, a sound that cracked stone and
extinguished fires across a hundred yards of battlefield, and
descended on Zuel's avatar with the full fury of his burning blade.
The impact shook the fort. White-hot steel met black iron in a
detonation of light and sound that blinded every mortal eye within a
quarter mile. The two avatars grappled, Quarzel's speed against
Zuel's mass, fire against hunger, and for a span of seconds that
felt like hours the issue was in doubt.

Zuel caught the burning blade in one clawed hand. The metal shrieked.
The flames died. And from Zuel's other hand, a column of blue energy,
burning, annihilating, unlike any fire the mortal world had ever
produced, poured into Quarzel's chest and through him and beyond him.
The God of War convulsed. His avatar arched backward, his mouth open
in a soundless scream, his wings of burning bronze crumbling to ash.
The blue energy consumed him from within, eating his form the way
acid eats metal, and where the energy passed, nothing remained. Not a
body. Not residual divine force. Nothing.

Quarzel, the entity of war, was permanently destroyed.

The psychic shockwave of a god's death rolled across the battlefield.
Every sentient being within miles felt it: a sudden, vertiginous
absence, as though a color had been removed from the spectrum or a
note from the musical scale. Soldiers on both sides staggered. The
Artharans on the eastern wall flinched in unison, their shared
consciousness rippling with the magnitude of what had just been lost.

Mordrion did not flinch. He did not stagger. He charged.

The Dwarffather's avatar crossed the distance between the western
wall and Zuel's position with a speed that belied his massive frame,
Grund blazing in his hands, his rune-carved armor singing with power.
He struck Zuel's avatar with a blow that would have felled a
mountain, and the impact cratered the earth beneath both entities to
a depth of twenty feet. Zuel staggered. Mordrion struck again, and
again, driving the Devourer backward with the relentless fury of a
god who had just watched a fellow deity die and who understood, with
the clarity of immortal intelligence, that this was the last stand of
everything he had built and protected since his arrival in this
universe.

Zuel's knee came up. The spike that protruded from his greave, a spur
of black iron as long as a man's arm, drove through Mordrion's
breastplate and into his chest. The Dwarffather's avatar seized. Zuel
twisted. Divine energies erupted from the wound like arterial spray,
streaking upward into the sky in ribbons of white and gold that faded
as they rose, climbing toward the planes from which they had been
sent, returning to the entity who would survive this death diminished
but intact.

Mordrion's avatar fell. His warhammer, Grund, rang against the
cratered stone with a sound like a cathedral bell tolling its last
note. His body did not dissolve as Quarzel's had. It lay still, a
monument of mithril and adamantine, its runes fading one by one, and
the dwarven defenders saw it fall, and the sound they made was not a
scream but a moan, low and vast and terrible, the sound of an entire
people watching their father die.

Zuel pulled the spike from the corpse. He straightened. He looked
across the battlefield at the walls of Fort Hope with his sulphurous
eyes, and his tusked mouth split in a smile that was worse than any
weapon he carried.

On the walls, in the trenches, among the shattered ramparts, the
alliance broke.

Not all at once. Not everywhere. But the fractures spread. Human
cavalry turned their horses. Gnomish engineers abandoned their
engines. The halfling auxiliaries, who had held their positions
through everything, began to fall back toward the interior of the
fort. Only the elves held firm, the Artharans' psychic network
binding them to their positions with a discipline that transcended
the despair washing over the field, and even among the elves, the
link was fraying, individual warriors breaking formation as the scale
of the loss overwhelmed the shared will.

And from the dwarven lines, a single figure moved against the tide.

He was not tall for a dwarf. He was broad, as all dwarves are broad,
but there was something about the way he carried his weight that
spoke of a density beyond mere muscle, as though the stone of the
mountains he called home had settled into his bones and made him
heavier than flesh should be. His armor was dented, scorched, streaked
with blood that was mostly not his own. His beard was red, cut short
in the fashion of Gormdawi front-line warriors, and his face beneath
the iron helm was a mask of something that was not courage and not
madness but existed in the space between, where a man has decided
what he is going to do and has stopped caring whether he survives it.

In his hands he carried a battleaxe.

It was an absurd weapon. Oversized even by dwarven standards,
double-bladed, its twin crescents of blessed steel as wide as his
shoulders, its haft wrapped in leather that had been replaced and
rewrapped so many times the original wood was invisible. It should
have been too heavy for a being of his stature. It was not. He
carried it the way the sea carries a wave: naturally, inevitably, as
though the axe and the dwarf had been made for each other and neither
was complete alone.

Demious Stoneshaper walked toward Zuel.

The defenders saw him. The ones who were still fighting, the ones who
had not yet broken, turned their heads and watched a single dwarf
walking across a battlefield strewn with the corpses of two gods, his
oversized axe resting on his shoulder, his short legs eating the
distance with the steady, unhurried pace of a miner walking into a
shaft he has walked into every morning of his working life. He did
not run. He did not shout. He did not look back at the walls where
his people were watching, because looking back was something you did
when you wanted to remember, and Demious Stoneshaper was not
interested in remembering. He was interested in what came next.

The orcs between him and Zuel moved aside. Not because he frightened
them. Because the god they served was watching, and Zuel's
sulphurous gaze was fixed on the approaching dwarf with an
expression that might, in a being capable of such things, have been
curiosity.

Stoneshaper reached the crater where Mordrion's avatar lay. He did
not look down at the body of his god. He stepped over it. His boots
crunched on the fractured stone. Zuel's avatar loomed above him,
fifteen feet of grey-green flesh and black iron, the blue energy
still crackling along its claws, the blood of two gods staining its
armor.

Demious Stoneshaper raised his axe.

He jumped.

It was a leap that should not have been possible for a body built
like a boulder. He launched himself from the lip of the crater with
every ounce of strength his mountain-bred frame possessed, his legs
driving him upward, the battleaxe swinging in an arc over his head,
both blades catching the moonlight, the blessed steel blazing white
as Mordrion's last gift activated in the presence of the entity that
had murdered its maker. Stoneshaper's mouth was open. He was
screaming, or praying, or both. The sound was dwarven and ancient
and raw.

The axe reached the apex of its arc. Zuel's avatar turned toward the
ascending dwarf, one massive hand rising to swat this insect from
the air.

Between one heartbeat and the next, something changed.

The air behind Stoneshaper tore. Not visibly, not with light or
sound, but with a displacement of presence, a sudden wrongness in the
fabric of the space between the dwarf and the god, as though a door
had been opened in a wall that should not have had a door. The
temperature dropped. The ozone scent of mana surged, sharp and
metallic, layered beneath something colder and older, the taste of a
dimension where physical law was a suggestion rather than a rule.

A shape materialized from the Ethereal Plane.

He was enormous. Over eight feet tall, blue-skinned, his body dense
with the heavy musculature of an ogre-magi in the prime of his
power. Small horns rose from his forehead above heavy brows, curving
slightly backward. His face was younger than it would one day
become: the lines of command and centuries of war had not yet carved
themselves into his features, and the dark amber eyes that would
later hold the patient calm of a being who had outlived civilizations
still burned, in this moment, with something closer to hunger. He
wore armor of dark steel, well-made but plain, the functional plate
of a general who had not yet conquered enough to warrant
ornamentation. In his right hand he carried a heavy scimitar, its
edge honed to a brightness that caught the moonlight and held it.

Rangithyr was not yet what he would become. He was not yet the
Godslayer. He had not yet hunted entities across the planes of
existence or commanded the conquest of eleven worlds. He was young,
by the reckoning of his kind, barely a century old, and the patience
that would one day define him existed only as a seed, a potential
glimpsed by the god who had elevated him above the other twelve
generals for reasons that the young ogre-magi did not fully
understand. What he understood was simpler: he was fast, he was
strong, he was smarter than anything he had yet fought, and the god
he served had given him a task.

He had been waiting in the Ethereal Plane for six hours. He had
watched the battle unfold from a dimension adjacent to the physical
world, his form ghostly and transparent, occupying the same space as
the battlefield but removed from it by the thin membrane that
separated the material from the ethereal. He had watched Quarzel die.
He had watched Mordrion fall. He had waited, because Zuel had told
him to wait, and even at a century old, with the hot blood of an
ogre-magi urging him toward the fight with every passing minute,
Rangithyr possessed the one quality that separated him from every
other general in the horde: he could be still when stillness was
required.

And so, when the dwarf jumped, Rangithyr stepped from the Ethereal
Plane and into the path of the descending axe.

The scimitar met the blessed battleaxe with an impact that produced
no sound. For a fraction of a second, the forces involved were too
vast for the air to transmit: divine blessing against honed steel,
the last gift of a murdered god against the strength of an ogre-magi
who had put everything he was into a single act of interception.
Then the sound arrived, a crack that split the sky, and the shockwave
blew outward in a ring of compressed air that flattened every
standing figure within a hundred yards.

The battleaxe stopped.

Stoneshaper hung in the air, his full weight and the momentum of his
leap driving the blessed blades downward, and Rangithyr held him
there with one arm, the scimitar absorbing the blow, the muscles of
his shoulder and back rigid with effort, his teeth bared, his amber
eyes blazing with a fury that was also, beneath the fury, something
like joy, because this was the moment he had been made for and he
knew it in the marrow of his bones. Their eyes met. Amber into
iron-grey. The dwarf saw what had happened. The ogre-magi saw the
dwarf understand.

Rangithyr turned the blade.

The battleaxe slid sideways, deflected, its killing arc redirected
from Zuel's avatar into empty air. Stoneshaper fell. He hit the
cratered stone hard, rolling, his axe skittering from his grip, and
before he could rise, before he could reach for the weapon, before he
could do anything but look up at the towering form of Zuel's avatar
standing over him with its sulphurous eyes and its tusked smile, the
god moved.

Zuel's clawed hand descended. The dwarf's head left his shoulders
with a sound that was, in comparison to everything that had come
before, very small.

The body toppled. The battleaxe lay three feet from the outstretched
hand that would never reach it again. Mordrion's last gift faded from
the blessed steel, the white glow dimming to grey, to nothing, and
the weapon became what it had always been beneath the enchantment:
cold metal, inert, purposeless.

Zuel's avatar raised its head. The sulphurous eyes swept the
battlefield, the walls, the sky. The tusked mouth opened, and from
it came a sound that was not a roar and not a word but something more
fundamental, a declaration of supremacy that vibrated in the bones of
every living creature within miles. It was the sound of a god
proclaiming victory, and it was the sound of a world ending, because
every defender on those walls and in those trenches heard it and
understood, with the finality of a door closing, that the blow which
should have ended this war had been blocked by a single blue-skinned
warrior who had appeared from nowhere and changed everything.

The alliance shattered.

The elves held longest. The Artharans' psychic network sustained them
for another hour, their silver armor growing dim with blood and dust
as they fought a retreating action toward the eastern gate. But even
the shared will of a goddess could not hold against the mathematical
certainty of what had just occurred. One by one, the links in the
network frayed and broke, as individual warriors fell or fled or
simply stopped, their immortal minds overwhelmed by a grief that no
tactical discipline could contain. The last Artharan to fall was a
woman with white hair and armor that still blazed with Minerva's
light, and she died on the eastern wall with her sword in one hand
and her other hand pressed to the stone, as though she were trying to
hold the fort together by force of will alone.

Fort Hope fell before dawn.

---


The three moons watched from above, as they always had. Io'el and
Randir'el, full and blazing, and between them Zhur'el, small and
red, casting its faint rust-colored light across a battlefield where
the alliance of free peoples had just died.

Because there had always been worlds where the blow landed. Worlds
where Demious Stoneshaper's axe split Zuel's avatar from crown to
sternum and the orcish host broke and fled and a dwarf became a king
and a war ended and the free peoples rebuilt, slowly and painfully,
under a sky of two moons where the space between Io'el and Randir'el
was empty and had always been empty and no one thought to look for
what was not there. Those worlds existed, as real and as solid as the
stone beneath the dead.

And there had always been worlds where the blow did not land.

This was one of them.

In the infinite architecture of reality, every moment that could
occur did occur, somewhere, in some version of the world that was as
true and as valid as any other. The philosopher-priests of Mau, the
Oracle, the Watcher who had been the first consciousness to awaken
in the universe, taught that all possible worlds were real, that
existence was not a single river flowing in one direction but an
ocean of infinite depth, every wave a world, every current a
history, every drop a life lived differently than the life beside it.
Most mortals found this teaching either incomprehensible or
irrelevant. What did it matter that somewhere, in some unreachable
version of reality, their choices had gone differently? They lived in
the world they lived in. Their pain was their pain. Their dead were
their dead.

But on this night, in this world, under three moons instead of two,
the distinction mattered absolutely. Because in the worlds where the
blow landed, the orcs remained what they had always been: scattered
tribes in remote mountains, dangerous but contained, a threat that
could be managed by vigilant borders and strong walls. And in the
worlds where the blow did not land, something else happened. Zuel's
armies swept the continents. The surviving races were broken and
enslaved. The elven lands were razed until not a drop of life
remained. Dwarves were herded into camps for experiments that would
not bear their terrible fruit for generations. Humans were bred for
obedience. Halflings were exterminated because their island was
strategically valuable and their population was not useful enough to
justify preservation.

And the orcs built an empire.

Not a horde. Not a kingdom of savages. An empire of precision and
ambition and ruthless, systematic intelligence, an empire that would
one day reach beyond the boundaries of its own reality and into the
infinite ocean of worlds that Mau's priests had always known existed.
An empire that would conquer eleven of those worlds before, almost by
accident, it discovered another.

A world with two moons instead of three.

But that discovery lay centuries in the future, and on this night,
there was only the battle and its aftermath. Zuel's avatar stood
among the ruins of Fort Hope, and Rangithyr stood beside his god
with blood on his scimitar and the first stirrings of a legend in
his name, and Io'el and Randir'el cast their silver and gold light
across a field of the dead, while Zhur'el, the small red moon that
had no twin in those other, luckier worlds, watched from between
them with the patience of a thing that had always been there and
always would be.

Three moons. One victory. And a silence that would last for
centuries, until the empire born beneath this sky learned to reach
across the void and touch the worlds where only two moons rose.
