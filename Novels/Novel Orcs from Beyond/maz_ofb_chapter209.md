# Chapter Nine
## Rallying the World

The Council of War convened in the common room above
Eccentricities on a morning when the harbour bell rang
the sixth hour and the smell of Yella's cooking drifted
through the open window and the ordinary machinery of
Amara turned beneath them with the indifferent persistence
of a city that had not yet learned what had happened at
Winderbourgh and would not learn for several days, because
the riders Bastien had dispatched to confirm the heroes'
account had not yet returned, and the Baron of Amara did
not act on unconfirmed intelligence regardless of who
delivered it.

Bastien sat at the head of the table.

He had arrived without escort, which was itself a
communication, the calculated informality of a ruler who
wished his guests to understand that this meeting was not
an audience but a consultation, that the intelligence they
possessed was valued but that the value would be assessed
on his terms and according to his methods and at a pace
that his three generations of mercantile governance had
taught him was the difference between decisive action and
expensive mistakes. He was a large man, broad across the
shoulders, dressed in the dark wool and silver-thread
embroidery that the Bastien family wore as uniform rather
than ornament, his face carrying the particular composure
of someone who had been managing crises since before any
of the people at the table had arrived in his city.

His eyes moved across the room. They took in the heroes
with the systematic attention of a man whose ability to
read people was the sharpest instrument in a considerable
arsenal: the elven priest whose measured formality and warm Minerva clasp
spoke of conviction rather than ambition; the gnome whose
magnificent moustache and scorched waistcoat spoke of
competence tested under fire; the Sabonaar bard whose torn
coat and bandaged shoulder spoke of a man who had bled for
information he was now presenting for free; and the gaunt
elf who sat in the corner with a quarterstaff across his
knees and grey eyes that tracked things no one else could
see, whose presence Bastien had noted without comment and
whose nature he had not asked about because a Baron who
survived three generations of Amaran politics knew when a
question's answer was more expensive than its silence.

The young wood elf woman with the green-tinted skin and the
yellow eyes sat apart from the others near the window. She
watched. She said nothing. Bastien noted this too.

"Tell me what happened at Winderbourgh," Bastien said.

Aizune told him.

He told it the way the Cundo'elda had trained him: facts
first, assessments second, implications third. The Orceri
crossing. The transit. The battle. Forty disciplined soldiers,
a biomancer, an acolyte, all destroyed. Eleven civilian dead.
The Crossed Keys inn burned. The mana-enhanced weapons and
armour fragments that Smiggles had collected from the field,
which sat on the table between them in a leather pouch that
hummed faintly with residual energy.

Bastien listened without interruption. When Aizune finished,
the Baron reached forward and opened the pouch and removed
a fragment of Orceri armour, a curved piece of dark material
the size of his palm, and turned it in his fingers. The
residual mana shimmer was visible even in daylight: a faint
cold bluish luminescence that pulsed once as his skin made
contact and then faded, the stripped entity power within it
responding to the proximity of a living being and finding
the being insufficient to sustain a reaction.

"This is not from any smithy I know," Bastien said. "And I
know every smithy between here and the Frozen Isles."

"It is not from this world," Smiggles said. "The mana
lattice structure is consistent with the Orceri equipment
we recovered from Zanthar's Keep and that the Thaumaturgic
Council confirmed as originating from an alternate prime
material plane. The metallurgical properties exceed anything
produced in Mazariim. The mana enhancement is powered by
energy stripped from destroyed entities." He paused. The
pause was the gnomish craft idiom surfacing: the assessment
of whether the listener was equipped to process what
followed. "Baron, the civilisation that sent those soldiers
has conquered eleven worlds. They have four hundred years
of interdimensional military experience. The force at
Winderbourgh was a probe. A test. The real invasion will
be orders of magnitude larger."

Bastien set the armour fragment on the table. His fingers
were steady. His face showed nothing that his composure did
not permit, which was nothing at all, the practiced blankness
of a ruler whose survival depended on never revealing which
piece of information had changed his assessment before he was
ready to act on the change.

"What do you need from me?" he said.

"Access to your intelligence network. Permission to recruit.
Resources for a journey east to the Kirak Mountains, where
the dimensional boundary is weakest and where the Orceri will
construct their invasion portal." Aizune's voice was measured,
steady, carrying the quiet authority that was not performance
but conviction, the authority of a priest of Minerva who had
stood in a world where Minerva was dead and had returned with
the knowledge of what happened when no one stood at all. "And
your voice. When the other city-states ask why they should
listen to a group of mercenaries claiming an interdimensional
invasion is imminent, your endorsement will carry weight that
ours cannot."

Bastien considered. The consideration was brief, which meant
the decision had been made before the question was asked and
the consideration was courtesy.

"You will have access to my intelligence network. You will
have permission to recruit within Amara's jurisdiction. You
will have supplies and mounts for your eastern journey." He
stood. The meeting was over. The brevity was not dismissal
but efficiency: a Baron who had decided did not linger over
the deciding. "My voice, however, I will deploy at my
discretion and in my timing. I do not endorse what I cannot
verify. When my riders return from Winderbourgh, and when
the evidence confirms what you have told me, my voice will
follow. Not before."

He paused at the door. The composure shifted by a fraction,
the blankness admitting a quality that was not warmth exactly
but its professional cousin: the acknowledgement that the
people in the room had risked their lives for intelligence
they were sharing without demand for payment.

"Broomspatch will coordinate the logistics. He has resources
that extend beyond gold, and he is motivated. Something about
your account of the Orceri has made him very motivated indeed."

He left. The door closed. The harbour bell rang the seventh
hour.

---

Aldorph Broomspatch was, as Bastien had noted, motivated.

The large, rotund merchant with the red hair and the monocle
and the long moustache and beard arrived at the common room
within the hour, carrying a ledger that was larger than
Smiggles and considerably better organised. He spread it
across the table and the pages revealed a network that the
heroes had not suspected: trade contacts in Cecastaa, banking
relationships in Naarlam, shipping partners in the Frozen
Isles, a cousin in Fort Hope who sat on the Mercantile
Advisory Board and who owed Aldorph a favour that Aldorph
had been saving for an occasion of sufficient magnitude.

"Gold moves faster than armies, friends," Aldorph said. His
voice carried the enthusiastic energy that was his resting
state, the confidence of a man whose understanding of the
world was built on the principle that every problem was a
transaction and every transaction had a solution if you knew
the right currency. "The Red Raven Trading Company has
contacts in fourteen cities. If the threat is real, and I
have seen the evidence and I believe the threat is real, then
the gold will find allies before the armies find their boots."

He began writing letters. The quill moved with the speed of
a man who had been writing commercial correspondence since
before the heroes were born and whose penmanship was, despite
the speed, immaculate. Each letter was tailored: to the
cousin in Fort Hope, a request for an emergency session of
the Advisory Board; to the banking house in Naarlam, an
inquiry about the noble houses' appetite for military
investment; to a shipwright in Cecastaa, a commission for
fast courier vessels. The letters were not pleas. They were
opportunities. Aldorph understood, as the heroes did not,
that the language of crisis was ineffective with merchants
and that the language of opportunity was not.

"You are selling them a war," Smiggles said.

"I am selling them the chance to be on the winning side of
a war," Aldorph corrected. The monocle glinted. "The
distinction is everything."

---

Smiggles and Aizune departed for Instar the following
morning.

The journey took two days by the trade road through the
Steppes of Broken Stone, the same road the heroes had
travelled months earlier when the keep was still standing
and the Orceri were a vision in a dead wizard's machine
rather than soldiers bleeding on Mazariim cobblestones.
The landscape was unchanged: desolate scrubland, sparse
vegetation, the distant shapes of rock formations that
looked, from certain angles and in certain light, like
the ruins of things that had never been built. Smiggles
rode with the mana engine in his satchel, the device's
steady civilian hum a constant companion, and beside
it, wrapped in oilcloth, the Orceri armour fragments
from Winderbourgh.

Aizune rode beside him with the prayer journal at his
hip, heavy with entries, and the Minerva clasp warm at
his throat. They did not speak much. The silence between
them was the silence of two people who had said what
needed saying and were now conserving their words for
the audience that waited at the end of the road, and the
audience would be more difficult than any they had faced
because the audience was a council of the most powerful
mages in the Merchant Nations and the most powerful mages
in the Merchant Nations did not frighten easily and did
not commit resources without empirical justification.

Instar received them with its usual indifference to the
non-magical.

The city's permanent magical streetlights, which had seemed
wondrous on their first visit, now carried an association
that neither of them voiced: the cold blue-white mana
streetlamps of the Orceri town, steady and eternal, burning
on the stolen essence of dead entities. Instar's lights
were different, arcane rather than biomantic, powered by
mana wells rather than harvested divinity. But the aesthetic
similarity was uncomfortable, and the discomfort was a
measure of how thoroughly the reconnaissance had changed
their perception of things they had once found merely
impressive.

The gate assessment was the same as before: two fingers to
the wrist, three seconds, the guard's expression shifting
through the readings with the professional neutrality of
someone who performed the procedure dozens of times daily.
Aizune's divine signature drew a respectful nod. Smiggles'
arcane signature drew a longer pause, the guard's fingers
pressing slightly harder as the mana assessment registered
something that required a second reading, and whether the
second reading was prompted by the quality of Smiggles'
arcane output or by a name on a list that the Guilded Cog
maintained through intermediaries was impossible to
determine and was, in Smiggles' assessment, not worth the
energy of worrying about when there were larger concerns
than gnomish trade politics occupying his attention.

The Thaumaturgic Council convened in the high chamber.

---

The room was circular, tiered, the seats arranged in a
descending arc around a central floor where petitioners
presented their cases to the assembled power of Instar's
ruling body. Magical illumination filled the space with a
warm, even light that cast no shadows, the enchantment
designed to eliminate the visual ambiguity that shadows
permitted, because the Thaumaturgic Council valued clarity
above all things and had built their chamber to reflect
the value.

Sixteen seats. Fifteen occupied. River ni'Bathrun, the
silver dragon, was absent, her chair empty, and the
emptiness drew Smiggles' attention because the absence of
a dragon from a council session about an interdimensional
threat involving dragons seemed, to his analytical mind,
like the absence of a locksmith from a discussion about
locked doors.

Yelinda Featherstep presided. The Ya'sehrim enchantress
was ancient by elven standards, her age visible not in
the way that human age was visible, through the degradation
of skin and the whitening of hair, but in the quality of
stillness that she carried, the particular composure of a
consciousness that had been processing information for
centuries and had refined the processing to the point where
nothing that entered her perception arrived as a surprise.
Her dark eyes moved from Aizune to Smiggles and back with
the unhurried assessment of someone who had already formed
a preliminary opinion and was waiting for the evidence to
confirm or revise it.

Yigba sat beside her. The Ejiin wu-jen was smaller, older
in appearance, his robes carrying the subtle geometric
patterns of eastern magical tradition, his face carrying
the calm of a man whose power was so considerable that the
exercise of patience cost him nothing. He was, by
reputation, the most individually powerful spellcaster on
the Council. He watched the proceedings with eyes that
missed nothing and revealed less.

Aizune presented the evidence.

He laid the Orceri armour fragments on the central table
beside the mana engine, the dark material and the humming
device occupying the same surface with the uncomfortable
proximity of things that should not exist in this reality
and did. He opened his prayer journal and read from it:
Giganzalt's testimony, transcribed in Elda'lam, the words
carrying the weight of an ancient dragon's eight centuries
of observation rendered in the precise script of a
Cundo'elda priest whose training had prepared him to
record exactly this kind of intelligence, even if nothing
in the training had prepared him for the content.

The War. The War of the Heavens. Eleven conquered worlds.
Biomancy. Sensoriums. Sky citadels. Rangithyr.

The Council listened. Some took notes. Others did not,
their recall sufficient for any information that warranted
retention. Myth'raal Dragonsbane, the Yav'daerim evoker,
leaned forward when Aizune described the mana-enhanced
armour's resistance to conventional arcane assault.
Ariana Ignescreo leaned back in her seat when Aizune spoke
of dimensional scars and destroyed entities, her
flame-flickering eyes closing, her head tilting to one side
in a posture that was not inattention but the particular
focus of someone whose divine tradition gave her direct
perception of the damage Aizune was describing, and whose
body expressed concentration the way other bodies expressed
rest: languidly, with an ease that drew the eye along the
line of her throat and the fall of crimson-black hair across
her shoulder. The ember-dark skin of her collarbones caught
the chamber's warm light and held it. She opened her eyes
before Aizune had finished the sentence, and the twin points
of orange-gold found his across the chamber with an intimacy
that had nothing to do with the subject matter and everything
to do with the nature of the being behind the gaze.
Salrutis, the Varkonan sorcerer, sat very still throughout,
his deep crimson robes catching the chamber's shadowless
light, and his face was unreadable, and his stillness was
the stillness of someone for whom the testimony carried
resonances that the other Council members could not hear.

Smiggles presented the mana engine.

He placed the Kren-7 series device on the table and
activated it with a touch, and the steady civilian hum
filled the chamber, and the ambient mana noise that the
device produced washed over the assembled mages with a
frequency that several of them felt in their teeth and
that all of them recognised as alien, as other, as
belonging to a tradition of mana manipulation that
Mazariim's magical theory had not conceived and that the
device's casual existence demonstrated was not theoretical
but manufactured, mass-produced, available in shops for
the price of a crystal and the promise of return business.

"This is a civilian heating device," Smiggles said. "Not
a weapon. Not a military instrument. A household
convenience. It runs on mana stripped from entities that
the Orceri destroyed during their invasion of the
extra-dimensional planes. The energy signature is
consistent with divine mana that has been harvested,
processed, and integrated into a standardised power
distribution system." He looked at the Council. His hazel
eyes were sharp and his voice carried the clipped precision
of the Zhonhylite craft idiom, every consonant struck
clean, and beneath the precision was the controlled fury
of a man who had walked through a civilisation that had
turned the divine into infrastructure. "Their streetlights
run on this. Their floating carriages run on this. Their
surveillance constructs run on this. Every piece of
technology in their civilisation is powered by the
harvested remains of beings that the people of this world
call gods. This is what we are facing. Not an army. A
system."

The chamber was quiet. The mana engine hummed.

Yelinda Featherstep spoke. "The dimensional boundary
weakness. You mentioned the Kirak Mountains."

"The ruins of Gaal'Tarak," Aizune said. "A dwarven citadel
that was destroyed fifty years ago by forces that no one
has been able to explain. The boundary between realities
is damaged there, weakened, and the Orceri are exploiting
the weakness to construct a portal. Our companion, the one
whose planar abilities allow us to cross between worlds,
can feel the construction in progress. He estimates weeks
before completion."

"We are aware of Gaal'Tarak's collapse," Ariana Ignescreo
said. The ifrit's voice was warm and unhurried, carrying
the harmonic undertone of a being whose elemental heritage
gave her speech a heat that was literal rather than
figurative, each word arriving with the slow confidence of
someone who had never needed to raise her voice to hold a
room's attention and who found the concept faintly amusing.
She uncrossed her legs and recrossed them in the opposite
direction, a motion that caused the draping of sheer
Varkonan silk she wore in place of robes to shift across
her ember-dark thighs with a whisper that several Council
members heard and none acknowledged. "I have studied the
dimensional scarring at the site. Personally. The boundary
there is not merely thin. It is wounded. Something tore
through it with catastrophic force, and the wound has not
healed." Her eyes, those twin furnace-points of
orange-gold, found Aizune's across the chamber, and the
finding was deliberate, and the warmth in it exceeded the
professional. "Your account of the Orceri's capabilities
suggests they have the means to exploit such a wound. I
find this... credible." She let the word settle. A smile
touched the corner of her mouth, brief and private, as
though the word "credible" amused her in a context she did
not intend to share. "Very credible."

"Credible is not sufficient for resource commitment,"
Yelinda said. The words were not dismissal. They were
process. "Myth'raal. The armour fragments."

Myth'raal Dragonsbane rose from his seat and descended
to the central table. The Yav'daerim evoker was tall,
lean, his force-specialisation evident in the way the air
around him carried a faint density, the habitual
compression of someone whose magic operated through the
manipulation of physical forces and whose proximity to
his own power was constant. He took an armour fragment
and held it, turning it, and his free hand traced a
pattern in the air above the dark material, and the warm
gold light of an arcane analysis spread across the
fragment's surface like water across stone, revealing
the internal mana lattice in glowing lines that the
assembled Council could see.

"The lattice structure is consistent with the materials
we analysed from the first recovery," Myth'raal said.
"Alternate-reality origin. Vibrational frequency outside
our dimensional parameters. But this sample shows
something the original equipment did not: combat damage.
Thermal stress. Arcane impact residue." He looked at
Smiggles. "You hit this with a fireball."

"Among other things," Smiggles said.

"The mana-enhancement layer absorbed approximately sixty
percent of the thermal energy before failure. That is
higher than any enchantment currently deployed by any
nation on this peninsula." Myth'raal set the fragment
down. "For reference, the best Gormdawi dwarven plate
enchantment absorbs approximately thirty-five percent.
These soldiers were wearing armour twice as effective as
our finest."

The number settled into the chamber. Twice. The word
was small. The implication was not.

Snarf Copperthumb spoke from his seat in the upper tier.
The gnomish alchemist had been watching Smiggles since the
presentation began, his small sharp eyes tracking the
younger gnome's movements with an attention that was not
scholarly curiosity but something more specific, the gaze
of someone who recognised a name and was deciding what the
recognition required. "The mana engine. You acquired it
through trade? In the Orceri city?"

"In a shop," Smiggles said. He did not look at Copperthumb.
His voice was level, the practiced indifference of a man
who had been aware of the Guilded Cog's interest in his
movements since leaving Zhonhylite and who had learned that
the most effective response to surveillance was the refusal
to acknowledge it. "I paid with a mana-conductive crystal.
The shopkeeper was satisfied with the transaction."

"The crystal's origin?"

"Amara's Market District. Purchased at Eccentricities.
The provenance is immaculate, if that is your concern."

The exchange was brief. The undercurrent was not. Snarf
Copperthumb sat back in his chair and his small sharp
eyes did not leave Smiggles and the not-leaving was its
own communication, and Smiggles received it and filed it
in the compartment of his mind labelled slag and did not
permit it to occupy any further space because the Guilded
Cog's petty commercial grudge was a stripped thread
beside the Orceri invasion and he would not allow a
stripped thread to distract him from a machine that was
about to consume the world.

Yelinda Featherstep spoke the Council's decision with
the economy that characterised her governance. "The
evidence is compelling. The Council will commit resources.
Myth'raal will lead a working group on countermeasures
for the Orceri mana-enhancement technology. Ariana will
study the Gaal'Tarak boundary weakness in coordination
with your planar specialist. Salrutis..." She looked at
the Varkonan sorcerer. "Salrutis will liaise with the
ancient dragon you have brought to our world, at a
location of the dragon's choosing. A dragon with eight
centuries of intelligence on the Orceri is a resource
this Council cannot afford to ignore."

Salrutis inclined his head. The motion was precise,
unhurried, the acknowledgement of a man accepting a task
that he had anticipated. His expression remained
unreadable. Near the rear of the chamber, in a seat that
was not a Council seat but an observer's bench where
guests were permitted during open sessions, Lai sat with
her legs crossed and her yellow eyes on the Varkonan
sorcerer, and her face was very still, and the stillness
was the stillness of someone who was watching a door that
might open and deciding whether to be present when it did.

She said nothing. She offered nothing. A dragon with ties
to this Council, whose adopted parent sat fifteen feet
from her in deep crimson robes, could have opened doors
that the heroes' evidence alone could not. An introduction.
A word. A confirmation of identity that would have elevated
the heroes' credibility from compelling to unassailable.
She provided none of these things. Whether this was
strategic patience, the instinct of a creature that
guarded its secrets the way it guarded its hoard, or
something more personal, the reluctance of a daughter
who had left without explanation and was not prepared to
explain, was a question the silence posed and the silence
did not answer.

---

Iskar rode south alone.

The road to Blowing Rock descended from the Amaran
highlands through farmland that was gold with late-season
wheat, the fields rolling in gentle waves toward the coast,
and the farmers working the rows looked up as he passed
and nodded and the nodding was the ordinary courtesy of
people who lived in a place where riders were common and
strangers were not threatening and the sky held two moons
and the moons were the right number and the world was, for
the people in these fields, the same world it had been
yesterday and would be tomorrow, and the sameness was
precious, and the preciousness was the thing that tightened
Iskar's throat as he rode past because he had seen the
alternative and the alternative wore grey tunics and
carried bread with empty eyes.

His shoulder ached. The wound from Winderbourgh was healing,
the shallow cut scabbing beneath the bandage, but the ache
was useful because it confirmed that the battle had been
real and the confirmation was necessary because the days
since Winderbourgh had acquired the quality of acceleration,
events piling upon events with a speed that his sailor's
sense of weather associated with the approach of something
large and unavoidable, and the ache beneath the bandage was
the proof that the something was not metaphor but metal and
it had already drawn his blood.

He passed a farm where a young boy was attempting to lead
a goat that had no interest in being led, the animal
planting its hooves with the immovable stubbornness that
goats deployed as their primary contribution to the
agricultural enterprise, and the boy was talking to it
in the rapid, exasperated Sabo of someone who had been
negotiating with this particular goat since dawn and was
losing. Iskar slowed his horse. "My frrriend," he called
down, "I have negotiated with pirates, smugglers, and a
gnome who believes his moustache is a national trreasure.
Allow me." He dismounted, took the rope from the boy's
hands, leaned close to the goat's ear, and said something
in a voice too low to carry. The goat considered. The goat
moved. The boy stared. "What did you say to it?" he asked.
Iskar remounted, tipped his hat, and smiled. "I told him
where we were going. Goats are like sailors. They just
want to know the heading."

The monastery appeared above the village: the Valiant Halls
of the Dragon on its hill, the marble statue of Pala'dinae
visible against the sky, the draconic features and elaborate
plate mail catching the afternoon light. The interior
courtyard's Ejiin bridges and gardens were peaceful in the
way that places dedicated to faith were peaceful, the calm
not an absence of awareness but an expression of it, the
particular quality of a community that knew the world was
dangerous and had chosen to face the danger with discipline
rather than despair.

Lionous Harker met him at the gate.

The Lord of Justice looked as he had when the heroes had
first come to Blowing Rock carrying Remular's body: blonde
hair pulled back, blue eyes that told of deep loss and
equally deep devotion, the fist-in-palm bow of his order
delivered with the sincerity of a man who meant every
gesture and performed none of them for show. He looked
younger than forty-five. He looked like a man whose faith
had preserved something that time normally consumed.

"You are wounded," Lionous said.

"A scratch."

"You are lying."

"I am understating." Iskar smiled. The smile was thinner
than his usual, the warmth present but tempered by the
hardness that Winderbourgh had installed beneath it, and
Lionous saw the tempering the way a smith saw the
tempering in steel: the change that made the material
stronger and the strength that was purchased by heat.

"Come inside. Eat. Then tell me what has happened."

They sat in the courtyard beside the fountain whose water
fell from a stone dragon's mouth into a basin of pale
marble. Iskar told Lionous everything. The Orceri town.
The enslaved humans. The dwarven hulks. The sensoriums.
The War of the Heavens. Winderbourgh. The probe. The
portal. He told it the way he told all stories, with the
rhythm and the detail and the particular Sabonaar gift
for making the listener see what the teller had seen,
except this time the gift was a burden because what the
teller had seen was a civilisation that had perfected the
consumption of everything that was not itself and the
listener was a man whose faith was built on the belief
that such consumption could be opposed.

Lionous listened as Bastien had listened: without
interruption. But where Bastien's silence had been the
silence of calculation, Lionous's silence was the silence
of comprehension, the particular quiet of a man who was
not deciding whether to act but how.

"Half my order is on crusade in the Ancient South,"
Lionous said. "The Champions who remain number eighteen.
Fourteen paladin-monks and four priests. It is not a
large force."

"It is a committed one," Iskar said. "And commitment is
what we need. The political leaders calculate. The mages
analyse. The merchants sell. What we need, Lord of
Justice, is people who will stand at a portal in the
mountains and hold the line because holding the line is
right, regardless of whether the line can be held."

Lionous looked at the statue of Pala'dinae above the
courtyard. The draconic features. The longsword. The
crossed lances. The face of an entity who had come from
another world to this one and had taught mortals that
justice was not a concept but a practice, that the strong
defended the weak not because it was wise but because it
was necessary, and the necessity was the thing that the
faithful carried when wisdom ran out.

"Remular went to the keep alone," Lionous said. "He went
because the orc raids were hurting people and no one else
was going. He died there. You brought his body home." The
blue eyes found Iskar's. "You have brought us something
harder than a body, my friend. You have brought us the
knowledge of what killed him and why, and the knowledge
that it is coming for all of us."

He stood. The fist-in-palm bow, slower this time,
deliberate, the gesture of a man who was not greeting an
ally but pledging himself to a cause.

"The Champions of Blowing Rock will march with you. Every
one of us. Where you need us, when you need us, for as
long as we stand."

Iskar bowed in return. The Sabonaar sailor's bow, from
the waist, the hat removed and held against his chest,
the gesture carrying the weight of a man who had entered
every port and every tavern and every battle with the
confidence that his voice and his blade would be enough,
and who was discovering, in the courtyard of a monastery
where the fountain murmured and the statue watched, that
confidence was not enough and that the thing that
supplemented it was the presence of people who believed
in something larger than their own survival.

"Thank you," he said. The words were simple. The Sabonaar
accent did not embellish them.

"Do not thank me," Lionous said. "Thank Pala'dinae. He
teaches that the strength of the righteous is measured
not in numbers but in the willingness to stand. Eighteen
who are willing are worth more than a thousand who are
not."

---

The other doors were harder.

Aizune sent messages through Aldorph's network, through
Bastien's intelligence contacts, through the Liberators
at the Shrine to the Wanderer whose operatives moved
through the Merchant Nations with the quiet efficiency
of people who had been smuggling humans out of slavery
for decades and who understood, when the message reached
them, that the stakes had expanded from the liberation
of individuals to the liberation of reality itself.
Finkdonklonk Shortstack, the gnomish oracle who led the
Liberators, sent back a single line: "Our networks are
yours. Tell us where the chains will fall."

The Druid Circle of Larue Falls responded through
Jalanthia, the Tawarim elf who led the circle. Her
answer was practical rather than martial: the druids
would watch the eastern approaches to the Kirak Mountains,
providing reconnaissance along routes that no urban
intelligence network could cover, the wild places where
the land itself would report what moved through it to
those who knew how to listen.

Iskar's contacts in the Dock District, the sailors and
dockhands and harbour masters who constituted the informal
intelligence apparatus of Amara's waterfront, began
reporting within days. Unusual activity in the eastern
mountains. Tremors that the miners in the foothills
attributed to geological settling and that Iskar, who
had felt the boundary wounds through Aleron's testimony,
attributed to something else entirely.

But beyond these: resistance.

Tentigo, the Tiovinist theocracy, sent no reply at all.
Their crusade in the Ancient South consumed their attention
and their resources and their theological certainty that
the threats worth fighting were the threats they had
already identified, and an interdimensional orc invasion
described in a message from a free city they considered
insufficiently devout did not meet the threshold.

Naarlam's noble houses acknowledged the message through
Queen Neven Konovara's diplomatic office and expressed
measured concern and committed nothing. The seven houses
that had governed Naarlam for two thousand years had
survived by committing nothing until the cost of inaction
exceeded the cost of action, and the cost of inaction
against a threat they had not seen was, by their
calculation, still less than the cost of deploying
resources away from the internal politics that actually
determined their survival.

Cecastaa did not trust outsiders. The message was received
and filed. The horsebreeders who had broken from Naarlam
in 412 BW and had maintained their fierce independence
for twelve centuries were not inclined to ride to the
defence of cities they did not trade with on the word of
adventurers they did not know.

Moamon and Asim were actively hostile. Baron Helfast
Simir's government in Moamon viewed any request from Amara
as a potential trap, the residue of the war in 733 that
neither city had forgiven. King Byrne Simir in Asim viewed
it as an opportunity to assess Amara's military readiness
while committing nothing of his own. The messages
disappeared into bureaucracies that were designed to
absorb communication without producing response.

The Federation of Man, far to the north, considered the
Merchant Nations a regional matter. King Francent Thoman
of Beneshire, who led the Praetorian Council, sent a
formal acknowledgement through diplomatic channels that
the Federation had noted the report and would monitor
the situation. The monitoring would consist, Aizune
understood, of doing nothing until the situation reached
the Federation's borders, at which point it would be too
late.

The world the heroes were trying to save was not
interested in being saved by people it did not know.

They reconvened in Amara.

Aleron sat in the corner of the common room. He had spent
the days of the heroes' absence recovering, and the
recovery had returned him to functionality that was not
health but its approximation, the body finding a new
equilibrium that was lower than the previous equilibrium
and that would be the baseline from which the next
expenditure would subtract. His grey eyes were focused,
both of them, which was a better sign than the alternative,
and the quarterstaff leaned against the wall beside him
rather than across his knees, which meant he did not need
it as an anchor, which meant the echoes were at their
manageable murmur rather than their overwhelming roar.

He listened as the others reported. Bastien's conditional
support. Instar's resource commitment. Blowing Rock's
unconditional pledge. The Liberators' networks. The druids'
reconnaissance. And the refusals: Tentigo, Naarlam,
Cecastaa, Moamon, Asim, the Federation.

The accounting was brief. The accounting was insufficient.

Smiggles summarised. "We have Amara's intelligence and
logistics, pending confirmation that Bastien's riders will
provide. We have Instar's analytical resources and
countermeasure development. We have eighteen paladin-monks
from Blowing Rock. We have a network of spies who are very
good at moving people through hostile territory. We have
druids who can watch the mountains. And we have an ancient
dragon that no one except us and Instar knows about." He
paused. The moustache twitched. The hazel eyes swept the
room. "Against a civilisation that has conquered eleven
worlds and is building a portal in the Kirak Mountains.
The arithmetic is not ticking."

"The arithmetic has never been in our favour," Aizune said.
"It was not in our favour at Winderbourgh. It was not in
our favour in Ta'Puh. It was not in our favour when we sat
in a common room in Amara and a man we did not know told
us he needed our help to save the world."

Aleron's thin smile, from the corner. The grey eyes found
Aizune's and held them. The smile was the one his
companions had learned to value: the smile that appeared
when the echoes were quiet and the man behind them was
present and the man was grateful, not for the odds but
for the company.

The room settled into the particular silence of people
who had heard the numbers and were choosing to act despite
them. It was not optimism. It was not denial. It was the
silence that preceded commitment, the pause between the
understanding that the task was impossible and the
decision to attempt it anyway, and the decision was made
not in a single moment but in the accumulation of moments
that had brought them to this room: the machine's vision,
the spy's body, the dragon's testimony, the enslaved
humans, the dwarven hulks, the child with the bread, the
soldiers at Winderbourgh, the clasp that had gone cold
and come warm again.

Lai found Aizune on the roof that evening.

The harbour lay below, the water holding the last gold
of the sunset, the fishing boats returning, the harbour
bell ringing the evening watch. The Minerva clasp caught
the fading light and held it.

"You are trying to build an alliance against a force
that has conquered eleven worlds," she said. The v/w
substitution was absent. The musical lightness was absent.
What remained was the voice that the tree line in Ta'Puh
had revealed: precise, unperformed, belonging to something
older than the mask it usually wore. "And you are doing
it by asking nicely."

"The alternative is compulsion," Aizune said. "And
compulsion builds nothing that endures."

"I admire the conviction. I question the method."

"Pala'dinae teaches that the strength of an alliance is
not in its numbers but in the conviction of those who
choose to stand." He looked at her. The grey eyes that
occasionally flashed silver were steady, carrying the
weight of everything he had learned in the cave and the
town and the battle and the Council chamber and the
silence of the refusals, and the weight had not broken
him, and the not-breaking was its own kind of argument.
"I would rather have ten who believe than a thousand who
were compelled."

She regarded him for a long moment. The yellow eyes,
vertical pupils bright in the fading light, held an
expression that he could not read and that she did not
offer to translate.

"You may get your wish," she said. "Ten is approximately
what you have."

She turned and went inside. Aizune remained on the roof.
The harbour lights were coming on, one by one, the warm
orange glow of oil lamps and candles replacing the sun's
gold with the smaller, closer light of a city settling
into its evening, and each light was a life and each life
was precious and the preciousness was what he carried
beside the knowledge and the faith and the understanding
that what he had built in the days since Winderbourgh
was not an army and was not an alliance and was, instead,
something smaller and more stubborn: a group of people
who had chosen to stand.

The harbour bell rang. The two moons rose, silver and
gold, over a city that was beginning, slowly and
incompletely and with the reluctance that characterised
all beginnings that mattered, to understand what was
coming.
