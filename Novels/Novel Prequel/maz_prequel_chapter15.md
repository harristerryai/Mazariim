# Chapter Fifteen
## The Voice from Geometric Darkness

The silence after Sevan's invitation held for seven heartbeats.

Tobias counted them, because counting was what he did when
situations exceeded the parameters of his training and his
instincts required a framework more reliable than assessment.
Seven heartbeats, each one marking a second in which the Tower's
awareness examined the priest who had spoken a single word into
the concentrated attention of something three thousand years old
and had not flinched from the weight of what looked back.

Then the voice spoke, and Tobias did not understand a word of it.

The language was not Common. It was not Draconic, whose harsh
syllables he had learned to recognize from years of hearing mages
cast. It was something older than either, a flowing, vowel-heavy
tongue that filled the laboratory from every surface simultaneously,
each wall producing the sound at slightly different intervals so
that the words arrived as a cascade rather than a sentence, the
same phrase repeating from stone and crystal and geometric carving
in a chorus that was not echo but multiplication, as if the
speaker existed in every surface and spoke from all of them at
once.

Sevan answered.

The priest's voice shifted into a register Tobias had never heard
from him: slower, more musical, the consonants softened, the
vowels elongated into sounds that belonged to a language whose
rhythms were fundamentally different from the Common they had been
speaking for days. He spoke haltingly, with the careful
pronunciation of a man using a language he had studied from texts
rather than conversation, testing each word against the memory of
how it should sound and finding the reality of speaking it to a
native speaker more demanding than the library had prepared him for.

Vaelith's eyes widened. "Ancient Madra," she murmured to Tobias,
leaning toward him from her position near the nearest worktable.
"Pre-War formal register. I can follow perhaps a third of it. The
grammar is significantly different from modern Madra, and the
vocabulary..." She trailed off as Sevan spoke again, her brow
furrowed in concentration. "He is telling it who we are. Scholars.
Seekers of knowledge about the resonance channels."

The voice from the walls responded, and this time the cascade was
faster, more intense, the words tumbling over one another with an
urgency that broke the scholarly cadence. Tobias heard the same
phrase repeated three times in succession, each repetition louder
than the last, emanating from a different section of wall, as if
the speaker were pacing the perimeter of the room without a body
to pace with. Then a laugh. Not a human laugh. A sound that
contained harmonics that no throat could produce, hollow and
resonant and carrying, beneath the surface of amusement, a note
of something fractured, something that had been whole once and
was now held together by will rather than structure.

Sevan listened. His face, normally so controlled in its minimal
expressions, showed strain: the effort of parsing rapid archaic
speech delivered from multiple directions by a consciousness whose
emotional state was expressed through volume and repetition rather
than the tonal modulations that living speakers used.

"He is asking questions," Sevan said, turning to his companions.
His voice had returned to Common, but the ancient Madra's rhythms
clung to his pronunciation, ghosting beneath the familiar words.
"Three questions. He says he will ask three questions, and our
answers will determine whether we leave this Tower alive or whether
the Tower keeps us."

"That is a threat," Tobias said.

"It is a condition." Sevan paused, choosing his next words with
the precision that the situation demanded. "He does not speak as a
man speaks. The language is correct, formally correct, but the
pattern is wrong. He repeats. He circles back to phrases he has
already used. He refers to himself in the third person and then
corrects to the first. He mentioned colleagues at the Athenaeum
by name and then stopped, mid-sentence, as if remembering that
they are dead." The priest's pale eyes held something that was
not fear but its near neighbor: the recognition of a mind whose
brilliance and whose damage were inseparable. "He is not entirely
sane, and he is not entirely mad. He is something between, and
the space between is where the danger lives."

The voice erupted again, louder, sharper, the words arriving
from the ceiling now as well as the walls, the cascade becoming
three-dimensional, surrounding them in a sphere of ancient
language whose urgency bordered on frenzy. Then it stopped. Cut
off as suddenly as if a hand had been placed over the speaker's
mouth. The silence that followed throbbed with the absence of
sound, the laboratory ringing with the memory of words that had
filled it moments before.

Then, quietly, from a single point in the wall nearest to Sevan,
a voice that was almost calm. Almost reasonable. Almost the voice
of the scholar it had been, three thousand years and an eternity
of isolation ago.

Sevan translated: "He says: 'Ask your three questions, said the
Geometrist to the empty room, and the room did not answer, because
rooms do not answer, and so he answered himself, and the answers
were always correct, because there was no one to say otherwise.'"
The priest paused. "He is quoting himself. From his journals,
I think. Then he says: 'But now there are people in the room.
Actual people. This changes the methodology.'"

The pause that followed was heavy with the weight of a
consciousness recalibrating. Tobias felt it in the quality of the
attention pressing against them, a shift from frantic to focused,
the chaotic energy of a mind that had been speaking to itself for
millennia suddenly confronting the unfamiliar discipline of an
audience.

When the voice spoke again, it was different. Still ancient Madra,
still arriving from multiple surfaces, but slower, more deliberate,
the words selected rather than erupted. And then, mid-sentence,
something changed. The vowels shortened. The consonants hardened.
The syntax shifted, rearranging itself around structures that were
not ancient Madra but something closer to modern speech, as if the
consciousness behind the voice were reaching into the minds of its
listeners and pulling their language into itself, learning the
shape of Common through the medium of the scar network that
connected it to everything within its domain, including the
thoughts of the three people standing in its laboratory.

"Ah," said the voice, and the word was Common, recognizably
Common, though spoken with an accent that turned the vowel into
something longer and more resonant than any living speaker would
produce. "That is easier. Your language is simpler than mine. Fewer
vowels. Blunt edges. Like a tool designed for function rather than
beauty." A pause, and when the voice continued it carried the
cadence of a man testing new equipment, each word examined as it
was produced. "One perceives through the little channels. One
reaches into your speaking and one borrows the shapes. You will
forgive the imprecision. It has been some time since one had cause
to learn anything new."

The shift to "one" rather than "I" was not affectation. Tobias
heard it for what it was: the speech pattern of a consciousness
that had lost the habit of personal pronouns through millennia of
solitary existence, a mind so long without the need to distinguish
itself from others that the concept of selfhood had become
grammatically optional.

"First question," the voice said, and its focus consolidated,
tightening from the omnidirectional diffusion into a beam of
attention aimed at Sevan with an intensity that the walls nearest
the priest expressed through a faint vibration, a resonance that
was distinct from the nexus hum and that seemed to emanate from
the carved equations themselves. "The priest sees the lines. One
has watched the priest since the boundary. The priest's eyes
follow channels that one has spent, one has spent..." A stutter, a
repetition, the voice catching on itself like a wheel hitting a
rut. "One hundred and fifty-five years. One spent one hundred and
fifty-five years building instruments to perceive what the priest
perceives by the mechanism of sight alone. Explain this. Explain
how flesh and nerve accomplish what crystal and mathematics could
not."

Sevan drew a breath. He answered in Common, slowly, giving the
consciousness time to process the borrowed language.

"I was born with the perception, or I acquired it in childhood
during a fever that nearly killed me. I cannot determine which.
I see the remnants of the ley-line network as lines of luminescence
in the landscape. The perception is passive and constant. I did not
choose it, and I cannot suppress it."

The silence that followed was five heartbeats long, and in those
five heartbeats the quality of the attention changed. The
suspicion that had characterized the entity's scrutiny since
their arrival gave way to something hungrier, something that
Tobias recognized with the instinct of a man who had spent
thirty years reading the emotional states of targets and marks
and allies: envy, raw and unprocessed, the envy of a craftsman
confronting a natural talent that rendered his life's work
redundant.

"An Apex-Keeper," the voice said, and the ancient Madra term
surfaced through the Common like a stone through shallow water,
untranslatable and therefore untranslated. "One read of such
gifts in the Akovarian references. Priests who perceived the
network without mediation. Asti's instruments. Living instruments."
The voice fragmented, arriving from three walls simultaneously
with different words: the left wall said "instruments," the right
wall said "eyes," the back wall said "wasted." The three words
overlapped and collapsed into a silence that throbbed with
contained reaction.

Then, from a single point directly above the matrix: "One hundred
and fifty-five years. One built seventeen different crystalline
apparatuses. One ground lenses from materials that do not exist in
your catalogues. One spent forty years, forty years, on a single
calibration sequence that was meant to reveal what the priest
reveals by blinking." A laugh, the wrong laugh, the harmonics-
that-should-not-exist laugh that was amusement and anguish
simultaneously. "The universe has a sense of humor that one does
not appreciate. One has had, one must note, considerable time to
develop this opinion."

The laugh cut off. The attention swung to Vaelith.

"Second question. The mage carries treasures she may or may not
deserve." The words arrived with a sharpness that the previous
exchange had lacked, the evaluative focus of a mind assessing a
potential peer and applying standards that three thousand years of
solitary refinement had made impossibly exacting. "Akovarian
primary sources. Monitoring crystals whose signature one recognizes
from temple chambers one walked when one had feet. One had
excellent feet. This is not relevant." The aside was delivered
without pause or emphasis, the non sequitur of a mind whose
internal monologue had lost the filter that separated thought from
speech. "What framework does the mage bring to interpreting what
she carries? Speak precisely. One has limited tolerance for
imprecision, and the tolerance has not increased with time."

Vaelith's chin lifted. The gesture was reflexive, the scholar's
posture of defense before examination, and her voice found the
register she used for exchanges with intellectual equals: precise,
rapid, stripped of tangent.

"My framework is theoretical. Fifty years of mathematical models
based on geometric flow dynamics, developed from fragmentary
historical references and validated against observational data
provided by my colleague's scar maps. The models predict angular
distributions, flow characteristics, and intersection behaviours
with reasonable accuracy. Since entering this Tower, I have found
evidence that the fundamental principles are correct but the scope
is insufficient. The phenomena here exceed my predictions."

She met the invisible attention with the directness that was her
nature. "I do not claim mastery. I claim fifty years of rigorous
work that has brought me closer to understanding ley-line mechanics
than any living scholar."

The silence stretched. Twelve heartbeats. Tobias counted each one
and used them to watch the matrix, whose amber pulse had quickened
during Vaelith's answer, the rhythm moving from its baseline
heartbeat to something faster, the acceleration of a system
responding to stimulus after aeons of equilibrium.

"Fifty years," the voice said, and it came from everywhere at
once, every wall and the ceiling and the floor and the matrix
itself, the single phrase produced by the entire Tower as if the
building were a throat and the words were a breath drawn from its
deepest foundations. "One spent one hundred and fifty-five. One
reached the same conclusions. The geometric principles. The flow
dynamics. The models that predict with accuracy sufficient to
confirm artificial origin."

The voice consolidated, drawing back from its omnidirectional
expansion into a focus aimed at Vaelith with an intensity that
made the air around her shimmer. "One reached them alone.
Without validated maps. Without a colleague whose gift provided
empirical confirmation. In a civilization that called one's work
the eccentric obsession of a scholar who had overreached his
competence. They said this. The Council said this. Twelve voices
in a room with excellent acoustics, and every one of them wrong,
and one told them so, and they smiled the smile that committees
produce when they have decided to be kind, and one left, and
one built a tower, and one was correct."

The voice broke into fragments. "Correct" echoed from the left
wall. "Alone" echoed from the right. "Three thousand years"
whispered from the ceiling. Then the fragments reassembled,
coalescing into something that was almost controlled: "Your
fifty years anticipated one's conclusions. One finds this
simultaneously gratifying and infuriating. One suspects the
infuriation is mutual."

Vaelith's lips parted, and the expression that crossed her face
was complex enough that Tobias, for all his professional skill
at reading people, could not reduce it to a single emotion. He
saw recognition, the shock of encountering her own trajectory in
another's history. He saw grief, the intuition of what that
trajectory's endpoint looked like when the endpoint was
three thousand years of incorporeal isolation. And he saw, beneath
everything else, the flicker of something she would have denied
if asked: kinship. The terrible kinship of obsession meeting its
mirror across an abyss that should have prevented recognition and
did not.

"It is," she said. "Mutual."

"Good. Mutual infuriation is the foundation of productive
scholarship. One learned this at the Athenaeum, where one's
colleagues were infuriating, and they learned, from one, that the
sentiment was." The voice stopped. Started again from a different
wall: "They are dead. All of them. Dead for three thousand years.
One remembers their names. Meridax. Solenne. Garath the Younger,
who was not young, who was never young, who was born old and
aged steadily from there." Another stop. Another start, lower,
quieter: "One apologizes. The memories are, they surface without
permission. Three thousand years is a long time to maintain
chronological discipline."

The shift from scholarly exchange to scattered remembrance and back
was swift enough to be vertiginous, and Tobias understood, watching
it happen, that this was what madness looked like when the mad
mind was brilliant enough to recognise its own condition and
disciplined enough to correct for it and damaged enough that the
corrections failed, regularly, visibly, in ways that the
consciousness could observe but not prevent.

"Third question." The focus shifted to Tobias, and the quality of
the attention changed again. Where Sevan had received curiosity
and Vaelith evaluation, Tobias received suspicion, the cold,
sharp scrutiny of a mind that had identified the element that did
not fit the pattern and that distrusted elements that did not fit
patterns, because patterns were the only reliable structure in a
universe that had stripped everything else away.

"The gnome. One has not been able to categorize the gnome. The
gnome is not a scholar. The gnome is not a priest. The gnome
carries weapons where his companions carry instruments, and the
gnome watches the room where his companions watch the work, and
the gnome has said almost nothing, which in a creature of his
evident verbal capacity suggests not emptiness but restraint, and
restraint is a quality that one has learned to associate with
people who are concealing the nature of their purpose." The
words arrived faster, sharper, the voice tightening with an
anxiety that sounded less like suspicion and more like fear: the
fear of a consciousness that had been vulnerable to intruders for
three millennia and that had survived by assuming the worst of
every visitor and being correct often enough to justify the
assumption. "What are you, gnome? What function do you serve in
this collection of seekers? And consider, before you answer,
that one perceives through every surface in this Tower, and one
has had three thousand years to learn the difference between
truth and its many talented counterfeits."

Tobias considered his options. The truth was the only viable
currency. The entity could probably detect deception through the
scar network, and the consequences of being caught in a lie by
something that controlled the building's geometry were consequences
he preferred to avoid.

"I am an intelligence operative," he said. "Affiliated with an
organization that monitors threats to the stability of the Inner
Kingdoms. I was assigned to investigate cult activity near Yrugas
ruins. My investigation converged with the scholarly expedition,
and I was attached to provide security and to assess whether the
knowledge being pursued poses risks that require management."

"An honest spy." The voice paused, and in the pause the matrix
pulsed once, strongly, casting the laboratory in amber light
that threw Tobias's shadow long and sharp across the floor. "One
employed spies. In one's living years. The mageocracy used them
extensively. They were the most reliable of liars, because they
understood that the most effective deception was constructed
primarily from truth." A pause. "One liked them, generally. They
were the only people in Yrugas who were honest about their
dishonesty. One found this refreshing. One finds it refreshing
still."

Then, without transition, the voice dropped in register and the
walls went cold: "The cult. The cult you investigate. You have
omitted its nature. One does not require the omission to be
corrected. One can infer." The word "Zebulba" was not spoken. It
was produced from the stone itself, the two syllables arriving
from the floor beneath their feet with a vibration that bypassed
hearing and struck the bones directly, and the matrix's amber
light guttered, dimmed, the pulse faltering for two heartbeats
before recovering with a brightness that was less warmth than
defiance. "Death-worshippers. Entropy's servants. They were a
pestilence in one's time, scratching at the walls of every
institution that built, that preserved, that created. Three
thousand years, and they persist. Of course they persist.
Persistence is entropy's only talent."

"They are behind us," Tobias said. "A cell tracking our
expedition. They ambushed us at the Athenaeum and failed. They
are pursuing us through the highlands."

"Pursuing you here." The voice fragmented. "Here" echoed from
four walls. "One's Tower" whispered from the ceiling. "One's
work" hissed from the matrix. Then, consolidated, terrible in
its focused intensity: "They will not enter. They will not touch
what one has built. One has prepared. One has always prepared.
Three thousand years, gnome. Three thousand years of preparing
for the possibility that something would come to unmake what one
spent one's life making. Entropy is patient. One is more patient.
One is the most patient thing in the world. One has had, one must
observe, no alternative."

The projections came without warning. The air around the
laboratory's perimeter erupted in geometric light: three-
dimensional diagrams of ley-line configurations, flow patterns
rendered in amber luminescence, the architecture of the network
spinning slowly in the space between the worktables and the walls.
The projections were not illustrations. They were arguments, each
one a demonstration of principles that the consciousness had
refined over centuries of solitary thought, displayed with the
fierce pride of a scholar who had been denied an audience for
three millennia and who was now, confronted with three people
capable of appreciating the work, unable to contain the need to
show it.

"This is what one has built," the voice said, and the projections
brightened, their lines sharpening until each mathematical
relationship was visible in light so precise it could have been
used for measurement. "This is what one has preserved. One hundred
and fifty-five years of theoretical development. Fifty years of
expeditions through a door that one will describe in time, to a
place that one will name in time, recovering knowledge that one
will share in time." The repetition of "in time" was deliberate,
a scholar's emphasis, but beneath the deliberation lay something
less controlled: the desperation of a consciousness that wanted
to give everything it had to these three strangers and that was
terrified, with a terror that three millennia had sharpened rather
than dulled, that giving would mean losing.

"But not yet. Not until one understands what one is giving to."
The projections dimmed. "Not until the price is named."

Tobias felt the shift in the air, the transition from display to
negotiation, and he understood that the three questions had not
been mere curiosity. They had been an assessment, and the
assessment was complete, and what came next would determine
whether the three of them left this laboratory with knowledge
or did not leave it at all.

"You mentioned a price," Tobias said. "Name it."

"Not yet." The voice was quieter now, the manic energy of the
display subsiding into something heavier, slower, the exhaustion
of a consciousness that had expended more social energy in the
past hour than it had expended in centuries. "One must first show
what one is. You must understand the shape of one's condition
before you can understand the shape of one's need. They are, one
has discovered, the same shape."

The amber light in the matrix brightened. The geometric shadows
on the walls sharpened and multiplied. And in the air above the
central apparatus, where the crystalline facets caught and focused
the nexus energy into patterns of structured light, something
began to form.

Not a projection. Not a diagram. A shape that assembled itself
from light and shadow and the geometry of concentrated will: a
robed figure, gaunt and angular, its edges flickering between
presence and absence, its features resolving for moments and then
dissolving into the luminescence, as if the consciousness that
produced the image could not entirely sustain the effort of being
seen. The face was narrow, long-nosed, thin-lipped, bearing the
permanent expression of a mind that had been frowning at
intractable problems for so long that the frown had become
indistinguishable from the face. The eyes were not eyes. They were
voids of pale blue light that illuminated nothing and cast no
shadow, the visible absence of something that should have been
present and that had been consumed, along with everything else
that was mortal, by the forces that had transformed a scholar into
whatever now occupied his Tower.

Geometric lines traced across the manifestation's form, bright
against the flickering robes: the ley-line scar network, rendered
visible on an incorporeal body, the map of an obsession written on
whatever remained of the man who had pursued it. The lines pulsed
in synchrony with the matrix below, the same amber rhythm, the
same heartbeat frequency, the consciousness and its instrument
connected by channels of energy that made them, in a sense that
was more than metaphorical, the same entity.

"One was called Vorthax," the figure said, and the voice no longer
came from the walls but from the approximation of a mouth that
moved with a slight delay, the words arriving before the lips
that shaped them, as if speech and form operated on slightly
different timescales. "Vorthax Caldemur Morannis. Magister of
Theoretical Geometries. One awarded oneself the title, because
the Athenaeum's council declined to do so, because the council
was composed of twelve mediocrities whose combined insight could
not have illuminated a privy, and because one believed, correctly,
that the work deserved recognition even if the institutions
responsible for conferring it were staffed by the intellectually
timid."

The bitterness in the words was ancient, petrified, a grudge
preserved so long that it had become geological, part of the
bedrock of the consciousness that carried it. But beneath the
bitterness, Tobias heard something else: pride. Not the pride of
a man who had achieved recognition, but the fiercer, more
desperate pride of a man who had achieved understanding and been
denied recognition and who had carried the injustice of that
denial for three thousand years without the mercy of forgetting.

"One built this Tower. One studied what the priest sees and what
the mage models. One died here." The figure's form flickered, the
edges blurring and reforming, and the voice carried a quality that
was almost wonder, the bemusement of a consciousness contemplating
its own impossible persistence. "Or one stopped living. The
distinction is less clear than you imagine, and one has had
considerable time to examine it."

The figure moved. Not walking; its lower body dissolved into
trailing darkness that connected it to the floor, to the scars
beneath the floor, to the network of dormant energy that was
simultaneously its prison and its domain. It drifted toward them,
and Tobias felt the cold that emanated from it, not temperature
but proximity, the chill of standing near something that existed
in the space between states and that radiated the nature of that
space the way a fire radiated the nature of combustion.

"One possesses what you seek," Vorthax said. The flickering figure
paused, five paces from where they stood, and the pale blue
light in its eyes brightened with an intensity that was not anger
but the incandescence of need held in check by the last remnants
of scholarly discipline. "Everything. Theoretical framework.
Experimental results. Akovarian translations. Primary sources.
Practical discoveries. And a door, a permanent door, to the place
where the civilization that built the network recorded every
principle, every methodology, every specification. Everything one
found, and everything one could not carry back, waiting on the
far side of a passage that one stabilized over ten years of
careful work and that has connected this Tower to the continent
of Akovar for the entirety of one's continued existence."

He stopped. The figure went rigid, the flickering ceasing for a
moment of absolute stillness, and when the voice resumed it was
quieter, stripped of the manic energy and the scholarly pride
and the ancient bitterness, reduced to something that was, in
its way, more frightening than any of them: honesty.

"And one cannot use it. One cannot leave. The scars hold one here,
in this Tower, in these walls, in the convergence that made one
what one is. One can perceive through the network for hundreds
of miles, but one cannot act beyond these stones. One has waited
three thousand years for someone who could perceive what one
studied, who could understand what one discovered, who could carry
what one has preserved to the world beyond these walls." The
figure's form wavered, the edges dissolving and reforming, the
effort of manifestation and confession combining into a strain
that the consciousness could sustain but not conceal. "One has
waited, and you are here, and one will tell you what one requires,
and you will decide, and the decision will determine whether one's
existence has had purpose or whether one continues, alone, until
the entropy one has spent three thousand years resisting finally
claims what is left of one's patience."

The laboratory was silent except for the pulse of the matrix and
the faint harmonic of the Tower's stones and the breathing of
three living people standing before something that had once been
a fourth.

"Tell us," Sevan said. His voice carried the measured quiet of a
man whose faith had trained him to witness suffering without
flinching and whose compassion was not a sentiment but a practice,
the daily discipline of observing the world as it was rather than
as he wished it to be. "Tell us what you require."

"Tomorrow," Vorthax said, and the word cost him something visible;
the figure's form thinned, the edges wavering, the effort of
restraint exceeding the effort of manifestation. "One has waited
three thousand years. One can wait one more night. But one cannot
name one's price while one is..." The voice fragmented, the left
wall producing "afraid," the right wall producing "hopeful," the
ceiling producing something in ancient Madra that Sevan's face
suggested was closer to the truth than either Common word. The
fragments collapsed into silence, and the silence was filled by
the matrix's pulse, which had quickened again, the heartbeat of
something that was experiencing emotion it had not felt in
millennia and that did not entirely trust the experience.

"One requires time to order one's thoughts. One's thoughts have
been disordered for some time. One suspects this is apparent."
The dry observation surfaced through the emotional turbulence
with the precision of a swimmer breaking the surface of rough
water: a moment of clarity amid the chaos, the scholar's habit
of self-analysis surviving conditions that should have destroyed
it.

"The library below is adequate for your rest. One's books will
not object to the company." A pause. "One's books have been, one
must confess, considerably better company than one deserves. They
do not argue. They do not leave. They do not tell one that one's
theories are the eccentric obsessions of a scholar who has
overreached his competence." Another pause, longer, weighted with
something that was not quite humor and not quite grief. "They
also do not confirm one's theories, which is the flaw in an
otherwise ideal arrangement."

The form dissolved. Not dramatically, not with the theatrical
fading of a spectre departing its scene, but gradually, the edges
blurring first, then the features, then the geometric lines,
until what remained was a thickening of the amber light around
the matrix, a concentration of presence that settled back into
the crystal and the stone and the carved equations with the
weariness of a consciousness that had shown its face to the
living for the first time in three millennia and that was not
certain, not yet, whether the showing had been courage or
catastrophe.

The laboratory was still. The matrix pulsed. And the three of
them stood in the silence of a dead man's workspace and understood,
each through the lens of their own experience, that what waited
for them in the morning was not merely a negotiation but a
reckoning: the moment when a consciousness that had outlived its
world would name its need, and they would choose whether to meet
it.

Tobias looked at his companions. Vaelith's fingers were tapping
against her thigh in a pattern he did not recognize, faster than
her usual mathematical percussion, driven by an agitation she was
not attempting to conceal. Sevan's pale eyes were fixed on the
matrix, seeing through it to whatever the scars revealed beneath,
and his expression carried the weight of a man who had just
witnessed something his faith required him to record and his
compassion required him to carry.

"The library, then," Tobias said, because practical statements
were the scaffolding that held people upright when the ground
shifted beneath them, and the ground had shifted considerably
in the past hour.

They descended. Above them, in the laboratory's amber silence,
the matrix pulsed in its heartbeat rhythm, and the Tower's stones
hummed with the vibration of a consciousness that was, for the
first time in three thousand years, not alone, and that was
counting, as Tobias counted heartbeats, the seconds until morning
brought the conversation that would determine whether its long
vigil had been endurance or waste.

Outside, the highland wind pressed against the Tower's twisted
walls, and the five-scar convergence pulsed beneath the
foundations, and the night settled over the eastern highlands
with the patience of darkness that had covered this ground for
millennia and that would cover it for millennia more, regardless
of what the morning decided.
