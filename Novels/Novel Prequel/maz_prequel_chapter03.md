# Chapter Three
## The Mask Beneath the Smile

The Misty Dawn Inn smelled of woodsmoke and barley soup and the particular
wet-wool warmth that accumulated in any tavern where farmers gathered after
a day of autumn rain. It was not an unpleasant smell. Tobias Windrake had
spent enough years in enough taverns to have developed a connoisseur's
appreciation for the subtle variations: the sharp tang of spilled ale that
marked a prosperous house, the sour undertone of damp rushes that betrayed a
lazy proprietor, the faint sweetness of beeswax that spoke of a keeper who
took pride in surfaces. The Misty Dawn fell comfortably in the middle of
this taxonomy, a clean establishment with honest food and a proprietor who
watered her ale less than most.

He sat at a corner table with his back to the wall and his cittern case
propped against his knee, nursing a cup of cider he had been making last for
the better part of an hour. From this position he could observe the inn's
common room in its entirety: the bar where Tanya, the Laveri innkeeper,
polished tankards with the focused efficiency of a woman who did not tolerate
idle hands; the hearth where a fire crackled against the evening chill,
throwing orange light across the faces of a dozen farmers who had gathered to
share the day's complaints; the door, which opened onto the village square of
Littlebridge and beyond it the darkening tree line of the Misty Forest.

His eyes moved constantly, cataloging details with the automatic precision of
long practice. The two farmers at the bar were regulars, their postures loose,
their voices carrying the unhurried rhythms of men who had known each other
for decades. The woman by the fire was new, a Varkonan trader he had seen
arrive that afternoon with a small cart of exotic goods. The old man in the
corner opposite was Joseph, the village priest of Tiovin, who nursed his own
cup of cider with the patient contentment of a man who had made his peace
with the world's imperfections and wished only to sit near the fire while it
continued to turn.

Tobias noted all of this, filed it away, and smiled at no one in particular
with the easy, open expression of a gnomish entertainer who found the world
endlessly delightful and wished only to share in its pleasures.

The smile was, like most things about Tobias Windrake, a carefully maintained
fiction.

"Another song, master gnome?" Tanya called from behind the bar, not looking
up from her tankard. She had a voice that carried, low and direct, the voice
of a woman who had run an inn alone for long enough that volume and honesty
had become the same thing. "The boys are getting restless."

"The boys are always restless, good lady," Tobias replied, pitching his own
voice to carry the warmth and easy humor of a man who lived for exactly
these moments. He was good at this. He had been good at it for decades, the
cheerful troubadour whose songs brightened long evenings and whose presence
was welcomed at every hearth. It was a comfortable mask, worn smooth by long
use, and it fit him so well that he sometimes wondered if there was still a
face beneath it. "But as it happens, I have just the remedy. A ballad from
the northern hills, guaranteed to soothe restless farmers and fractious
innkeepers alike."

"I am not fractious," Tanya said, in a tone that suggested she was, in fact,
somewhat fractious, and that anyone who commented further on the subject
would find their cider privileges revoked.

Tobias grinned, a genuine expression this time, because Tanya was the sort of
person he liked instinctively: direct, protective of her people, and entirely
unimpressed by charm. He lifted his cittern from its case with the practiced
ease of a man who had been handling the instrument for most of his eighty-nine
years. The wood was dark with decades of oil and handling, the neck worn
smooth where his fingers had pressed countless notes, and the sound it
produced when he struck the first chord was rich and warm and slightly
melancholy, the voice of an instrument that had absorbed a lifetime of songs
and remembered them all.

He played a harvest ballad, something old and familiar, the kind of tune
that farmers' grandmothers had hummed over bread dough and that required no
thought to enjoy. His voice was a pleasant baritone, not remarkable but
well-controlled, carrying the melody with the confidence of a performer who
understood that technique mattered less than sincerity, or at least the
convincing appearance of sincerity. His copper eyes moved across the room as
he sang, his bushy eyebrows rising and falling with the melody's
inflections, his expression projecting the simple contentment of a man who
wanted nothing more from life than a warm fire, a willing audience, and
another cup of cider.

Behind the expression, his mind was working.

He had arrived in Littlebridge four days ago, traveling under his standard
cover as a chronicler of local folklore. The cover was old and comfortable,
tested over thirty years of service to the Emerald Shields, and it gave him
everything he needed: a reason to visit small villages, a reason to ask
questions, a reason to talk to people for hours without arousing suspicion.
Everyone had a story. Everyone wanted to tell it. And a small gnomish fellow
with a friendly smile and a ready instrument was exactly the sort of person
to whom stories were told freely, without caution, without the guarded
reserve that a human stranger might provoke.

The Shields had sent him to Littlebridge because people had been
disappearing.

Not many. Not dramatically. Not in the way that provoked alarm or attracted
official attention. Three people in the past four months, from villages
scattered across the northern edge of the Misty Forest: a woodcutter from
Thornham who had not returned from his rounds, a herbalist from Millbrook
whose cottage was found empty one morning with her supper still on the table,
and a young farmer from the outskirts of Littlebridge itself whose wife had
reported him missing after he failed to return from checking his sheep in the
upper pastures.

Three disappearances in four months across a region of scattered settlements.
Not enough for a pattern that local authorities would notice. Enough for the
Emerald Shields, whose business was noticing patterns that local authorities
did not.

Tobias had been investigating for six months before receiving the Littlebridge
assignment. The disappearances were the latest thread in a larger tapestry
that he had been weaving together from fragments gathered across Beneshire:
reports of strange symbols carved into trees near isolated farmsteads,
whispered rumors of gatherings in forest clearings at night, and, most
recently, a name spoken in fear by a tavern keeper in Cabrook who had found
one of the symbols scratched into the wood of his cellar door. The name was
Zebulba.

The Entropic Void. The Old God of death and entropy, whose worship was
outlawed throughout the Federation and whose cultists, the Void-Touched,
operated in cells so secretive that even the Shields struggled to track them.
Zebulba's followers did not seek converts through persuasion or promise. They
sought to accelerate the natural process of decay, to hasten the universe's
slide toward the cold and silent end that they believed was both inevitable
and desirable.

Tobias had encountered Zebulban activity twice before in his career. Both
times the cultists had been small, desperate groups, more pathetic than
dangerous, clinging to nihilistic philosophy as a justification for petty
cruelty. This felt different. The symbols were consistent across locations
separated by dozens of miles, suggesting coordination. The disappearances
followed a pattern that implied selection rather than opportunism. And the
locations, when Tobias plotted them on the small map he kept folded in the
inner pocket of his vest, clustered around areas that shared a single common
feature.

They were all near sites of ancient ruins. Yrugas ruins, to be specific.
The crumbling remnants of the mageocracy that had dominated the Inner
Kingdoms over three thousand years ago, whose fall had created the Hollow
Sepulcher and whose legacy still poisoned the land in places where the
stones remembered what had been done upon them.

The ballad ended. The farmers applauded with the comfortable enthusiasm of
men who had enjoyed the music without really listening to it. Tobias
accepted their appreciation with a flourishing bow that brought his
forehead nearly to his knees, a piece of physical comedy that always drew
laughter from human audiences and that he performed so naturally that no one
ever noticed how it allowed him to scan the room from an inverted
perspective, checking for faces that watched too carefully or hands that
moved toward concealment.

Nothing. The room held only farmers, an innkeeper, a Varkonan trader, and
an elderly priest. Tobias straightened, tucked his cittern back into its
case, and made his way to the bar with the easy stride of a man in search
of a refill.

"Tanya, good lady, your establishment remains the finest in northern
Beneshire. I shall compose an ode to your cider and perform it at every
crossroads between here and Wyebluff."

"You will do no such thing." Tanya refilled his cup without being asked,
which Tobias noted as a sign of approval more meaningful than any verbal
compliment. "I do not need every wandering layabout in the kingdom showing
up at my door expecting free drinks and a fireside."

"You wound me, good lady. I am not a wandering layabout. I am a traveling
chronicler of folklore and local tradition, a distinction of considerable
professional significance."

"You are a gnome with a lute who asks too many questions." Tanya set the cup
before him with a definitive thud. "But you pay your tab and you do not start
fights, which places you ahead of most of my custom."

"High praise indeed." Tobias took the cup and sipped. The cider was good,
sharp and dry, with the particular bite of late-autumn apples. "Speaking of
questions, good lady, I wonder if I might impose upon your knowledge of the
area. In my capacity as a chronicler, naturally."

Tanya's expression did not change, which was itself informative. Most people
shifted, however slightly, when a conversation turned from pleasantry to
inquiry. Tanya did not. She was either genuinely unconcerned or skilled at
concealment, and Tobias's professional assessment favored the former. Tanya
was not a woman who concealed things. She was a woman who simply did not
consider other people's curiosity to be her problem.

"Ask."

"I have been collecting accounts of the old places. The ruins in the forest,
the standing stones, the sites where the ancients built before the War. Local
legends, folk memories, that sort of thing. Marvelous material for a
chronicle." He kept his tone light, the inquiry dressed in the harmless
clothing of academic curiosity. "Several of the farmers have mentioned a
particular site north of here, deeper in the Misty Forest. Ruins of some
kind, though the descriptions vary. One fellow called it a tower. Another
called it a circle of stones. A third insisted it was a cave, though he
admitted he had never actually seen it himself."

Tanya polished a tankard with focused precision. "There are ruins all through
the forest. Most are nothing. Fallen walls. Old foundations. Not worth the
walk."

"And the one north of here? The one the farmers cannot agree upon?"

"That one is worth even less than the walk." Tanya set the tankard down and
met his eyes with the level directness that he had come to associate with her.
"It is old. It is in a part of the forest that sensible people do not visit.
And in the past year, anyone who has gone near it has come back unsettled, or
has not come back at all."

The common room's noise filled the silence between them: the crack of the
fire, the murmur of conversation, the scrape of a chair leg against the
wooden floor. Tobias held Tanya's gaze and let the pause extend, because
pauses told more than questions when the person across from you was ready to
speak.

"Aldric Fenn," Tanya said. The name came out flat, without inflection, the
way people spoke the names of the dead. "The young farmer. His wife Marta
has not stopped asking after him since he went missing. He was checking the
upper pastures, aye, but the upper pastures are south of here. Not north.
And Marta found his dog on the north road, three miles into the forest,
barking at nothing. The dog will not go further. Will not even face that
direction now."

Tobias drank his cider slowly. "Has anyone reported this to the authorities?"

"The authorities." Tanya's mouth tightened around the word. "Regent Bakerson
sent two of his militia to look. They walked the north road for an hour,
found nothing, and came back to report that Aldric Fenn had probably gotten
drunk and fallen into a ravine. The Regent accepted this explanation with the
enthusiasm of a man who does not wish to spend resources on problems he
cannot solve." She resumed polishing. "I have sent word to Henryk. The ranger.
He is three days out, tracking something else, but he will come when he can."

"A ranger." Tobias nodded. "That is wise."

"I am not a fool, master gnome, whatever else you may think of me."

"I think," Tobias said, and meant it, "that Littlebridge is fortunate to
have you."

Tanya looked at him for a long moment, and in that look Tobias saw the quiet
assessment of a woman who had spent years reading the people who passed
through her inn, sorting the honest from the dishonest, the dangerous from
the harmless, the sincere from the performing. He held her gaze and let her
look, because the best way to pass such an inspection was not to resist it
but to present a version of yourself that was close enough to the truth that
the seams did not show.

"You are more than a chronicler," she said. It was not a question.

"I am a chronicler who asks too many questions. You said so yourself."

The ghost of a smile crossed her face, there and gone like a shadow on water.
"Keep your ears open, master gnome. And keep your questions quiet. Whatever
is in that forest, it has made people afraid, and afraid people do foolish
things."

"Keep your ears open." Tobias raised his cup in a small salute, the
gnomish blessing that sounded like casual farewell and carried, for those
who understood it, considerably more weight. "Sound advice, good lady. I
intend to follow it."

---

Later that night, in the small room above the common hall that Tanya rented
to travelers, Tobias Windrake sat on the edge of his bed with his boots
still on and his cittern case open beside him, and considered the shape of
what he was seeing.

The room was modest: a narrow bed, a washstand, a chair, a window that
looked out over the village square toward the dark mass of the Misty Forest.
The moon was half-full, its light silvering the rooftops and throwing the
forest into sharp relief against the sky. Somewhere in the distance, an owl
called. Closer, the muffled sounds of the inn settling for the night crept
through the floorboards: Tanya banking the fire, the last farmers making
their way home, the creak of old timber adjusting to the cold.

Tobias reached into his vest, past the outer pockets that held coins and a
tin whistle, past the middle pockets that held his pan pipes and a set of
lock picks concealed in a leather roll, and into the innermost pocket where
he kept the things that mattered. He withdrew a folded sheet of paper, a
stub of charcoal, and a small notebook bound in oilskin.

The notebook was not the one he showed to villagers when he played the part
of the chronicler. That notebook was larger, leather-bound, filled with the
folklore and local histories he genuinely collected because the cover worked
best when it was mostly true. This notebook was smaller, its pages covered
in a personal shorthand that would mean nothing to anyone who found it, and
it contained the actual product of six months of investigation.

He unfolded the paper on the bed. It was his map, hand-drawn and annotated
over the course of his assignment, showing the northern reaches of Beneshire
from Cabrook to the Misty Forest. Small marks indicated locations of
interest: circles for disappearances, triangles for cult symbols, squares
for Yrugas ruins. Lines connected related events, and notes in his cramped
shorthand recorded dates, witness accounts, and his own assessments.

Three new marks needed adding. A circle for Aldric Fenn, north of
Littlebridge. A triangle for the symbol he had found yesterday, carved into
a beech tree half a mile into the Misty Forest on the north road, where the
cuts were fresh enough that the exposed wood had not yet darkened. He had
stood before that symbol for longer than professional necessity required,
studying the crude rendering of a skull consumed by black flames, and he had
felt something beyond the intellectual recognition of a Zebulban marker.
The air near the carving had carried a chill that did not match the
surrounding temperature, a localized cold that clung to the freshly cut
wood like breath on a winter mirror. The exposed grain of the beech had
darkened at the symbol's edges in a pattern that did not follow the natural
oxidation of cut wood, blackening inward from the carved lines as if the
symbol were a wound and the wood around it was slowly dying. He had not
touched it. Thirty years of professional instinct had kept his hands at his
sides, and afterwards, walking back to the village, he had been unable to
shake the impression that the cold had followed him for several paces before
dissipating, like something reluctantly letting go.

He was not a mage. He possessed no trained sensitivity to magical phenomena.
But he had spent three decades operating in a world where magic was real and
where the residue of dark workings left traces that even untrained senses
could register if those senses belonged to a person who had learned to pay
attention to the small wrongness that most people's minds smoothed over
and forgot. The symbol on the beech tree was not merely a mark. It had been
consecrated, charged with something that turned the autumn air cold and the
living wood dark, and whatever that something was, it was newer and more
deliberate than the ancient ruins it pointed toward.

And a square for the ruins that the farmers could not agree upon, the site
that Tanya said made people afraid.

He made the marks with his charcoal and studied the result.

The pattern was tightening. Over six months, the cult activity had drifted
steadily northward and eastward, from the lowland farms near Cabrook toward
the highlands where the Misty Forest thinned and the terrain grew rocky and
sparse. The disappearances tracked the same drift. And the Yrugas ruins,
scattered across the region like broken teeth in a ancient jaw, formed a
loose chain that the cult's movement seemed to follow.

They were searching for something. Not randomly, not opportunistically, but
systematically, moving from site to site with a purpose that Tobias could
sense but not yet define. The Yrugas ruins were the key. Whatever the
Zebulbans wanted, it was connected to the ruins, to the remnants of the
mageocracy that had practiced death magic with such fervor that the land
itself had torn open and created the Hollow Sepulcher.

But what could Zebulban cultists want with three-thousand-year-old academic
ruins? The major Yrugas sites, the ones that held genuine power or danger,
were well-known and well-guarded. Fort Stoneshaper and the Circle of the
Eastern Watch existed precisely to monitor the supernatural threats that
lingered near such places. The ruins in this region were minor: collapsed
towers, overgrown foundations, the remnants of facilities that had served the
mageocracy's administrative and scholarly apparatus rather than its military
or magical might.

Tobias stared at his map and felt the familiar itch of a pattern that was
almost visible, a shape pressing against the surface of the evidence like a
face behind a curtain.

He opened his oilskin notebook and reviewed his most recent entries. Three
days ago, in the village of Millbrook, he had shared drinks with a
traveling merchant who had mentioned, in the offhand way that people shared
information they did not realize was significant, that a group of scholars
from Wyebluff had been making inquiries about the same ruins that the cult
seemed interested in. The merchant had described them as academics, polite
and bookish, asking about local legends and ancient sites with the earnest
enthusiasm of people who studied the past for its own sake.

Two days ago, in a tavern outside Thaewood, Tobias had overheard a
conversation between two men he had identified as minor agents of House
Ravenwood, the Beneshire noble house that specialized in trade and
information. They had been discussing, in carefully oblique terms, a
"research expedition" being organized from Wyebluff with the support of
"certain scholarly institutions." The agents seemed unsure whether to be
interested or bored. They had settled on cautiously interested, which
suggested that someone with authority had told them to pay attention.

And now, in Littlebridge, Tobias had found fresh cult symbols on the same
road that led to ruins the locals feared, in a village where a man had
gone missing in a direction that made no sense unless he had been drawn or
taken toward something in the forest.

Scholars searching ruins. Cultists searching ruins. The same ruins, in the
same region, at the same time.

Tobias did not believe in coincidence. Coincidence was what people called
patterns they had not yet understood, and Tobias had spent thirty years
learning to understand patterns. The scholars and the cultists were
following the same trail, though whether they knew about each other was a
question he could not yet answer.

He closed the notebook and sat for a long moment in the moonlit room,
listening to the sounds of the village settling into sleep. The owl called
again, closer now, and the wind shifted to carry the scent of pine and
wet earth from the forest. Somewhere north of here, in the ruins that
frightened dogs and swallowed farmers, something waited that was important
enough for both scholars and death-worshippers to seek it.

The quiet bell rings longest. The gnomish proverb surfaced in his mind with
the wry comfort of an old friend offering counsel. Patience. Information.
Wait for the shape to clarify before you act.

He would send a report to the Shields in the morning, encoded in the
standard cipher and entrusted to the courier network that operated through
selected innkeepers along the main roads. He would describe the convergence
of cult and scholarly activity. He would recommend that the Shields
investigate the expedition from Wyebluff, identify its organizers and
sponsors, and determine what exactly these academics were looking for in the
ruins of a three-thousand-year-old mageocracy.

And he would stay in Littlebridge, because the cultists were close, because
a man was missing, and because Tobias Windrake, for all the masks he wore
and the lies he told and the weariness that settled deeper into his bones
with each passing year, had not yet lost the stubborn conviction that
protecting people mattered more than the personal cost of protection.

He put away his map and his notebook. He pulled off his boots and set them
beside the bed, within easy reach, because a man in his profession learned
early that the difference between life and death could be measured in the
time it took to find one's shoes. He laid his cittern case on the chair
where he could reach it without rising, because the case contained more than
an instrument, though the instrument was the part he would miss most if
everything went wrong.

Then he lay back on the narrow bed and stared at the ceiling and tried, as
he did every night, not to wonder how much of the cheerful gnomish
entertainer was performance and how much was the man he had once been before
thirty years of secrets had worn the distinction smooth.

The question had no answer. Like most of the important questions in his
life, it simply hung in the darkness above him, patient as the moon,
unanswerable as the owl's call, waiting for a morning that might bring
clarity or might bring only another day of wearing the comfortable,
well-fitted, quietly suffocating mask that was, after three decades, the
closest thing he had to a face.

Sleep came slowly. When it arrived, he dreamed of music, and in the dream
the music was honest, and in the dream that was enough.
