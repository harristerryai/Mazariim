# Chapter Twenty-Two

## What the Dust Remembers

Tobias had learned, in thirty years of work that required him to
notice what others overlooked, that danger announced itself
through absence before it announced itself through presence. The
missing sound. The absent footprint. The detail that should exist
and did not, or that should not exist and did. His training had
refined this instinct into methodology, a systematic attention to
the spaces between things that most observers filled with
assumption, and the methodology had kept him alive through
situations that would have killed a man who trusted only what was
obvious.

The Engineering Core's southern corridors were wrong.

He had begun his exploration of the Temple's unmapped sections
while Sevan and Vaelith continued their documentation in the Flow
Chamber and the Archive Vault, the scholars working with the
focused intensity of people who understood that the knowledge
surrounding them was both priceless and fragile and that the time
available to record it was neither infinite nor predictable. His
purpose was practical: establish the full extent of their
operational environment, identify alternative routes to the portal
terminus in case the primary corridor became impassable, and
confirm that the Engineering Core contained no threats beyond the
structural and environmental hazards they had already assessed.

The northern corridors, where the Flow Chamber and Archive Vault
and Monitoring Station occupied their functional positions along
the main axis, were consistent with what Vorthax had described
and what their own exploration had confirmed. Preserved,
functional, their enchantments maintaining the atmosphere and
structural integrity that seven millennia of isolation had not
degraded beyond the limits of their design. The southern
corridors, branching from the central axis in the direction
opposite the primary functional chambers, should have been the
same. The preservation enchantments extended throughout the
Engineering Core. The construction methods were uniform. There
was no architectural reason for the southern section to differ
from the northern.

But the dust was wrong.

The Engineering Core's preserved corridors maintained a thin,
uniform layer of dust on their floors, the accumulation of
millennia settling through air that the enchantments kept
breathable but did not filter to perfection. In the northern
corridors, this dust was undisturbed except where the expedition's
own footprints marked their passages: boot prints of three
different sizes, the round impression of Sevan's staff, the
parallel lines of dragged equipment. The dust told a simple story.
No one had walked here for a very long time, and then three
people had arrived.

The southern corridors told a different story.

Tobias noticed it thirty feet past the junction where the southern
branch diverged from the main axis. The dust was present, the
same thin uniform layer, but its surface carried variations that
the northern corridors did not exhibit. Not footprints. Not the
clear evidence of recent passage. Something subtler: areas where
the dust was fractionally thinner than its surroundings, as if it
had been disturbed and had resettled, the original disturbance
old enough for new accumulation to partially conceal it but not
old enough for the concealment to be complete.

He knelt. The bioluminescent light was sufficient for navigation
but not for the kind of examination he needed, and he produced a
candle from his pack and lit it with a match, the small yellow
flame providing the directional illumination that the fungi's
diffuse blue-green could not. In the candle's light, the dust
variations became legible.

Something had moved through this corridor. Not recently, not
within years, but within a timeframe that the dust could record:
decades, perhaps. A century at most. The disturbance pattern was
linear, following the corridor's centre, and the width suggested
a single entity of moderate size, neither the broad passage of a
large creature nor the narrow track of something small. The
pattern was consistent for fifteen feet, then faded where the
dust had had sufficient time to resettle to uniformity.

Tobias followed the corridor south.

The evidence accumulated. A second section of disturbed dust,
forty feet further on, this one more pronounced, the thinning
more visible, as if the entity had paused here or moved more
slowly. A mark on the wall at shoulder height, not a carving or
an inscription but a scuff, the kind of mark produced by contact
between a hard surface and something carried or worn, the stone's
patina disrupted in a line four inches long that the millennia of
mineral accumulation on the surrounding wall made conspicuous by
contrast. The scuff was clean-edged, not weathered, not softened
by the slow chemistry of stone and air that would have blurred it
over centuries. Decades old. Perhaps less.

A chamber opened to the left. Tobias entered it with the candle
held low, his other hand near his knife, the automatic positioning
of a man whose body prepared for threats that his mind had not yet
identified. The chamber was small, twenty feet square, its
shelves holding sealed containers of a type he had seen in the
Archive Vault: metal canisters with geometric seals, designed for
long-term preservation of materials that required protection
beyond what the ambient enchantments provided.

The shelves were not full. Gaps existed in the orderly rows, spaces
where canisters had been removed, the dust on the shelf surfaces
showing the rectangular outlines of objects that had sat there
long enough to shield the stone beneath from accumulation and that
had then been taken, leaving clean geometric shadows on surfaces
that the surrounding dust had not yet reclaimed.

Three canisters missing from the upper shelf. Two from the lower.
The gaps were not adjacent, suggesting selective removal rather
than casual theft, someone who had known what they were looking
for and had taken specific items rather than sweeping the shelves
indiscriminately.

Tobias counted the remaining canisters. He examined the geometric
shadows. He measured the dust depth on the shelf surfaces with
the tip of his knife, comparing the accumulation inside the
shadows to the accumulation outside them, the differential
providing a rough estimate of how long the removed objects had
been absent.

The mathematics was imprecise. Dust accumulation rates in a
magically preserved environment did not follow the patterns he
understood from natural settings. But the differential was
measurable, which meant the removal was not ancient. Not
millennia. Not centuries, plural. The dust had had time to begin
filling the shadows but not time to complete the process.

Within the last two hundred years. Possibly within the last
hundred.

He left the chamber and continued south. The corridor curved,
following the Engineering Core's circular layout, and as it
curved it passed two more chambers with similar evidence: gaps
on shelves, dust disturbances, the quiet testimony of a place
that had been visited by someone who came, took specific things,
and left.

Not Vorthax. The wraith's expeditions had ended approximately
3,500 years ago when his transformation bound him to the Tower's
nexus. Whatever had visited the Temple's southern corridors had
done so millennia after the Geometrist's last expedition, and
the selective nature of the removal suggested intelligence,
purpose, knowledge of what the canisters contained and which
ones were worth taking.

Tobias stood in the curved corridor and let his training process
the evidence without the interference of speculation. The facts:
something intelligent had accessed the Engineering Core through
a route that was not the portal. It had navigated to specific
storage chambers. It had removed specific materials. It had done
this within the last one to two hundred years. And it had left
evidence that was subtle enough to escape casual observation but
visible to someone trained to look for what was not where it
should be.

The route of access was the critical unknown. The portal connected
the Tower to the Engineering Core's deepest level, and the portal
was Vorthax's creation, stabilised by his knowledge, sustained by
his nexus. No other portal existed, as far as they knew. Which
meant the visitor had entered from above, through the collapsed
upper levels, through the jungle, through whatever remained of
the Temple's surface structures. This implied physical presence
on Akovar. Not a magical connection from across an ocean but a
body, moving through jungle, descending through ruins, navigating
to the Engineering Core through passages that the expedition had
not explored because they had arrived from below rather than above.

Who visited Akovar? The reference points were limited. New Sabonaa,
the colonial outpost on the western coast, had existed for
approximately sixty years. Dragonborn clans occupied portions of
the archipelago, descendants of Akovarians transformed by the
ley-line detonation. Other native populations, scattered and
fragmented, retained oral traditions but not the knowledge or
motivation to access a sealed Engineering Core and selectively
remove preserved materials.

The dragonborn were the most likely candidates. They were
intelligent, they were native to the archipelago, they retained
ancestral connections to the Temple sites, and some clans were
known to be territorial about ruins they considered sacred. A
dragonborn clan with knowledge of the Temple's layout, with the
physical capability to navigate collapsed passages, and with
sufficient understanding of Akovarian preservation systems to
identify and remove specific materials, could account for
everything Tobias had observed.

But could did not mean did, and the identity of the visitor
mattered less, at this moment, than the implication: the Temple
was not as isolated as they had assumed. Something knew it was
here. Something had accessed it. Something might return.

He made his way back to the Flow Chamber at a pace that balanced
urgency with the need not to alarm his companions unnecessarily.
The scholars were where he had left them: Vaelith at the matrix's
base, surrounded by tablets and notebooks, her pen producing the
rapid dense notation of a mind that understood it was racing
against constraints it could not fully predict. Sevan in the
Archive Vault's entrance, his journal open, his right hand
working the pen with the restored precision that healing had
returned, recording the vault's cataloguing structure in the
careful hand that his training demanded.

"We need to leave," Tobias said.

Vaelith's pen stopped. Sevan looked up from his journal. The
words carried a tone that both had learned to recognise across
weeks of shared travel and shared danger: not panic, not
speculation, but the professional assessment of a man whose
instincts had proven reliable every time they had been tested.

Tobias told them what he had found. The disturbed dust. The wall
marks. The missing canisters. The timeline. He presented the
evidence without interpretation, allowing them to reach their own
conclusions, because evidence presented cleanly was more
convincing than evidence wrapped in analysis.

Vaelith's response was immediate and predictable. "We have barely
begun the documentation. The engineering specifications alone
would require weeks of systematic recording. The theoretical
foundations on the stone tablets, the procedural manuals, the
monitoring data, the matrix's geometry at the upper levels that
I cannot reach without climbing equipment. We are standing in
the most significant repository of magical knowledge in the
history of the world and you are asking me to leave."

"I am telling you that someone else knows this repository exists.
That they have accessed it within living memory. That we do not
know who they are, what they took, or when they will return.
And that we are three people with no reinforcements, no
communication with anyone who could send reinforcements, and a
portal that is weakening every hour we remain on this side of it."

The silence that followed was not the silence of disagreement but
the silence of a mind, disciplined and analytical, processing
variables it did not want to process and arriving at a conclusion
it did not want to reach.

"How long?" Vaelith asked. The question was not a challenge but a
negotiation, the scholar's instinct to extract maximum value from
diminishing time.

"Hours. Not days. We take what we can carry, we record what we
can record in the time we have, and we return to the portal
before whatever margin we possess disappears."

Sevan closed his journal. "He is right," he said, and the words
carried the quiet authority of a man whose gift had shown him
the network's full architecture and who understood, with the
clarity that the seeing had provided, that the knowledge they
had gathered was sufficient for the work ahead and that the
knowledge they had not gathered would wait for expeditions better
equipped and more numerous than three. "We have the matrix's
geometry in Vaelith's notebooks. We have the engineering tablets.
We have the Fragments and the Atlas and the tome. We have my
survey maps. What we carry is enough to begin. What we leave
behind will endure. The Akovarians built this place to survive
catastrophe. It will survive our departure."

Vaelith looked at the matrix. Fifty feet of encoded methodology,
self-verifying, self-preserving, containing in its crystal
geometry the complete blueprint for the reconstruction of the
ley-line network. She could not take it. She could not even
fully document it in the time available. But she had read its
language, extracted its principles, confirmed its parameters
against her century of theoretical work, and recorded in twelve
notebooks the interpretive framework that would allow future
scholars to continue where she left off.

"Four hours," she said. "I need four hours to complete the
documentation of the lower three levels and to make measurements
of the upper levels that can be extrapolated from the scaling
ratios I have already confirmed."

"Three," Tobias said.

"Three hours and the ability to work without interruption."

"Done."

They worked.

Vaelith at the matrix, her measurements precise, her notation
rapid, her concentration absolute. Sevan in the Archive Vault,
selecting the crystalline tablets that contained the most
critical engineering specifications, wrapping each one in cloth
from their packs, distributing the weight across three loads that
would be heavy but manageable. Tobias at the perimeter, watching
the southern corridors with the attention of a man who had
identified a threat he could not yet see and who intended to
ensure that the threat did not materialise before the people he
was protecting had completed the work that justified the risk of
remaining.

Three hours. The sourceless light of the Flow Chamber did not
change. The matrix stood in its dark crystal patience. The scars
cycled beneath the mosaic floor. And three people, the farthest
from home that any of them had ever been, gathered the knowledge
that a fallen civilisation had preserved against the possibility
of its own destruction, and prepared to carry that knowledge back
to a world that did not yet know how much it needed what they
had found.
