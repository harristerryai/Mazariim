# Chapter Seventeen
## The Cult Strikes

Vorthax interrupted Vaelith's question about crystalline matrix
calibration with a sound that was not a word.

The sound came from every surface in the laboratory simultaneously,
a vibration that bypassed language and struck the body's oldest
instincts with the force of a warning that predated speech,
predated civilisation, predated everything except the animal
recognition that something was wrong. The amber light in the
matrix flared, its steady pulse shattering into rapid, arrhythmic
strobing that threw the laboratory's geometric shadows into violent
motion, and the carved equations on the walls began to glow with a
luminescence that was not amber but red, the colour of a system
shifting from equilibrium to alarm.

"They are here," Vorthax said, and his voice was different. The
measured scholarly cadence, the careful placement of borrowed
Common words, the dry asides and the fragments of ancient memory:
all of it was gone. What remained was something stripped to its
essential function, a consciousness that had detected a threat to
its domain and that was responding with the focused intensity of a
mind that had spent three thousand years preparing for exactly
this possibility. "Fourteen. Armed. Approaching from the northwest
across the boundary. Three carry the signature of entropic
consecration. One of the three is stronger than the others. They
will reach the entrance in approximately nine minutes."

Tobias was on his feet before the final word arrived. His hands
moved with the automatic precision of thirty years of operational
readiness: knives into their positions, cord looped and secured,
cittern case closed and slung across his back, pack cinched tight.
His eyes found Sevan and Vaelith and assessed them in the single
glance that his training made sufficient: Sevan calm, staff in
hand, pale eyes steady; Vaelith already gathering her satchel and
notebooks, her wand appearing in her right hand with the speed of
a reflex that required no conscious decision.

"Fourteen is more than the Athenaeum cell," Tobias said. "Mordecai
committed reinforcements. He received Dael's report and decided
that a failed ambush required a stronger response."

"The gnome understands tactical escalation. This is reassuring."
Vorthax's voice had acquired a harmonic edge, each word arriving
with a resonance that vibrated in the laboratory's crystal
apparatus and set the smaller instruments humming in sympathy. "One
perceived their approach through the scar network forty minutes
ago. One chose not to interrupt the mage's questions because the
questions were productive and because one wished to observe their
deployment pattern before raising the alarm. They are organised.
The three consecrated individuals anchor a formation that places
the remaining eleven in a crescent behind them. The formation is
designed for assault against a fixed position."

"You waited forty minutes," Tobias said, and the temperature of
his voice dropped by several degrees.

"One waited until the tactical picture was complete. Consider the
geometric implications." The phrase emerged with the particular
precision of a consciousness applying a favourite expression to a
novel context. "Their formation reveals their intent: breach and
seizure, not reconnaissance. They intend to enter the Tower, take
what they find, and depart. They do not intend negotiation. One
finds this clarifying. Negotiation requires patience, and one's
patience for entropy's servants was exhausted approximately three
thousand years ago."

"Can you stop them?" Vaelith asked, her analytical mind already
calculating variables: fourteen attackers, an incorporeal defender
whose power within his domain was enormous but whose stability
was uncertain, three companions whose combat capabilities had been
tested at the Athenaeum but not against a force of this size.

"Stop them." The word echoed from four walls, each echo carrying
a different inflection: amusement, contempt, anticipation, and
something darker that Tobias identified as hunger. "The mage asks
whether one can stop fourteen mortals from entering a structure
that one has inhabited for three thousand years, a structure whose
geometry obeys one's will, whose magical field one controls, whose
every stone and corridor and twisted staircase is an extension of
one's consciousness." The red luminescence in the wall carvings
brightened, and the temperature in the laboratory dropped, the
ambient chill of the nexus concentrating into something sharper,
more directed, the cold of a presence that was gathering itself.
"This is elementary."

"Then we go down," Tobias said. "Through the portal. Now. While
you hold them."

"Not yet." The voice consolidated into a single point above the
matrix, dense with authority. "They have not entered. One will not
act against threats that remain outside one's walls. This is not
mercy. It is efficiency. The Tower's defences operate most
effectively against those who have committed to entering. Once
they cross the threshold, the geometry becomes one's weapon. Until
then, they are merely approaching, and approaching is not yet a
crime worthy of the response one intends."

Tobias processed this with the rapid calculation of a man who
understood tactical advantage and who recognised, beneath the
scholarly justification, the reasoning of a predator that wanted
its prey inside the den. "You want them to come in."

"One wants them to understand what they have chosen. There is a
difference." A pause, and the harmonic edge softened fractionally.
"One also wants the three of you below the fourth level before
the engagement begins. The upper floors will become, one
anticipates, unpleasant for biological organisms. The lower levels
will remain stable. The portal chamber will remain secure. One
gives this assurance."

"How long do we have?" Sevan asked. His voice carried the quiet
steadiness of a man who had moved, in the space of Vorthax's
warning, from scholar to something older: the priest whose faith
included the acknowledgment that observation sometimes required
the observer to act, and that action sometimes required leaving
others to fight battles that could not be shared.

"Seven minutes. Perhaps six. They are moving with purpose." The
matrix pulsed, and the pulse was faster than its baseline rhythm,
the heartbeat of a consciousness whose anticipation was
translating into the physical medium of its existence. "Go now.
Take everything you cannot replace. Leave the rest. One will
preserve what one can. One has, one must observe, considerable
experience with preservation."

They descended.

The staircase, which had lied about its dimensions on every
previous traversal, now told the truth with an urgency that
suggested Vorthax was straightening the geometry to speed their
passage: each flight contained precisely fourteen steps, each
riser the same height, each landing level and true. The spiral
delivered them from the sixth floor to the fourth in less than a
minute, and Tobias noted the change with the professional
awareness of a man registering that the building's controller
was actively reshaping the environment, a capability that was
reassuring when directed in their favour and terrifying to
contemplate from the other side.

At the fourth-level landing, Vaelith stopped.

"The journals," she said. Her voice was controlled, but the
control was effortful, the discipline of a woman whose every
scholarly instinct was screaming that irreplaceable materials were
about to become collateral in a battle between a wraith and a
death cult. "Vorthax's research journals. A hundred and fifty-five
years of theoretical development. We cannot carry them all. We
have six, perhaps seven, that I selected last night. The rest..."

"Will be here when you return," Vorthax's voice said from the
library walls, and the tone carried something that had not been
there before: protectiveness, not of the people descending his
staircase but of the work they represented, the validation they
carried, the promise they had made. "One's books have survived
three thousand years. They will survive this. Go."

Vaelith went.

They passed the third level, the second, the ground floor. The
entry hall was dim, the overcast daylight filtering through the
open doorway to illuminate the debris and the bone-scattered
floor and the inscription that warned of costs the seeker must be
willing to pay. Through the doorway, visible against the barren
slope, figures moved.

Tobias counted in the heartbeat he allowed himself to look.
Fourteen, as Vorthax had reported. The formation was a crescent,
its points advancing with the disciplined spacing of a unit that
had rehearsed its approach. Three figures occupied the crescent's
centre, and even at a distance of two hundred yards Tobias could
distinguish them from the outer circle members by their bearing
and their equipment: robes rather than travelling clothes, staves
and implements rather than crossbows and swords, the posture of
practitioners rather than foot soldiers.

One of the three walked ahead of the others. Average height,
average build, moving with an economy that rendered him
unremarkable and therefore, to Tobias's trained eye, remarkable
in exactly the way that a man cultivating invisibility was
remarkable: the effort of not being noticed was itself noticeable
to someone who knew the technique. Mordecai Vellen. The Whisper
of the Void. The man whose intelligence network had tracked them
from Wyebluff and whose ambition had committed fourteen lives to
seizing knowledge he intended to corrupt.

"Down," Tobias said, and they descended into the basement levels.

The stairs spiralled into the bedrock with the same corrected
geometry, each step true, the darkness receding before Sevan's
staff-light with its cool white luminescence. First basement
level: sealed containers and preserved supplies, untouched. Second
basement level: the heavy workshop, stone-cutting tools casting
shadows in the silver radiance. Third basement level: the portal
chamber, its fifty-foot diameter and thirty-foot dome and the
geometric patterns on the walls glowing with their steady amber,
and at the chamber's centre the portal itself, the discontinuity
that shimmered with its patient luminescence and that showed, in
its depths, the warm light and dense green of a continent that
waited seven thousand years away.

"We wait here?" Vaelith asked.

"We cross," Tobias said. He was already assessing the chamber's
defensive properties: single entrance from the staircase, fifty
feet of open ground, the portal at centre. If the cult forces
fought through Vorthax and descended, this chamber became a dead
end. The portal was the only exit, and an exit that led to an
unknown continent was preferable to a sealed room with enemies
above. "We cross now. Before the fighting starts. Vorthax holds
the Tower; we hold the objective. If the cult breaks through, we
are already gone."

Sevan shook his head. The gesture was small, definitive, and it
carried the particular weight of a man whose conscience had
reached a conclusion that his tactical sense could not override.
"We cannot leave without knowing. If Vorthax falls, the portal
may destabilise. The Tower's enchantments may fail. The research
we have not yet copied may be destroyed. We must know the outcome
before we commit to a crossing that may not allow return."

"The priest demonstrates an inconvenient commitment to
comprehensive observation," Vorthax said from the chamber walls.
The voice was tighter now, compressed, the consciousness dividing
its attention between the conversation and the approaching threat
and the preparations it was making in the Tower's upper levels.
"One appreciates the sentiment. One suggests a compromise: the
mage and the gnome cross immediately with the materials. The
priest remains until the engagement is decided, then follows. The
portal will remain stable as long as one exists, and one intends
to exist for some time yet."

"No." Sevan's voice was quiet, firm, and carried the authority of
a man who had made his decision and who would not be moved from it
by the arguments of the living or the dead. "We cross together or
not at all. I will not leave a consciousness to fight alone while
I watch from safety. That is not witness. That is abandonment."

The silence that followed held for four heartbeats. When Vorthax
spoke, his voice came from a single point in the wall directly
beside Sevan, close, almost intimate, the proximity of a
consciousness addressing an individual rather than a room.

"The priest unsettles one." The third use, and the last, Tobias
sensed, that the phrase would carry its original weight; after
this, it would become memory rather than discovery. "One
will... consider the priest's position. Rapidly. Events above are
accelerating."

They heard it then: from the Tower's upper levels, transmitted
through the stone and the staircase and the geometric connections
that bound basement to summit, the sound of the eastern doorway's
threshold being crossed. Not feet on stone, though feet were
crossing stone. The sound was the Tower itself responding, a
deep harmonic shift in the nexus vibration, the baseline hum
changing pitch as the structure registered the intrusion of
fourteen bodies through its entrance and the consciousness that
inhabited it turned its full attention, for the first time in
three thousand years, from contemplation to violence.

The amber light in the portal chamber's wall patterns flared red.

---

In the entry hall, the first cultist died without understanding
what killed him.

He was an outer circle member, a young man from Wyebluff who had
been recruited eighteen months ago through promises of purpose
and power and who had found, in the Umbral Hand's rituals, a
meaning that his life as a warehouse clerk had not provided. He
crossed the threshold two paces ahead of his assigned position,
eager, nervous, his crossbow raised against shadows that his
torch could not fully illuminate.

The shadows moved.

They did not move as shadows moved in response to changing light.
They moved with intent, with direction, with the focused
malevolence of a consciousness that controlled darkness as
completely as it controlled the stones that cast it. The shadows
detached from the walls in angular shapes that obeyed no natural
geometry, shapes that cut, and the young man from Wyebluff felt
cold across his throat and then felt nothing at all, and the
body that had been a warehouse clerk and then a cultist and then
briefly a soldier fell to the entry hall's floor beside the bones
of birds and mice that had entered the Tower before him and had
met the same custodian.

The second and third cultists died in the corridor beyond the
entry hall, where the geometry shifted beneath their feet and the
floor that had been level became a slope that was not a slope but
a pit, a sudden absence of ground that the darkness concealed
until they had stepped into it, and the fall was not far but the
landing was stone and the stone was cold and the cold was not
temperature but the touch of something that drained warmth and
life and the capacity for either.

Mordecai Vellen, standing in the entry hall's centre with his
obsidian skull holy symbol in his left hand and his thin dagger
in his right, watched three of his outer circle members die in
less than thirty seconds and revised his assessment of the
situation with the rapid, emotionless calculation that was his
most dangerous quality.

He spoke, and the Draconic flowed from his tongue with six
precise syllables shaped by sources that preferred darkness, each
pitched to a tone that vibrated below conversational register,
resonant in the teeth and jaw of everyone within earshot. The
scent of cold stone and dry earth thickened the air, and with it
came the manifestation of his god's protection: spectral forms,
translucent and grey, orbiting his position in a radius of
fifteen feet, their shapes suggestive of skeletal figures draped
in entropy's vestments, their presence a declaration that the
space they occupied belonged to Zebulba and that the forces
inhabiting this Tower would find his immediate vicinity less
hospitable than the darkness beyond it.

The spectral guardians met the Tower's shadows, and where they
met, the shadows recoiled. The divine authority of an Old God,
even channelled through a mid-level priest whose faith was
transactional, carried sufficient weight to push back the ambient
darkness that Vorthax wielded as weapon. Within the radius of
Mordecai's protection, torchlight held. Beyond it, the Tower
consumed illumination with the appetite of something that had
been provoked.

"Althea. Wards on the formation. Cassius, stay behind me. Outer
circle, close ranks." Mordecai's voice carried the measured
authority of a man who had anticipated resistance and who was now
implementing the contingency plan he had prepared for exactly this
scenario. He had not anticipated a wraith of this power, that
much was clear from the speed of the casualties, but his response
was not panic or retreat but adaptation, the professional's reflex
to contract, consolidate, and advance through the threat rather
than away from it.

Sister Althea Crane moved to his left, her hands already shaping
the Draconic syllables of a protective working. Four syllables,
brisk and clinical, each pitched to a precise tone that hummed in
the bones of those standing near her, and a field of grey-violet
energy expanded from her position to encompass the surviving
formation: a ward against necrotic interference that would not
stop a direct attack but that would blunt the ambient life-draining
cold that filled the Tower's corridors. The scent of grave earth
and old linen accompanied the casting, mixing with the mineral
tang of the nexus to produce an olfactory signature that was part
battlefield medicine and part mortuary.

The Whisper was not visible. Tobias, had he been present, would
have noted the absence and understood it: the assassin operated
independently, using the chaos of the main assault as cover for
a separate approach. The Whisper would be looking for another
entrance, another route, a way into the Tower's depths that
avoided the killing ground of the main corridors.

Mordecai advanced.

The entry hall's inscription, *to those who seek the patterns
underlying all things*, caught the torchlight as his formation
moved past it, and Mordecai read it with the detached appreciation
of a man who valued information regardless of its source and who
filed the inscription's warning alongside his tactical
observations without allowing either to alter his course.

The staircase awaited. Mordecai looked up into the spiral's
darkness and perceived, with the professional instinct that served
him as reliably as Tobias's served the gnome, that the darkness
was not empty. Something watched from above. Something that had
killed three of his people in thirty seconds and that was now
waiting, with the patience of a consciousness that measured time
in millennia, for him to commit to the ascent.

"You would destroy what you cannot understand?" The voice erupted
from the staircase walls with a force that sent dust cascading
from the stone and that struck Mordecai's spectral guardians like
a physical blow, the translucent forms flickering and reforming
under the pressure of a will that was not divine but that drew on
energies vast enough to contest divinity within the boundaries
of its domain. "You come to one's Tower. You bring the stench
of entropy into chambers that one has preserved against entropy
for three thousand years. You seek knowledge that you intend to
corrupt, to twist, to feed to the void that consumes all things."

The walls blazed red. The geometric carvings that covered every
surface ignited with a luminescence that was not light but rage
made visible, the mathematical precision of the Geometrist's
life's work transformed into an expression of fury that three
millennia of isolation had compressed into something denser and
more dangerous than any weapon forged from metal. The temperature
in the entry hall plummeted. Frost formed on the cultists'
clothing, on their weapons, on the moisture of their breath as
it left their bodies in clouds of crystallised air.

"One has had considerable time to prepare for this eventuality,"
Vorthax said, and the voice was everywhere, omnidirectional,
pouring from stone and air and the frost that formed on every
surface with a precision that suggested the crystals themselves
were speaking. "You fail to comprehend what you have entered.
This is not a ruin. This is not an abandoned tower. This is a
mind. And you are inside it. And the mind is not pleased."

An outer circle cultist broke formation and ran.

The corridor behind the formation, the one they had entered
through, was no longer the corridor they had entered through. The
walls had shifted. The angles had changed. What had been a
straight passage to the eastern doorway was now a curve that led
back to the entry hall from the opposite direction, a loop that
the fleeing cultist ran through twice before Mordecai's sharp
command halted him with terror of his leader outweighing terror
of the Tower.

"Spatial manipulation," Mordecai said, and his voice betrayed
nothing: not fear, not surprise, not the cold recognition that
the tactical situation had deteriorated beyond his contingency
planning. He had expected a haunted tower. He had not expected a
consciousness that controlled the building's geometry with the
fluency of a man rearranging furniture. "The wraith controls the
architecture. Maintain formation. Do not separate."

He raised his obsidian skull and spoke five syllables of Draconic
pitched to a tone that cut through the ambient noise like a blade
drawn across glass, each word tasting of cold stone and the
spaces between stars. An unravelling, directed not at a specific
enchantment but at the fabric of the spatial distortion itself,
a blunt instrument applied to a surgical phenomenon. The
violet-black light that characterised his divine workings pulsed
from the skull, and the scent of dry earth and sealed tombs
accompanied the wave as it spread outward, meeting the Tower's
shifted geometry and contesting it.

For a moment, the corridor straightened. The loop collapsed. The
eastern doorway was visible, fifty feet behind them, the grey
highland light filtering through the entrance that was also an
exit that was also the last connection to the world outside the
Geometrist's mind.

Then the Tower pushed back. The spatial distortion reasserted
itself with a force that cracked the stone beneath Mordecai's
feet, the nexus energy overwhelming the divine unravelling,
forcing the mana that Mordecai's casting had smoothed back toward
neutrality into new and more violent configurations, the raw
power of a five-scar convergence channelled through a
consciousness that had been studying geometric manipulation for
longer than any nation in the Inner Kingdoms had existed. The
corridor twisted again, not into a loop this time but into a
spiral that mirrored the staircase above, the architecture
echoing its creator's obsessions.

"Impressive," Mordecai said, and meant it, and filed the
information. "Althea. Raise the fallen."

Sister Althea Crane knelt beside the body of the young man from
Wyebluff and spoke six syllables of Draconic pitched to a low
tone that seemed to press against the eardrums, each word
carrying the scent of cold earth intensified, the smell of graves
opened in winter when the ground fought the shovel and the dead
lay closer to the surface than the living preferred to
acknowledge. The syllables were precise, clinical, the casting of
a woman who treated necromancy as a medical discipline and
reanimation as a practical application of available resources. The
air around her hands cooled until moisture condensed on her
knuckles, and the negative energy that flowed from her palms into
the body was visible as a brief darkening, a shadow that had
weight. The body moved, and the movement was wrong in the way
that all animated dead were wrong: the joints bending at
functionally correct but somehow incorrect angles, the eyes
opening to see nothing, the limbs responding to instructions that
bypassed volition and operated on the mechanical obedience of
flesh that had been repurposed.

"Three more available along the corridor," she reported, her
tone carrying the brisk efficiency of a field medic inventorying
supplies. "Shall I raise them all?"

"Raise them. Send them up the stairs. Let the wraith spend its
attention on the dead while the living advance behind them."

---

In the portal chamber, the sounds of combat filtered through
three levels of stone and staircase with a quality that was less
sound than sensation: vibrations that the body interpreted as
impact, as force, as the collision of magical energies whose
effects were felt in the bones long before they reached the ears.
The walls pulsed between amber and red, the portal chamber's
geometric patterns reflecting the Tower's condition the way a
face reflected pain, and the portal itself flickered, its
luminescence wavering as the forces above it shifted and clashed
and the structure that anchored it shuddered under stresses it
had not experienced since the night of its creator's
transformation.

"He is holding them," Sevan said. His perception, extended through
the scar network that connected the portal chamber to the Tower's
foundations and the foundations to the nexus and the nexus to the
consciousness that fought above them, provided a view of the
engagement that transcended the physical: he perceived the
struggle as patterns of energy, the wraith's geometric
manipulations clashing with the entropic signatures of Zebulba's
divine magic, the two forces meeting along the ley-line scars
that threaded the Tower's structure in a contest that was
simultaneously physical and metaphysical. "He is using the
geometry itself. The corridors, the staircase, the rooms. He is
reshaping the Tower around them, trapping them in spaces that
loop, in passages that narrow, in rooms that have no exits."

"And they are fighting through it," Tobias said. He stood at the
base of the staircase, where the sounds from above were clearest,
and his assessment was tactical: the sounds were changing. The
initial chaos of the Tower's ambush, the screams and crashes and
the deep harmonic groans of stone being reshaped, was giving way
to something more ordered. The cult was adapting. The formation
was contracting, concentrating its divine magic into a wedge that
pushed through the spatial distortions, the combined authority of
Zebulba's priests contesting Vorthax's control of the
architecture. "They are moving down. Slowly, but they are
descending."

A sound from above that was not combat: a voice, amplified by the
staircase's spiral into a resonance that filled the basement
levels with its authority. Mordecai's voice, speaking Draconic
with the cold precision that the consistency of his character
demanded, each invocation pitched to the same exacting tone, the
syllables carrying the weight of divine power shaped by deliberate
will. Five syllables. Then five more. The same unravelling, cast
repeatedly, each casting peeling away a layer of the Tower's
shifted geometry and replacing it, for moments, with the building's
original architecture. The cult was using Mordecai's unravellings
as a battering ram, advancing through the momentary windows of
normal space that his castings created before Vorthax's will
reasserted the distortions.

"The mathematics do not support that conclusion," Vorthax
snarled, and the voice was ragged, the scholarly precision fraying
under the strain of maintaining defensive manipulations against a
sustained magical assault that was, Tobias realised with a
professional's respect for an enemy's competence, exactly the
correct counter-strategy: not overpowering the wraith's
defences but dismantling them, one casting at a time, forcing
Vorthax to expend energy on reconstruction that could not then be
spent on offence.

The red luminescence in the portal chamber's walls intensified.
The portal flickered again, and this time the flicker was longer,
the shimmering surface of the discontinuity dimming for a full
second before recovering.

"The portal is destabilising," Vaelith said. Her detection working,
maintained since they entered the Tower, registered the energy
fluctuations with a precision that her voice conveyed without
embellishment. "The nexus energy that sustains it is being
diverted to the upper-level defences. If Vorthax continues to
draw on the full convergence to fight the cultists, the portal's
power supply will be insufficient to maintain coherence."

"How long?" Tobias asked.

"At current rates of diversion, minutes. Perhaps ten. Perhaps
fewer."

Tobias looked at the portal. He looked at the staircase. He
looked at his companions: the priest whose conscience would not
allow retreat, the mage whose life's work waited on the other
side of a closing door, the gnome whose professional assessment
was that the situation had moved from difficult to critical and
was approaching impossible.

"We cross now," he said.

"The fighting above..." Sevan began.

"Will continue whether we are in this chamber or on Akovar. If
the portal closes, everything we have accomplished becomes academic.
The Atlas, the Fragments, the Stones, Vorthax's seed-problem
solution, the Temple's crystalline matrix: all of it means nothing
if we cannot reach the primary source. The cult wants the
knowledge. If we take the knowledge through the portal, the cult
has no reason to continue into the basement levels. Vorthax holds
a Tower with nothing in it worth dying for."

The logic was clean, and Sevan recognised it, and the recognition
cost him something visible: the tightening of his jaw, the
momentary closure of his eyes, the internal negotiation between
the priest who would not abandon a consciousness and the scholar
who understood that the mission's success required exactly the
abandonment his faith resisted.

"Vorthax," Sevan said, and his voice carried upward through the
staircase and the stone and the scar network that connected them.
"We must cross. The portal is failing."

The response came from the portal chamber's walls, and the voice
was distant, distracted, the attention behind it divided between
conversation and combat in proportions that left little room for
the careful word-selection of previous exchanges: "Yes. Cross.
One perceived the destabilisation. One cannot, the demands are,
one is attempting to." Fragments. The scholarly discipline
collapsing under the pressure of sustained magical combat. Then,
with a visible effort of will that manifested as a brief
brightening of the wall patterns from red to amber and back:
"Cross now. One will stabilise the portal long enough for
passage. One cannot guarantee stability beyond, beyond."

The word repeated, the consciousness unable to complete the
sentence, and Tobias understood that "beyond" meant "after you
are through," and that the wraith was not promising the portal
would survive their crossing, only that it would survive long
enough for their crossing to occur.

"The validation approaches," Vorthax said, and the words emerged
from a single point in the wall beside the portal with a clarity
that cut through the chaos of the upper levels, a moment of
focused intention amid the storm: the scholar, surfacing through
the combatant, delivering the message that mattered more than the
battle that surrounded it. "One's work. The mage's work. The
priest's maps. All of it, approaching validation on the other
side of a door one opened and cannot walk through. Go. Go now.
And remember one's name."

Vaelith stepped toward the portal. She adjusted her satchel,
the gesture automatic, the Fragments and Stones and notebooks
secure against her body. Her wand was in her hand. Her jaw was
set. Her green eyes, bright with the particular fire that
accompanied her most significant moments, fixed on the shimmering
discontinuity that separated one continent from another, one era
of understanding from the next.

She stepped through.

The portal swallowed her in silence. No flash, no sound, no
dramatic transition. She was there, and then the shimmer rippled,
and then she was not there, and the portal's surface steadied,
fractionally, as if the passage of a single body had cost it
something but not more than it could bear.

Tobias went next. He paused at the portal's edge and looked back,
up the staircase, toward the sounds of a three-thousand-year-old
consciousness fighting to defend the work that was its only
reason for existing. The sounds were closer. The cult was
descending. The second basement level, perhaps. Minutes away.

"Guard the door," Tobias said, echoing his own words from the day
before, and the echo was not repetition but completion: the
closing of a circle that had begun when a gnome told a wraith to
guard its own door and the wraith had answered with three thousand
years of patience.

He stepped through.

Sevan stood alone in the portal chamber.

The walls pulsed red. The sounds from above were distinct now:
Draconic syllables in Mordecai's cold cadence, the clash of
divine energies against geometric manipulation, the groans of
stone being reshaped, and beneath it all Vorthax's voice, no
longer words but a sustained harmonic that was the wraith
pouring its consciousness into the Tower's structure, becoming
the defence rather than directing it.

Sevan looked at the portal. He looked at the staircase. He
listened to the sounds of a mind that had been alone for three
thousand years and that was now, in defending three strangers
who had promised to remember its name, experiencing something
that approximated purpose.

"I see you," he said. To the walls. To the scars. To the
consciousness that filled both.

Then something came down the staircase.

Not a cultist. Something that had been a cultist, reanimated by
Althea's necromancy and sent ahead as a probe, its dead eyes
seeing nothing, its limbs moving with the mechanical wrongness
of flesh operating without volition. Behind it, a second. Behind
the second, the sound of living boots on stone, descending with
purpose.

Sevan raised his staff.

The working was not the gentle release he had performed in the
Athenaeum's archive. It was four syllables, sharp and bright,
pitched to a tone that rang with the clarity of a struck bell in
the confined stairwell, the divine authority of Mau's mandate to
observe and preserve directed against things that were neither
observed nor preserved but merely used, the animated remains of
people who deserved better than to be driven down a staircase as
disposable instruments of a faith that treated existence as raw
material. The scent of copper and something cleaner, like
starlight given an olfactory form, displaced the stale air. The
warm white light flowed from his palm and struck the two figures,
and they stopped, and the false luminescence in their eyes failed,
and they fell with the quiet finality of things returning to the
rest they should never have been denied.

Behind the fallen undead, on the staircase, a shape moved.

Not an outer circle cultist. Not one of the three consecrated
leaders. Something smaller, faster, moving with a silence that
was not merely quiet but practised, the silence of a professional
whose life depended on not being heard. The Whisper. The
assassin that Mordecai employed for operations requiring
precision rather than force, the operative whose absence from
the main formation Tobias would have noted and whose separate
approach through the Tower's lower levels, through routes that
the spatial distortions above had not reached, had brought her
to the one place the defenders had not fortified.

Sevan saw the blade. A thin line of darkness, catching no light,
descending toward his neck from behind in the arc of a strike
designed to silence before the target could cry out.

He moved. Not fast enough. The blade missed his throat but found
his shoulder, cutting through the midnight-blue robes and the
flesh beneath with the clean efficiency of a weapon maintained
for exactly this purpose. Pain, bright and immediate, lanced
down his arm, and the staff slipped from fingers that suddenly
could not grip, and he turned to face the attacker with his
left hand rising in a gesture that was not combat but casting:
three syllables spoken through clenched teeth, a prayer for
protection pitched to a tone that held despite the pain, and the
translucent radiance manifested between himself and the small,
dark-clad figure that was already moving, already repositioning
for a second strike with the fluid economy of someone who killed
for a living and who regarded the first cut as introduction
rather than conclusion.

The shield caught the second strike. The blade scraped across
the translucent radiance and deflected downward, and the
Whisper, whatever face lay behind the dark cloth that covered
everything but grey, assessing eyes, withdrew two steps and
settled into a stance that communicated patience: the shield
would not last, and the Whisper had time, and the priest was
bleeding, and the equation favoured the side that did not bleed.

Then the walls screamed.

Not a metaphor. The stone itself produced a sound that was
Vorthax's rage given voice through the medium of his domain,
a sound that bypassed ears and struck the mind directly with the
force of a consciousness that had perceived, through the scar
network that connected every surface in his Tower, the injury
inflicted upon the man who had promised to remember his name.
The portal chamber's geometric patterns blazed crimson. The
temperature plummeted. And the stone beneath the Whisper's feet
moved.

Not the subtle spatial manipulation of the corridors above. This
was direct, violent, personal: the floor rippling like water,
the stone flowing with a fluidity that stone should not possess,
rising around the Whisper's boots in a grip that was not physical
restraint but geometric impossibility, the three-dimensional
space occupied by the assassin's feet becoming a space that did
not admit the concept of movement. The Whisper was not pinned.
The Whisper was contained, held in a pocket of frozen geometry
that the nexus energy enforced with the authority of mathematical
law.

"One's Tower," Vorthax said, and the voice was not the scholar
or the madman or the desperate consciousness that had negotiated
and confessed and showed its face to the living. It was the
wraith. Pure. Cold. The voice of something that had been human
once and that had spent three thousand years becoming something
else and that was now, in this moment, entirely that something
else: a greater wraith at the height of its power, in the heart
of its domain, defending the last thing it cared about with every
force at its command. "One's priest. One's promise."

The Whisper struggled. The grey eyes above the dark cloth showed
the first emotion Tobias would have identified as fear, the
professional's recognition that the tactical situation had
exceeded the parameters of any contingency plan.

"Cross," Vorthax said to Sevan. "Cross now. The portal holds.
Cross, and one will hold the rest. Cross, and take one's name
with you."

Sevan retrieved his staff with his left hand. His right arm hung,
the shoulder wound bleeding through the midnight-blue robes,
the blood dark against the fabric's near-black surface. He
looked at the Whisper, held in geometric impossibility, and he
looked at the portal, shimmering with the last of its diverted
energy, and he looked at the walls that blazed with a
consciousness that had called him "one's priest" and that meant
it in a way that transcended possession and approached something
that neither the living nor the dead had adequate vocabulary to
describe.

"Clear sight, Vorthax," he said. The Mauian farewell, the
blessing of perception, given to a consciousness that had
perceived the scars he perceived and the mathematics Vaelith
modelled and the loneliness that Tobias recognised and that had
waited three thousand years for someone to see it clearly and
to name it aloud.

He stepped through the portal.

The shimmer rippled. The luminescence wavered. And behind him,
in a Tower that had been a prison for three millennia and that
was now, briefly and terribly, a battlefield, a consciousness
that could not follow turned its full attention upward, toward
the cult that descended through its levels, and the walls blazed
with a light that was not amber and not red but white, the colour
of a mind that had nothing left to protect except its own
defiance and that intended, with the patience and the fury of
three thousand years, to make that defiance cost the intruders
everything they had brought.

The portal flickered. Dimmed. Held.

Above, the Tower fought on.
