# THE GEOMETRY OF SCARS
### by Callum Ashvane

---

## Chapter Two
## Theories Without Proof

The equation was wrong.

Vaelith Syrren stood before the slate wall of her study and stared at the
error with the particular intensity of a woman who had written the same
calculation in eleven different configurations across seven days and produced
the same impossible result each time. The chalk dust on her fingers had worked
itself into the creases of her knuckles and beneath her trimmed nails, and a
smear of white crossed her left cheekbone where she had pushed an escaped
strand of auburn hair from her face without thinking. She had not thought
about her face in some time. She had not thought about much of anything except
the equation, which was wrong, and which should not have been wrong, and which
was therefore telling her something she did not want to hear.

"The flow coefficient," she said aloud, to no one, because the study was
empty and had been empty since before dawn, "cannot exceed the geometric
mean of the channel dimensions. This is not a matter of opinion. This is
arithmetic."

The equation disagreed.

She stepped back from the slate and pressed the heels of her hands against
her eyes. The study surrounded her in its familiar disorder: books stacked on
every horizontal surface, some open to pages she had been cross-referencing
for weeks, their margins dense with her angular handwriting; scrolls of graph
paper pinned to the walls, each bearing mathematical models rendered in ink
of varying ages, black for recent work, brown for calculations that had
oxidized over decades, near-invisible grey for theorems she had proposed and
abandoned in her first years at the tower; a desk buried so completely
beneath correspondence, diagrams, and empty teacups that its surface had not
been visible since the previous autumn. The room smelled of chalk dust and
cold tea and the faintly astringent scent of the alchemical ink she preferred
for final drafts, and through the single tall window the morning light of
Beneshire fell across the floor in a column of pale gold that illuminated
dust motes drifting in the still air.

The Astral Circle's tower rose above the farmland south of Wyebluff, a
slender structure of grey stone that most passersby mistook for a disused
watchtower or an eccentric nobleman's folly. This was by design. The Circle
did not advertise its presence, its purpose, or the nature of the research
conducted within its walls. Six mages occupied the tower's upper floors,
each pursuing lines of inquiry that the broader magical community would have
found either fascinating or alarming, depending on temperament. Vaelith had
occupied the fourth floor for twenty-three years, since her transfer from
the Circle's tower in Saerrus where she had spent her first twenty-seven
years of membership. She had rearranged the furniture twice in that time,
once when she needed more wall space for slate boards, and once when a failed
transmutation experiment had reduced her desk to a pile of oak shavings and
she had been forced to requisition a replacement.

She lowered her hands and looked at the equation again.

The problem, stripped of its mathematical complexity, was this: her models
predicted that ley-lines, the theoretical channels of magical energy that had
once connected major locations across the continent of Akovar, operated
according to principles of geometric flow dynamics. Mana moved through
these channels the way water moved through aqueducts, following paths of
least resistance, pooling at intersections, accelerating through
constrictions. She had spent a century developing the mathematics to describe
this behavior, and her models were elegant, consistent, and predictive. They
explained every fragmentary observation of "resonance phenomena" that scholars
had documented over the millennia since Yrugas fell. They accounted for the
anomalous magical effects reported at certain locations throughout the Inner
Kingdoms. They even predicted the existence of specific intersection points
that subsequent surveys had confirmed.

The models worked. They were, she was prepared to argue before any academic
body in the Federation, correct.

But they could not explain how ley-lines had been created in the first place.

This was the wall against which her research had broken for the past thirty
years. Her equations described flow dynamics beautifully, capturing how mana
moved once a channel existed. What they could not describe was the initiation
event: the moment when undifferentiated ambient mana was first forced into a
geometric channel, the instant when randomness became pattern, when diffusion
became flow. Her mathematics predicted that this initiation required an energy
input of staggering magnitude, far beyond anything mortal magic could produce,
far beyond anything recorded in the millennia of post-Yrugas arcane
scholarship.

The fragmentary Akovarian references that survived in various libraries and
archives offered a single, maddening explanation: the ley-lines had been
initiated by Asti. The entity called the Pyramid, the Healer, the Old God
who had guided the Akovarian civilization and whose essence had powered their
network for millennia. Divine intervention. A god had done it.

Vaelith did not accept this explanation. She could not accept it, not because
she doubted the existence of entities, for their reality was as well
documented as gravity, but because "a god did it" was not an explanation. It
was an abdication of inquiry. It was the intellectual equivalent of drawing a
curtain across a window and declaring that the landscape beyond had ceased to
exist.

And yet.

The equation on her slate wall, computed for the eleventh time, told her that
the energy required to initiate a ley-line exceeded mortal capacity by a
factor of approximately nine hundred. Not nine hundred percent. Nine hundred
times. An abyss between what mortals could produce and what the mathematics
demanded, unbridgeable by any technique or technology she could envision.

She picked up her chalk and drew a single line beneath the final figure.
Then she wrote, in the margin, a word she had never committed to slate
before: *Problematic.*

"Problematic," she said aloud, tasting the inadequacy of it. "Profoundly,
catastrophically, career-endingly problematic."

Her hands had already begun to move, sketching invisible diagrams in the air
as her mind raced ahead of her capacity to speak. This was her habit when
thinking, and her fingers traced geometric shapes, tapped out mathematical
sequences on whatever surface was nearest, drew connections between concepts
that existed only in her mind's architecture. She was aware of the habit. She
had been made aware of it by colleagues, students, and on one memorable
occasion a diplomatic envoy from Saerrus who had mistaken her aerial
sketching for an offensive hand gesture. She could not stop it, any more than
she could stop the current of her thoughts, which moved with the relentless
rapidity of a river that has found its gradient and will not be diverted.

"Consider this," she murmured, pacing the length of her study, stepping over
book stacks with the automatic precision of long practice. "The Akovarian
texts reference a 'seed,' a catalyst that initiates the geometric flow. All
sources agree on this point, fragmentary as they are. The seed was provided
by Asti. This much is documented. But what was the nature of the seed? What
did it actually provide?" She paused at the window, her green eyes narrowing
against the morning light, gold flecks catching the sun like sparks in
emerald. "Energy? Precision? Both? The distinction matters enormously. If the
seed was purely energetic, if it was raw power channeled into the initiation,
then we are defeated, because the energy requirement is beyond mortal reach.
But if the seed was primarily geometric, a template of divine precision that
organized the energy rather than providing it, then the energy requirement
might be vastly lower than my models predict, because the models assume
random-to-ordered conversion, which is the most energy-intensive possible
pathway."

She stopped pacing. Her fingers froze mid-gesture.

"Vith'na," she breathed. The Elven curse came without thought, dragged from
the linguistic substrate beneath her fluent Common by the sudden electrical
clarity of an idea arriving. "The models assume random-to-ordered conversion.
But what if the seed provided order first and the energy followed?"

She lunged for the slate wall, chalk raised, and then stopped with her hand
an inch from the surface. There was no room. Every inch was covered with the
eleven iterations of the equation that had defeated her. She looked at the
wall with something approaching betrayal, then turned to the nearest stack of
graph paper, seized a sheet, dropped to her knees on the floor, and began
to write.

The calculation took her twenty minutes. The verification took another ten.
The result, when she stared at it from the floor of her study with her
auburn braid half-unwound and chalk dust coating her robes from hem to
collar, was not the answer she had hoped for.

The energy requirement dropped. It dropped significantly, by a factor of
nearly twelve, if the seed provided geometric precision rather than raw power.
But it did not drop enough. The remaining factor was still approximately
seventy-five times mortal capacity. A smaller abyss. Still an abyss.

She sat back on her heels and released a long, slow breath through her nose.

"Seventy-five," she said. "Seventy-five times. Down from nine hundred. That
is progress. Objectively, measurably, that is progress."

Her voice lacked conviction.

---

The knock came shortly after midday, when Vaelith had migrated from the
floor to her desk chair, having cleared a space among the correspondence by
the simple expedient of pushing everything to one side, and was staring at her
graph paper calculations with the fixed intensity of a woman willing the
numbers to change through sheer force of academic displeasure.

"Yes, hello," she said, without looking up, because the knock could only
belong to one of five people and none of them would leave simply because she
failed to invite them in.

The door opened to admit Castellan Drevon, the Astral Circle's tower-master
and the closest thing the organization maintained to a local authority. He was
a tall, spare man in his sixties, his silver hair cropped close to a
long-jawed face that wore its permanent expression of mild exasperation with
the dignity of long practice. He dressed in the muted browns and greens
that the Circle preferred, colors that could belong to a merchant, a minor
academic, or a country gentleman, depending on context. In his hand he
carried a letter.

Vaelith registered the letter's existence, noted the quality of the paper and
the weight of the seal, and returned her attention to the graph paper. "If
that is another request from the University of Wyebluff asking me to peer-
review Magister Aldhen's latest treatise on mana-flow thermodynamics, you may
tell them that I have reviewed it, that it is wrong, and that I have explained
why it is wrong in three previous letters which Magister Aldhen has evidently
chosen not to read."

"It is not from the University."

Something in Drevon's tone made her look up. The tower-master's exasperation
had been replaced by an expression she could not immediately categorize, one
that was alert, careful, the expression of a man delivering news whose
implications he had already calculated and found significant.

"The Library of the Ages," he said, and extended the letter.

Vaelith took it. The seal was midnight blue wax stamped with an open eye
surrounded by stars, the symbol of Mau, and therefore of the Watchers who
guarded the Library's collection. She broke the seal with her thumbnail and
unfolded the letter with hands that she noted, with clinical detachment, were
not entirely steady.

The letter was written in a precise, economical hand. It was brief, only
five sentences. Vaelith read it once quickly, then again slowly, then a third
time while her lips moved and her fingers tapped a rapid sequence against the
edge of the paper.

"Castellan Drevon." Her voice had changed. The distracted, half-present tone
of a scholar interrupted mid-thought had been replaced by something sharper,
more focused, a blade drawn from its sheath. "When did this arrive?"

"This morning. By courier from the mountain passes. The courier was..."

"A tome has been recovered from Akovar." Vaelith stood. The chair scraped
back across the stone floor. "An Akovarian text, Castellan. Primary source
material. Recovered from a pyramid-temple by Sabonaar explorers operating out
of New Sabonaa and transported to the Library of the Ages, where it is
currently undergoing preliminary examination." Her words came faster now,
tumbling in the rapid cascade that her colleagues had learned either to follow
or to endure. "The Library's Archivist-General describes the text as
containing, and I am quoting directly, 'geometric diagrams depicting what
appear to be network structures consistent with theoretical models of
ley-line architecture.' Consistent with theoretical models, Castellan. My
theoretical models. The models that the Circle's own review board described
as, and I am quoting from memory but I assure you my memory is excellent, 'an
elaborate thought experiment with no practical application.'"

She was pacing again, the letter clutched in one hand, the other hand drawing
shapes in the air that only she could interpret. "Consider the implications.
A primary Akovarian source, not a fragment, not a third-hand reference, not
a mistranslation of a copy of a summary, but an actual text recovered from
an actual temple. If it contains what the Archivist-General suggests it
contains, this is the first direct evidence of Akovarian ley-line engineering
methodology to reach the Inner Kingdoms since..." She paused, calculated,
dismissed the calculation as unnecessary. "Ever. The first direct evidence
ever."

Drevon waited for the torrent to subside. He had worked with Vaelith for
twenty-three years and had developed the patience required. When her pacing
slowed and her eyes refocused on his face, he spoke.

"The Library requests that the Astral Circle send a representative to assist
with translation and analysis. Given the nature of the material, they are
specifically requesting someone with expertise in theoretical ley-line
research." He paused. "I am not aware of anyone in the Circle who possesses
such expertise. Are you?"

Vaelith's mouth opened, closed, and opened again. An expression crossed her
face that Drevon had never seen there before, not the fierce satisfaction of
an intellectual victory, nor the sharp frustration of an analytical defeat,
but something younger and more vulnerable than either. For an instant, the
two-hundred-and-forty-seven-year-old theoretical arcanist looked like a
student who has just been told that the examination she spent her life
preparing for will, at last, be administered.

"I will need three days to organize my current notes," she said. "And
transport. The mountain passes will require..."

"I have already arranged a horse and escort for the day after tomorrow."

She stared at him. "You anticipated my answer."

"Vaelith." Drevon's exasperation returned, but warmly, carrying an
undercurrent of something that might have been affection. "You have spent
fifty years in this tower and the Saerrus facility before it working on a
theory that everyone respects and no one believes. A primary source has
arrived that might prove you right or prove you wrong, and you are asking me
whether I anticipated that you would want to examine it. I anticipated it the
moment I read the letter. I anticipated it, in fact, the day I recruited you
to this tower."

She did not smile. Vaelith smiled rarely, and when she did it transformed her
sharp features into something warmer than her usual demeanor suggested was
possible. But the set of her mouth softened, and she inclined her head in the
precise, formal nod that elves used to acknowledge a debt of courtesy.

"Thank you, Castellan."

"Do not thank me yet. The Library is a two-day journey through mountain
passes in autumn, you will be working with Mauian scholars whose concept of
intellectual urgency differs markedly from your own, and if the tome fails
to support your theories, you will have traveled a very long way to be
disappointed."

"It will support them." The words came with a certainty that surprised even
Vaelith. She glanced at the slate wall, at the eleven iterations of the
equation that had tormented her for a week, at the graph paper on her desk
showing a factor of seventy-five that she could not bridge. "Or it will tell
me why they are wrong, which is equally valuable, though considerably less
pleasant."

Drevon nodded and turned to leave. At the door he paused. "One additional
detail. The Library's letter mentions that they have assigned a member of
their own staff to the translation work. A Watcher named Sevan Miros. The
Archivist-General describes him as possessing," and here he consulted the
letter he had retained, reading carefully, "'unique perceptual capabilities
relevant to the geographical content of the text.' I have no idea what that
means."

"Nor do I." Vaelith's fingers were already tapping a sequence on her desk,
her mind racing ahead to supply lists, travel preparations, the specific
notebooks she would need, the theoretical frameworks she would want at hand.
"But I intend to find out."

Drevon left. The door closed. Vaelith stood alone in her study, surrounded
by the accumulated evidence of a century of work, the equations, the models,
the diagrams, the correspondence, the towering stacks of books whose margins
bore her annotations in inks of varying age, and felt, for the first time in
longer than she cared to examine, the unfamiliar sensation of hope.

She distrusted it immediately. Hope was not a methodology. Hope was not
evidence. Hope was the seductive whisper that told researchers their theories
were correct before the data had been examined, the warm glow that preceded
the cold shock of falsification. She had trained herself, over a century of
rigorous practice, to resist hope's pull, to demand evidence before belief,
to hold her theories as provisional even when every instinct screamed that
they were right.

But standing in her chalk-dusted study with the autumn light falling through
the window and the letter from the Library clutched in her ink-stained hand,
Vaelith Syrren, who had spent a hundred years building a cathedral of
mathematics that described a system she had never seen, could never test, and
might not exist, permitted herself, for precisely three heartbeats, to
imagine that the cathedral was not a monument to beautiful futility.

Then she shook her head, set the letter on her desk, and reached for a clean
sheet of paper.

"Logically speaking," she said to the empty room, "the first step is to
catalog which theoretical frameworks will be most relevant to the
translation effort. The fundamental principle here is that primary source
material must be interpreted through existing models while simultaneously
being allowed to challenge those models. The literature suggests..." She
caught herself using the academic hedge and, for reasons she could not
entirely articulate, corrected it. "I believe that the geometric flow
equations will be most directly applicable, but the initiation models should
be included as well, because if the text addresses the 'seed' problem in any
capacity, even peripherally, the implications for..."

She stopped. Drew a breath. Realized she was lecturing an empty room on the
proper methodology for an analysis she had not yet begun, regarding a text
she had not yet seen, located in a library she had not yet reached.

"I must consider this," she said, her habitual farewell, spoken to no one,
which was appropriate because she was, as usual, already thinking about
something else.

---

She spent the remainder of the day in preparation, and the preparation
revealed, with uncomfortable clarity, how much of her life had been spent
waiting for exactly this moment.

The notebooks came first. She selected them from the shelves that lined the
study's interior wall, leather-bound volumes arranged chronologically, each
covering a period of her research, each filled with the mathematical
notation and theoretical frameworks that constituted her life's work. She
needed the early volumes, where she had first formulated the geometric flow
hypotheses. She needed the middle volumes, where she had refined those
hypotheses against every scrap of observational data she could find. And she
needed the recent volumes, where the equations had grown increasingly
sophisticated and the conclusions increasingly troubling.

Fifty years of work. Twelve notebooks. She laid them out on the study floor
in a row, and they formed a line of leather and paper that began with the
hopeful, slightly naive theorizing of a mage who believed she was on the
verge of a breakthrough and ended with the rigorous, slightly desperate
mathematics of a scholar who had been on the verge of the same breakthrough
for half a century and was beginning to wonder whether the verge was all
there was.

She knelt beside the earliest notebook and opened it. The handwriting was
her own but younger, more flowing, less precise, the hand of a woman who had
not yet learned that beautiful penmanship was less important than clear
notation. The first page bore a title she had not looked at in decades:

*On the Geometric Principles Underlying Theoretical Ley-Line Function:
A Preliminary Investigation*

By Vaelith Syrren, Astral Circle Tower, Saerrus
750 AW

Preliminary. She had written *preliminary* fifty years ago, in a different
tower, in a different nation, when the Circle's Saerrus facility still
considered her work worthy of active institutional support. The investigation
had never stopped being preliminary, because there had never been data to
advance it beyond that stage. She had built her theoretical framework from
fragments, a reference here, an anomalous reading there, mathematical
patterns extracted from observational data that other scholars had collected
for entirely different purposes. She had constructed a model of a system she
had never observed operating, like an architect designing a cathedral from
the echo of a hymn heard through a closed door.

And it had worked. Against all reasonable expectation, against the polite
skepticism of her colleagues in Saerrus and the pointed silence of the
Circle's review board, her models had worked. They predicted behaviors that
were subsequently confirmed. They explained phenomena that had puzzled
researchers for centuries. They were consistent, elegant, and almost
certainly correct.

Almost.

She closed the notebook and added it to the travel stack. The uncertainty
did not trouble her. Uncertainty was the natural state of knowledge at its
frontier, and she had long since made her peace with it. What troubled her
was the specific shape of her uncertainty. Not whether her models were
correct, but whether they were complete. The seed problem loomed behind
every equation like a shadow behind a door, the possibility that the
fundamental mechanism of ley-line creation involved a component that could
not be measured, modeled, or reproduced by mortal means.

If that were true, then her century of work was not wrong but irrelevant.
A perfect description of a system that mortals could never build. A
blueprint for a house that could only be constructed by gods.

Vaelith placed the last notebook on the travel stack and stood. Her knees
protested. She had been kneeling for longer than she realized, a common
occurrence when her mind was engaged and her body became an afterthought.
She stretched, winced, and crossed to the slate wall where the eleven
iterations of her defeated equation stared back at her in white chalk.

Seventy-five times mortal capacity. Down from nine hundred, if the seed
provided geometric precision rather than raw energy. A smaller abyss.

"But still an abyss," she murmured. Then, because she was a scholar and
scholars did not leave conversations unfinished, even conversations with
walls: "Unless the Akovarians knew something I do not. Which is, logically
speaking, almost certainly the case, given that they actually built the
system and I have merely theorized about it."

She picked up the chalk and drew a small asterisk beside the final figure.
Beside the asterisk she wrote: *Review pending primary source analysis,
Library of the Ages, Mauian 800 AW.*

It was the most optimistic thing she had written on the slate in thirty
years.

---

That evening, as the autumn light faded to amber and the farmlands south of
Wyebluff settled into the quiet rhythms of harvest-time, Vaelith sat in the
tower's small common room with a cup of tea she had forgotten to drink and a
piece of bread she had forgotten to eat, and wrote a letter.

It was addressed to no one in particular. She wrote such letters occasionally,
documents intended not for any recipient but for herself, a practice she
had developed during her years at the human universities, where she had
discovered that the act of explaining her thoughts in plain language forced
a clarity that mathematical notation sometimes obscured. She wrote in Common
rather than Yav'lam, because Common's simpler grammatical structures
demanded simpler expression, and simplicity was what she needed now.

*The fundamental question,* she wrote, *is not whether ley-lines can be
understood. I have spent one hundred years demonstrating that they can be.
The geometric flow models are consistent, predictive, and supported by every
scrap of observational evidence available. The fundamental question, the one I
have avoided writing in explicit terms until tonight, is whether understanding
is sufficient.*

*The Akovarians understood ley-lines. They also had Asti. Without Asti, their
understanding would have remained theoretical, a beautiful model of a system
that could not be built. Is this my position? Have I spent a century
constructing an exquisite theory of something that requires a god to
implement?*

*If the answer is yes, then my work is a contribution to knowledge but not
to practice. It describes what was, not what could be. It is archaeology, not
engineering.*

*If the answer is no, if the divine component can be replicated, approximated,
or circumvented through mortal means, then my work is the foundation upon
which the greatest magical achievement since the fall of Akovar might
eventually be built.*

*I do not know which answer the Akovarian tome will provide. I do not know
whether it will provide either answer. I know only that I have waited fifty
years for primary source material and that primary source material has
arrived, and that I must examine it with the same rigor I would bring to
any analysis, regardless of what I hope to find.*

She set down her quill and stared at the letter. The common room was empty;
the other mages had retired to their own floors, their own researches, their
own obsessions. The tower was quiet save for the faint sound of wind against
the stone walls and the distant barking of a farm dog in the valley below.

Vaelith picked up the cup of cold tea, grimaced, and drank it anyway. She
picked up the piece of bread, discovered it had gone stale, and ate it
regardless. Elves did not require as much sustenance as humans, but they
were not immune to the effects of forgetting to eat for an entire day, and
the slight tremor in her hands was not entirely attributable to intellectual
excitement.

She reread the letter one final time. Then she folded it, slipped it between
the pages of her most recent notebook, and carried the notebook upstairs to
her study, where twelve leather-bound volumes sat in a careful row on the
floor, waiting to be packed for a journey to the mountains.

At the doorway she paused and looked back at the slate wall. The equation
stared at her, eleven iterations, one answer, one asterisk, one small note of
provisional hope.

"Mira'vel," she whispered. *Wonder-sight.* The Elven word for the moment
when something long studied reveals an aspect never before perceived. She
had not spoken it in years. She was not certain, standing in the doorway of
a study that contained a century of her life rendered in chalk and ink, that
she had ever truly earned the right to speak it at all.

But she spoke it now, to the equations and the silence and the possibility
that somewhere in a mountain library, bound in terracotta leather and written
in a script no living person could read, there existed an answer to the
question she had carried for a hundred years.

Then she turned away, because Vaelith Syrren was not a woman who lingered in
doorways when there was work to be done, and there was a great deal of work
to be done, and the morning when she would begin it was only one sleep away.
