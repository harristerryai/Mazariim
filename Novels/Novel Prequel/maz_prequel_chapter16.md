# Chapter Sixteen
## Terms of Exchange

They did not sleep well.

The Tower's vibration, which had been uncomfortable on the
approach and oppressive in the laboratory, became in the library
something intimate and inescapable: a presence that settled into
the body and refused to leave, that made the space behind the
eyes ache and the jaw clench and the muscles of the shoulders
tighten against a tension that was not physical but that the body
interpreted as physical because it had no other framework for
what the nexus produced. Tobias lay on the library floor with his
bedroll between him and the stone and his cittern case within
arm's reach and achieved perhaps three hours of fragmented rest,
each fragment interrupted by the sensation that the books on the
shelves around him were watching, which was not paranoia but
accurate perception, because the books were the medium through
which Vorthax observed and Vorthax did not sleep and had not
slept for three thousand years.

Vaelith did not attempt sleep. She sat cross-legged on the floor
with her notebook on her knee and the Geometrist's journals
stacked beside her and read by the light of a cantrip that glowed
warm and golden at her pen's tip, her eyes moving between the
ancient handwriting and her own notations with the speed of a
woman conducting a dialogue across millennia. The journals were
written in formal Yrugas Madra, whose academic register she could
follow with effort, and the mathematical content required no
translation at all, because mathematics was a language that
transcended the civilizations that spoke it. She recognized
Vorthax's theoretical framework within the first hour: the
geometric flow dynamics, the angular distribution predictions,
the intersection behavior models. He had reached her conclusions
three thousand years before she was born, by a different road
through the same mathematics, and the recognition produced in her
an emotion she could not have named and did not attempt to,
because naming it would have required acknowledging that fifty
years of her life's work had been anticipated by a man who was
now something that flickered between visibility and transparency
and spoke of himself as "one."

Sevan meditated. He sat at the library's center with his staff
across his knees and his eyes open to the luminous storm of the
five-scar convergence, and the scars burned around him in their
channels of impossible brightness while the awareness that
inhabited them pressed against his consciousness with the patience
of something that had waited long enough to find waiting itself a
form of communication. He did not speak. He did not write. He
observed, because observation was his gift and his duty and his
faith, and what he observed in the long hours of that night in
the Geometrist's library would take years to fully process and a
lifetime to fully understand.

Morning arrived without sunrise. The overcast sky lightened from
black to grey through the library's single east-facing window,
and the Tower's interior shifted from its nighttime state, where
the amber pulse of the laboratory matrix above provided the only
illumination besides their own magical lights, to a dim twilight
that revealed the dust motes suspended in air that had been still
for centuries and that their presence had disturbed into slow,
spiraling motion.

The voice came with the light.

"One has ordered one's thoughts." The words emerged from the walls
with a deliberation that had been absent the previous evening, each
syllable placed with care, the consciousness behind them having
spent the night preparing for a conversation it regarded as the
most important in three thousand years of existence. The Common was
clearer than before, the borrowed language settling into patterns
that more closely approximated natural speech, though the accent
remained, elongating vowels and softening consonants in ways that
marked the speaker as someone for whom this tongue was a garment
worn over older, more familiar shapes. "One has prepared what one
wishes to say. One asks that you permit one to say it without
interruption, because one has found, in the ordering, that the
thoughts connect, and that breaking the connections would require
one to begin again, and one does not trust one's patience to
survive a second attempt."

"We will listen," Sevan said.

"The priest speaks for the group." A pause. "One accepts this.
The priest has demonstrated a quality that one has not encountered
in three thousand years of visitors. The quality is not
intelligence, though the priest possesses that. It is not
perception, though the priest possesses that in measures one
envies. The quality is..." The voice searched, the walls producing
faint sounds that were not words but the shadows of words
considered and rejected. "Witness. The priest witnesses. One has
been observed by many things, by the scars and the nexus and the
slow regard of entropy. But one has not been witnessed. The
distinction is, one has discovered, significant."

Sevan said nothing. His silence was itself an answer, the Mauian
practice of receiving without judgment, and Tobias saw the effect
of that silence on the walls around them: a subtle relaxation of
the resonance, as if the Tower's stones had been clenched and
were now, marginally, easing.

"One will begin with the price," Vorthax said. "Because the price
is simple, and simplicity should precede complexity, and because
one has found, in three thousand years of thinking, that beginning
with what one needs is more honest than building toward it through
a scaffolding of what one offers."

The matrix above them pulsed. The library's ambient light shifted
from grey to the faintest amber, the Tower responding to its
inhabitant's emotional state the way a face responded to the
feelings behind it.

"One requires acknowledgment."

The word fell into the library and lay there, bare, stripped of
the academic ornamentation and the defensive bitterness that had
characterized the previous evening's speech. One word. The
architecture of the request that had been building behind it for
three millennia.

"If you succeed. If the knowledge one provides enables the
recreation of ley-lines, by your hands or by the hands of those
who follow you, in one's existence or in whatever passes for
one's existence, or in the lifetimes of scholars not yet born."
The voice was steady, controlled, the ordered thoughts delivered
as promised, but beneath the control Tobias heard the strain of
a mind holding itself together through discipline rather than
stability. "One requires that the contribution of Vorthax
Caldemur Morannis be recorded. That one's name appear in whatever
document describes the recreation. That those who complete the
work know that a scholar spent one hundred and fifty-five years
pursuing the understanding that made their achievement possible,
and that the scholar's name was Vorthax, and that he built a
tower in the highlands and died there and waited there and
preserved what he knew because he believed, against all evidence
and all reason and all the patient arguments of entropy, that
someone would come."

The amber light pulsed once, strongly, and the pulse traveled
through the library's walls and floor and ceiling with a force
that was not physical but that the body registered as physical:
a heartbeat, magnified, the cardiac rhythm of something that no
longer possessed a heart using the only medium available to
express what a heart would have expressed if it still beat in a
chest of flesh and bone.

"That is the price. That is all of it. One does not require
power. One does not require worship. One does not require the
restoration of one's living form, which would be, one suspects,
an aesthetic disappointment to all parties." The dry aside surfaced
through the emotion with the particular buoyancy of a mind whose
humor survived conditions that destroyed everything else. "One
requires that one not be forgotten. One requires that the silence
of three thousand years not be the final word. One requires that
one's work, if it bears fruit, bear one's name upon the branch."

The silence that followed was complete. The matrix pulsed. The
books on their shelves held whatever peace books hold, the
accumulated patience of bound pages waiting to be read. And the
three living people in the library understood, each through their
own experience, that the price was not a price at all. It was a
need. And the need was not monstrous. It was, in its way, the
most human thing that the inhuman consciousness had expressed.

Sevan was the first to speak. He rose from his seated position,
his staff in his hand, and he stood facing the wall from which
Vorthax's voice had most recently emerged, addressing the
consciousness with the directness of a man who had spent the
night in contemplation and had found, in the contemplation, his
answer.

"Vorthax Caldemur Morannis," he said, and the name, spoken in
full, rang in the library like a bell struck after a long silence,
the sound carrying through the Tower's stone with a resonance that
the architecture amplified until the name seemed to exist in every
room and corridor and twisted staircase simultaneously. "Magister
of Theoretical Geometries. Builder of the Tower at the five-scar
convergence. Scholar of the ley-line network and its geometric
principles."

He opened his journal. He held it before him, the small
leather-bound book that had accompanied him from the Library of
the Ages through the Athenaeum's depths and across the highland
moorland to this place, and he wrote, in the careful hand that
his training demanded, the words he spoke aloud:

"I will record your name, your work, and your contribution in the
Library of the Ages, where it will be preserved alongside the
knowledge of every civilization that has sought to understand the
world. This I promise as a Watcher of Mau, whose mandate is to
observe, to record, and to ensure that what has been witnessed is
never lost."

He looked up from the journal. His pale eyes, steady and clear,
addressed the awareness in the walls with the weight of a promise
made not merely to a wraith but to his faith, his institution,
and the tradition of preservation that both represented.

The Tower's response was immediate and involuntary. The amber
light flared. The walls vibrated with a frequency that was not
the nexus hum but something layered over it, a harmonic that the
consciousness could not control because it was not a decision but
a reaction, the response of something that had been promised the
one thing it needed by a man whose profession was the keeping of
promises. The frequency was high, almost painful, and it lasted
for three heartbeats before the consciousness caught it, contained
it, forced it back into the measured control that scholarly
discipline demanded.

"The priest," Vorthax said, and the words were rough, less
controlled than anything he had said since the ordered thoughts
began, the borrowed Common cracking under the pressure of whatever
the consciousness was experiencing. "The priest unsettles one. One
did not expect. One prepared for negotiation. One prepared for
calculation, for barter, for the mercantile exchange of value for
value. One did not prepare for..." The voice fragmented, the left
wall producing "sincerity" and the right wall producing "faith"
and the ceiling producing a word in ancient Madra that had no
Common translation. The fragments did not resolve. They hung in
the library's air like notes from an instrument that had been
played once and never again.

Vaelith spoke next. She had risen from her position on the floor,
her notebook closed, her green eyes bright with the particular
intensity that accompanied her most significant statements: the
moments when the analytical framework she maintained as armor
was set aside and the person beneath it spoke without calculation.

"Your name will appear in my research," she said. "If my work
produces results, if ley-lines are recreated through principles
that your research established, I will credit your contribution
in every publication, every communication, every record that bears
my name. This I promise as a scholar whose own work was dismissed
for decades and who understands, perhaps better than anyone else
in this room, what it means to pursue truth without recognition."

"The mage understands," Vorthax said, and his voice was quieter,
steadier, the consciousness regaining its equilibrium through the
familiar ground of scholarly recognition. "One knew the mage would
understand. One recognized the shape of it in the mage's
equations. The shape of a mind that works alone because no one
else perceives the significance of the work. One has worn that
shape for a very long time. One had forgotten that others might
wear it too."

Tobias looked at the wall where the voice concentrated, at the
carved equations that were the visible evidence of a mind's
complete obsession, and he made his own assessment: not of the
entity's emotional state, which was transparent, but of the
entity's reliability, which was the variable that mattered for
what came next. The wraith was genuine. The need was real. The
price was modest by any standard except the standard of a
consciousness that had nothing else to give and nothing else to
ask for. And the promises his companions had made were promises
they would keep, because the priest's faith demanded it and the
mage's integrity demanded it and neither of them was capable of
offering words they did not mean.

"I will ensure that the right people know," he said. "The people
who make decisions, who allocate resources, who determine which
names are remembered and which are forgotten. This I promise as
a man whose profession is information and whose word, when given
honestly, has never been broken."

"The gnome," Vorthax said, and the voice carried something that
was not warmth, because the consciousness was not capable of
warmth, but that occupied the adjacent territory: the grudging
acknowledgment of a mind that had assessed a person and found them,
against expectation, acceptable. "The gnome speaks of honest words
as if honesty were a tool that could be deployed or withheld at
will. One finds this disturbing and admirable in equal measure.
One suspects the gnome finds it the same."

"I do," Tobias said.

"Then we have an accord." The amber light steadied. The harmonic
in the walls resolved into the baseline nexus hum, and the
Tower's atmosphere shifted from the charged intensity of
negotiation to something calmer, something that was not relaxation
but the reduction of tension from unbearable to merely
considerable. "And now one will share what one has promised,
because one has waited three thousand years to explain what one
has discovered to someone capable of comprehension, and one
finds, now that the moment has arrived, that one's patience for
further delay has been entirely exhausted."

---

Vorthax's explanation began in the laboratory, where the amber
matrix pulsed with the steadied rhythm of a consciousness that
had found its audience and intended to use it.

He did not manifest visually. The effort of the previous evening's
appearance had cost something that the night had not fully
restored, and the voice from the walls carried a transparency that
suggested the consciousness was conserving its resources for the
intellectual expenditure ahead. But the projections returned: the
three-dimensional diagrams of ley-line configurations that had
served as a display of pride the evening before now served as
illustrations, each one summoned with the practiced timing of a
lecturer who had spent one hundred and fifty-five years preparing
for a class and who was determined, now that the students had
finally arrived, that the lecture would be flawless.

"The seed problem," Vorthax said, and the words carried the weight
of a scholar naming the subject to which he had devoted his life.
Amber light coalesced above the matrix into a geometric diagram:
a simplified representation of a ley-line, a channel of energy
flowing between two nexus points, its behavior described by
mathematical relationships that glowed in the air with the
precision of inscriptions carved in light. "The question that
one spent one's living years attempting to answer and one's dead
years contemplating. How were the ley-lines created? What
initiated the flow of mana through the geometric channels? What
was the nature of the catalyst that the Akovarians called
sacred and that modern scholars call divine?"

Vaelith's notebook was open. Her pen was moving. Tobias noted
that her handwriting, normally rapid but legible, had acquired a
tremor that was not physical weakness but the strain of a hand
trying to keep pace with a mind that was absorbing information
faster than notation could capture it.

"The answer is precision," Vorthax said, and the projection
shifted, the simplified ley-line expanding into a complex web of
intersecting channels whose geometric relationships were described
by equations that materialized in the air beside them. "Not
energy. This was one's error for ninety years. This was, one
suspects, the mage's error for much of her fifty." He paused,
and the pause was not the fragmented searching of the previous
evening but the deliberate timing of a lecturer allowing a
crucial point to settle before elaborating. "The initiation of a
ley-line does not require divine power in the sense of raw magical
force. It requires divine exactitude. The geometric configuration
that initiates mana flow must be established with an accuracy
that exceeds mortal calculation by a margin that no instrument
one built could bridge."

The projection brightened. New equations appeared, layered over
the channel diagram, describing relationships that Vaelith's pen
chased across the page with increasing speed.

"Asti did not power the network through energy," Vorthax
continued. "Asti powered the network through precision. The entity
perceived geometric relationships with a fidelity that permitted
the initial configuration to be established with zero error, and
once established, the flow sustained itself, because the geometry
of a correctly initiated ley-line is self-reinforcing. The
channel's own structure maintains the flow, the way the banks of
a river maintain the water's course. The divine contribution was
not the water. It was the shaping of the banks. It was the
perfection of the geometry."

"The precision hypothesis," Vaelith breathed. The words emerged
without deliberation, the instinctive response of a mind
recognizing a truth it had approached from the other side. "I
proposed it. In my most recent theoretical work. The factor
reduction from nine hundred to seventy-five."

"The mage was correct in principle." The projections shifted again,
the equations rearranging themselves to incorporate new terms that
Vaelith's pen scrambled to follow. "The mage was incorrect in
magnitude. The factor is not seventy-five. It is twelve."

Vaelith's pen stopped. She looked up from her notebook with an
expression that Tobias had never seen on her face, an expression
that combined the shock of mathematical revelation with the
almost physical impact of having a variable she had struggled
with for decades suddenly resolve into a value she had not
predicted.

"Twelve," she said. "That is, the implications of that, the
energy requirement at a factor of twelve would be..."

"Within the scope of mortal institutions, given sufficient
coordination and resources. Yes. One is aware." The dryness in
the voice was the dryness of a man who had arrived at that same
conclusion one hundred and fifty-five years into his research and
who had spent the remaining decades of his life attempting to
clear the final obstacle and failing. "The reduction is steeper
than the mage's models predict because the models do not account
for a property of the scar network that one discovered only
through decades of direct perception."

The projections changed again. The ley-line diagram faded,
replaced by a representation of the scar network itself: dormant
channels, their luminescence dim, their edges wavering, but their
geometric relationships precise, maintained across millennia of
dormancy by a property that the diagram now illustrated through
pulses of residual light that traced the original flow patterns
like water remembering the path it once carved through stone.

"The scars retain geometric memory," Vorthax said. "They remember
the configurations they once held. A ley-line initiated along an
existing scar requires less precision than one initiated in virgin
space, because the scar provides a template. The residual
coherence of the dormant channel reduces the accuracy requirement
by the ratio of retained geometry to accumulated entropy. The
factor of twelve already accounts for this reduction. Along an
existing scar, the geometry does half the work. The mortal
contribution need only supply the other half."

The laboratory was silent except for the scratch of Vaelith's
pen, which had resumed at a speed that bordered on violence, and
the pulse of the matrix, which had quickened with the rhythm of
a consciousness sharing its most significant discovery and
finding, in the sharing, a satisfaction that three millennia of
solitary contemplation had been unable to provide.

"The scar network is not merely a record of what was," Vorthax
said, and for a moment his voice lost the cadence of the lecturer
and carried instead the quiet intensity of a man speaking from
conviction so deep it had survived the death of everything else
he believed. "It is a foundation for what might be. The geometry
persists. The template endures. What one spent one's life proving,
and what the mage has spent her decades approaching, is not merely
that the network existed but that the network can exist again.
Not through divine intervention. Through mortal understanding of
divine geometry, applied along channels that remember what they
once carried."

He stopped. The projections faded. The laboratory returned to its
baseline amber pulse, and the consciousness that had been
speaking with the focused intensity of a century and a half of
accumulated understanding settled back into the ambient resonance
of the Tower's stones.

"One required a moment," Vorthax said, after a silence of
several heartbeats. "One has not spoken these thoughts aloud
since, since." The repetition was not the fragmented searching
of the previous evening but something simpler and sadder: the
inability to remember the last time he had shared his work with
another mind. "One apologizes. The experience of being understood
is, one has discovered, physiologically overwhelming, which is
notable given that one no longer possesses a physiology."

---

The portal came next.

Vorthax described it with the precision of a man who had spent
ten years learning its properties and fifty years using it and
three thousand years watching it from the prison of a Tower he
could not leave and a door he could not walk through.

"It was not created," he said. "It was discovered. When the
ley-line network detonated approximately 6,830 years before the
War, the energy release tore openings between locations that had
been connected by the network's strongest channels. Most collapsed
within hours. A few, at points where convergent scars concentrated
the residual energy, persisted." The voice paused, and the pause
carried a quality that Tobias identified as the particular pain
of a man describing something precious that was also a source of
torment. "The opening beneath this Tower connects the five-scar
nexus to a seven-scar nexus at the Temple of the Seventh
Convergence on Akovar. One found it seventy years into one's
research. One spent ten years learning to stabilize it. One spent
fifty years walking through it to the place where the ley-line
network was born."

"Walking through it," Vaelith said, and her voice caught on the
implication. "You made expeditions. To Akovar. Through a stable
portal. For fifty years."

"Fifty years. Dozens of expeditions. One recovered texts, artifacts,
observations. One translated inscriptions. One studied the Temple's
crystalline matrix, a focusing device fifty feet tall, compared
to which the apparatus in one's laboratory above is a child's toy
constructed from inadequate materials by a mind that understood
principles but lacked the engineering tradition to implement them
properly." The self-deprecation was not false modesty; it was the
honest assessment of a scholar who had seen what mastery looked
like and who knew, with the uncomfortable clarity of genius
confronting greater genius, that his own achievements were
preliminary. "The matrix encodes the exact parameters of ley-line
creation. Its geometric structure is a record, a blueprint
preserved in crystal. Everything the Akovarians knew about
building the network is there."

"Then why did you not succeed?" Tobias asked. The question was
direct, and he asked it directly, because the narrative had a gap
in it and gaps were where the important information lived.

The silence that followed was different from the silences that
had preceded it. It was the silence of a consciousness confronting
its own failure, and the amber light dimmed, fractionally, as if
the admission cost the matrix energy it could not spare.

"Because one could not achieve the precision," Vorthax said.
"Even a factor of twelve exceeds the accuracy of any instrument
one built. One came close. One came very close. In one's final
decades, one attempted alternatives: binding celestial essences,
concentrating draconic resonances, focusing the nexus energy
itself into configurations that approximated the required
geometry. Nothing worked. The final margin, the last increment of
accuracy between what one could achieve and what the initiation
required, was beyond one. One was still attempting to close that
margin when."

The voice stopped. Did not trail off, did not fade, simply
stopped, the way a mechanism stops when a gear breaks: mid-
motion, without completion.

"When one stopped living," Vorthax finished, after a pause of
five heartbeats. "The details of the transition are not, one
finds, pleasant to recount. The Hollow Sepulcher's creation sent
its influence through the scar network. The nexus concentrated
that influence. One was in the midst of an experiment. The
energies interacted. One became what one is."

The library was very quiet. Tobias heard the wind outside the
Tower's walls, the first sound from the exterior world that had
penetrated the building's interior since they entered. The wind
sounded very far away, as if the Tower existed at a remove from
the ordinary world that distance alone could not explain.

"The portal," Tobias said, redirecting toward the practical
because the practical was where his competence resided and because
the conversation had entered territory whose emotional depth
exceeded what his professional training equipped him to navigate.
"You said it connects this Tower to the Temple. Is it stable? Is
it safe? Can we use it?"

"It is stable. It is as safe as a wound in the fabric of space
can be, which is to say that it is stable and one has used it
dozens of times without incident and one must also acknowledge
that it is a wound in the fabric of space and that wounds are, by
their nature, not entirely predictable." The measured response
reasserted the scholarly cadence, and Tobias heard the
consciousness's gratitude for the redirection, the relief of
returning to technical description after the exposure of personal
history. "The portal is self-maintaining, powered by the nexus
energy on both endpoints. The passage requires approximately
three seconds from the traveler's perspective. The primary risk
is temporal: the portal occasionally experiences phase variance,
a misalignment between the temporal frames of the two endpoints
that can displace the traveler's arrival by minutes or, rarely,
hours. One has never experienced a displacement exceeding one day,
and the variance has never operated in reverse. You will always
arrive after you depart."

"And the Temple?" Vaelith's pen had not stopped moving since the
seed problem's explanation, her notebook filling with the dense
notation of a mind cataloguing everything it received against the
framework it possessed. "What will we find there?"

"Ruin and preservation in equal measure," Vorthax said. "The upper
levels of the pyramid were destroyed in the ley-line detonation
and consumed by jungle in the millennia since. The administrative
levels have suffered partial collapse and water damage. But the
Engineering Core, the deepest level, where the portal opens, is
substantially intact. The Akovarian preservation enchantments
were designed to survive catastrophe, and they have fulfilled
their design."

A projection materialized above the matrix: not equations this
time but a diagram, a schematic of the Temple's underground
levels rendered in amber light, its corridors and chambers and
functional spaces labeled in geometric characters that Vorthax
translated as he described them.

"The Flow Chamber, where the crystalline matrix stands. The
Monitoring Station, where the network's operation was tracked.
The Archive Vault, where the engineering documentation is stored:
specifications, procedures, theoretical foundations. Approximately
one third of the Vault's contents were destroyed by water
intrusion. The remainder is the most comprehensive collection of
ley-line knowledge in existence."

He paused, and the projection shifted, focusing on a chamber
adjacent to the Archive Vault. The diagram showed a circular
room, smaller than the others, marked with symbols that Vorthax
did not immediately translate.

"And the guardians," Sevan said.

The voice was quiet for five heartbeats. When it resumed, the
tone had changed: not the lecturer's precision, not the madman's
fragmentation, but something between, the careful speech of a
consciousness approaching a subject it found difficult.

"Seven priests," Vorthax said. "Geometer-priests of the Temple's
technical order, present in the Engineering Core when the
detonation occurred. The wild magic that flooded the deep levels
offered them a choice that was not entirely a choice: dissolution,
or preservation in a state that one cannot classify by any
taxonomy one possesses. They are not dead. They are not alive.
They are not undead, as one is undead. They are suspended. Held
in fields of energy that the Akovarian enchantments maintain."

"Can they be communicated with?" Vaelith asked.

"One does not know. In fifty years of expeditions, one did not
attempt contact." The admission was delivered with a discomfort
that suggested the omission had not been oversight but avoidance.
"One's interest was in the documentation, not the documentarians.
One acknowledges that this was, perhaps, a limitation of
perspective."

"We may need to make a different choice," Sevan said, and the
words carried a gentleness that was also a correction, the quiet
insistence of a man whose faith did not permit him to treat
preserved consciousness as an inconvenience to be worked around
rather than a presence to be acknowledged.

Vorthax did not respond immediately. The silence held the quality
of a mind confronting a perspective it had not considered and
finding the confrontation uncomfortable. When the voice returned,
it was careful, each word selected with the deliberation of a
consciousness that had been challenged on ethical ground it had
not known it occupied.

"The priest unsettles one," Vorthax said. "One has observed this
before. One observes it again. The priest treats one's condition
with a regard that one did not request and does not know how to
receive. One is accustomed to being feared, or pitied, or studied.
One is not accustomed to being witnessed." The pause that followed
was long enough that Tobias began to count heartbeats again, and
he had reached nine before the voice resumed. "One will consider
the priest's implication. One has, one notes, considerable time
for consideration."

---

They prepared throughout the morning.

Tobias organized supplies with the systematic efficiency of a man
packing for an operation whose parameters were unprecedented in
his experience: water, food for two days, scholarly materials in
their protective containers, weapons, tools, light sources. He
distributed the weight across three packs, keeping the most
critical materials, the Akovarian Fragments and the Conjunction
Stones in Vaelith's satchel, the Resonance Atlas in its
protective tube across his own back, and the journals and
notebooks in Sevan's care.

Vaelith spent the morning in the laboratory, asking Vorthax
technical questions about the Temple's layout with the rapid
precision of a woman planning a research expedition and the urgent
awareness of a woman planning an infiltration of a seven-thousand-
year-old ruin on an uninhabited continent. The Geometrist answered
with a patience that surprised Tobias, the madness of the previous
evening held in check by the intellectual engagement that was,
apparently, the most effective medicine for whatever ailed a
three-thousand-year-old consciousness.

Sevan sat in the library and wrote. He recorded everything: the
seed problem's solution, the portal's properties, the Temple's
layout, the guardians' existence. He wrote with the steady hand
of a man performing a sacred duty, each word chosen with the
precision that his training demanded, each observation placed
in the context of the thirty-six years of preceding observation
that gave it meaning.

At midday, Tobias descended the staircase to the Tower's
basement.

The stairs continued below the ground floor in a tight spiral
carved from the living bedrock, the walls unadorned by the
geometric patterns that covered the upper levels. The air grew
cooler and drier with each turn, and the nexus vibration changed
character: less a hum and more a pulse, a rhythmic variation in
intensity that matched the amber light of the laboratory matrix
far above, as if the Tower's entire structure resonated at a
single frequency from its highest chamber to its deepest
foundations.

Three basement levels. The first held sealed containers and
preserved supplies. The second held workshop space with stone-
cutting tools and metalworking apparatus too large for the upper
laboratories. The third, the deepest, held the portal.

The portal chamber was circular, fifty feet in diameter, with a
domed ceiling that rose thirty feet at its apex. The walls were
covered in geometric patterns that Vorthax identified, through
the voice that followed them even here, as Akovarian in origin:
copied from the Temple's own inscriptions during his decades of
expeditions and adapted to stabilize the portal's connection.
The patterns glowed with the same amber light as the matrix above,
but steadier, less pulsing, as if the energy that powered the
portal had achieved an equilibrium that the laboratory's
instrument had not.

The portal occupied the chamber's center.

Tobias had seen magical phenomena in thirty years of work across
the Inner Kingdoms. He had encountered illusions, wards, enchanted
objects, divine manifestations, and the occasional display of raw
force that left observers shaken. He had never seen anything like
the portal.

It was not a door. It was not a window. It was a sphere: ten feet
in diameter, suspended above the chamber floor at the precise
point where the five ley-line scars converged beneath, drawn
together and held by forces that operated according to the same
geometric principles that governed the stars and the planets and
every body of sufficient mass or sufficient magic that cohered
into the shape the universe preferred. The magic had gathered here
across millennia, pooling at the wound the detonation had torn,
and it had stabilized in the only form that balanced perfectly in
all directions at once, the same form that water assumed in the
absence of gravity, that molten iron assumed in the heart of a
forge before the smith's hammer imposed a lesser shape. A sphere,
because a sphere was what magic became when nothing compelled it
to be otherwise, when the forces acting upon it were equal from
every direction and the energy found its own equilibrium.

Its surface, if surface was the correct term, shimmered with a
luminescence that was neither light nor darkness but something
between, the visual equivalent of the nexus vibration's sub-
auditory hum, a phenomenon that the eyes registered without the
mind's capacity to categorize it.

And through that curved surface, the world it opened upon was
inverted. Tobias saw the Temple's distant interior as if reflected
in a glass globe held at arm's length: the image wrapped around
the sphere's curvature, bending at the edges, compressed and
distorted by the geometry of the connection so that straight
corridors became arcs and flat ceilings curved toward themselves
and the warm light of the far side seemed to emanate from every
point of the sphere simultaneously, as though the entirety of the
Temple's portal chamber had been folded inward and pressed against
the interior of the sphere's skin. Stone of a different color
and texture than the local bedrock, inverted and curving away from
the eye. Vegetation, green and dense, visible as a warped blur at
the sphere's lower hemisphere where the connection wavered. The
closer he looked, the more the image resisted comprehension, the
geometry of a distant place rendered in the grammar of a sphere
that insisted on showing everything at once and nothing in the
proportions the eye expected.

"Akovar," Vaelith whispered. She had followed him down, drawn by
the same gravity that pulled scholars toward primary sources and
explorers toward uncharted maps.

Sevan arrived last. He stood before the portal and perceived what
his companions could not: through the sphere, through the
connection that the ley-line detonation had torn and Vorthax had
stabilized, the scar network continued. The five scars that
converged beneath this Tower did not terminate at the portal. They
passed through it, connecting to the far more complex network that
had once served the Temple of the Seventh Convergence, and beyond
the portal the scars burned with an intensity that made the
Tower's five-scar nexus seem a candle held against the sun. Seven
scars, converging at a point that was not merely bright but
incandescent, a knot of luminescence so concentrated that his
gift could not resolve its details, could perceive only the
overwhelming radiance of a nexus that had been the heart of the
greatest magical achievement in the history of the world and that
burned, seven thousand years after its destruction, with a light
that nothing had extinguished. Through the sphere's curvature the
scars bent and wrapped, their luminous channels following the
portal's geometry, so that the entire Akovarian convergence
appeared to him as a radiant web folded upon itself, compressed
into a sphere of fire and memory.

"It is still alive," he said. "The Akovarian nexus. The scars
are not dormant. They cycle energy in patterns that have persisted
since the detonation."

"Active cycling at a seven-scar convergence," Vaelith said, and
her voice carried the tone of a woman whose theoretical models
were being confirmed and exceeded simultaneously. "If the
crystalline matrix is intact and the Archive Vault contains the
engineering documentation, then everything we need is on the
other side of that portal."

She looked at Tobias. He looked at her. The exchange carried the
weight of a decision that analysis alone could not make.

"Tomorrow," Tobias said. "We cross tomorrow. Tonight we rest, we
prepare, and we have a very thorough conversation about what we
are walking into. The Temple has waited seven thousand years. It
can wait one more night."

"One concurs with the gnome," Vorthax said, his voice emerging
from the portal chamber's walls with a quieter resonance than the
upper levels produced, the geometric patterns here conducting his
speech with a different tonal quality. "Preparation is the
difference between expedition and misadventure. One learned this
through extensive experience with misadventure."

Tobias turned from the portal, from the shimmer of a continent
seven thousand years removed from the world he knew, from the
warm light wrapped around a sphere of impossible geometry and the
green blur curving inward upon itself and the promise of answers
that had been waiting since before the nations of the Inner
Kingdoms existed.

"One more night," he said. And then, to the awareness in the
walls, to the consciousness that had guarded this door for three
millennia and that would, tomorrow, watch three strangers walk
through it in his stead: "Guard the door."

"One has guarded it for three thousand years," Vorthax replied.
"A few hours more will not strain one's patience."

They ascended. Behind them, in the portal chamber's amber
silence, the sphere of gathered magic hung in its perfect
equilibrium, and upon its curved surface the ruins of Akovar
waited in their inverted reflection, the image of a world folded
inward with the stillness of a place that had been holding its
breath for seven thousand years and that could, it seemed, hold
it a little longer.
