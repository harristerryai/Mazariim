# Chapter 1: Core Concepts & Character Progression

This chapter explains how characters grow in power over the course of a campaign. It covers the core mechanics of stat Pools, Edge, and Effort, and provides progression charts for different campaign styles.

---

## Core Concepts

### Stat Pools

Every player character has three defining statistics: Might, Speed, and Intellect. Each stat has a Pool, which represents your raw, innate ability in that area.

**Might** defines how strong and durable your character is. It governs actions from forcing doors open to walking for days without food to resisting disease. It is also the primary means of determining how much physical damage your character can sustain. Physical characters, tough characters, and characters interested in fighting should focus on Might.

**Speed** describes how fast and physically coordinated your character is. It governs dodging attacks, sneaking around quietly, throwing a ball accurately, and similar actions. Nimble, fast, or sneaky characters will want good Speed stats, as will those interested in ranged combat.

**Intellect** determines how smart, knowledgeable, and likable your character is. It governs solving puzzles, remembering facts, telling convincing lies, and using supernatural powers. Characters interested in communicating effectively, being learned scholars, or wielding magical powers should stress their Intellect stat.

When your character is injured, sickened, or attacked, you temporarily lose points from one of your stat Pools. Physical damage reduces your Might Pool, effects that make you clumsy reduce your Speed Pool, and mental or magical attacks reduce your Intellect Pool. You can also spend points from your stat Pools to apply Effort or activate special abilities. You can rest to recover lost points through recovery rolls.

### Edge

Your Edge represents knowing how to use what you have — efficiency and effortlessness. When something requires you to spend points from a stat Pool, your Edge for that stat reduces the cost. It also reduces the cost of applying Effort to a roll.

For example, if activating a special ability costs 3 points from your Intellect Pool and you have an Intellect Edge of 2, you only spend 1 point. If your Edge reduces the cost to 0, you can use the ability for free.

Your Edge can be different for each stat. A character who has a low Might Pool but a high Might Edge can perform Might actions consistently and efficiently, because their Edge reduces the cost of spending points from their Pool.

### Effort

When your character really needs to accomplish a task, you can apply Effort. Applying Effort costs points from the appropriate stat Pool and eases the task by one step per level of Effort applied. You must decide to apply Effort before you attempt the roll. Every character has an Effort score, which indicates the maximum number of levels of Effort that can be applied to a single roll.

The cost per level of Effort is determined by your campaign's progression chart. In most campaigns, each level of Effort costs 2 points from the appropriate stat Pool. In grittier campaigns, each level may cost 3 points instead. Your relevant Edge is subtracted from the total cost of applying Effort.

For example, if Effort costs 2 points per level and you apply two levels of Effort to a Speed roll, the base cost is 4 points. If you have a Speed Edge of 2, you subtract that from the cost, spending only 2 points from your Speed Pool.

#### Effort and Damage

Instead of applying Effort to ease your attack roll, you can apply Effort to increase the amount of damage you inflict. For each level of Effort applied to damage, you inflict 3 additional points of damage. When using Effort to increase the damage of an area attack, you inflict 2 additional points of damage per level instead of 3, but the additional damage applies to all targets in the area. Even if one or more targets resist the attack, they still take 1 point of damage.

#### Multiple Uses of Effort and Edge

If your Effort is 2 or higher, you can apply Effort to multiple aspects of a single action. For example, if you make an attack, you can apply Effort to your attack roll and also apply Effort to increase the damage. The total amount of Effort you apply across all aspects cannot exceed your Effort score. You can use Edge for a particular stat only once per action, regardless of how many ways you spend points from that stat's Pool.

### Skill Ranks

Training in skills follows a tiered progression determined by your campaign's progression chart. The Max Rank column on the chart indicates the highest level of training available at each character level.

**Trained** (Rank 1): You ease related tasks by one step. Available at levels 1–3 in most campaigns.

**Specialized** (Rank 2): You ease related tasks by two steps. Typically available starting at level 4.

**Expert** (Rank 3): You ease related tasks by three steps. Typically available starting at level 7.

The exact level at which each rank becomes available is set by the Max Rank column of your campaign's progression chart. Some campaign styles may restrict maximum ranks further.

### Assets

Assets are benefits that ease a task, similar to skills. They can come from favorable circumstances, help from allies, equipment, or other advantages. The maximum number of assets that can apply to a single task is determined by your campaign's progression chart — typically 2, but some campaigns allow up to 3 while grittier campaigns may restrict it to 1.

## Progression Charts

Each progression chart defines how characters advance from level 1 to level 10. The columns are defined as follows:

**LVL**: The character's current level.

**Edge**: The total number of Edge points the character has gained from leveling (distributed among Might, Speed, and Intellect as the player chooses, subject to type restrictions).

**Effort**: The character's maximum Effort score — the most levels of Effort they can apply to a single roll.

**Pool**: The number of new stat Pool points gained at this level (distributed among Pools as the player chooses).

**Max Rank**: The highest skill rank available at this level (1 = Trained, 2 = Specialized, 3 = Expert).

**Abilities**: The number of new special abilities gained from your type at this level.

**Focus**: An asterisk (*) indicates you gain a new focus ability at this level. Characters gain six focus abilities over ten levels, always at levels 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, and 8.

**Skill**: A checkmark (✓) indicates you gain training in one normal skill of your choice (such as Perception, Stealth, Persuasion, Athletics, or any other noncombat skill). If you are already trained, you become specialized (if your Max Rank allows). If already specialized, you become expert (if your Max Rank allows).

**Martial/Spell**: A checkmark (✓) indicates you also gain one advancement chosen from your type's martial skills list OR your type's spellcasting skills list. You choose only one; you do not gain one of each.

## Skill, Martial, and Spell Advancement

### Skill

At every level, you gain training in one normal skill of your choice (such as Perception, Stealth, Persuasion, Athletics, or any other noncombat skill). If you are already trained in that skill, you become specialized (if your Max Rank allows it). If already specialized, you become expert (if your Max Rank allows it).

### Martial/Spell

At levels where the chart indicates "Martial/Spell," you also gain one advancement chosen from your type's martial skills list OR your type's spellcasting skills list. You choose only one; you do not gain one of each. Not all types have access to both categories.

The options available depend on your type's category and current tier:

#### Warrior Martial Options (Fighter, Barbarian, Rogue)

- Low Tier (Levels 1–3): Skill with Attacks, Skill with Defense, Experienced in Armor
- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Mastery with Attacks, Mastery with Defense, Mastery in Armor
- High Tier (Levels 7–10): Expertise with Attacks, Expertise with Defense

Warriors have no spellcasting options.

#### Gish Martial Options (Paladin, Magus, Ranger)

- Low Tier (Levels 1–3): Skill with Attacks, Skill with Defense, Experienced in Armor
- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Mastery with Attacks, Mastery with Defense
- High Tier (Levels 7–10): Mastery in Armor

#### Gish Spellcasting Options (Paladin, Magus, Ranger)

- Low Tier (Levels 1–3): Spell Focus
- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): No spellcasting options
- High Tier (Levels 7–10): Greater Spell Focus

#### Caster Martial Options (Mage, Priest, Druid)

- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Skill with Attacks, Skill with Defense, Experienced in Armor

Casters have no martial options at low or high tier. No mastery or expertise in martial skills.

#### Caster Spellcasting Options (Mage, Priest, Druid)

- Low Tier (Levels 1–3): Spell Focus, Skill with Intellect Defense
- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Greater Spell Focus, Mastery with Intellect Defense
- High Tier (Levels 7–10): Supreme Spell Focus, Expertise with Intellect Defense

#### Explorer Martial Options (Bard)

- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Skill with Attacks, Skill with Defense, Experienced in Armor

The Bard has no martial options at low or high tier. No mastery or expertise in martial skills.

#### Explorer Spellcasting Options (Bard)

- Low Tier (Levels 1–3): Spell Focus, Skill with Intellect Defense
- Mid Tier (Levels 4–6): Mastery with Intellect Defense
- High Tier (Levels 7–10): Greater Spell Focus, Expertise with Intellect Defense

## Martial Skill Definitions

**Skill with Attacks**: Choose one weapon type or weapon group (such as light bladed, heavy bludgeoning, ranged, etc.). You are trained in attacks using that weapon type.

**Skill with Defense**: Choose one type of defense task (Might, Speed, or Intellect). You are trained in that defense.

**Experienced in Armor**: You reduce the Speed penalty for wearing armor by 1.

**Mastery with Attacks**: Choose one weapon type or weapon group. If you are already trained, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.

**Mastery with Defense**: Choose one type of defense task (Might, Speed, or Intellect). If you are already trained, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.

**Mastery in Armor**: You reduce the Speed penalty for wearing armor by an additional 1.

**Expertise with Attacks**: Choose one weapon type or weapon group. If you are already specialized, you become expert. If trained, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.

**Expertise with Defense**: Choose one type of defense task (Might, Speed, or Intellect). If you are already specialized, you become expert. If trained, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.

## Spellcasting Skill Definitions

**Spell Focus**: Choose one spell school, damage type, or focus. You are trained in Spellcraft rolls to cast spells of that school, damage type, or focus. Alternatively, if the spell is not an attack, reduces its Intellect cost by 1 point.

**Greater Spell Focus**: Choose one spell school, damage type, or focus in which you have Spell Focus. You become specialized in Spellcraft rolls to cast spells of that school, damage type, or focus. Alternatively, if the spell is not an attack, reduces its Intellect cost by 2 points.

**Supreme Spell Focus**: Choose one spell school, damage type, or focus in which you have Greater Spell Focus. You become expert in Spellcraft rolls to cast spells of that school, damage type, or focus. Alternatively, if the spell is not an attack, reduces its Intellect cost by 3 points.

**Skill with Intellect Defense**: You are trained in Intellect defense tasks.

**Mastery with Intellect Defense**: If you are already trained in Intellect defense, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.

**Expertise with Intellect Defense**: If you are already specialized in Intellect defense, you become expert. If trained, you become specialized. If untrained, you become trained.
