# Ruins and Lost Places of Nigal

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

The continent of Nigal is littered with the wreckage of civilizations that rose, flourished, and fell — some consumed by war, others by magical catastrophe, still others by the slow erosion of time and the indifference of the world that grew over their bones. Every region of the continent holds ruins of some kind, from the shattered ziggurats of the Dukhan Empire to the vine-strangled temple-pyramids of abandoned Azala city-states, from the frozen foundations exposed by retreating glaciers in the Frozen Isles to the cursed mass graves where the Negative Energy Plane presses close to reality and the dead do not rest.

These ruins are not merely historical curiosities. They are active features of the landscape — sources of danger, opportunity, knowledge, and power that shape the lives of the peoples who live near them. A farmer in the Jinook Valley who plows too close to a Dukhan ziggurat may unearth bones that twitch and reassemble in the moonlight. A dwarven miner who breaks through into a pre-War tunnel may find tools still warm from forges that burned out eight centuries ago — or may find what killed the miners who left them behind. A Chak'ti rider who camps near certain standing stones on the steppe may wake to find hoofprints circling the camp that belong to no living horse.

The ley-line reawakening of 815 AW has transformed the continent's relationship with its ruins. Ancient sites that had been inert for centuries or millennia have begun to pulse with magical energy as ley-line flows intersect buried structures built according to principles their creators understood but their descendants forgot. Pyramids glow faintly in the dark. Sealed tomb doors groan against their hinges. Warding circles carved into stone floors flicker with light that has not burned since the artisans who inscribed them turned to dust. Scholars from the Astral Circle, the Line-Watchers of Asti, and less reputable treasure-seekers have descended on Nigal's ruins in growing numbers, seeking to understand — or exploit — the magical renaissance stirring in the bones of the old world.

Not all of these sites welcome investigation. Some are guarded by wards that have outlasted the civilizations that created them. Others are inhabited — by undead, by monsters, by cultists, by hermits, or by things that defy easy classification. A few are sealed for very good reasons, their contents too dangerous to be disturbed by anyone, regardless of intention. The Hollow Sepulcher has been sealed for over four thousand years, and every generation produces someone fool enough to think they can handle what lies within. Most of them are never seen again. The rest come back changed.

This document catalogs the major and minor ruins of Nigal by region, describing their history, current condition, known dangers, and the effects — if any — of the ley-line reawakening on their dormant structures. It is not comprehensive. Nigal is vast, and new ruins are discovered every decade as erosion, earthquake, and magical disturbance expose what was buried. What follows is what is known — or at least what is rumored with enough consistency to warrant documentation.

## THE INNER KINGDOMS

The Inner Kingdoms are the safest region of Nigal, their roads patrolled and their monsters largely driven out by centuries of Federation governance. But the Madronian Peninsula's long history of habitation means that ruins lie beneath or beside nearly every settlement, and the oldest of them predate the Federation, the War, and even the founding of Beneshire. The Yrugas mageocracy ruled these lands for over a thousand years, and the scars of its collapse have never fully healed. More recent events — the Elemental Crisis of 819 AW, the Far Realm incursion of 824 AW, and the ongoing ley-line reawakening — have demonstrated that the Inner Kingdoms' ruins are not merely relics of the past but active fault lines where old dangers find new life.

### Major Ruins

#### The Athenaeum of Echoes

In the rocky highlands of eastern Beneshire, approximately ninety miles northeast of Wyebluff and twelve miles east of the village of Thornwick, the ruins of the Athenaeum of Echoes mark the site of one of Yrugas's most unusual institutions. Founded around 3,600 BW by a consortium of wizard-lords, the Athenaeum was a center for studying pre-Yrugas magical phenomena — locations where magic behaved anomalously, where spells were stronger or weaker, where the fabric of reality seemed permeable. Unlike most Yrugas facilities, which focused on accumulating personal power or controlling populations, the Athenaeum was dedicated to scholarly inquiry, making it an anomaly among the mageocracy's institutions. Over nine centuries of operation, the complex grew from a single tower into a sprawling campus of interconnected buildings, underground vaults, and specialized research chambers employing over two hundred mages, scribes, and support staff.

The Athenaeum organized its research into three divisions. The Resonance Observatory, built around a two-hundred-foot tower in the eastern wing, studied what Yrugas mages called "resonance phenomena" — locations where magic behaved differently — using a massive apparatus called the Oculus Terra that combined enchanted lenses, scrying pools, and crystalline matrices to perceive magical geography across vast distances. The Translation Chamber in the western wing was devoted to linguistic and historical research, where scholars worked to decipher texts from pre-Yrugas civilizations, including seventeen fragments of Akovarian text whose partial translations hinted at "lines of power," "geometric flows," and "the pyramid's heart." The Conjunction Archive occupied the underground levels, a vast library containing every text, map, observation record, and theoretical treatise the Athenaeum produced, along with "anomalous artifacts" — objects exhibiting unexplained magical properties recovered from the resonance zones the Observatory studied.

The Athenaeum's location was chosen for its magical properties. Early Yrugas surveyors identified the highland site as a "zone of persistent resonance" where divination showed unusual clarity and magical effects lingered longer than normal. What the founders did not know was that they had built atop a minor ley-line nexus — three dormant ley-line scars intersecting beneath the highlands, remnants of channels that had once carried magical energy before the Akovarian catastrophe over three thousand years earlier. The scars no longer functioned, but they retained a shadow of their former nature, amplifying spellwork conducted within the complex and making the Oculus Terra effective. The nexus also made the Athenaeum dangerous: researchers reported vivid dreams, disorientation, and glimpses of places that did not exist, and several mages went mad or vanished after extended exposure to the deeper research areas.

Over its nine centuries of operation, the Athenaeum produced findings of lasting significance for ley-line research. The Resonance Atlas — a comprehensive map documenting over three hundred locations where magic behaved anomalously across the Inner Kingdoms and parts of the Merchant Nations — represented the most thorough record of ley-line scar locations created before the modern era. A scholar named Meridax the Listener proposed around 3,100 BW that the resonance zones were not natural phenomena but remnants of an artificial system — "echoes of a structure that no longer exists" — and was ridiculed by contemporaries despite being essentially correct. The Athenaeum also collected over forty Conjunction Stones — crystalline artifacts recovered from resonance zones that grew warm near other such zones, hummed at frequencies below hearing, and occasionally projected brief images of locations the holder had never seen. These were Akovarian ley-line monitoring devices, depleted but faintly active, though the Yrugas scholars never determined their origin.

The Athenaeum fell with Yrugas around 2,700 BW. The slave revolt that swept the Inner Kingdoms reached the highland complex on the third day of the uprising, and the facility's defensive wards — designed to repel rival mages rather than massed infantry — held for less than an hour. The surface buildings burned, researchers who did not escape were killed, and the great Oculus Terra was toppled from its tower, shattering into thousands of fragments across the highlands. The Translation Chamber's working copies of the Akovarian fragments were destroyed. But the underground Conjunction Archive survived: its vault doors, designed to protect priceless knowledge from magical attack, proved equally effective against mundane assault. The slaves could not breach them, and the magical catastrophe that created the Hollow Sepulcher, while destabilizing the nexus, did not penetrate the Archive's deep shielding.

Today, the surface ruins are a maze of collapsed walls, fire-blackened stone, and encroaching highland vegetation. The Observatory tower's lower forty feet remain standing as a hollow stone cylinder open to the sky, with fragments of the Oculus Terra still recoverable in the surrounding debris field — shards of enchanted crystal and twisted metal that occasionally spark with residual magic. The entrance to the Conjunction Archive is concealed beneath what appears to be a natural rock formation on the complex's northern edge, positioned directly over a ley-line scar intersection. The Archive extends three levels below ground: processing halls on the first level (partially flooded), the Primary Archive on the second level (where climate-control enchantments have preserved texts in remarkable condition), and a Secured Vault on the third level containing the original Akovarian fragments, the Conjunction Stones, and the most dangerous artifacts. The nexus has kept the underground levels magically active in ways the builders did not anticipate — reality is unstable in the deeper chambers, time occasionally skips or loops, shadows move independently of their sources, and sounds echo from events centuries past, giving the site its modern name. Undead remnants of researchers who died in the underground during the fall persist as shadows and animated bones, sustained by the nexus energy and the Hollow Sepulcher's distant influence.

In 800 AW, a joint expedition organized by the Library of the Ages and the Astral Circle successfully located and entered the Conjunction Archive, recovering materials of extraordinary significance for ley-line research — including the Resonance Atlas, surviving Akovarian fragments, Conjunction Stones, and centuries of research notes. The expedition also confirmed that the Athenaeum's nexus constituted a point where the boundary between the material world and other planes was dangerously thin — a finding that would prove prescient when the Far Realm entity Ry'le later exploited similar planar weakness at a different Yrugas site in 824 AW. The recovery of the Resonance Atlas provided critical cartographic data that complemented independent ley-line scar mapping conducted by scholars at the Library of the Ages over the preceding decades, and the Akovarian fragments — cross-referenced with an Akovarian tome recovered from the ruins of Akovar itself — significantly advanced understanding of the ancient network's engineering principles.

Since 815 AW, the nexus beneath the Athenaeum has intensified. The dormant ley-line scars that intersect beneath the highlands have begun carrying faint energy flows as part of the global reawakening, amplifying the instability that was already present. The temporal echoes in the deeper chambers have grown more vivid and more frequent. The Astral Circle maintains monitoring equipment at the site and has classified it as restricted, though the recovered materials now reside in secure archives at the Library of the Ages and the Astral Circle's research facilities.

#### The Geometrist's Tower

In the remote highlands of eastern Beneshire, near the border with Arogror and approximately sixty miles east of the Athenaeum of Echoes, the ruins of a seven-story tower rise from a landscape so saturated with magical disturbance that no settlement has existed within thirty miles for centuries. Known locally as the Twisted Spire or Thornspire, and to scholars as the Geometrist's Tower, the structure was built around 2,850 BW by a Yrugas wizard named Vorthax — a former researcher at the Athenaeum of Echoes who was marginalized by the mageocracy's establishment for proposing that the resonance zones they studied were remnants of an artificial network built by a pre-Yrugas civilization.

Vorthax chose the site because it sat at the intersection of five major ley-line scars — the most complex nexus he had identified, exceeding even the Athenaeum's three-scar intersection. He spent five years designing and constructing the tower using geometry derived from his ley-line research, incorporating angles and proportions he believed would resonate with the underlying magical structure. The completed tower's silhouette was distinctively twisted, each level rotated slightly from the one below to create a subtle spiral, and the interior geometry was stranger still — rooms larger inside than the exterior allowed, corridors curving in ways that defied the walls containing them, and a central staircase that did not maintain a consistent number of steps.

For 155 years, Vorthax pursued his research in isolation, working along three tracks: developing mathematical models of ley-line mechanics, attempting to manipulate the dormant scars through practical experimentation, and seeking evidence of the civilization that built the original network. His theoretical framework — filling thousands of pages with equations, diagrams, and proofs — represented the most comprehensive mortal attempt to understand ley-line engineering prior to the modern era. Around 2,780 BW, Vorthax discovered that the nexus had formed around — or because of — a portal buried beneath his tower, a wound in reality created by the Akovarian ley-line detonation of 6,830 BW that connected to a ruined pyramid-temple on the continent of Akovar itself. He spent a decade stabilizing the portal and then made dozens of expeditions through it over fifty years, recovering texts, artifacts, and observations from the Akovarian Temple of the Seventh Convergence that validated his theories. He learned the ley-lines' true name, their connection to the entity Asti, and the geometric principles underlying their construction. He also learned that ley-line creation required a divine or draconic catalyst — a "seed" he could not replicate — a limitation that consumed his final decades in fruitless attempts at alternatives.

When Yrugas fell around 2,700 BW, the magical catastrophe that created the Hollow Sepulcher sent shockwaves through every ley-line scar on the continent. The five-scar nexus beneath Vorthax's tower channeled enormous necrotic energy directly into the structure. The wards, designed to repel magical attack, contained the energy rather than dissipating it. Vorthax was in his highest laboratory at the moment of the shockwave, attempting yet another experiment to create a ley-line seed, and the interaction between the necrotic surge and his concentrated magical working transformed him into something between life and undeath. What remains of Vorthax is a greater wraith — incorporeal, intelligent, retaining the magical power of a wizard-lord — but bound to the ley-line scars that anchor him to the material world. He cannot leave the nexus, but through the scars his perception extends along every branch and intersection for hundreds of miles. He has existed in this state for approximately 3,500 years, aware, obsessive, and increasingly eroded in personality, more force than person — the embodiment of brilliance consumed by isolation and frustration.

The tower remains standing, preserved by magical forces its creator did not intend. The seven-story structure maintains its twisted silhouette, though weathering has softened the stonework. Inside, proportions do not match exterior observations, rooms shift in apparent size, and the central staircase's step count changes with each ascent. The lower floors (storage, former slave quarters) have suffered decay. The middle floors house Vorthax's personal library — thousands of volumes and over a hundred personal journals documenting his research, frustrations, and gradual descent into obsession. The upper floors contain his experimental apparatus — crystalline matrices, geometric frameworks, mana-focusing devices — many still faintly active. The highest chamber, where Vorthax transformed, is covered wall-to-wall with notes, diagrams, and equations in his own hand: a complete summary of his life's work in charcoal and chalk on stone. The basement levels descend three stories into bedrock, with the portal to Akovar on the lowest level — a vertical disc of shimmering energy, ten feet in diameter, opening onto the Temple of the Seventh Convergence. The portal remains active, the only known gateway between Nigal and the Akovarian continent that does not require ocean passage.

The area surrounding the tower radiates wrongness perceptible even to the magically insensitive. Animals avoid the region, plants grow stunted and twisted, and the converging ley-line scars create a web of disturbance affecting perception and cognition — travelers report losing time, walking in circles, and experiencing hallucinations of a robed scholar who watches from a distance before vanishing. An invisible threshold marks the boundary where the nexus effects intensify dramatically, beyond which retreat becomes increasingly difficult. Within the tower itself, magic is profoundly unreliable — spells produce unexpected effects, and the structure responds to magical activity by shifting its internal geometry.

In 800 AW, the same expedition that explored the Athenaeum of Echoes reached the Geometrist's Tower and recovered significant portions of Vorthax's research, including theoretical frameworks and Akovarian materials that substantially advanced mortal understanding of ley-line engineering. The expedition's findings regarding the "seed problem" — Vorthax's confirmation that ley-line creation requires a divine or draconic catalyst — provided critical theoretical context that informed subsequent research. The portal to Akovar was documented and its existence reported to the Astral Circle, though the logistical and safety challenges of regular portal use have limited subsequent expeditions. The encounter with Vorthax himself demonstrated the catastrophic consequences of ley-line forces interacting with living beings under extreme conditions — a warning that has informed safety protocols for all subsequent ley-line research.

Since 815 AW, the five-scar nexus beneath the tower has responded dramatically to the ley-line reawakening. The dormant scars now carry active energy, amplifying every phenomenon the site already exhibited — the spatial distortions, the perceptual interference, and Vorthax's own power and awareness. The Astral Circle has classified the tower as a Priority Two monitoring site (below only the Hollow Sepulcher in the Inner Kingdoms) and maintains remote observation, though no researcher has entered the structure since the 800 AW expedition. The portal to Akovar remains active and represents both an extraordinary scholarly resource and a significant security concern — a permanent gateway to another continent, maintained by forces no living person fully understands, in a location haunted by an entity of wizard-lord power whose cooperation cannot be guaranteed.


#### The Hollow Sepulcher

The Hollow Sepulcher is the most dangerous ruin on the continent of Nigal — and arguably on all of Mazariim. Located in the deep interior of the Madronian Peninsula, in a region of barren hills that no settlement has occupied since the fall of Yrugas, the Sepulcher is the site where millions died when the mageocracy collapsed around 2,700 BW. The mass death event — caused by a combination of catastrophic magical failure, slave revolts, and the chain reaction of collapsing wards that had sustained Yrugas's cities — created a permanent breach between the material world and the Negative Energy Plane. This breach is Zebulba's greatest temple, a wound in reality that has never closed and, according to the Void-Touched who maintain vigil within it, never will.

The surface above the Sepulcher is a wasteland. Nothing grows within a mile of the central depression — not grass, not lichen, not even the hardiest weeds that colonize abandoned quarries and slag heaps elsewhere in the Inner Kingdoms. The soil is gray and powdery, cold to the touch regardless of season. Animals avoid the area instinctively; birds alter their flight paths to pass around it rather than over it. The air is still and carries a faint smell that visitors describe variously as old stone, iron filings, and the interior of a sarcophagus — a dry, mineral deadness that coats the back of the throat.

Beneath the surface, the Sepulcher extends downward through multiple levels of ruin. The upper levels are the remains of the Yrugas city itself — collapsed walls, shattered towers, the stumps of magical laboratories where arcane experiments were conducted for over a millennium. These upper ruins are dangerous but navigable, infested with minor undead (skeletons, zombies, shadows) that animate spontaneously from the ambient negative energy. Treasure-seekers occasionally penetrate this far, recovering Yrugas-era artifacts — spellbooks, enchanted tools, alchemical components — that command extraordinary prices in markets that do not ask questions about provenance. All such spellbooks and arcane writings follow the universal convention of being written in Draconic, the language in which dragons first taught humans the arcane arts during the founding era of Yrugas itself.

The middle levels are where the true horror begins. Below the city ruins lie the catacombs — vast chambers where the dead of the final catastrophe were swept by the magical shockwave that ended Yrugas. Millions of bodies were compressed, fused, and partially reanimated by the flood of negative energy, creating walls and floors of intertwined bone and desiccated flesh that pulse with a slow, rhythmic energy scholars have compared to a heartbeat. The catacombs are inhabited by more powerful undead — wraiths, spectres, bodaks, and things that have no name in any language, creatures formed from the merger of hundreds of souls into single entities of screaming anguish. The ambient negative energy is strong enough at this depth to drain the life from unprotected living creatures within hours, and magical protections erode rapidly in the corrosive atmosphere.

The lowest level — the Breach itself — has been described by exactly three living witnesses in recorded history, all of them Void-Touched wardens who emerged briefly to deliver warnings to the surface world before returning to their vigil. Their accounts, recorded by terrified Federation scribes, describe a chamber of impossible geometry where the floor is a membrane of solidified negative energy, translucent and cold, through which the absolute darkness of the Negative Energy Plane is visible. The membrane flexes and bulges, pressed from below by forces that seek entry into the material world. The wards that contain this Breach — carved into the chamber walls by a coalition of druids, Tiovinian clerics, and Mordrionist paladins in the centuries after Yrugas's fall — are maintained by the Void-Touched wardens who have lived within the Sepulcher for generations, sustained by the negative energy they simultaneously guard against and draw power from.

The Void-Touched wardens number between thirty and fifty at any given time, though precise counts are impossible because the wardens rarely communicate with the outside world. They exist in a twilight state between life and undeath — their bodies aged and withered, their skin gray and papery, their eyes lightless, but their minds intact and their devotion unwavering. New wardens are recruited from among Zebulba's faithful who make the pilgrimage to the Sepulcher's sealed entrance — a massive stone door carved with Zebulba's symbol, located at the base of the central depression. Those who knock are admitted. Those who enter do not leave. Whether this is imprisonment or choice depends entirely on one's theological perspective.

The Federation of Man maintains a permanent garrison — Fort Ashward — three miles from the Sepulcher's perimeter, staffed by a rotating complement of soldiers, clerics of Tiovin, and members of the Circle of the Eastern Watch. Fort Ashward's mission is not to enter the Sepulcher but to prevent unauthorized access and to monitor for signs that the Breach is weakening. The garrison maintains a network of detection wards around the perimeter that alert the commanding officer if undead activity exceeds baseline levels. Twice in the Federation's history — in 234 AW and again in 601 AW — the wards triggered mass alerts, indicating surges of negative energy that brought the garrison to full readiness. Both surges subsided without a breach event, but the records of those incidents are studied at the Circle of the Eastern Watch as case studies in what a full Sepulcher breach might look like.

Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the Sepulcher has exhibited increased activity. Ley-line flows passing near the site are deflected or absorbed, creating dead zones in the emerging magical network. The garrison at Fort Ashward has reported more frequent undead sightings on the perimeter, stronger readings on detection wards, and — most alarmingly — faint sounds emanating from the sealed entrance on still nights. The sounds have been described as breathing, as grinding stone, and as a low harmonic vibration that makes teeth ache and vision blur. The Circle of the Eastern Watch has classified the Sepulcher as a Priority One monitoring site, and the Astral Circle has dispatched researchers to study the interaction between ley-line energy and the Breach — though these researchers work from Fort Ashward and have no intention of entering the ruins themselves.

#### The Madron Crater

Five miles east of the Hollow Sepulcher, in a shallow depression that was once the central plaza of the city of Madron — the capital of Yrugas and the seat of the wizard-king whose name became the Madronian people's — lies a circular crater approximately half a mile in diameter and two hundred feet deep at its center. The crater was created during the final catastrophe that destroyed Yrugas, when the accumulated magical energy of the mageocracy's greatest city detonated in a single cataclysmic release.

The crater is geologically anomalous. Its walls are smooth, glassy, and fused — stone melted by temperatures that no natural process could produce and then frozen in place as the energy dissipated. The glass is black, shot through with veins of deep purple and electric blue that scholars have identified as crystallized magical residue — solidified mana, in effect, trapped in mineral form during the explosion. These crystallized mana veins are extraordinarily valuable as magical components, and periodic expeditions to harvest small quantities are organized by the Astral Circle under Federation license. The expeditions are dangerous — the crater's interior is saturated with residual magical energy that causes unpredictable effects in spellcasters (spontaneous cantrip discharge, temporary loss of spell preparation, vivid hallucinations of Yrugas in its final moments) and the lower depths of the crater are home to arcane oozes and crystalline constructs that formed spontaneously from the ambient magical contamination.

The crater's center contains a formation that defies easy explanation: a spire of fused glass and crystal approximately sixty feet tall, shaped by forces unknown into a rough approximation of a human figure — robed, crowned, and reaching upward with both hands. Local tradition holds that this is the petrified remains of Madron himself, caught in the instant of the explosion and transformed into glass. Scholars dismiss this as folklore, noting that the figure is too large and too symmetrical to be a natural formation but too crude to be deliberate sculpture. The Astral Circle has proposed that the spire is a "mana scar" — a point where magical energy was so concentrated during the explosion that it permanently altered the physical properties of the rock beneath, creating a structure that reflects the shape of whatever channeled the energy in its final moments. If Madron was indeed at the center of the detonation, the spire may be a magical echo of his form at the moment of death — not his body, but the shadow his body cast in solidified mana. Given that Madron is known to have ruled Yrugas for some nine hundred years through arcane means, the sheer volume of mana his body may have contained at the moment of death lends plausibility to this theory.

Since 815 AW, the crystallized mana veins in the crater walls have begun to glow faintly at night, pulsing with a rhythm that does not correspond to any known ley-line flow. The spire at the center has been observed to hum at frequencies just below human hearing, and expeditions that approach it report a sensation of being watched — not by a presence within the spire, but by the spire itself, as though the structure possesses a rudimentary awareness. The Astral Circle has increased the frequency of its monitoring expeditions and has placed the Madron Crater on its restricted access list, limiting harvesting operations until the nature of the new activity is understood.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Shattered Towers of Calthene

Scattered across the hills of northern Beneshire, approximately forty miles northeast of Wyebluff, the ruins of Calthene consist of seven broken tower foundations arranged in a precise heptagonal pattern. Calthene was a Yrugas-era magical research complex, one of dozens that dotted the pre-collapse landscape, and its towers served as linked observation posts for astronomical and planar research. The towers were destroyed during the Yrugas collapse but not by the central detonation — they appear to have been deliberately demolished from within, their stones blown outward by internal explosions set by their own occupants. Scholars theorize that the researchers at Calthene, realizing what was happening in Madron, destroyed their own facility to prevent its magical reserves from feeding the chain reaction.

The tower foundations remain magically active. Runic circles carved into the base stones still hold faint charges, and on clear nights, faint columns of blue-white light occasionally manifest above the foundations — phantom towers, visible for seconds before fading. The Protectors of the Shire patrol the area and discourage treasure-seekers, though the site has been picked clean of portable artifacts over the centuries. The heptagonal arrangement of the foundations suggests a geometric relationship with ley-line convergence points, and Line-Watchers have expressed interest in studying the site. Since 815 AW, the phantom tower manifestations have become more frequent and last longer — up to several minutes in some reports.

#### Fort Ashward Undercroft

While Fort Ashward itself is a Federation military installation rather than a ruin, the fort was built directly atop an older structure — a pre-War watchtower that itself was constructed on the foundations of what appears to be a Yrugas-era way station. The undercroft beneath the fort's main keep extends three levels below the surface, the lowest of which contains stonework of clearly Yrugas origin: smooth-cut blocks fitted without mortar, bearing inscriptions in a script that predates modern Madra by millennia. The inscriptions have been partially translated by scholars from the Library of the Ages and appear to be directional — distance markers and compass bearings pointing toward Madron, the Hollow Sepulcher, and at least two other locations that do not correspond to any known Yrugas site.

The undercroft is used by the garrison for storage and as an emergency shelter, but the lowest level is sealed by order of the Circle of the Eastern Watch after an incident in 798 AW in which a soldier on punishment detail reported hearing voices emanating from the walls. Investigation revealed no supernatural source, but the discovery of an unsealed passage leading deeper into the hillside prompted the decision to seal the level until a proper survey could be conducted. The survey has been requested annually since 798 AW and has never been funded. Since 815 AW, soldiers stationed in the undercroft's upper levels have reported that the sealed door to the lowest level is warm to the touch.

#### The Weeping Stones of Anheath

In the countryside south of Anheath, seat of House Hargenfeld in Beneshire, a circle of twelve standing stones rises from a barley field that has been cultivated around them for as long as anyone can remember. The stones are not Yrugas-era — they predate the mageocracy by millennia, carved from granite that does not match any quarry within two hundred miles. Their surfaces bear spiral carvings that resist weathering with suspicious effectiveness, remaining as sharp and deep as the day they were cut despite exposure to centuries of rain, frost, and wind.

The stones earn their name from a phenomenon that occurs during heavy rain: water runs down the spiral grooves in patterns that, from certain angles, resemble tears streaming down a face. Local folklore attributes the stones to giants, to elves, to the Old Gods, and to a dozen other origins, none supported by evidence. The Protectors of the Shire have monitored the site for decades and report no magical activity — until 815 AW, when the stones began to hum during thunderstorms, producing a low tone that resonates in the chest and causes mild nausea in those who stand within the circle. The humming has been recorded by scholars from the University of Wyebluff and identified as consistent with ley-line resonance patterns observed at other ancient sites. House Hargenfeld has restricted access to the field during storms, more to prevent liability for injuries than out of any genuine concern about the supernatural.

#### The Haunted Keeps

During the Elemental Crisis of 819 AW — when the shadow dragon Noctuminalis, posing as the "Elder Elemental Eye," manipulated elemental cults into causing widespread destruction across the Inner Kingdoms — the cults established forward bases in a series of ancient fortifications scattered through the countryside surrounding Fort Hope. These structures, quickly dubbed the "Haunted Keeps" by the Federation soldiers sent to investigate them, were pre-War strongholds and Yrugas-era outposts that had stood abandoned for centuries before the cultists reoccupied and corrupted them with elemental magic.

Each keep was claimed by a different elemental cult and warped to reflect its patron element — stone walls fused with volcanic glass, corridors filled with perpetual gale-force winds, chambers flooded to the ceiling with water that did not drain, foundations destabilized by localized earthquakes. The heroes who dismantled the cults during the crisis cleared the keeps of their occupants and disrupted the elemental rituals that had been causing regional disasters, but the keeps themselves were not fully cleansed. The elemental corruption left scars on the stonework — discoloration, structural warping, and residual magical contamination that makes the air taste of sulfur, salt, or ozone depending on which cult occupied which keep.

The Federation has posted warning markers at each site but has not garrisoned them. Periodic inspections by the Circle of the Eastern Watch confirm that the elemental contamination is slowly fading, but some keeps have attracted new squatters — bandits, minor cultists, and opportunistic monsters drawn to the residual magical energy. The keeps also contained teleportation circles that the cults used to travel to their underground headquarters at Tyar-Zaruk; these circles were disrupted during the crisis but not physically destroyed, and the Astral Circle has expressed interest in studying them.

Beyond the keeps themselves, the Elemental Crisis had broader archaeological consequences. The earthquakes, seismic upheavals, and elemental disturbances the cults caused were powerful enough to physically reshape terrain across the Inner Kingdoms and neighboring regions, cracking open sealed vaults, exposing buried structures, and bringing artifacts to the surface that had been interred for centuries or millennia. The most consequential of these exposed artifacts was the Devil Cube — a Taovanian prison artifact unearthed in the Lands of Chaos by Crisis-era earthquakes, which would go on to merge a portion of the Lavantine Peninsula with the Shadow Plane in 832 AW. But the Devil Cube was not the only discovery: across the affected regions, farmers, miners, and treasure-seekers reported finding previously unknown ruins, sealed chambers, and buried objects disturbed by the seismic activity. The full scope of what the Elemental Crisis unearthed has not been cataloged, and new discoveries continue to surface as erosion works on terrain that the earthquakes destabilized over two decades ago.


#### Tyar-Zaruk

Deep beneath the Inner Kingdoms, accessible through the teleportation circles in the Haunted Keeps, lies Tyar-Zaruk — an ancient underground complex of pre-War origin that the elemental cults used as their combined headquarters during the Elemental Crisis of 819 AW. The complex is vast, with distinct sections that the cults dedicated to their respective elements, connected by a central hub of corridors and chambers.

The origins of Tyar-Zaruk predate the elemental cults by centuries at minimum. The architecture is neither Yrugas nor any other readily identifiable style — the stonework is massive, the chambers oversized, and the geometric proportions suggest builders who thought on a scale larger than human norms. Some scholars have proposed a connection to the same unknown civilization that built the stone circles of the eastern steppe, though this remains speculative.

The heroes who pursued the elemental cult generals through Tyar-Zaruk during the crisis reported that the complex extended far beyond what the cults had occupied — sealed corridors, collapsed passages, and deeper levels that no one had explored. The elemental corruption introduced by the cults layered atop whatever original enchantments the complex possessed, creating a magical environment that is unpredictable and dangerous. The Federation has sealed the known surface entrances, and the Astral Circle has classified Tyar-Zaruk as a site requiring formal expedition before further access is permitted. As of 842 AW, no such expedition has been funded.

#### The Yrugas Rift Site

In 824 AW, the Federation and the Astral Circle discovered that a Far Realm entity called Ry'le had been infiltrating Beneshire through shapeshifting aberrations called the Ry'lehe. Investigation revealed that these creatures were entering Mazariim through a rift opened in a previously uncharted section of Yrugas ruins — a complex of chambers deep beneath the Madronian countryside where the ancient mages' planar research had left the boundary between dimensions dangerously thin.

The rift site was located in ruins that bore the hallmarks of Yrugas's most dangerous research: containment circles scored into the floors, warding glyphs on every wall, and the remnants of apparatus designed for communication with — or summoning from — other planes of existence. The Yrugas mages had probed the Far Realm millennia before Ry'le exploited the weakness they created, and their notes, recovered from the site in fragmentary form, suggest they were aware of the entities that lurked beyond and were attempting to study them. Whatever safeguards they installed degraded over the millennia, and the planar disturbances caused by the Orceri Incursion of 811 AW and the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW weakened the remaining barriers enough for Ry'le to force a breach.

The vulnerability of Yrugas ruins to planar exploitation had been identified prior to the Ry'le incursion. An 800 AW expedition to two Yrugas sites in eastern Beneshire — the Athenaeum of Echoes and the Geometrist's Tower — documented that ley-line nexus points beneath Yrugas structures created zones where the boundary between planes was dangerously thin. The Athenaeum's nexus produced temporal anomalies and perceptual distortions consistent with planar membrane degradation, and the Geometrist's Tower contained an active cross-planar portal that had been stable for millennia. These findings, reported to the Astral Circle, contributed to the growing understanding that Yrugas sites built on ley-line scars posed ongoing planar risks — a conclusion that proved tragically prescient when Ry'le exploited precisely this type of weakness two decades later.

The heroes who stopped the Ry'lehe infiltration closed the rift and destroyed the apparatus maintaining it, but the site itself remains a point of concern. The Astral Circle's efforts to help restart a small ley-line during this same period inadvertently revealed the psychic connections between Ry'lehe thralls and their masters — a discovery that proved instrumental in defeating the threat. The rift site has been sealed and warded by the Astral Circle, with monitoring equipment in place to detect any recurrence of Far Realm activity. The discovery that Yrugas ruins could serve as breach points for planar incursions prompted the Circle of the Eastern Watch to reassess its survey of known Yrugas sites across the Inner Kingdoms — a survey now informed by the Resonance Atlas recovered from the Athenaeum, which identifies over three hundred sites of ley-line anomaly that may share the same planar vulnerability. The resources for comprehensive investigation remain insufficient.

## THE MERCHANT NATIONS

The Sabon Peninsula's long history of empire-building and empire-breaking has left it richly decorated with ruins. The Dukhan Empire's ziggurats, the Empire of Simir's abandoned fortifications, pre-Sabonaa tribal sites, and the wreckage of countless smaller conflicts dot the landscape from the coast to the interior. The Jinook Valley — the fertile heartland of the region — is particularly dense with ruins, many of them still radiating the necromantic contamination left by the Dukhan Empire's catastrophic fall in 213 BW.

### Major Ruins

#### The Ziggurats of the Jinook Valley

The Dukhan Empire, a Zebulba-worshiping theocracy that expanded from the city-state of Dukhan beginning around 1,001 BW to dominate six major cities across the Jinook Valley and the Taskaun Peninsula, fell in a single catastrophic day in 213 BW when Soros un-Kahn's ritual failed. Negative energy poured through every ziggurat in the empire simultaneously, killing all living things within miles of each structure. The ziggurats themselves survived — they were built to channel dark energy, and the ritual's failure merely overloaded them rather than destroying them. Today, between nine and fourteen Dukhan ziggurats remain standing in the Jinook Valley (the exact number is disputed because some structures are so overgrown or damaged that their identification as ziggurats versus natural formations is uncertain).

The ziggurats are stepped pyramidal structures of dark basalt, typically between forty and eighty feet tall, with flat summits where sacrificial altars once stood. Their surfaces are carved with Zebulban iconography — skulls, black flames, the stylized entropy spirals that the Dukhan priesthood used as meditation foci — and many still bear traces of the dark staining that results from centuries of blood sacrifice. The basalt itself is unnatural, quarried from deposits that geologists cannot locate in the surrounding terrain, suggesting that the Dukhan priests either transported it from a distant source or created it through magical means. During the empire's nearly eight centuries of existence, every town and village under Dukhan rule had a ziggurat erected, and human or non-human sacrifice to Zebulba was performed on most equinoxes and solstices. The priests used fell magics to extend their own lives through these sacrifices, gaining incredible age, wisdom, and eventually madness.

The ziggurats are distributed along the Jinook Valley in a pattern that Astral Circle researchers have identified as a ley-line grid — a network of convergence points designed to channel magical energy between structures. This grid was the mechanism by which Soros un-Kahn's ritual was supposed to work: energy generated at each ziggurat would flow through the grid to a central point, where it would be concentrated and used to manifest Zebulba's avatar. The ritual's failure reversed this flow, sending negative energy outward through the grid instead of inward, which is why the destruction was so widespread and so simultaneous.

The ziggurats' immediate surroundings are dead zones where the negative energy discharge killed everything organic and left the soil permanently tainted. These zones range from a few hundred yards to nearly a mile in radius, depending on the size of the ziggurat. Within these zones, nothing grows, animals sicken and die within days, and unprotected living visitors experience progressive symptoms — chills, fatigue, depression, and eventually the draining of life force that characterizes exposure to negative energy. The dead zones are clearly visible from a distance as patches of gray, barren earth amid the valley's otherwise green farmland, and they serve as effective deterrents against casual exploration.

Beyond the dead zones, the ziggurats' influence is subtler but persistent. Farmers whose fields border the contaminated areas report lower crop yields, livestock that age prematurely, and a higher incidence of stillbirths — effects that have diminished over the centuries but never fully disappeared. Villages within five miles of a ziggurat have higher rates of depression, insomnia, and night terrors than the regional average, a statistical anomaly that the Church of Tiovin attributes to spiritual contamination and the Astral Circle attributes to low-level negative energy exposure.

The ziggurats themselves are structurally sound but deeply cursed. Entering a ziggurat is inadvisable under any circumstances. The interior chambers — accessible through narrow doorways at the base — contain the Dukhan priesthood's ritual spaces, storage vaults, and the sacrificial preparation rooms where victims were readied for the altar. These spaces are infested with undead that formed spontaneously from the ritual's backlash: shadows, wraiths, and the particularly dangerous Dukhan Remnants — undead that retain fragments of the priestly knowledge their living selves possessed, capable of casting necromantic spells and directing lesser undead with tactical intelligence. Some ziggurats also contain sealed lower chambers that the Dukhan priests used for communion with the Negative Energy Plane, chambers whose contents have never been cataloged because no one who has entered them has returned to file a report.

The Thaumaturgic Council of Instar maintains monitoring wards on the three ziggurats closest to the city, and the Free City of Amara funds periodic surveys of the two ziggurats that lie within its territorial claims. The remaining ziggurats in the valley's interior are effectively unmonitored, their locations known to locals who avoid them and to treasure-seekers who do not always return. Since 815 AW, the dead zones around the ziggurats have expanded slightly — an average of ten to fifteen feet per year — and the undead activity within the structures has intensified. The ley-line grid that connected the ziggurats appears to be reactivating, with faint energy flows detected between structures by Astral Circle instruments. Whether this reactivation poses a genuine threat or is merely a passive response to the general increase in ambient magical energy remains under study.

#### The Simir Bastions

When the Empire of Simir ruled the southern Merchant Nations from 291 to 686 AW, King Petrov Simir and his successors constructed a network of fortified outposts along the empire's borders — massive stone bastions designed to project military power and control trade routes. At the empire's height, over two dozen of these bastions guarded the approaches to Asim, Amara, Instar, and Moamon. When the empire collapsed in 686 AW following the assassination of King Tiberius Simir and the subsequent civil wars ravaged the region, most bastions were abandoned, sacked, or deliberately demolished by the cities they had once controlled.

Approximately eight bastions survive in recognizable form, scattered across the Jinook Valley and the hinterlands between Amara and Asim. They are squat, thick-walled structures of local limestone, built for function rather than aesthetics, with deep foundations, narrow windows, and interior layouts designed to be defensible even after the outer walls were breached. Most have been picked clean of anything valuable — the civil wars were thorough — but their structural integrity means they occasionally serve as shelters for travelers, bandit hideouts, or makeshift fortifications during the minor conflicts that periodically erupt between Merchant Nations city-states.

The largest surviving bastion, known locally as the Iron Fist, stands on a hilltop twelve miles north of Asim along the road to Instar. The Iron Fist was the personal command post of Petrov Simir's general staff and retains its original iron-banded gates, portcullis mechanism, and a partially intact dungeon level that local legends claim connects to a tunnel network extending all the way to Asim's foundations. The tunnels have never been verified — the dungeon level is flooded to waist depth and infested with subterranean vermin — but Asim's King Byrne Simir has been observed sending small parties toward the Iron Fist on several occasions, suggesting that the tunnels may exist and may be of strategic interest to someone who dreams of rebuilding the empire.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Plague Pits of the Taskaun Road

Along the road connecting the Jinook Valley to the Taskaun Peninsula (and ultimately to Tentigo), a series of mass graves marks the path of the plague that devastated the Dukhan Empire's western territories decades before Soros un-Kahn's ritual delivered the final blow. These plague pits — shallow trenches where hundreds of bodies were dumped and covered with quicklime — are visible as long depressions in the roadside terrain, their surfaces slightly sunken and devoid of the scrub grass that grows elsewhere. Local teamsters know their locations and avoid camping near them, not from superstition but from experience: livestock tethered near plague pits sicken overnight, and travelers who sleep too close report nightmares of drowning in bodies.

The pits are not considered actively dangerous, but they are classified as minor Zebulban sites by the Church of Tiovin, which sends priests to bless them every few decades in an effort to prevent spontaneous undead formation. The blessings appear effective — there are no verified reports of undead emerging from the plague pits — but the Void-Touched who travel the Taskaun Road are known to leave offerings of bone and ash at the pit margins, honoring the dead in their own fashion.

#### The Glass Garden of Instar

In the hills north of Instar, hidden in a fold of terrain that makes it invisible from the main road, lies a field of approximately two hundred freestanding glass columns, each between three and eight feet tall, arranged in no discernible pattern. The columns are smooth, slightly translucent, and pale green in color, and they produce a faint chiming sound when the wind blows through them. Local tradition attributes the Glass Garden to an unnamed wizard of the pre-Simir era who attempted a mass transmutation spell that went wrong, turning an orchard to glass. The Thaumaturgic Council has studied the site and determined that the columns are not transmuted wood but rather crystallized magical energy — similar in composition to the mana veins in the Madron Crater but formed through a slower, less violent process.

The Garden is not dangerous, merely strange. Visitors report a sense of calm within the field that contrasts with the mildly unsettling appearance of the glass columns. Animals graze among the columns without apparent distress. Since 815 AW, the columns have begun to glow faintly at dusk, producing a pale green luminescence that fades over the course of an hour after sunset. The Thaumaturgic Council has increased its monitoring of the site but has not restricted access.

#### The Drowned Temple of Cho

At the mouth of the River Cho, where Amara's harbor meets the Bay of Urgot, the remains of a pre-Dukhan temple lie submerged beneath approximately fifteen feet of water. The temple — dedicated to Iquinias, based on the carved wave motifs visible on its walls — was apparently built when the river's mouth was narrower and the water level lower, and it was submerged gradually by centuries of sedimentation and rising sea levels. The temple's upper walls are visible at extreme low tide, and divers have explored the interior, recovering corroded bronze implements, fragments of coral-encrusted statuary, and clay tablets inscribed with prayers in an archaic form of Sabo that predates the Dukhan period.

The temple is occasionally used as a navigational hazard marker by Amara's harbor pilots, who know its location precisely and steer traffic around it. The Church of Iquinias in Amara has expressed interest in excavating the site but lacks the funding and magical expertise to conduct underwater archaeology at scale. Local fishermen leave offerings of fish and wine over the submerged walls — a tradition that predates anyone's memory and that the fishermen continue not from devotion but from the pragmatic observation that catches are reliably better near the temple than elsewhere in the harbor.

#### Zanthar's Tower and the Orceri Breach Site

In the Steppes of Broken Stone — the arid, rock-strewn wasteland between Amara and Instar — the ruins of a wizard's tower mark the site where the Orceri Incursion of 811 AW began. The tower was built around 310 BW by a wizard named Zanthar, a fugitive from the Dukhan Empire who settled in the remote steppe and constructed a hidden domicile disguised as a cluster of boulders, with excavated tunnels extending beneath the surface. Within the tower, Zanthar built a prototype magical device capable of peering into alternate prime material planes — an achievement of extraordinary ambition that ended in catastrophe when a surge of magical energy vaporized the wizard and collapsed the tower's upper structure. The device and its underground chambers lay buried and forgotten for over a thousand years.

By the early ninth century AW, orcs and goblins infested the Steppes of Broken Stone. A group of orcs led by the shaman Kru'ka'ta occupied a small keep that hobgoblins had built from the tower's rubble decades earlier. These orcs, hired by unscrupulous Amaran merchants to harass competing trade caravans, were unaware of what lay beneath their crude fortress. Their excavation of the lower levels inadvertently exposed Zanthar's machine, and its brief accidental activation by a monk named Remular sent an interdimensional vibration that was detected by the Orceri of Ta'Puh — an advanced orc civilization from an alternate reality where orcs had won the War to End All Wars, conquered their world, and spent four centuries invading eleven other prime material planes.

The Orceri sent scouts through to Mazariim, using Zanthar's machine as a beacon. The heroes who investigated the orc-occupied keep discovered both the machine and evidence of the interdimensional breach, setting in motion the chain of events that would culminate in the sealing of the Orceri portal at Gaal Tarak. Zanthar's machine was either destroyed during the subsequent conflict or rendered inoperable — accounts differ, and the site's post-incursion condition has not been formally surveyed.

Today, the ruins consist of the keep's remnants — crude stone walls built from ancient rubble — atop the excavated lower chambers of Zanthar's original tower. The site is of historical significance as the starting point of the most serious extraplanar threat to Mazariim since the War to End All Wars, and of scholarly interest for what Zanthar's machine reveals about pre-War magical research into alternate realities. The Thaumaturgic Council of Instar, whose wizards assisted in creating the tuning forks that enabled heroes to pursue the Orceri, has expressed interest in surveying the site but has not committed resources. The steppe remains orc-and-goblin-infested territory, and the ruins are unguarded, their significance known primarily to the veterans of the Incursion and the scholars who studied its aftermath. Since 815 AW, no changes at the site have been reported, though the possibility that ley-line energy could interact with whatever remains of Zanthar's interdimensional apparatus is a concern that has been raised but not investigated.

## THE DWARVEN NATIONS

The Dragon Spine mountains hold ruins that span the full breadth of dwarven civilization — from the earliest pre-Mordrion holds carved with primitive tools to the War-era fortifications that fell to Zuel's horde and were reclaimed at terrible cost. The dwarves are meticulous record-keepers, and the locations of most major ruins in their territory are documented in the archives of the Chambers of Slate. Accessing them, however, is another matter. The Dragon Spine's terrain is brutal, many ruins lie deep underground in tunnels that have collapsed or flooded, and the orcs who overran dwarven holds during the War left behind populations that have bred and expanded in the centuries since, turning abandoned holds into hostile warrens.

### Major Ruins

#### Gaal Tarak

The dwarven citadel of Gaal Tarak, located in the Kirak Mountains — a southern spur of the Dragon Spine extending toward the Merchant Nations — was a thriving Bryndawi settlement until 757 AW, when the citadel was destroyed in a sudden and total collapse. One day Gaal Tarak was a functioning community of several thousand dwarves; the next, the mountain above it had caved in, burying the hold under millions of tons of rock. Thousands of dwarven refugees poured into Moamon, the nearest major city, where they established a tent settlement that was itself destroyed by suspicious fires two years later in 759 AW.

The cause of Gaal Tarak's collapse was a mystery for over fifty years. The official explanation — seismic activity triggering a natural cave-in — satisfied no one who understood dwarven engineering. Dwarven holds are designed to withstand earthquakes; the Dragon Spine is seismically active, and holds that could not survive tremors would not survive their first century. Gaal Tarak had stood for over two thousand years. The Stonekeepers of Dirgin, when consulted, offered only that "the mountain chose to close" — a cryptic response that could indicate geological prescience, theological obscurantism, or simple ignorance. The Cult of the Fundament — the ancient order of Stonekeepers who have maintained vigil in the deep Dragon Spine for over fifteen thousand years and who claim to translate Dirgin's slow speech from geological strata — declined to elaborate further.

The mystery was partially resolved in 811 AW during the Orceri Incursion, when heroes pursuing an interdimensional threat discovered that the ruins of Gaal Tarak had been used as the site of a one-way portal connecting Mazariim to an alternate prime material plane called Ta'Puh — homeworld of the Orceri, an advanced orc civilization that had conquered its own world and eleven others. The portal, located deep within the collapsed citadel's ruins, was sealed through the sacrifice of the priest Aleron Windstrider, a Chosen of Thalaman, whose interdimensional magic closed the breach and blocked Orceri access to Mazariim. The sealing released a wave of destructive magical energy that — through a temporal phenomenon consistent with Thalaman's domain over time — traveled backward through the decades, causing the very collapse that had buried Gaal Tarak in 757 AW. The citadel's destruction was not the result of geological failure or subterranean breach; it was the retroactive consequence of its own salvation.

This temporal causation is not widely understood. The heroes who sealed the portal recognized that their actions were connected to the citadel's collapse, but the precise mechanism — magical energy propagating backward through time — defies conventional arcane theory and remains a subject of debate among the few scholars aware of the details. The Stonekeepers' cryptic response takes on new meaning in this context: "the mountain chose to close" may have been an accurate description of a geological event that was, in some sense, willed into being by forces that had not yet acted. The Cult of the Fundament's archives — vast cavern walls covered in geological records carved by fifteen millennia of Stonekeepers — reportedly contain references to temporal disturbances in the Kirak range, but these records are not shared with outsiders.

The ruins of Gaal Tarak remain largely inaccessible by conventional means. The collapse filled the hold's entrance tunnels with rubble to a depth of hundreds of feet, and the mountain above has settled into a new configuration that makes excavation extraordinarily dangerous. Bryndawi clans with ancestral ties to the citadel have organized several attempts to dig access tunnels from adjacent holds, but none have succeeded — the rubble shifts unpredictably, water seeps in from unknown sources, and the workers report a pervasive feeling of wrongness that intensifies the closer they approach the estimated location of the citadel's main chambers. Whether this wrongness reflects residual energy from the portal sealing, lingering temporal distortion, or the simple psychological weight of approaching a mass grave is unclear.

The dwarven refugees who settled in Moamon have never been welcomed by the human city, and the suspicious destruction of their tent settlement in 759 AW deepened mutual resentment. Most refugees have since dispersed to other settlements, but a core community remains in Moamon, maintaining ancestral claims to Gaal Tarak and lobbying — so far unsuccessfully — for resources to mount a serious excavation. The growing Zebulban influence in Moamon has complicated matters: some refugees fear that the cult's interest in death and entropy makes them dangerous neighbors, while a disturbing minority have themselves been drawn to Zebulba worship, their grief and displacement making them vulnerable to the nihilistic philosophy that despair makes palatable. The revelation that Gaal Tarak's collapse was connected to an interdimensional incursion rather than natural disaster has, paradoxically, deepened rather than resolved the refugees' anguish — their home was not merely lost but was always destined to be lost, its destruction woven into the fabric of time by events that had not yet occurred when the mountain fell.

Since 815 AW, Stonekeepers monitoring the Kirak Mountains have reported anomalous readings from the Gaal Tarak site — deep vibrations, heat signatures inconsistent with natural geology, and what one Stonekeeper described as "the mountain trying to speak." Whether these anomalies reflect the ley-line reawakening interacting with residual energy from the sealed portal, whether the temporal distortion left permanent scars in the mountain's geology, or whether the sealed breach retains some latent potential that the returning ley-line flows are stimulating remains an open and deeply concerning question. The Dwarven Empire has increased monitoring of the site and coordinates with the Federation's Circle of the Eastern Watch, both organizations aware that a location where interdimensional travel was achieved once — even if sealed — represents a point of vulnerability that must be watched indefinitely.

#### The Fallen Hold of Karak Vorn

In the northern Dragon Spine, approximately eighty miles south of the Chambers of Slate, the ruins of Karak Vorn occupy a mountain whose peak was sheared off during the War to End All Wars. Karak Vorn was one of the Dwarven Empire's major holds — a city of an estimated fifteen thousand dwarves that fell to Zuel's horde in the opening months of the War when orc sappers detonated explosive charges in the hold's foundation tunnels, collapsing the mountain's peak onto the city below.

The ruins are partially accessible. The War-era destruction was devastating but not total — the hold's deeper levels survived the collapse, and the rubble that filled the upper chambers has settled over eight centuries into a rough but navigable terrain of broken stone, rusted metal, and the bones of those who died in the assault. The Dwarven Empire conducted a partial reclamation of Karak Vorn's lower levels between 200 and 350 AW, recovering valuable resources and clearing orc warrens from the tunnels. The effort was abandoned when the cost exceeded the return: the hold was too damaged to be rehabilitated as a functioning settlement, and the orc populations in the deeper tunnels proved impossible to eradicate without committing military resources needed elsewhere.

Today, Karak Vorn exists in a state of uneasy equilibrium. The upper levels — the collapsed peak and the rubble-choked chambers beneath it — are uninhabited and serve as a somber memorial. Dwarves from surrounding holds make pilgrimages to the ruins on Stoneshaper's Vigil, leaving offerings of weapons and tools at the collapsed entrance in honor of the dead. The middle levels, partially cleared during the 200–350 AW reclamation, are used intermittently by mining operations that extract residual ore from veins the original inhabitants had been working when the hold fell. These operations are dangerous — the structural integrity of the cleared tunnels is questionable, and collapse events occur with uncomfortable frequency — but the ore quality justifies the risk.

The lower levels belong to the orcs. Several hundred orc descendants of the original War-era garrison inhabit the deep tunnels, breeding and fighting among themselves in the darkness. They are not organized enough to pose a serious threat to the surface, but they make deeper exploration of Karak Vorn hazardous. The Dwarven Empire maintains a permanent guard post at the boundary between the cleared middle levels and the orc-held depths, monitoring for any signs of organized activity or expansion. The orcs, for their part, seem content with what they have — which is fortunate, because the resources required to dislodge them would be considerable.

Karak Vorn's most significant feature, from a scholarly perspective, is its War-era fortifications. The hold's defenders fought a desperate last stand in the lower levels, and the remains of their defenses — magical wards built through dwarven rune-smithing techniques, mechanical traps, barricade positions — provide some of the best-preserved examples of mid-War dwarven military engineering extant. The rune-work is particularly valuable: dwarven runes follow a "three-stone logic" of wake, judge, and act — a trigger rune that decides when to activate, a condition rune that decides who it applies to, and an effect rune that performs the function. Many of the War-era runes at Karak Vorn still function centuries after the runesmiths who carved them perished, a testament to the durability of dwarven enchantment. Scholars from the Chambers of Slate periodically visit the cleared middle levels to study these defenses, and the King's Own have conducted training exercises in the ruins to test doctrine against the kind of tunnel-fighting conditions that a future conflict might require.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Singing Shafts of Thoral Deeping

In the central Dragon Spine, a network of abandoned mine shafts penetrates a mountain that has been worked and abandoned at least three times in recorded history. The first operation was pre-Mordrion, the second was established during the golden age of dwarven expansion, and the third was a short-lived post-War venture that was abandoned after miners reported "singing" emanating from the deepest tunnels — a sustained harmonic tone that varied in pitch and volume but never ceased entirely.

The singing has been investigated by Stonekeepers, Gearwrights, and scholars from Graniteshield without conclusive results. The sound is real — it can be recorded, measured, and analyzed — but its source remains elusive. The most plausible natural explanation is wind passing through a specific configuration of tunnels and chambers, creating a resonance effect. The most popular supernatural explanation is that the shafts intersect a space where Dirgin's presence is concentrated, and the singing is the Old God's "voice" — the slow speech of geological processes compressed into an audible frequency by the tunnel geometry. Stonekeepers from the Cult of the Fundament have visited the site and declined to comment, which the local dwarven population interprets as confirmation of the supernatural explanation. The Fundament's own archives — vast cavern walls covered in geological records carved by fifteen millennia of Stonekeepers — reportedly contain references to "singing stone" in several locations across the Dragon Spine, but these records are not shared with outsiders.

Since 815 AW, the singing has changed in character — the pitch has dropped, the volume has increased, and miners who venture near the deepest shafts report that the sound now occasionally forms what might be syllables or words in no known language. The local clan has sealed the deepest access points and posted warnings. The singing continues.

#### The Ancestor Tombs of the Southern Passes

Scattered through the passes and valleys of the southern Dragon Spine, dozens of ancestor tombs — dwarven burial chambers carved into cliff faces and hillsides — mark the resting places of pre-War clan leaders and heroes. These tombs were the Gormdawi and Bryndawi's most sacred sites before Mordrion's arrival in 1,866 BW redirected worship toward temple-forges and the living rather than the dead. Many were looted during the War when orc raiders stripped them of weapons and grave goods, but the structural elements — carved facades, interior chambers, memorial inscriptions — remain intact.

The tombs vary enormously in size and elaboration. The simplest are single chambers with carved inscriptions identifying the occupant and their achievements. The most elaborate are multi-room complexes with carved relief sculptures, mechanical locks, ventilation systems, and traps designed to protect the contents from desecration. A few of the largest tombs are rumored to contain sealed chambers that have never been opened, their mechanical locks too complex to crack without the specific clan knowledge that was lost when the War destroyed the families that built them. These locks follow the principles of dwarven rune-smithing — trigger, condition, and effect runes chained together in sequences that require specific clan-marks, physical tokens, or spoken keystone words to bypass.

The Bryndawi tattoo-keepers have expressed particular interest in the ancestor tombs, many of which bear inscriptions in archaic Rhundawi that contain runic patterns similar to those used in tattoo magic. The connection between ancient burial runes and modern tattoo magic is a subject of active research in Khubilai, where tattoo-keepers believe that the oldest tombs may contain the foundational designs from which their entire tradition descended.

#### The Graniteshield Vaults

In 839 AW, the discovery of a fragment of the legendary Machine of Gyru within Graniteshield's territory sent shockwaves through the dwarven world and beyond. The Machine — created by the primordial being Gyru, who ascended to godhood in 1,111 BW through an Apotheosis Engine that drew energy from a dozen ley-lines — was disassembled at some point after Gyru's ascension, its five component parts scattered across Mazariim. The fragment's location in Graniteshield was traced to an ancient vault beneath the city's commercial district, a sealed chamber of pre-War construction whose existence was unknown to the Merchant Council until magical resonance from the ley-line reawakening revealed its presence.

The discovery drew scholars, Gearwright artificers, agents of Alagalothor seeking to suppress the find, representatives of multiple human and dwarven governments, and adventurers of every description to the neutral city. Guildmaster Brenna Goldvein and the Merchant Council navigated the crisis with characteristic pragmatism, ultimately allowing a group of heroes to secure the fragment before it could be seized by any single power. The vault itself — now emptied of the Machine fragment — remains sealed, as the Astral Circle has requested time to study the chamber's construction and determine whether other artifacts might be concealed within its walls.

The broader race for the Machine of Gyru's remaining parts played out across Nigal and beyond, with fragments recovered from the Frozen Isles, the Mespoian Peninsula, Instar, and a final location revealed only after the others were assembled. The proto-entity Khemset — one of five cosmic beings that crashed into the Varkonan desert in 839 AW — sought the Machine to drain mana from the other proto-entities and achieve true godhood. The Machine's ultimate fate depends on the actions of the heroes who collected its parts, but the vaults and hiding places where the fragments rested for centuries are now sites of scholarly interest in their own right, studied for what they reveal about the engineers who disassembled and concealed a device of divine power.

## THE GNOMISH REALMS

The Yazawee Mountains are younger, geologically speaking, than the Dragon Spine, and gnomish civilization is younger than dwarven — but the gnomish talent for experimentation has produced a distinctive category of ruins: sites destroyed not by war or natural disaster but by accidents. Gnomish ruins are the remains of things that went wrong — experiments that exploded, devices that malfunctioned, inventions that worked exactly as designed but produced consequences no one anticipated. These ruins are fascinating, dangerous, and occasionally still active, their mechanisms ticking away in the darkness long after their creators fled or perished.

### Major Ruins

#### The Gauntlight Ruins (Upper and Middle Levels Sealed; Lower Levels Unexplored)

The Gauntlight Ruins beneath Chumbawamba are described in detail in the Gnomish Realms geography document, but their significance as a ruin site warrants additional discussion here. The underground complex created by Salvatore Togglesprocket around 738 AW represents one of the most dangerous concentrations of necromantic contamination in the Gnomish Realms — a distinction that the gnomes, who pride themselves on their philosophical maturity and technological sophistication, find deeply embarrassing.

The upper levels have been sanitized and converted into a cautionary exhibit maintained by the Church of Alagalothor, displaying Togglesprocket's early experiments and the progression of his work from legitimate (if eccentric) magical research into outright necromancy. The exhibit is educational, well-curated, and profoundly disturbing — the transition from innocent curiosity to obsessive madness is documented in Togglesprocket's own meticulous workshop logs, which read like a gnomish engineering manual gradually transforming into the diary of a lunatic. As with all magical writings on Mazariim, the arcane portions of these logs are written in Draconic, though Togglesprocket's personal annotations shift between Draconic, Rhungnom, and an increasingly erratic personal cipher as his sanity deteriorated.

The middle levels — Togglesprocket's primary experimental chambers — are sealed with reinforced wards maintained by Warden Tizzle Brightspark. These levels contain the remains of Togglesprocket's most ambitious creations: hybrid constructs of flesh and machinery, spirit-binding devices, and containment cells whose occupants may or may not have been fully destroyed during the 838 AW crisis. The wards are checked daily and reinforced monthly, and Brightspark has developed specialized detection spells that alert her to any necromantic activity within the sealed area.

The lower levels have never been fully explored. During the 838 AW campaign, the heroes who contained the crisis penetrated to the deepest accessible point of the complex and encountered spatial anomalies — rooms that were larger inside than outside, corridors that looped back on themselves, and chambers where the normal rules of physics seemed to apply intermittently. These anomalies were attributed to sustained necromantic experimentation warping the fabric of reality in the deepest sections of the complex, a phenomenon that Togglesprocket's workshop logs reference obliquely as "the thinning" without further explanation. The heroes contained the immediate threat and withdrew; the lower levels were sealed with the heaviest wards available and have not been entered since.

The ley-line reawakening of 815 AW was the catalyst that revived Togglesprocket's ghost and reanimated his experiments in the first place, and the ley-line flows through the area have not ceased. Warden Brightspark monitors these flows constantly and has reported that energy continues to seep into the sealed lower levels at a rate that her wards can manage but not fully block. What this energy is doing in the unexplored depths — whether it is feeding remaining constructs, expanding the spatial anomalies, or producing effects that no one has anticipated — is unknown. Brightspark's position is that the seals will hold for years, possibly decades, but that a permanent solution to the Gauntlight problem requires either a full exploration and cleansing of the lower levels or a method of diverting ley-line energy away from the site entirely. Neither option has been funded.

The political crisis that accompanied the Gauntlight reawakening — the conflict between the traditionalist Clan Azure and the radical Children of Pyre that nearly tore Chumbawamba apart in 838 AW — left scars that complicate the ruin's management. The factional extremists Xogim Flintsweeper (Clan Azure) and Bombus Clockmort (Children of Pyre) were exposed and dealt with during the crisis, but artifacts recovered from the Gauntlight Ruins had been used to fuel the political conflict, and the revelation that necromantic power could be weaponized for political ends has left Chumbawamba's fractured council wary of allowing further exploration that might yield similar temptations.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Steamhowl Crater

In the hills east of Zhonhylite, a circular depression approximately three hundred feet across marks the site of what gnomish historians call the Steamhowl Event of approximately 620 AW. A Durazgrom engineering team testing an experimental high-pressure steam containment vessel lost control of the device, producing an explosion that vaporized the test facility, its operators, and a significant volume of the hillside they were working in. The crater remains, its walls scorched and glassy from the superheated steam, and the depression has filled with rainwater to form a small lake that is perpetually warm — heated from below by geothermal activity exposed by the explosion.

The Steamhowl Crater is considered a landmark rather than a ruin in the conventional sense, but the debris field surrounding it occasionally yields fragments of the experimental vessel and other artifacts from the testing facility. Gearwright scholars have recovered enough pieces to reconstruct the device's basic design and have determined that the containment failure resulted from a mathematical error in the pressure calculations — a finding that is taught in every Gearwright workshop as an object lesson in the importance of checking one's figures.

#### Tinwhisle's Observatory

On a ridgeline above Dammarth, the remains of an astronomical observatory built by a Rothgrom inventor named Pelara Tinwhisle in approximately 490 AW stand open to the sky. Tinwhisle, a follower of Rivoleen with an obsessive interest in stellar observation, constructed a system of interlocking crystal lenses mounted on a clockwork tracking mechanism that she claimed could observe individual stars during daylight hours. The device worked — for three days, after which the lenses focused sunlight onto the clockwork mechanism with sufficient intensity to set the wooden housing on fire, destroying the observatory and severely burning Tinwhisle herself.

The fire-damaged remains of the observatory include the stone foundation, the mounting brackets for the lens system (the lenses themselves shattered in the fire), and a partially intact clockwork assembly that gnomish artificers have studied as an example of Tinwhisle's innovative tracking design. Tinwhisle survived the fire, rebuilt a smaller version of the device with appropriate sunlight shielding, and went on to produce astronomical observations of considerable value before her death in 551 AW. The ruined observatory is maintained by her descendants as a family memorial and a reminder that brilliance and caution are complementary virtues.

## THE HORSELANDS

The vast central steppe of Nigal holds few ruins in the conventional sense — the Chak'ti are nomadic and build nothing permanent, and the peoples who preceded them on the plains left little that survived the millennia of weathering, grazing, and the slow geological processes that reshape open grassland. What ruins exist in the Horselands are either products of other cultures that once extended into the steppe or anomalous features whose origins predate recorded history.

### Major Ruins

#### The Stone Circles of the Eastern Steppe

Scattered across the eastern Horselands, in the transitional zone between the open grassland and the foothills of the Dragon Spine, over sixty stone circles of varying sizes and configurations have been documented by Bryndawi cartographers and Soractian explorers. The circles range from simple rings of five or six standing stones to elaborate multi-ring complexes with central pillars, connecting causeways, and carved surfaces bearing symbols in no known language.

The stone circles predate Chak'ti settlement by millennia — possibly by tens of millennia. Carbon dating of organic material trapped in the mortar (where mortar exists; many circles are dry-stacked) produces dates so ancient that scholars question the accuracy of the methodology. The stones themselves are granite and basalt, often transported from sources dozens or hundreds of miles away, a feat of logistics that implies an organized society of considerable sophistication — yet no other archaeological evidence of such a society has been found anywhere in the Horselands.

The Chak'ti regard the stone circles with superstitious respect, classifying them as "places where the ancestors were before the ancestors" — sites sacred not to their own forebears but to an older people whose identity has been lost to time. Chak'ti clans camp near the circles during seasonal migrations and leave offerings of horse blood and kumiss at the largest stones, honoring whatever spirits might dwell there. Shamans who enter the circles report unusually vivid visions during ancestor-communion rituals, and some claim to contact spirits that are older and stranger than any Chak'ti ancestor — vast, slow presences that communicate in images rather than words and that seem puzzled by the passage of time since they were last consulted.

Since 815 AW, the stone circles have exhibited increased activity. Stones that had been stable for millennia have shifted position slightly — not fallen, but rotated a few degrees on their bases, as though adjusting to a change in some force their builders understood but modern observers cannot measure. The carved symbols on the most elaborate circles have begun to glow faintly at dawn and dusk, producing a pale blue light that lasts for approximately twenty minutes before fading. Astral Circle researchers who have studied the phenomenon believe the circles are ley-line markers — navigational aids or focusing devices built by a civilization that understood the ley-line network before the Akovarians did. If this is true, the implications are staggering: it would mean that ley-line technology was not unique to Akovar but was developed independently by at least one other civilization — a civilization that existed on Nigal, left these stones, and vanished without any other trace. Asti's Line-Watchers, when consulted, have neither confirmed nor denied this theory, noting only that Asti's ley-line network was established at the creation of the universe and that the network's geometry is shaped by Mazariim's deep geology — the same geology that would have been apparent to any civilization sophisticated enough to observe it.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Bone Road

Along the southern edge of the Horselands, where the steppe gives way to the scrubland separating the central plains from the Ancient South, a stretch of terrain approximately twelve miles long is littered with animal bones — primarily horse, but also antelope, aurochs, and species that modern naturalists cannot identify. The bones are ancient, bleached white by millennia of sun exposure, and they are arranged in what appears to be a deliberate pattern: parallel lines of bones forming a road-like surface approximately twenty feet wide, bordered by larger bones (skulls, pelvises, femurs) set upright like posts.

The Chak'ti call this feature the Bone Road and avoid it. Their oral traditions include stories of a great hunt that went wrong in the distant past — a hunt in which the prey ran itself to death in such numbers that the bones of the fallen created a path across the steppe. Whether this is historical memory or mythological explanation is unclear. Scholars who have examined the bones note that they show no cut marks, no evidence of butchery, and no signs of predator damage — the animals that died here died intact and in enormous numbers, as though struck down simultaneously by some force that killed without wounding.

The Bone Road is not dangerous in any conventional sense — it is simply bones on the ground, ancient and inert. But travelers who follow the Road for its full length report a growing sense of unease, a feeling of being watched by something at a great distance, and — in a few accounts — the distant sound of hoofbeats from a direction that shifts whenever the listener turns to face it. These reports are anecdotal and may reflect the psychological effect of walking through a field of bones for twelve miles. Since 815 AW, no changes in the Bone Road have been reported.

## THE FROZEN ISLES

The Frozen Isles' ruins are uniquely preserved — the extreme cold and the glaciers that periodically advance and retreat across the islands have encased structures in ice that protects them from weathering while making them inaccessible for centuries or millennia at a time. The Glacier's Retreat of 305 AW exposed ruins that had been buried under ice for an unknown period, and the ongoing warming trend has continued to reveal new sites on the northern islands. These ice-ruins are the most mysterious ancient structures on Nigal, predating Nond settlement by millennia and bearing no resemblance to any known culture's architecture.

In 839 AW, the Frozen Isles gained additional significance when a fragment of the Machine of Gyru was discovered hidden in an ancient vault on one of the northern islands, drawing treasure-seekers and agents of the proto-entity Khemset to the remote archipelago. The vault's proximity to Grimhold has prompted speculation about whether the unknown civilization that built the ice-ruins was connected to the engineers who disassembled and concealed the Machine's components.

### Major Ruins

#### The Ice-Ruins of the Northern Islands

The ruins exposed by the Glacier's Retreat are concentrated on three islands in the northern portion of the Frozen Isles archipelago, all within a day's sail of the settlement of Nond. The structures share common characteristics: they are built of a blue-gray stone not found naturally in the Frozen Isles, fitted with extraordinary precision (joints so tight that a knife blade cannot be inserted between blocks), and inscribed with symbols in a language that has not been deciphered by any scholar who has examined them.

The largest complex — known to the Nond as Grimhold — occupies a valley on the largest northern island, partially exposed by the retreating glacier. The exposed portion includes a series of connected chambers, a broad avenue flanked by low walls, and what appears to be a central structure of considerable size, the majority of which remains embedded in the ice. The chambers that are accessible contain no furniture, no artifacts, and no organic material — they are empty stone shells, cleaned by millennia of glacial scouring or never furnished in the first place. The walls bear the ubiquitous inscriptions, and the floors are scored with geometric patterns — circles, spirals, and angular forms — that have been carved into the stone with a precision that suggests mechanical rather than manual tools.

The Nond approach Grimhold with superstitious caution. Jarl Halvard Frostborn permits no overnight stays within the ruins and requires that any visitor be accompanied by at least one gothi. This caution is not merely traditional — the ruins exhibit properties that justify wariness. The interior chambers are significantly warmer than the exterior environment, regardless of the ambient temperature, suggesting a heat source within the stone itself. Sounds carry strangely in the corridors — whispers are amplified, shouts are muted, and in certain chambers, visitors report hearing their own words repeated back to them after a delay of several seconds, as though the walls were recording and replaying speech. The geometric floor patterns in the largest accessible chamber produce a faint phosphorescent glow at night, illuminating the space with a cold blue light that casts no shadows.

Since 815 AW, the phenomena within Grimhold have intensified. The interior temperature has risen noticeably — from merely warmer-than-expected to genuinely warm, sufficient to melt ice that forms on the entrance thresholds. The glow from the floor patterns has become visible during daylight hours in the deepest chambers. And on three separate occasions, Nond fishermen passing the island at night have reported seeing light emanating from the valley where Grimhold lies — a steady blue-white glow visible for miles across the open water, lasting several hours before fading.

The Astral Circle has made repeated requests to study Grimhold in detail, but the logistics of reaching the northern Frozen Isles, combined with Nond reluctance to allow extended foreign presence on their territory, have limited investigation to a single brief visit by an Astral Circle team in 836 AW. The team's report, classified as restricted within the Circle, reportedly concluded that Grimhold's construction predates any known civilization on Nigal by a significant margin and that the geometric patterns on its floors are consistent with ley-line focusing devices of extraordinary sophistication. If this assessment is accurate, Grimhold may predate even the Akovarian civilization's ley-line technology — raising the question of who built it, why they built it in one of the most inhospitable locations on the planet, and what happened to them.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Hearthstones

On several islands in the central Frozen Isles, near established Nond settlements, large flat stones — typically five to eight feet across and two to three feet thick — lie partially embedded in the ground. These stones are warm to the touch year-round, maintaining a consistent temperature that is comfortable for bare skin even in the depths of winter. The Nond call them Hearthstones and have built some of their settlements around them, using the stones' warmth to supplement hearth fires and to dry fish and clothing.

The Hearthstones are clearly artificial — their undersides, where visible, show the same precision-cut stone and geometric inscriptions found in the ice-ruins of the northern islands. They appear to be fragments of a larger system, possibly broken sections of flooring from a structure similar to Grimhold. How they reached their current locations — scattered across islands hundreds of miles apart — is unknown. The Nond do not question the Hearthstones' origins; they are gifts from whatever power placed them, and questioning gifts is poor manners.

## THE ANCIENT SOUTH

The Ancient South holds some of the oldest ruins on Nigal — structures built by the Varkonan refugees who arrived from Akovar in 6,830 BW, fleeing the catastrophic explosion of the ley-line network that destroyed their original civilization. The extreme aridity of the region has preserved these ruins with remarkable fidelity; pyramids that would have been reduced to rubble by the jungle's assault in the Mespoian Peninsula stand in the Varkonan desert largely intact after six millennia, their limestone faces scoured by wind and sand but their structural integrity undiminished. The danger of these ruins lies not in their age but in what they contain — traps set by tomb-builders who expected eternity, curses invoked by priests who commanded powers their modern descendants have forgotten, and the sheer accumulation of death and preservation that makes every major tomb a potential flashpoint for necromantic activity.

The Varkonan pyramids are of particular interest to scholars studying the ley-line reawakening, because the Varkonans' Akovarian ancestors were the first mortal civilization to create ley-lines — working alongside the entity Asti around 8,200 BW to energize channels of magical energy between their pyramid cities. The geometric principles embedded in Varkonan pyramid construction reflect this heritage, and the ley-line reawakening has caused even pyramids thousands of miles from any known convergence point to exhibit faint magical activity.

### Major Ruins

#### The Abandoned Pyramids of the Western Desert

Beyond the Varkonan Canal, in the arid interior west of the River of Life, a scattered complex of over thirty pyramids rises from the desert — the remains of a settlement that thrived during the period immediately after the Canal's construction (around 2,100 BW) and was abandoned within a few centuries when the canal branch that supplied it silted up and failed. The settlement — whose Varkonan name has been lost — withered as its water supply dwindled, its population migrating back to the River of Life valley and leaving behind their pyramids, their tombs, and whatever they could not carry.

The abandoned pyramids are smaller than those of Ra'khet — the largest stands approximately forty feet tall — but they are numerous and largely unexcavated. The settlement's rapid depopulation meant that tombs were sealed with their contents intact, and the desert's preservative dryness has maintained what lies within. The few tombs that have been opened by Varkonan archaeologists and foreign relic-hunters have yielded mummified remains, grave goods, and — in one case — a sealed clay jar containing papyrus scrolls bearing astronomical calculations that Shi'ar Nebtawi has identified as consistent with Akovarian mathematical methods rather than later Varkonan developments, suggesting that the settlement's founders retained more of the original Akovarian knowledge than most of their contemporaries.

The abandoned pyramids are protected by Pharaonic decree — the Pharaoh considers all pyramids, regardless of location or condition, to be sacred property of the throne — but the remoteness of the site and the difficulty of patrolling the western desert mean that protection is nominal. Tomb robbers, both Varkonan and foreign, raid the site periodically, and the Medjay warrior nobility conducts punitive expeditions against known robber camps when they can spare the troops. The most significant danger at the site is not human — the sealed tombs contain preservation magic that, in the absence of the priestly maintenance that would have sustained it in a living settlement, has degraded in unpredictable ways. Some tombs are harmless. Others contain undead guardians that activate when the seal is broken. At least one tomb is rumored to contain a preservation field so powerful that anything entering it is frozen in time — a treasure-seeker who opened the wrong door could find themselves preserved indefinitely, conscious but unable to move, until someone else opens the door from outside.

Since 815 AW, the abandoned pyramids have exhibited the same faint golden glow observed in the active pyramids of the Necropolis — a phenomenon attributed to ley-line energy flowing through the geometric principles embedded in the pyramids' construction. The glow is fainter here than at Ra'khet, but its presence in pyramids this far from any known ley-line convergence point has prompted Line-Watchers to theorize that the ley-line network's reach extends further than previously estimated — consistent with the Akovarian-era network that once connected pyramid cities across an entire continent.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Oasis of Whispers

Approximately two days' travel northeast of Osymunhotep, a small oasis surrounded by palm trees and scrub brush conceals the entrance to an underground complex of unknown age and origin. The complex consists of a descending staircase cut into bedrock, a series of connected chambers at the bottom, and a central room containing a shallow pool of water that never evaporates and never grows stagnant despite having no visible source or outlet. The walls of the chambers are inscribed with hieroglyphic texts in an archaic form of Varkonae that predates the founding of Nepherites — texts that the Shi'ars who have studied them believe may date to the earliest period of Varkonan settlement, when memories of Akovar were still fresh in the refugees' minds.

The oasis earns its name from a phenomenon reported by every visitor: within the underground chambers, whispered voices are audible, speaking in a language that resembles Varkonae but is not quite comprehensible. The whispers are not directional — they seem to come from the walls themselves, or from the water in the central pool, or from the air. Some visitors report understanding individual words — "remember," "light," "home" — but these may be pareidolia, the mind imposing meaning on ambiguous sound. The whispers are not threatening, merely persistent, and they cease immediately when a visitor leaves the underground chambers.

The Shi'ars who have studied the Oasis of Whispers believe it may be a memory archive — a site where the earliest Varkonans, still partially aware of their Akovarian origins, attempted to record what they remembered before the knowledge was lost entirely. If this is true, the whispers may be degraded recordings of Akovarian language and knowledge, preserved in a medium (the stone, the water, or both) that the archive's creators understood but modern scholars do not. The connection to the Akovarian ley-line tradition — which used geometric principles and natural materials as conduits for magical energy — is suggestive but unproven.

#### The Proto-Entity Craters

When five proto-entities crashed into the Varkonan desert in 839 AW, they created impact sites that are technically ruins in the making rather than ruins of the past. The five craters — scattered across the northeastern desert within a two-day radius of Osymunhotep — range from a quarter-mile to nearly a mile in diameter, and their interiors still radiate magical energy detectable by any trained spellcaster within several miles.

The craters are not empty. The impacts fused the desert sand into glass-like surfaces shot through with veins of crystallized mana similar to those found in the Madron Crater — but where the Madron Crater's veins are inert remnants of an ancient explosion, the proto-entity craters' crystalline formations are active, pulsing with energy that corresponds to the elemental affinities of the entities that created them. The Zonth crater radiates a sense of mathematical precision. The Khemset crater produces feelings of hunger and acquisitiveness in visitors. The Menot crater's glass surface shifts color constantly. The Nakar crater is surrounded by a ring of shattered rock that continues to fracture and break apart, months after the impact. The Anumata crater emits a low harmonic tone that can be heard from a mile away on calm nights.

The Pharaonic court has declared the craters sacred ground and restricted access, but enforcement in the remote desert is minimal. Scholars, treasure-seekers, and the simply curious visit regularly, and Yak-Men raiders — brutal slavers who control portions of the desert and who may have allied with the destructive entity Nakar — have established semi-permanent camps near two of the five sites. The proto-entities themselves have moved on from their crash sites, but the energy their impacts released has permanently altered the desert's magical landscape in ways that will be studied for generations.

## THE MESPOIAN PENINSULA

The Mespoian jungle is, in a sense, one enormous ruin — a landscape so effective at reclaiming human construction that the line between active settlement and abandoned site is perpetually blurry. An Azala city-state that loses its population can be swallowed by the forest within two generations, its pyramids buried under vines, its causeways cracked by roots, its reservoirs choked with sediment and turned to marshland. The peninsula's ruins are therefore uniquely challenging: they are difficult to find, difficult to access, and, once found, difficult to distinguish from the living forest that has consumed them.

In 839 AW, the Mespoian Peninsula gained additional attention when a fragment of the Machine of Gyru was located among ancient Azala ruins, drawing foreign agents and treasure-seekers into the deep jungle and straining the already-tense relationship between the Azala city-states and outsiders who view their territory as a repository of unclaimed artifacts.

### Major Ruins

#### Xochicallan — The City That Fed the Trees

Approximately one hundred and fifty miles southeast of the city-state of Azala, in the deep jungle where no maintained trail reaches, the ruins of Xochicallan slumber beneath a canopy so dense that the forest floor receives less than one percent of available sunlight. Xochicallan was a major Azala city-state during the Classical Period, founded around 1,100 BW and abandoned around 400 BW under circumstances that the surviving codices record only as "the trees demanded it back."

Modern archaeological understanding suggests that Xochicallan's priest-kings pushed the balance between civilization and jungle too far. The city expanded aggressively, clearing more forest than the Azala's traditional ecological management practices could sustain. Whether the jungle's response was natural (ecological collapse as deforestation disrupted watershed, pollination, and soil stability) or supernatural (Gruhathyrir or the local nature spirits actively retaliating against overexploitation) is debated. The result was the same: within a generation, the city's agricultural terraces failed, its water management systems were overwhelmed by uncontrolled flooding, and its population dispersed to neighboring city-states. The jungle closed over Xochicallan like water over a dropped stone.

Today, Xochicallan is almost invisible from above. Three temple-pyramids are detectable as anomalous hills in the canopy — their stone cores too large for the forest to fully absorb, they rise above the surrounding terrain as tree-covered mounds. The pyramids' terraces have become natural growing platforms for enormous trees whose roots grip the stonework and whose canopies tower above the surrounding forest. The ball court, identified by its distinctive shape visible in the terrain, is a long depression filled with leaf litter and inhabited by a family of jaguars that Rothgrom visitors have described as unusually large and unusually unafraid of people.

The city's codex library was presumably evacuated when the population departed, but local Azala legends claim that the innermost chambers of the largest pyramid were sealed before the evacuation was complete — that the priest-king's final act was to ward the library vault rather than carry its contents to safety. If this is true, Xochicallan's sealed library may contain codices dating to the city's founding over a thousand years before its abandonment — texts that could significantly expand understanding of the Classical Period and possibly contain astronomical knowledge closer to the original Akovarian tradition than anything in the surviving Azala archives.

Reaching Xochicallan requires weeks of jungle travel from the nearest maintained trail, and the Wyld-Bound druids who inhabit the deep forest surrounding the site have discouraged exploration — not aggressively, but through the subtle manipulation of trails, weather, and wildlife that makes the jungle itself unwelcoming to those who approach uninvited. Since 815 AW, the pyramids at Xochicallan have been observed glowing faintly through the canopy during equinox periods — a behavior consistent with ley-line activation in pyramid structures and one that has increased interest in the site among Astral Circle researchers, if not their willingness to brave the jungle to study it.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Causeways of the Lowland Swamp

In the swampy coastal lowlands of the peninsula's eastern shore, a network of raised stone causeways extends through the mangrove forest for approximately thirty miles. The causeways are expertly constructed — dressed stone, drainage channels, and graded surfaces designed for foot traffic — but they connect nothing visible. They begin at the coast, run through the mangroves on a series of parallel and intersecting routes, and terminate in the swamp without reaching any identifiable destination.

The causeways are attributed to a pre-Azala culture — possibly connected to the Varkonan refugees who arrived on Nigal in 6,830 BW, during their period of adaptation to the peninsula before the Azala culture fully developed, or possibly a separate group entirely. Their construction quality exceeds what the earliest Azala settlers could have achieved, and their location in the inhospitable coastal lowlands suggests builders who valued maritime access more than the highland Azala ever have. Local fishing communities use the causeways as convenient pathways through the swamp and as drying platforms for their catch. The causeways' original purpose — trade route, pilgrimage path, military road, or something else entirely — remains unknown.

## THE LANDS OF CHAOS

The Lavantine Peninsula's chaotic history has produced ruins in extraordinary density and variety. Yrugas-era magical sites, Varkonan tribal remnants, Dukhan outposts, Tentigo crusade fortifications, Simir border forts, bandit strongholds, abandoned settlements, and the Shadow-Touched Lands all contribute to a landscape where the past is inescapable — buried under every hill, hiding behind every cliff face, waiting in every cave for the unlucky traveler who stumbles through the wrong doorway.

### Major Ruins

#### The Shadow-Touched Lands

The region where a Taovanian artifact merged a portion of the Lavantine Peninsula's interior with the Shadow Plane in 832 AW is described in the Lands of Chaos geography document, but its nature as an active ruin site deserves further attention. The affected area — approximately fifty square miles of interior terrain — has not recovered fully in the decade since the artifact's destruction, and may never do so.

The artifact responsible was the Devil Cube, a powerful extradimensional prison created by the entity Taovan and buried for centuries beneath the Lavantine landscape. The Elemental Crisis of 819 AW — during which elemental cults caused widespread earthquakes, tidal waves, and seismic upheaval across the Inner Kingdoms and beyond — unearthed the Cube along with numerous other artifacts that had been buried for millennia. The earthquakes that the Crisis produced were powerful enough to physically reshape terrain across the region, exposing buried structures, cracking open sealed vaults, and bringing objects to the surface that had not seen daylight since the civilizations that created them turned to dust. The Devil Cube was the most dangerous of these exposed artifacts, though it was far from the only one — the broader archaeological consequences of the Crisis continue to manifest as erosion and settlement expose what the earthquakes disturbed.

The Cube's purpose was imprisonment and corruption. Created by Taovan as both a prison and a lure, it trapped the mage Davos — who had murdered his brother Salacan in a Taovanian ritual sacrifice, seeking power but receiving eternal captivity instead — and drew others into its extradimensional space, where the Shadow Plane's influence transformed them into sullen, hopeless versions of themselves. When the earthquakes exposed the Cube, its malevolent influence began to spread unchecked, ultimately merging the surrounding territory with the Shadow Plane and creating a nightmarish overlap where daylight was perpetually dim, reality twisted and unstable, and devils and kytons replaced the natural creatures of the land.

At the center of the merged zone, Davos ruled from a fortress of Shadow Plane architecture — a structure of dark stone that materialized during the planar overlap, serving as his seat of power within the cursed realm. The fortress was a sprawling complex of trapped corridors, corrupted halls, and chambers filled with infernal servants, its architecture reflecting both Taovanian design and the Shadow Plane's oppressive geometry. Davos himself had been transformed by centuries of imprisonment into something less than human but more than a mere prisoner — a tyrant of the nightmare realm who had become obsessed with a Seeker of Asti named Tatyana who had wandered into the cursed lands.

The heroes who entered the Shadow-Touched Lands recovered three artifacts — the Holy Symbol of Tiovin, the Tome of Davos, and a weapon of pure radiance called the Sunsword — and used them to assault Davos's fortress, destroy him, and shatter the Devil Cube. With the Cube's destruction, the Shadow Plane merger collapsed. The cursed lands began a slow, incomplete return to normalcy, and Davos's fortress — a structure that had existed only because of the planar overlap — largely disintegrated when the planes separated, leaving behind foundations, partial walls, and fragments of Shadow architecture that persist as physical remnants of a structure that no longer has a plane to exist in.

The landscape within the Shadow-Touched Lands is recognizable but wrong. Trees grow, but their leaves are gray-green rather than the vivid green of the surrounding forest, and they grow slowly — a decade of regrowth has produced saplings where mature trees should stand. The soil is darker than it should be, absorbing light with an efficiency that makes shadows deeper and sunlight dimmer. Streams flow, but their water is cold regardless of the season and carries a faintly metallic taste. Animals have begun to return to the margins of the affected area, but the interior remains largely devoid of wildlife — birds that fly over the zone alter course to avoid its center, and insects are notably absent.

The ruins within the Shadow-Touched Lands are of three types. The first are pre-existing structures that were present when the merger occurred — a few farmsteads, a stretch of road, a seasonal camp — warped by their exposure to the Shadow Plane but recognizable. The second are the remnants of Davos's fortress and other Shadow Plane architecture that materialized during the overlap: sections of wall made from a dark stone that is warm to the touch and slightly flexible, doorframes that open onto nothing, and the foundations of the fortress itself — a sprawling footprint of dark stone that marks where the central structure stood before the planes separated. The third, and most disturbing, are the chains. Hundreds of feet of black iron chain, hanging from Shadow-built frames, lying coiled on the ground, or stretched between structures in patterns that suggest they once restrained something. The chains are cold, corroded, and emit a faint sound when touched — a distant rattling, as though the chain extends further than its visible length and the sound is traveling along it from somewhere far away.

The Shadow-Touched Lands are avoided by locals, monitored cautiously by Soractian explorers, and studied at arm's length by Astral Circle researchers who have established a small field camp at the zone's eastern margin. The most significant ongoing concern is whether the Shadow Plane merger left any permanent thin spots — points where the boundary between planes remains weakened and could be breached again, deliberately or accidentally. The identification of the Devil Cube as a Taovanian artifact has prompted the Church of Tiovin and the paladins of Paladine to take particular interest in the site, as it represents confirmed evidence of Taovan's ability to create artifacts capable of merging planes — a capability whose implications extend far beyond a single incident in the Lavantine interior. The field camp's instruments have detected fluctuations consistent with planar thinning at three locations within the zone, all of which have been marked and warded. Whether these thin spots will heal naturally, persist indefinitely, or widen over time is unknown.
#### The Krutan Ruins of Vel Morath

In the hills between Kruta and Medan, the ruins of Vel Morath — a fortress-city built by the Krutan Overlords approximately 1,800 BW and abandoned after a magical catastrophe approximately 900 BW — stand as a testament to the destructive potential of concentrated arcane power wielded without restraint.

Vel Morath was constructed during Kruta's expansionist period as a forward military base for campaigns against the native Laveri city-states. The fortress was built to house a garrison of warriors and, more importantly, to serve as a magical staging ground — a facility where the Overlords could prepare and launch mass-effect offensive spells against targets too distant or too well-defended for conventional assault. The fortress's central feature was a focusing tower — a structure of enchanted basalt designed to concentrate and direct arcane energy projected by multiple spellcasters simultaneously, creating effects that no single mage could achieve alone. This approach bears a superficial resemblance to elven ritual accretion — the practice of building a charge of shaped mana over hours or days through coordinated circles of casters — but where elven nexus-weaving creates temporary loci for constructive purposes, the Krutan focusing tower was designed as a weapon, compressing offensive magical energy and directing it with devastating force.

The focusing tower killed Vel Morath. Around 900 BW, during a test of a new offensive spell configuration, the tower's enchantments destabilized. The focused energy discharged inward rather than outward, destroying the tower, the spellcasters operating it, and approximately two-thirds of the fortress in a detonation that witnesses in Kruta — over a hundred miles away — mistook for an earthquake. The surviving third of the fortress, protected by its own defensive wards, remained standing but uninhabitable — the magical contamination from the discharged energy rendered the area toxic to living creatures for centuries.

Today, Vel Morath is still partially contaminated. The fortress ruins — massive walls of blackened basalt, collapsed tower foundations, and the glass-lined crater where the focusing tower stood — are surrounded by a zone of dead vegetation approximately half a mile in radius. Within this zone, the air carries a sharp, ozone-like smell, metal corrodes rapidly, and exposed skin develops a tingling rash after prolonged exposure. The contamination is not necromantic (Zebulban priests have examined the site and found no negative energy) but arcane — raw magical energy leaking from the saturated stone, producing effects on living tissue and manufactured materials that healers and alchemists are still cataloging.

Krutan agents visit Vel Morath periodically, presumably to study the focusing tower's remains and to recover whatever enchanted materials the contamination has not destroyed. These visits are conducted by Overlord-level spellcasters protected by elaborate magical shielding, and they never last more than a few hours. What the Overlords learn from these visits, and whether they are attempting to rebuild the focusing tower technology, is a subject of considerable concern in Medan and Soractian.

### Minor Ruins

#### Crusader's Bane

Along the interior route that Tentigo's Third Crusade (397 AW) followed into the Lands of Chaos, a series of ruined encampments marks the crusading army's advance — and its retreat. The largest of these, known locally as Crusader's Bane, was the site of a significant engagement between Tentigo's forces and a coalition of Laveri defenders that halted the crusade's forward progress and began the long, costly withdrawal.

The ruins consist of earthwork fortifications, the foundations of wooden palisades (the wood itself rotted away centuries ago), stone fire pits, and the scattered detritus of a military camp that housed several thousand soldiers for weeks: rusted metal fragments, pottery shards, a few surviving stone monuments bearing Tiovinian inscriptions that the local population has neither removed nor defaced, regarding them with the indifference of people who have more pressing concerns than the religious slogans of a defeated invader.

The most notable feature of Crusader's Bane is the mass grave on its eastern edge — a shallow trench where the crusaders buried their dead after the battle. The grave has been undisturbed for over four centuries, and unlike the Dukhan plague pits or the Sepulcher, it shows no signs of necromantic contamination. The dead rest peacefully, which the Church of Tiovin attributes to the holy blessings the crusaders' priests placed on the grave and the local Laveri attribute to the dead simply being dead, with no special cosmic significance attached.

## THE LANDS OF HONOR

The Yashaki Islands' ruins are predominantly volcanic in origin — settlements destroyed by eruptions, temples buried under lava flows, and coastal communities submerged by the tsunamis that accompany major seismic events. The Ejiin have a philosophical relationship with their ruins that differs from the attitudes of mainland cultures: destruction by natural forces is understood as part of the cosmic cycle, and ruins are honored rather than mourned, their destruction accepted as Ashura's will or the inevitable consequence of building in a geologically active archipelago.

### Minor Ruins

#### The Sunken Torii of Hama Bay

In the waters off the Great Island's eastern coast, visible at low tide as a row of stone arches rising from the waves, the remains of a shrine complex dating to the early Miyasaki period stand as one of the Yashaki Islands' most recognizable landmarks. The shrine was built on a coastal shelf that subsequently subsided during a major earthquake approximately 800 BW, dropping the entire complex below sea level. The torii gates — stone archways marking the passage from mundane to sacred space — survived the subsidence and the subsequent centuries of wave action, their massive construction resisting erosion that would have destroyed lesser structures.

The shrine's submerged chambers are accessible to skilled divers and contain coral-encrusted carvings, bronze implements preserved by the anaerobic conditions of the sealed rooms, and — according to Veppankarai intelligence operatives who have explored the site — a sealed inner chamber whose door bears wards that remain active despite centuries of submersion. What the sealed chamber contains is unknown; the Miyasaki Shogunate has declared the entire site sacred and forbidden unauthorized exploration, a decree enforced by samurai patrols of the surrounding waters.

## CROSS-REGIONAL SITES

### The Ley-Line Scars

The ley-line network that Asti established approximately 20,000 BW and later refined alongside the Akovarian civilization around 8,200 BW left permanent traces in the fabric of the world when it was destroyed in the Akovarian catastrophe of 6,830 BW. These traces — termed "ley-line scars" by modern scholars — are the dormant remnants of channels that once carried magical energy across continents. They are not visible to ordinary perception, but they produce subtle effects on their surroundings: water flows differently where scars cross streams, vegetation grows taller and more vigorously along scar paths, and animals occasionally exhibit unusual behavior near intersection points. These effects are minor enough to be dismissed as natural variation by casual observers, and for millennia they were — cataloged by Yrugas scholars as "resonance zones" without understanding their true nature, mapped by individual researchers like the Watcher Sevan Miros at the Library of the Ages who perceived them through unique gifts, and theorized about by Astral Circle scholars who lacked the empirical data to confirm their models.

The scars follow precise geometric relationships, intersecting at consistent angles and forming network structures that are mathematically regular — evidence of an engineered system rather than natural phenomena. Where multiple scars intersect, they form nexus points of heightened magical activity, which throughout history have attracted settlement, construction, and — inadvertently — danger. The Athenaeum of Echoes was built atop a three-scar nexus. Vorthax's Geometrist's Tower sits on a five-scar nexus. Many of the "resonance zones" documented by the Yrugas-era Resonance Atlas correspond to scar intersections whose positions can now be correlated with the geometric principles embedded in Akovarian engineering texts.

Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the dormant scars have begun carrying active energy for the first time in over 7,600 years, transforming what had been subtle background phenomena into a new category of hazard. Ley-line energy follows paths determined by Mazariim's deep geology — paths established approximately 20,000 BW and later refined alongside the Akovarian civilization. Many of those paths have been built over in the intervening millennia — cities, holds, fortifications, and other structures now occupy ground that ley-lines need to flow through. In most cases, the energy flows around or through these structures without significant effect. But in a few cases — where the structure contains its own magical charges, where the geology creates a bottleneck that concentrates the flow, or where the structure's materials are particularly receptive to magical energy — the interaction produces damage.

Ley-line energy does not grant spells to the untrained, but it changes how mana behaves in a place and how easily it can be shaped. Near certain flows, magical effects that should fade cleanly can linger far beyond safe expectations, and workings may pick up secondary behaviors aligned to the line's temperament — heat or rime, shadow or radiance, resonance, growth, corrosion, storm, dream. Practitioners who work near ley-line scars report metallic tastes in the air, harmless luminous motes, persistent echoes, unseasonal winds, and odd animal behavior. Prolonged exposure can produce ley-sickness — headaches, vertigo, compulsive casting, warped perception of time, or spells that continue to "hum" faintly after they should have ended.

Documented ley-line scars since 815 AW include cracked foundations in buildings near convergence points (several structures in Fort Hope have required emergency repairs), spontaneous activation of dormant magical items in locations where ley-line flows pass through old armories or treasure vaults, and — in one dramatic case in the southern Dragon Spine — the collapse of an abandoned mine tunnel when ley-line energy superheated a vein of iron ore that ran parallel to the tunnel's path, causing thermal expansion that cracked the surrounding rock. At sites where scars were already producing heightened effects before the reawakening — such as the Athenaeum of Echoes and the Geometrist's Tower in eastern Beneshire — the intensification has been particularly dramatic, amplifying spatial distortions, temporal anomalies, and undead activity that the nexus energy sustains.

The ley-line scars are creating new ruins as the network continues to stabilize and strengthen. The Astral Circle, the Line-Watchers of Asti, and the Gearwright Guilds are collaborating on mapping the ley-line flows across Nigal, with the explicit goal of identifying structures at risk before the flows cause catastrophic failure. This project builds on earlier cartographic work — including the Yrugas-era Resonance Atlas recovered from the Athenaeum of Echoes in 800 AW and decades of independent scar mapping by scholars at the Library of the Ages — but the scope remains enormous, the funding insufficient, and the need desperate. The experience of Gyru's original Apotheosis Engine — which drew energy from a dozen ley-lines during his ascension in 1,111 BW, demonstrating both the extraordinary power available at convergence points and the catastrophic potential of uncontrolled ley-line energy — serves as both inspiration and warning for those who study the reawakening network.

### The Beneshire Ley-Line

In 824 AW, heroes assisting the Astral Circle successfully restarted a small ley-line in Beneshire — the first mortal-created ley-line since the Akovarian civilization's destruction in 6,830 BW. This achievement, though modest in scale compared to the ancient network, proved that mortals could create ley-lines without the direct involvement of divine entities. By 832 AW, the Beneshire ley-line had been formalized and stabilized, becoming both a proof of concept and a strategic asset that multiple factions sought to control, protect, corrupt, or study.

The 824 AW achievement did not emerge from nothing. Its theoretical foundations drew on decades of prior work, most significantly the findings of an 800 AW expedition jointly organized by the Library of the Ages and the Astral Circle. That expedition recovered materials from two Yrugas-era ruins in eastern Beneshire — the Athenaeum of Echoes and the Geometrist's Tower — that fundamentally advanced mortal understanding of ley-line engineering. The Athenaeum yielded the Resonance Atlas (a Yrugas-era map of over three hundred ley-line anomaly locations), original Akovarian text fragments, and Conjunction Stones (Akovarian monitoring devices). The Geometrist's Tower yielded 155 years of theoretical research by the wizard Vorthax, including mathematical models of ley-line mechanics, experimental records, and — crucially — his documentation of the "seed problem": the confirmation that ley-line creation requires a catalyst of divine or draconic power that mortal magic alone cannot replicate. The expedition also accessed Akovarian engineering archives through a portal in Vorthax's tower, recovering primary source documentation from the Temple of the Seventh Convergence on the Akovarian continent — the first direct scholarly access to Akovarian ley-line infrastructure. These combined findings, cross-referenced with an Akovarian tome recovered from New Sabonaa's expeditions into Akovar's interior, provided the theoretical framework within which the 824 AW ley-line creation became possible.

The ley-line's physical manifestation is subtle — it is not a ruin but a feature of the magical landscape, invisible to the untrained eye but detectable by spellcasters as a current of enhanced mana flowing along a specific geographic path. The sites where the ley-line was activated and where it intersects natural features, however, have become locations of interest. The activation site has been warded and placed under Astral Circle guardianship. Convergence points where the new ley-line intersects ambient magical flows have attracted Line-Watchers of Asti, who view the mortal creation of ley-lines as a fulfillment of their faith's deepest hopes — evidence that the network Asti established can be restored and expanded.

The Beneshire ley-line has also attracted less welcome attention. The Umbral Hand, led by the shadow dragon Noctuminalis, viewed the ley-line as a threat to plans that depend on magical scarcity. Agents of the Dark Whispers — the secret police of the Grand Confederacy of Pragos — sought to corrupt the ley-line for their own purposes. Nergalu Dragons, who believe ley-lines should be controlled exclusively by dragonkind, contested the mortal achievement. These threats were addressed during the events of 835 AW, but the Beneshire ley-line remains a sensitive site requiring ongoing protection. It is worth noting that Zebulban interest in ley-line research predated Noctuminalis's awakening: as early as 800 AW, a cult cell operating in Beneshire was systematically investigating Yrugas ruins for their ley-line resonance properties, a pattern of activity that foreshadowed the broader conflict between entropic and constructive forces that the ley-line reawakening would provoke.---
