# Geography of the Frozen Isles: The Nond Lands

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

Far to the north of the Gnomish Realms, beyond the last dwarven outposts and past waters that freeze solid for months at a time, lies a chain of islands so cold and inhospitable that most peoples of Nigal consider them uninhabitable. The Frozen Isles are aptly named — islands of bitter cold, howling winds, and winters that last more than half the year. The seas surrounding them are treacherous, choked with ice floes in winter and battered by storms in summer. The land itself is rugged beyond measure: tall mountains capped with glaciers, enormous rivers fed by snowmelt that carve deep valleys through ancient rock, waterfalls that plunge hundreds of feet into mist-shrouded fjords, and vast evergreen forests so dense that sunlight barely reaches the ground.

And yet people live here. Not merely survive — thrive.

The humans who call the Frozen Isles home are the Nond, a word that means "honor" in their regional dialect. They are the descendants of Madronian migrants who left the Inner Kingdoms around 2,000 BW, pushing north through the Gnomish Realms and across the frigid seas to settle islands that no other human culture wanted. What drove them north is debated by scholars — population pressure, spiritual calling, the desire to test themselves against the harshest environment Nigal could offer, or simply the restless human compulsion to see what lies beyond the horizon. Whatever the reason, the migrants who reached the Frozen Isles underwent a profound cultural transformation. Within a few generations, they had abandoned the settled agricultural traditions of their Madronian ancestors and forged a new civilization built around the sea, the sword, and an honor code as unyielding as the frozen ground beneath their feet.

The Nond speak Nondalhiem, a language derived from Madra but transformed by centuries of isolation into something harsh, rhythmic, and powerful — filled with vowels bearing varied accents and sometimes brutal double consonant sounds that carry across wind and wave. Common is known among traders and leaders who deal with the southern lands, but the vast majority of Nond conduct their lives entirely in Nondalhiem.

The Frozen Isles have technology comparable to the real-world Middle Ages, though their shipbuilding and weapon-making are very advanced — among the finest on Nigal. Nond longships are engineering marvels of wood, iron, and craftsmanship, capable of crossing the most treacherous northern seas and navigating shallow rivers that would ground deeper-hulled vessels. Nond weapons — axes, swords, and spears forged with techniques learned from dwarven neighbors — are prized for their quality and durability. Analogs to the various Nordic cultures of our world — Norse, Scandinavian, Viking-Age — can be found here.

The Frozen Isles occupy a large archipelago in the far northern waters off the continent of Nigal. The islands range in size from massive — large enough to contain entire kingdoms — to tiny rocky skerries barely visible above the waves. The terrain is dominated by mountains, fjords, dense evergreen forests, and tundra. Glaciers grind slowly down from the highest peaks. Rivers and waterfalls are abundant, fed by snowmelt and rainfall. The coastline is enormously complex — thousands of miles of bays, inlets, channels, and natural harbors that make the Frozen Isles a paradise for seafarers and a nightmare for invaders who do not know the waters.

The climate is extreme. Winters are long, dark, and brutally cold — in the northernmost islands, the sun does not rise above the horizon for weeks at a time, and temperatures plummet to levels that can kill an exposed person in minutes. Summers are brief but intense, with long days (the sun barely setting in the far north) and a burst of greenery and activity as the Nond work frantically to grow crops, repair ships, and prepare for the next winter. The transitional seasons are violent — autumn storms batter the coasts, and spring brings floods as rivers swollen with snowmelt burst their banks.

The Frozen Isles are bounded by the open northern ocean to the north and west, the Gnomish Realms to the south (across a stretch of cold sea), and unexplored waters and ice to the east. The Nond maintain trade routes south to the dwarven and gnomish lands, and their longships have been spotted as far south as the Merchant Nations and as far west as the coast of the Inner Kingdoms. These are not permanent trade relationships in the Federation sense but seasonal ventures — Nond merchants and raiders (the two roles are not always distinct) who sail south in summer and return home before the winter ice closes the sea routes.

**Current Political Organization (842 AW)**

The Frozen Isles are divided among independent settlements and petty kingdoms with no overarching government:

- **Javalh** — The oldest and largest Nond settlement, a major port and the closest thing the Isles have to a capital. Founded ~1,800 BW.
- **Brahjhin** — An honorable meritocracy known for its warrior traditions and philosophical depth. Founded ~1,200 BW.
- **Nond** — The youngest and northernmost major settlement, established during a warming period that allowed expansion into previously uninhabitable territory. Founded ~350 AW.

Dozens of smaller settlements, fishing villages, and isolated steadings are scattered across the archipelago, each governed by its own jarl or clan chief.

**Total Population (842 AW, estimated)**

- Humans (Nond): ~120,000 across all islands
- Dwarves (Bryndawi traders and settlers): ~5,000
- Gnomes (traders and engineers): ~2,000
- Halflings: ~1,000
- Other (elves, exotic visitors): ~500
- **Total: ~128,500**

## REGIONAL HISTORY

Nond history is preserved in the oral tradition of the skalds — poet-historians who memorize and perform the great sagas that recount the deeds of heroes, the founding of settlements, the outcomes of feuds, and the voyages of exploration that have shaped Nond civilization. Skalds hold a position of honor in Nond society comparable to the voice-carriers of the Chak'ti — they are historians, entertainers, diplomats, and keepers of law, their art form serving as the primary mechanism for preserving and transmitting culture across generations. Written records exist (Nondalhiem uses a runic script adapted from dwarven characters) but are secondary to the oral tradition. A saga sung by a skilled skald carries more authority than any carved stone.

### The Northern Migration (~2,000 BW)

The Nond trace their origins to the Madronian peoples of the Inner Kingdoms. Around 2,000 BW, a wave of Madronian humans migrated north from the settled lands of the northwest, pushing through the Gnomish Realms and across the cold northern seas to reach the archipelago that would become their homeland.

The reasons for this migration are preserved in the founding sagas — tales of great leaders who received visions of a land beyond the ice, of warriors who refused to bow to southern kings, of families seeking freedom from the increasingly stratified societies of the Madronian heartland. Scholars offer more prosaic explanations: population pressure, competition for resources, the human restlessness that drives expansion into marginal lands. The truth likely involves all of these factors and more.

What is certain is that the migrants who reached the Frozen Isles were transformed by their new environment. The temperate agriculture of the Madronian south was impossible here — the growing season was too short, the soil too thin, the climate too brutal. Survival required a fundamental reorientation toward the sea. The Nond became fishermen, whalers, seal-hunters, and sailors of extraordinary skill, their lives governed by the rhythms of the ocean and the seasons.

The language shifted as well. Nondalhiem, derived from Madra, evolved into a harsher, more emphatic tongue suited to communication in high winds and across open water. The flowing vowels of Madra were supplemented with forceful accented variations and harsh double consonants that could cut through the roar of a storm or carry across the deck of a ship in heavy seas.

### The Founding of Javalh (~1,800 BW)

The first major Nond settlement, Javalh, was established around 1,800 BW on the largest island in the southern part of the archipelago. The site was chosen for its deep natural harbor, its proximity to rich fishing grounds, and its position at the crossroads of the sea routes that connect the various islands. Javalh grew quickly, drawing settlers from across the archipelago with its access to trade, its defensible position, and the charisma of its early jarls.

Javalh's founding sagas describe the first jarl, Javal Stormborn, as a giant of a man who wrestled a sea serpent in the harbor and claimed the land by right of conquest. Whether Javal was a historical figure or a mythological composite, the settlement that bears his name became the dominant power in the southern Frozen Isles and has maintained that position for nearly four millennia.

### The Founding of Brahjhin (~1,200 BW)

Around 1,200 BW, a second major settlement was established on a large island to the northeast of Javalh. Brahjhin was founded by a coalition of clans dissatisfied with what they perceived as Javalh's growing decadence and moral laxity. Where Javalh was a trading port open to all comers — merchants, mercenaries, pirates, and foreigners — Brahjhin was established as a community dedicated to honor, martial excellence, and philosophical contemplation.

Brahjhin's founders instituted a meritocratic system of governance unlike anything else in the Frozen Isles. Leadership was earned through demonstrated excellence — not merely in combat (though martial skill was valued) but in wisdom, judgment, and service to the community. The jarls of Brahjhin are chosen by council vote rather than inheritance, and any free Nond — regardless of birth or wealth — can be elevated to leadership if they prove themselves worthy.

This meritocratic ideal attracted warriors, thinkers, and seekers from across the Frozen Isles, and Brahjhin developed a reputation as the moral conscience of the Nond people — a place where honor was not merely a word but a way of life. The settlement's warrior-philosophers developed a sophisticated ethical system that blends martial virtue with contemplative wisdom, producing a culture that values the sword and the saga in equal measure.

### Nond Expansion and the Viking Way

Throughout the centuries between their founding and the War to End All Wars, the Nond developed the seafaring culture for which they are known across Nigal. Nond longships — shallow-drafted, flexible, and capable of both ocean voyaging and river navigation — gave the Nond access to coastlines and waterways across the northern half of the continent.

The Nond relationship with the wider world was defined by a characteristic ambiguity: they were traders and raiders in equal measure. A Nond longship arriving at a foreign port might be there to sell furs, amber, and whale oil — or it might be there to plunder the warehouses and sail away with whatever it could carry. Often, the same crew engaged in both activities on the same voyage, trading peacefully where they were welcomed and raiding where they were not (or where the potential profit outweighed the risk).

This raiding culture was governed by its own code of honor. Raiding a settlement that had offered hospitality was deeply dishonorable. Killing noncombatants — women, children, the elderly — was considered cowardly. Taking slaves was practiced but controversial, with some clans embracing it and others condemning it as beneath a free people. The plunder from raids was shared according to strict rules, with the jarl receiving a larger share but every participant receiving their due.

Trade contacts with the dwarven and gnomish lands to the south were more stable and profitable than raiding. The Nond exchanged furs, amber, whale products, timber, and dried fish for metalwork, gems, tools, and the gnomish mechanisms that fascinated Nond craftsmen. Dwarven metalworking techniques significantly influenced Nond weapon-making, and the combination of dwarven metallurgy with Nond design produced weapons of exceptional quality.

### The Iron-Hull Experiment (720 BW)

In 720 BW, Nond shipwrights in Javalh attempted one of the most ambitious engineering projects in their history: plating a longship entirely in iron to create an armored warship. The experiment was a spectacular failure — the ship sank on its maiden voyage, taking a fortune in iron to the bottom of the harbor. But the techniques developed during the attempt — particularly the methods for forging and shaping iron plates — proved invaluable for other purposes. Within a generation, Nond smiths were producing superior plate armor using the techniques originally intended for shipbuilding, and Nond warriors became some of the most heavily armored fighters on Nigal.

### The Glacier's Retreat (305 AW)

Around 305 AW, a prolonged warming period caused glaciers across the Frozen Isles to retreat significantly, exposing new land and making the northernmost islands habitable for the first time in recorded memory. Nond explorers pushing into these newly accessible territories discovered ancient ruins encased in ice — structures of unknown origin that predated Nond settlement by millennia. The nature of these ruins remains a mystery; some scholars speculate they are remnants of a pre-human civilization, others suggest they are dwarven or gnomish in origin, and the Nond themselves attribute them to giants or the gods.

### The Founding of Nond (~350 AW)

The warming period that began around 305 AW allowed the Nond to expand further north than ever before. Around 350 AW, the settlement of Nond was established on one of the northernmost habitable islands — the last major Nond settlement and the furthest point of human habitation in the northern hemisphere.

The settlement was named simply "Nond" — honor — reflecting its founders' belief that establishing a foothold in the most extreme environment on Nigal was the ultimate expression of their people's character. The settlement's residents are the hardiest of the hardy, living in conditions that even other Nond consider extreme. Winters in Nond are so severe that the settlement effectively shuts down for months, its population surviving on stored food and fuel until the spring thaw.

### The War to End All Wars (4 BW – 1 AW)

The War to End All Wars affected the Frozen Isles less directly than the mainland, though the Nond were far from untouched. Orc raiding parties, while unable to reach the islands in significant numbers, disrupted the southern trade routes that the Nond depended on for metal, tools, and other essential imports. The frog-daemons that emerged from the oceans to attack coastal settlements struck several Nond fishing communities, and the massive sea creatures that Zuel unleashed made ocean travel suicidal for a period.

The Nond did not participate in the Concord of Unity or the alliance at Fort Hope. The distance was too great, the cultural gap too wide, and the Nond temperament too fiercely independent to accept subordination to a council of southerners. Individual Nond warriors and war-bands did fight in the wider war, however — drawn south by the promise of glory, plunder, and the opportunity to test themselves against an enemy worthy of saga. These volunteers fought with characteristic ferocity and earned a reputation among the allied forces as terrifyingly effective shock troops.

After the War ended, the Nond resumed their traditional way of life with minimal disruption. Their isolation had protected them from the worst of the devastation, and their lack of permanent infrastructure meant there was little to rebuild. The War's most lasting impact on the Frozen Isles was the strengthening of trade relationships with the dwarven and gnomish lands, as the post-War recovery created demand for Nond timber, furs, and fish that could be exchanged for the metal and tools the Nond needed.

### The Modern Frozen Isles (842 AW)

As of 842 AW, the Frozen Isles remain one of the most unchanged regions on Nigal. The Nond continue to live as they have for nearly four millennia — sailing, fishing, raiding, trading, and measuring worth in honor rather than gold. Their longships still ply the northern seas, their skalds still sing the old sagas, and their warriors still seek glory in battle and exploration.

The wider world has begun to take more notice of the Frozen Isles in recent centuries, however. The Federation of Man has established irregular trade contacts, seeking Nond timber and furs. The Cartographer's Guild, founded in Naarlam in 115 AW, discovered fast-moving trade winds that cut travel time to the Frozen Isles in half, making the route commercially viable for the first time. Scholars from the Astral Circle have expressed interest in the ancient ruins exposed by the glacial retreat, though few have been willing to brave the journey.

In 839 AW, the discovery of a fragment of the legendary Machine of Gyru in the Frozen Isles drew outside attention of a different sort. The machine fragment — located in or near one of the major settlements — attracted agents of multiple factions, adventurers, and scholars, temporarily disrupting the Isles' isolation. The fragment was ultimately secured and destroyed along with the others, but the incident demonstrated that even the most remote corners of Mazariim cannot entirely escape the currents of global events.

The ley-line reawakening of 815 AW has produced subtle effects in the Frozen Isles as well. The ancient ruins exposed by glacial retreat have begun to exhibit faint magical emanations. Storms seem more intense in some areas and less so in others. Nond shamans and priests report unusual visions and portents. Whether these changes are connected to the ley-line network or are simply the natural fluctuations of an extreme environment remains unclear.

## THE NOND PEOPLE

**"A man's word is his bond. A man's ship is his freedom. A man's sword is his truth."**
— Nond proverb

### Overview

**Alignment:** CN (with lawful tendencies regarding personal honor)
**Government:** Clan-based, with independent jarls and petty kings
**Capital:** None (Javalh is the largest settlement)
**Population:** ~120,000 across all islands
**Languages:** Nondalhiem, Common (limited, among traders and leaders)
**Religions:** Tiovin (adapted to Nond culture), Vuleala (significant), Iquinias (significant), Mordrion (among those with dwarven contact), Garnak (among hunters), ancestor spirits
**Primary Exports:** Timber, furs, amber, whale products, dried fish, mercenary warriors, longships
**Real-World Analog:** Norse/Viking cultures, 9th-11th century Scandinavia

The Nond are tall, broad-shouldered, and pale-skinned — built for cold weather and hard labor. They have blonde to red hair, blue or green eyes, and a bearing that combines physical confidence with a watchful intensity that foreigners sometimes find unsettling. Their builds tend toward powerful and thick-set, the product of generations of rowing, fighting, fishing, and hauling in an environment that demands constant physical effort.

Nond society is organized around clans led by jarls — leaders who earn and maintain their position through a combination of martial prowess, wisdom, wealth (measured in ships, weapons, and loyal followers), and the personal charisma that the Nond call "luck." A jarl who leads successful raids, adjudicates disputes fairly, and keeps their people fed and safe commands loyalty. A jarl who fails in these duties will be challenged, deposed, or simply abandoned as followers seek a more capable leader.

Honor — nond — is the central concept of Nond culture. A Nond's honor is their most valuable possession, more precious than gold, ships, or even life itself. Honor is earned through brave deeds, kept through honest dealing, and lost through cowardice, oath-breaking, or betrayal. A Nond who loses their honor is effectively dead to their community — shunned, unnamed in sagas, their deeds unremembered. This is considered a fate worse than physical death, which at least offers the possibility of a glorious afterlife and a place in the skalds' songs.

The Nond are feared across the northern seas as raiders of extraordinary ferocity and skill. A Nond war-band striking a coastal settlement is a terrifying sight — armored warriors leaping from longships, axes and swords in hand, moving with a coordinated aggression that speaks of lifetimes of practice. But the Nond are equally capable as traders, explorers, and craftsmen. Their shipbuilding is among the finest in the world. Their metalwork, influenced by dwarven techniques, produces weapons of exceptional quality. Their woodcarving and textile arts, though less celebrated, are sophisticated and beautiful.

### Government Structure

The Frozen Isles have no centralized government. Each settlement, each island, each fjord is governed by its own jarl or clan chief, whose authority extends only as far as their personal reputation and the loyalty of their followers will carry it. Alliances between jarls are personal rather than institutional — they form through marriage, shared raiding ventures, and mutual threats, and they dissolve when the individuals who created them die, quarrel, or simply lose interest.

The largest and most powerful jarls sometimes claim the title of king, asserting authority over multiple settlements or entire islands. These petty kingdoms are unstable by southern standards — they rarely survive more than two or three generations before fragmenting as rival jarls challenge the royal line. The Nond have no concept of a permanent, inherited monarchy. Kingship is earned, not given, and a king who cannot defend their claim will lose it.

Disputes between individuals and clans are resolved through a combination of negotiation, arbitration (often by skalds, whose knowledge of precedent gives them quasi-judicial authority), and — when all else fails — single combat or raiding. Formal legal assemblies called things (pronounced "tings") are held periodically at traditional sites, where free Nond gather to hear disputes, judge cases, and make decisions affecting the wider community. The thing is the closest thing the Nond have to democratic governance — every free man and woman can speak, and decisions are reached by acclamation rather than vote.

### Culture

Nond culture is defined by three pillars: the sea, honor, and the saga.

The sea dominates every aspect of Nond life. The Nond are born to the water — children learn to swim before they can run steadily, and a young Nond's first sea voyage (usually around age ten or twelve) is as significant a milestone as a Chak'ti child's first horse. Ships provide transportation across the vast distances between islands, economic livelihood through fishing and whaling, military power through raiding and coastal defense, and social status — a jarl's wealth is measured in ships as much as in gold or land. The bond between a Nond captain and their ship is deeply personal. Ships are named, decorated with carved prows (often depicting dragons, sea serpents, or other fearsome creatures), and maintained with obsessive care. When a great captain dies, their ship is sometimes burned as a funeral pyre — the most honorable send-off possible, sending both warrior and vessel to the afterlife together.

Honor governs all interactions. The Nond honor code is unwritten but universally understood — its principles are transmitted through the sagas, reinforced through social pressure, and enforced through the consequences of its violation. The code demands courage in battle (cowardice is the worst of all shames), honesty in dealings (an oath-breaker is lower than an animal), hospitality to guests (a traveler who reaches your door must be fed and sheltered, even if they are your enemy), loyalty to those who have earned it (betrayal of a sworn lord is unforgivable), and generosity with wealth (a jarl who hoards gold while followers go hungry will soon have no followers).

The saga tradition is the mechanism by which Nond culture preserves and transmits itself. Skalds — poet-historians trained from childhood in the arts of memorization, performance, and composition — carry the entire history of the Nond people in their minds. The great sagas recount the deeds of legendary heroes, the founding of settlements, epic voyages of exploration, and the outcomes of famous feuds. Shorter poems celebrate individual achievements, mock rivals, commemorate the dead, and comment on current events. A skilled skald can influence politics as effectively as any jarl — a devastating satirical poem can destroy a leader's reputation, while a heroic saga can elevate a warrior to legendary status.

Nond material culture is practical, durable, and often beautiful. Homes are built of timber and stone, designed to retain heat in the brutal winters. Clothing is made from wool, fur, and leather, layered for warmth and decorated with intricate embroidery and metalwork. Jewelry — arm-rings, necklaces, brooches — serves as both decoration and portable wealth, and the quality of a Nond's jewelry reflects their status and accomplishments.

Woodcarving is the Nond's primary visual art form. Ship prows, door frames, furniture, and everyday objects are decorated with elaborate interlocking patterns, animal motifs, and scenes from the sagas. The most accomplished carvers are held in high esteem, and their works are preserved for generations.

### Religion

Religious life among the Nond is practical, personal, and deeply intertwined with the natural forces that govern their existence. There is no organized priesthood comparable to the Hammerpriests of Mordrion or the Seekers of Rivoleen. Instead, each community maintains one or more gothi — spiritual practitioners who serve as intermediaries between the Nond and the divine forces they honor.

Tiovin has been adapted to Nond culture in ways that southern Tiovinians would find unrecognizable. The Nond do not worship Tiovin as the benevolent patron of all humanity but as a stern, demanding god who rewards courage and punishes cowardice. Nond Tiovinian practice emphasizes the warrior virtues — bravery, loyalty, self-sacrifice — rather than the mercy, charity, and forgiveness that characterize the faith in the Inner Kingdoms. Nond gothi of Tiovin are often warriors themselves, leading prayers before battle and performing funeral rites for the fallen. The concept of Tiovin's Heaven appeals to the Nond as a warrior's afterlife — a place where the brave feast and fight for eternity.

Vuleala, the entity of air and weather, receives significant veneration among the Nond. On the open sea, weather is the difference between life and death. Nond sailors offer prayers to Vuleala before every voyage, asking for favorable winds and calm seas. The Skysingers — Vuleala's wandering clergy — are welcomed in Nond communities, where their weather predictions are valued as essential survival information. Some gothi incorporate Vuleala's teachings into their practice, blending weather-reading with more traditional Nond spiritual observances.

Iquinias, the entity of water and oceans, receives the superstitious veneration of sailors who depend on the sea for their livelihood. Offerings are cast into the water before major voyages — coins, weapons, food, and occasionally animal sacrifices — to appease the vast, alien intelligence that governs the tides and storms. Iquinias is not loved or even particularly liked by the Nond; it is respected in the way that a sailor respects a force that can kill them at any moment. Shrines to Iquinias — usually simple carved stones on the waterfront — are found in every Nond port.

Ancestor veneration forms the personal core of Nond spiritual life. The Nond believe that the spirits of deceased warriors watch over their descendants, offering guidance and protection. Every Nond home contains a small shrine — usually a shelf or alcove near the hearth — where offerings are made to deceased family members. Before major decisions, Nond leaders consult the wisdom of their ancestors through ritual practices that vary by clan and region.

Mordrion has a small but genuine following among Nond who have had significant contact with the dwarven lands to the south. Dwarven metalworking techniques have deeply influenced Nond weapon-making, and some Nond smiths have adopted elements of Mordrion's worship alongside their traditional practices. These smith-gothi are respected for their craft but viewed with some suspicion by traditionalists who see foreign gods as threats to Nond identity.

Garnak, the entity of beasts and the hunt, receives veneration among Nond hunters who pursue the dangerous predators of the northern wilderness — wolves, bears, ice-cats, and the massive sea creatures that the Nond hunt for food and oil. Garnak's philosophy — hunt or be hunted, respect your prey, strength is earned — resonates with a people whose survival depends on their ability to take from a hostile environment.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** The Bryndawi dwarves (trade partners, source of metalworking knowledge), the Gnomish Realms (trade partners, source of mechanical devices and curiosities), individual Merchant Nations traders (pragmatic commercial relationships).

**Enemies:** Giants (frost giants and ice giants in the northern islands are a persistent and deadly threat), sea monsters (various predators that inhabit the cold northern waters), pirates and raiders from rival Nond clans (inter-clan raiding is common and not necessarily considered dishonorable), and occasionally the Federation of Man (whose patrols sometimes clash with Nond raiding parties along the northern coast).

**Threats:** The extreme climate (which can kill the unprepared with terrifying speed), the isolation that limits access to essential imports, inter-clan feuds that occasionally escalate into genuine warfare, the persistent danger of sea travel in northern waters, and the frost giants of the northern islands — ancient, intelligent, and implacably hostile to human settlement in what they consider their territory.

## JAVALH — THE FIRST HARBOR

**"The harbor that never closes. The mead that never runs dry."**
— Nond description of Javalh

### Overview

**Alignment:** CN
**Government:** Jarl (hereditary, subject to challenge)
**Capital:** Javalh (pop. ~8,000)
**Population:** ~8,000 (predominantly Nond, with Bryndawi dwarven and gnomish minorities)
**Languages:** Nondalhiem, Common, Rhunurbar
**Religions:** Tiovin (Nond adaptation), Vuleala, Iquinias, ancestor spirits, Mordrion (minor)
**Primary Exports:** Timber, furs, amber, whale products, dried fish, longships
**Real-World Analog:** Major Viking-Age trading port, comparable to Birka or Hedeby

Javalh is the oldest, largest, and most cosmopolitan settlement in the Frozen Isles — and the least typically Nond. Founded around 1,800 BW on a large island in the southern part of the archipelago, the city occupies a deep natural harbor that remains navigable year-round, even when ice closes ports further north. This geographic advantage made Javalh the natural hub for trade between the Frozen Isles and the southern lands, and over nearly four millennia it has grown into a bustling port city where Nond, dwarves, gnomes, and the occasional human or halfling merchant from the south rub shoulders in crowded market squares.

Javalh is a city of contrasts. Its waterfront is a forest of masts — longships, merchant vessels, fishing boats, and the occasional foreign-built ship all jostling for dock space. Its market district offers goods from across the northern world: Nond furs and amber alongside dwarven metalwork, gnomish mechanisms, Madronian textiles, and Sabonaar spices. Its mead-halls echo with the songs of skalds and the arguments of traders. Its shipyards — the finest in the Frozen Isles — produce longships of legendary quality.

But Javalh is also rough, dangerous, and morally flexible. The line between trading and raiding is blurry here, and the city's tolerance for questionable commerce is a source of both wealth and controversy. Stolen goods change hands openly in the market. Mercenary warriors are recruited for ventures that could be described as trading missions, raiding expeditions, or both. The city's gothi preach honor, but the practical definition of honor in Javalh is more elastic than in the stricter communities of Brahjhin or the northern settlements.

### Key Locations

**The Great Harbor** — Javalh's reason for existence, a deep natural harbor protected by rocky headlands and capable of sheltering hundreds of vessels. The harbor is the commercial heart of the Frozen Isles, handling the flow of goods between the islands and the southern trade routes. Dock space is controlled by the jarl's harbor-master, and the fees collected fund the city's defenses and public works.

**The Shipyards** — Located along the eastern shore of the harbor, the shipyards of Javalh produce the finest longships in the Frozen Isles. Master shipwrights — families who have practiced their craft for generations — build vessels to order for jarls, war-chiefs, and wealthy merchants. A Javalh-built longship is a mark of status across the Nond world, and the waiting list for a commission from the best shipwrights can stretch for years.

**The Jarl's Hall** — A massive timber structure on the hill above the harbor, the Jarl's Hall serves as the seat of government, the primary mead-hall, and the gathering place for the city's thing. The hall is decorated with carved pillars, tapestries depicting scenes from the founding sagas, and the weapons and trophies of generations of jarls.

**The Amber Market** — An open-air market specializing in amber — the fossilized tree resin that is one of the Frozen Isles' most valuable exports. Amber ranges in color from pale yellow to deep orange, and the finest pieces contain preserved insects or plant matter that can be millions of years old. Nond amber-workers carve the material into jewelry, decorative objects, and ritual items that command premium prices in southern markets.

**The Skald's Hearth** — A mead-hall dedicated to the performance of sagas, poetry, and song. The Hearth is the cultural heart of Javalh, where skalds compete for prestige, young apprentices learn their craft, and the community gathers to hear the stories that define Nond identity. Performances at the Hearth can last all night, with audiences contributing food, drink, and vocal appreciation.

**The Dwarven Quarter** — A district near the harbor where Bryndawi dwarven traders and craftsmen maintain permanent residences and workshops. The dwarven presence in Javalh dates back centuries, and the Quarter has become a fixture of the city's commercial life. Dwarven smiths here produce weapons and tools that blend Bryndawi metalworking techniques with Nond design sensibilities, creating goods prized by both cultures.

**The Temple of the Storm** — The largest religious site in Javalh, dedicated jointly to Tiovin (in his Nond warrior aspect), Vuleala, and Iquinias. The temple is built of timber and stone on a promontory overlooking the harbor, its position deliberately exposed to the full force of the northern storms. Gothi conduct rituals here before major voyages, during storms, and at the turning of the seasons.

### Key NPCs

- **Jarl Sigurd Wavecleaver** (CN male Nond Fighter/Champion) — Ruler of Javalh, a pragmatic and politically shrewd leader who balances the competing interests of traders, raiders, and craftsmen with a combination of personal charisma, calculated generosity, and the willingness to crack skulls when diplomacy fails. Sigurd is in his forties, broad-shouldered and scarred from decades of sea voyages, and he maintains Javalh's position as the dominant trading port through a network of alliances, marriages, and commercial agreements that would impress a Xequian merchant prince. He is distrusted by the more honor-focused Nond of Brahjhin, who consider him morally flexible, but his people are well-fed and his harbor is full of ships, and in the Frozen Isles that counts for more than philosophical purity.
- **Skald Yrsa Silvertongue** (N female Nond Bard) — The most celebrated skald in the Frozen Isles, a woman of perhaps sixty whose voice can silence a mead-hall full of drunken warriors with the first note of a saga. Yrsa has memorized every major saga in the Nond tradition and composed dozens of her own, and her performances at the Skald's Hearth are considered the finest artistic events in the archipelago. She serves as an informal ambassador between settlements — her status as a skald grants her safe passage everywhere — and her opinion on matters of honor carries weight that rivals a jarl's.
- **Masterwright Torben Ironkeel** (LN male Nond Expert/Artificer) — Head of the Javalh shipyards and the most accomplished shipwright in the Frozen Isles. Torben is a quiet, methodical man whose genius lies in the subtle art of hull design — the shaping of timber to create vessels that are simultaneously fast, stable, and seaworthy in conditions that would destroy lesser ships. A Torben-built longship is the most coveted possession a Nond jarl can own, and his waiting list stretches years into the future.

## BRAHJHIN — THE HALL OF HONOR

**"Any fool can swing a sword. Wisdom is knowing when not to."**
— Brahjhin saying

### Overview

**Alignment:** LN
**Government:** Meritocracy (Council of Elders, chosen by acclaim)
**Capital:** Brahjhin (pop. ~5,000)
**Population:** ~5,000 (predominantly Nond, with very few non-human residents)
**Languages:** Nondalhiem, Common (limited)
**Religions:** Tiovin (Nond warrior adaptation), Vuleala, ancestor spirits, Garnak (among hunters)
**Primary Exports:** Trained warriors, weapons, mercenary companies, philosophical texts (in Nondalhiem)
**Real-World Analog:** Idealized Norse warrior-culture, comparable to saga-age Iceland's intellectual traditions

Brahjhin is the moral heart of the Nond people — a settlement founded on the principle that honor is not merely a social convention but the foundation upon which all worthy life is built. Established around 1,200 BW by clans dissatisfied with what they perceived as Javalh's growing decadence, Brahjhin was conceived as a community where martial excellence and philosophical depth would be cultivated in equal measure.

The settlement occupies a dramatic position on a large island to the northeast of Javalh, built along the shores of a deep fjord surrounded by forested mountains. The location was chosen for its natural beauty, its defensibility, and its distance from the commercial temptations of Javalh — far enough to maintain cultural independence, close enough to maintain trade and communication.

Brahjhin's defining characteristic is its meritocratic governance. Unlike every other Nond settlement, where leadership is hereditary (subject to challenge) or seized by force, Brahjhin's jarls are chosen by the Council of Elders through a process of deliberation and acclaim. Any free Nond — regardless of birth, wealth, or gender — can be elevated to leadership if they demonstrate the qualities the community values: martial skill, wisdom, fairness, generosity, and the ability to inspire others. The council evaluates candidates not through a single test but through the accumulated evidence of a lifetime — their deeds, their reputation, their judgment in difficult situations, and the testimony of those who know them.

This meritocratic ideal has attracted warriors, thinkers, and seekers from across the Frozen Isles for over two millennia. Brahjhin produces the finest individual warriors in the Nond world — fighters whose training combines physical conditioning, weapons mastery, tactical thinking, and the philosophical grounding that prevents martial skill from becoming mere brutality. Brahjhin-trained warriors are sought as bodyguards, champions, and mercenary commanders across the northern half of Nigal.

The settlement also maintains a tradition of philosophical inquiry that is unique among the Nond. The warrior-philosophers of Brahjhin have developed a sophisticated ethical system — transmitted through specialized sagas and formal debates — that addresses questions of duty, honor, justice, and the proper use of force. These philosophical traditions are not written down (the Nond remain primarily an oral culture) but are preserved and transmitted by specialist skalds whose training emphasizes ethical reasoning as much as poetic skill.

### Key Locations

**The Hall of the Worthy** — Brahjhin's primary assembly hall, where the Council of Elders meets and where the most important rituals and ceremonies are conducted. The hall is decorated not with trophies of conquest but with the names of every jarl who has led Brahjhin since its founding — carved into the pillars in chronological order, each name accompanied by a brief account of their most significant deed. Young Nond study these pillars as lessons in leadership.

**The Training Grounds** — An extensive complex of outdoor and indoor training facilities where Brahjhin's warriors hone their skills. The grounds include weapons practice areas, wrestling rings, obstacle courses designed to simulate ship-board combat, and meditation spaces where warriors practice the mental discipline that Brahjhin considers as important as physical skill. Training is open to all — visitors from other settlements can participate, and many send their most promising young warriors to Brahjhin for a period of training.

**The Philosopher's Cliff** — A rocky promontory overlooking the fjord where Brahjhin's warrior-philosophers gather to debate ethical questions, discuss the sagas, and contemplate the nature of honor. The cliff is exposed to the full force of the northern winds, and sitting in meditation here during a winter storm is considered both a spiritual exercise and a test of endurance.

**The Hearth of Memory** — A mead-hall dedicated to the preservation of Brahjhin's philosophical tradition. Specialist skalds perform here — not the entertaining sagas of heroic adventure that fill the halls in Javalh, but the more demanding works that explore moral questions, examine the nature of courage and cowardice, and challenge the audience to think critically about the stories they have been told.

### Key NPCs

- **Jarl Astrid Truthseeker** (LG female Nond Fighter/Champion) — Current leader of Brahjhin, chosen by the Council of Elders five years ago after a lifetime of distinguished service as a warrior, diplomat, and teacher. Astrid is in her late fifties, white-haired and lean, with the quiet confidence of someone who has nothing left to prove. She leads by example rather than command, and her judgments in disputes are so consistently fair that even losing parties rarely complain. She is deeply concerned about the erosion of Nond honor traditions through increasing contact with the southern lands and has been vocal about the need to preserve what makes the Nond distinct.
- **Elder Bjorn Deepmind** (LN male Nond Monk/Philosopher) — The oldest and most respected member of the Council of Elders, Bjorn is a warrior-philosopher who has spent decades studying the ethical traditions of Brahjhin and developing new frameworks for applying them to the changing world. His philosophical sagas — complex works that explore questions of duty, honor, and the proper use of force — are considered the most important contributions to Nond ethical thought in a generation. He is frail in body but formidable in mind, and his approval or disapproval can make or break a candidate for leadership.

## NOND — THE NORTHERN HOLD

**"This is where the world ends and honor begins."**
— Nond founding inscription

### Overview

**Alignment:** CN
**Government:** Jarl (elected by settlers)
**Capital:** Nond (pop. ~2,500)
**Population:** ~2,500 (almost exclusively Nond, with virtually no non-human residents)
**Languages:** Nondalhiem
**Religions:** Tiovin (Nond adaptation), Vuleala, Garnak, ancestor spirits
**Primary Exports:** Furs, whale oil, ivory (walrus and narwhal), rare herbs, information about northern waters
**Real-World Analog:** Extreme northern settlement, comparable to Norse Greenland or Iceland's frontier communities

Nond is the youngest, smallest, and most remote of the Frozen Isles' three major settlements — and it is the most purely Nond in character. Founded around 350 AW during a warming period that made the northernmost islands habitable, the settlement was established by families who wanted to push further into the unknown, to test themselves against the most extreme environment Nigal could offer, and to build something entirely their own, free from the influence of older, more established communities.

The settlement sits on one of the northernmost habitable islands in the archipelago, in a sheltered bay surrounded by mountains that block the worst of the arctic winds. Even with this protection, conditions are extreme. Winters in Nond last more than half the year, with weeks of continuous darkness during the deepest cold. The sun barely sets during the brief summer, creating an intense period of activity as the population works frantically to fish, hunt, farm, repair buildings, and prepare stores for the next winter.

The name — simply "Nond," meaning "honor" — was chosen with deliberate ambition. The founders believed that establishing a foothold in the harshest environment on Nigal was the ultimate expression of the qualities that define their people: courage, endurance, self-reliance, and the refusal to accept limits. The settlement's residents are among the toughest humans alive, accustomed to conditions that would break people from gentler climates.

Nond's isolation is both its defining characteristic and its greatest vulnerability. The settlement has virtually no contact with non-Nond peoples — the few trade goods that reach Nond come through Javalh, carried by coastal traders who make the dangerous northern run only during the brief summer. Information from the wider world arrives months or years late, and events in the southern kingdoms have little impact on daily life. This isolation preserves Nond culture in its purest form but also means that when trouble comes — giant raids, severe famine, epidemic disease — the settlement must rely entirely on its own resources.

It was in or near Nond that the fragment of the Machine of Gyru was discovered in 839 AW — an event that briefly shattered the settlement's isolation as agents of multiple factions arrived seeking the artifact. The fragment was ultimately secured and destroyed, but the incident left a lasting impression on a community accustomed to being ignored by the wider world.

### Key Locations

**The Founder's Hall** — The central building of the settlement, a massive timber longhouse that serves as mead-hall, assembly space, winter shelter, and storage facility. During the worst of winter, much of the settlement's population gathers in the Founder's Hall, sharing body heat and stories as the darkness and cold close in. The hall's pillars are carved with scenes from the founding sagas and the names of every settler who made the original journey north.

**The Whale Station** — Located on the bay's northern shore, the whale station is the economic heart of Nond. Whale hunting provides meat, oil, bone, and baleen — essential commodities that sustain the settlement and provide its most valuable trade goods. The station includes processing facilities, storage cellars, and the launching points for the hunting boats that pursue whales in the summer waters.

**The Ice-Ruins** — The remains of ancient structures discovered during the Glacier's Retreat of 305 AW, located in the mountains above Nond. These ruins — stone foundations, carved passages, and enigmatic inscriptions in no known language — predate Nond settlement by millennia. Their origin remains a mystery. The Nond regard the ruins with superstitious caution, visiting them only rarely and offering gifts to whatever spirits might dwell there. Scholars from the Astral Circle have expressed interest in studying the ruins, but few have been willing to brave the journey, and the Nond are reluctant to allow outsiders access to what they consider sacred — or at least dangerous — ground.

### Key NPCs

- **Jarl Halvard Frostborn** (CN male Nond Barbarian/Fighter) — Leader of Nond, a hard, weathered man in his forties who exemplifies the settlement's ethos of self-reliance and endurance. Halvard led the defense against frost giant raids three years running and personally sailed the dangerous northern route to Javalh to negotiate emergency food supplies during a famine winter. He is suspicious of outsiders — the Machine of Gyru incident did nothing to ease his wariness — but fiercely protective of his people and willing to deal with anyone who offers fair terms and honest dealing.

## FROZEN ISLES ORGANIZATIONS

### The Skalds

The skalds are the Nond's historians, entertainers, diplomats, and cultural custodians — the equivalent of the Chak'ti's voice-carriers or the Inner Kingdoms' scholars, but with a distinctly Nond character. Trained from childhood in the arts of memorization, composition, and performance, skalds carry the entire oral history of the Nond people in their minds: genealogies stretching back dozens of generations, epic sagas recounting legendary voyages and battles, practical knowledge encoded in verse about navigation, weather, seamanship, and shipbuilding, and the legal precedents that serve as the closest thing the Nond have to a written legal code.

Skalds enjoy unique privileges in Nond society. They are granted safe passage through all territories, even between clans that are at war. They may speak freely at things and in jarl's halls, criticizing leaders and challenging decisions without fear of reprisal. They serve as neutral arbiters in disputes, their knowledge of precedent and tradition providing the legal framework that the Nond's unwritten law requires. The loss of a skald is mourned by the entire Nond people — the knowledge they carry is irreplaceable, and when a skald dies, a piece of Nond history dies with them.

The greatest skalds achieve a status comparable to jarls. Their performances can influence politics — a devastating satirical poem can destroy a leader's reputation, while a heroic saga composed about a living warrior can elevate them to legendary status. Kings and jarls compete for the services of renowned skalds, knowing that the stories told about them will determine how posterity remembers their deeds.

### The Gothi

The gothi are the Nond's spiritual practitioners — priests, shamans, and ritual specialists who serve as intermediaries between the Nond and the divine forces they honor. Unlike the organized clergy of southern religions, gothi operate independently, each serving their community according to their own understanding of the divine and the traditions of their particular clan.

Most gothi serve multiple deities simultaneously — performing rites to Tiovin before battle, offering prayers to Vuleala before voyages, making offerings to Iquinias when the sea is rough, and tending the ancestor shrines that are the most personal and intimate expression of Nond spiritual life. This polytheistic pragmatism reflects the Nond's practical approach to religion: they honor whatever powers can help them survive and prosper, without the theological exclusivity that characterizes faiths in the southern lands.

Some gothi specialize. Storm-gothi focus on Vuleala's weather magic, providing forecasts and storm-calming rituals for fishing fleets and trading voyages. Battle-gothi serve as Tiovinian war-priests, leading prayers before combat and performing funeral rites for the fallen. Bone-gothi specialize in ancestor communication, entering trance states to consult the spirits of the dead on behalf of the living.

### The Shipwright Guilds

Nond shipwrights are among the finest craftsmen in the world, and the guilds that organize their profession are among the most respected institutions in the Frozen Isles. Shipwright knowledge is passed from master to apprentice through years of hands-on training, and the techniques for building different types of vessels — ocean-going longships, coastal traders, fishing boats, river craft — are closely guarded professional secrets.

The guilds are organized by settlement, with the Javalh Shipwright Guild being the largest and most prestigious. Guild membership is earned through a combination of apprenticeship (typically seven years), a masterwork demonstration (building a vessel to the guild's exacting standards), and the approval of existing masters. The guilds set quality standards, arbitrate disputes between shipwrights and customers, and maintain the traditions of design and construction that have made Nond ships legendary.

The guilds also serve an economic function, managing the supply of seasoned timber — a critical and limited resource — and coordinating the complex logistics of large-scale shipbuilding. A major longship requires years of timber preparation (the wood must be carefully seasoned to prevent warping), dozens of skilled workers, and quantities of iron, rope, and sail-cloth that represent a significant investment.

### The Whale Hunters

Whale hunting is one of the most dangerous and important occupations in the Frozen Isles. The great whales of the northern seas — massive creatures that can dwarf a longship — provide meat, oil, bone, and baleen that are essential to Nond survival and commerce. Whale oil lights homes and fuels lamps during the long dark winters. Whale meat feeds communities through the lean months. Whale bone is carved into tools, weapons, and art. Baleen is used for everything from fishing nets to corset stays.

The whale hunters organize themselves into crews — tight-knit teams of specialists who work together season after season, developing the coordination and trust that hunting massive, dangerous animals in frigid waters demands. Each crew is led by a harpooner — the most skilled and experienced hunter — and includes rowers, lookouts, a navigator, and the essential "cutter" who processes the kill.

Whale hunting carries enormous prestige in Nond society. Successful hunters are celebrated in song and story, and a harpooner who has killed multiple great whales commands respect rivaling that of a war-chief. The danger involved — whales can capsize boats, drag hunters into the freezing water, and smash vessels with their tails — ensures that only the most skilled and courageous pursue the profession.

### Mercenary Companies

Nond warriors are prized as mercenaries across the northern half of Nigal. Their combination of physical toughness, weapons skill, fearlessness, and the iron discipline that Nond honor culture instills makes them exceptional soldiers, and the demand for Nond mercenary companies exceeds the supply.

Mercenary service is an accepted and even honored profession among the Nond. Young warriors seeking to make their reputations often spend several years in southern service before returning home with wealth, experience, and stories to add to their personal sagas. Some mercenary companies develop reputations that attract contracts from kings and merchant princes across the Federation of Man and the Merchant Nations.

The most prestigious mercenary companies are those that maintain Nond honor standards even in foreign service — fighting bravely, keeping their oaths, refusing to harm noncombatants, and returning home when their contracts expire rather than settling in the softer southern lands. Companies that compromise their honor for profit are despised, and warriors who return from mercenary service with dishonored reputations find themselves unwelcome in their home communities.

## GEOGRAPHICAL FEATURES

### The Archipelago

The Frozen Isles comprise dozens of islands scattered across the cold northern seas. The islands range from large — some are hundreds of miles across, with mountain ranges, forests, and river systems — to tiny rocky skerries barely visible above the waves. The archipelago extends roughly northwest to southeast, with the largest and most habitable islands concentrated in the southern portion and progressively smaller, colder, and more barren islands stretching toward the arctic north.

The southern islands, where Javalh is located, enjoy the most temperate conditions — "temperate" being a relative term in the Frozen Isles. Summers here can be almost pleasant, with temperatures warm enough for grain cultivation, and winters, while severe, are survivable with proper preparation. The central islands, home to Brahjhin and numerous smaller settlements, are colder and more mountainous, with shorter growing seasons and harsher winters. The northern islands, where Nond was established, are at the extreme edge of human habitation — ice-locked for much of the year, subject to months of continuous darkness in winter, and habitable only through the fierce determination and accumulated survival knowledge of their residents.

The channels between islands are complex and treacherous. Currents run unpredictably, rocks and shoals lurk beneath the surface, and ice can close navigable passages without warning. The Nond navigate these waters with a skill that astonishes foreign sailors — reading currents, tides, wind patterns, and ice formations with an expertise born of generations of experience. This navigational knowledge is as much a treasure as any cargo hold full of amber, and it is passed down within families with the same care that a dwarven smith passes down forging techniques.

### The Fjords

The most dramatic geographical features of the Frozen Isles are the fjords — narrow, deep inlets carved by glaciers over millions of years. These spectacular waterways penetrate miles into the islands' interiors, their steep walls rising hundreds or even thousands of feet above water so deep that it appears black. Many Nond settlements are located at the heads of fjords, where rivers empty into the sea and the surrounding mountains provide shelter from the worst of the winter storms.

The fjords serve as natural highways, allowing ships to penetrate deep inland without portaging. They also provide shelter — a fleet anchored in a fjord is protected from open-ocean storms, and the narrow entrances can be easily defended against hostile vessels. Several of the largest fjords contain permanent Nond settlements along their lengths, creating chains of communities connected by water rather than overland roads.

### The Northern Forests

The evergreen forests that cover the lower elevations of most islands are the Frozen Isles' most important natural resource. The forests provide timber for ships, buildings, and fuel — essential commodities in a land where trees grow slowly and every log represents years of patient growth. The Nond manage their forests carefully, understanding that over-harvesting would be catastrophic in an environment where replanting takes decades.

The forests also provide food and materials — game animals, berries, mushrooms, birch bark, and pine resin are all harvested. Forest management is a communal responsibility, with each settlement maintaining its own woodland territory and enforcing harvest limits. Disputes over forest access are among the most common causes of inter-clan conflict.

### The Glaciers

The highest mountains on the northern and central islands are crowned with glaciers — vast rivers of ice that grind slowly downhill, carving valleys and depositing rubble in their wake. These glaciers have been retreating since the warming period that began around 305 AW, exposing new land and, in some cases, ancient ruins that had been buried under ice for millennia.

The retreating glaciers have revealed stone foundations, carved passages, and inscriptions in languages that no living scholar can read. These ice-ruins, as the Nond call them, are the subject of intense curiosity among scholars from the southern lands, though the practical difficulties of reaching them — combined with Nond wariness about outsiders poking around in their territory — have limited investigation. The Nond themselves approach the ice-ruins with caution, leaving offerings at their entrances and avoiding prolonged stays. Whether this caution stems from genuine supernatural danger or simply from the practical hazards of exploring unstable ancient structures in a glacial environment is an open question.

Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the ice-ruins have exhibited subtle changes. Faint lights have been observed within some structures after dark. Unusual warmth radiates from carved stone surfaces that should be frozen solid. Nond gothi who have approached the ruins report strange visions and a sense of ancient, patient attention. These phenomena have reinforced Nond caution and added urgency to the Astral Circle's interest in studying the sites.

### The Northern Seas

The waters surrounding the Frozen Isles are among the most dangerous on Nigal. Cold enough to kill a submerged human in minutes, battered by storms that can appear with terrifying speed, and infested with ice floes, icebergs, and submerged rocks, the northern seas demand extraordinary skill and courage from anyone who sails them.

The seas are also extraordinarily productive. Cold, nutrient-rich waters support enormous populations of fish, seals, whales, and seabirds. Nond fishing fleets harvest these waters throughout the summer months, and the dried, salted, and smoked fish they produce is a staple food and a valuable export. Whale hunting, conducted in the deep waters beyond the archipelago, provides the oil, meat, and bone that sustain Nond communities through the winter.

Sea monsters — a catch-all term for the various large, dangerous, and occasionally supernatural creatures that inhabit the northern deeps — are a genuine threat to Nond sailors. Most are natural animals of enormous size: giant squid, colossal eels, and whale-sized sharks. A few are something stranger — creatures that seem to defy natural classification, appearing from deep water to attack ships before vanishing back into the abyss. The Nond regard these creatures with the same practical wariness they bring to all dangers: they can be fought, they can sometimes be avoided, and they can always be commemorated in a saga.

## TRADE AND ECONOMICS

### Nond Exports

The Frozen Isles' economy is built on the products of sea and forest. Timber — particularly the slow-grown, dense-grained wood of the northern evergreens — is the most strategically important export. Nond timber is prized by shipwrights across northern Nigal for its strength, flexibility, and resistance to rot. The finest logs are reserved for Nond shipbuilding, but surplus timber is traded south through Javalh.

Furs and pelts — beaver, fox, bear, seal, and the prized white winter fox — are among the most valuable trade goods by weight. Northern furs are denser, softer, and more luxurious than those available in warmer regions, and they command premium prices in the markets of the Inner Kingdoms and the Merchant Nations.

Amber, harvested from coastal deposits, is the Frozen Isles' luxury export. Nond amber-workers carve the material into jewelry and decorative objects of considerable artistry, though the raw material is also exported for processing by southern jewelers.

Whale products — oil, bone, baleen, ambergris — provide essential commodities for both domestic use and export. Whale oil is a critical fuel source, and the supply from Nond hunters helps light homes and workshops across the dwarven and gnomish lands during the winter months.

Dried and preserved fish — smoked salmon, salt cod, dried herring — is exported in large quantities, providing a reliable protein source for communities across northern Nigal.

Mercenary warriors represent an unusual but significant export. Nond fighting companies serve across the northern lands, and the gold they earn abroad flows back to the Frozen Isles, supplementing the limited local economy.

### Nond Imports

The Frozen Isles depend on imported metals — iron, steel, and copper — that the islands' geology does not provide in sufficient quantity. Dwarven metalwork is the most valued import, and the trade relationship between Nond and Bryndawi settlements is the most important commercial link in the archipelago's economy.

Grain and other agricultural products supplement the limited local harvest. The short growing season in the Frozen Isles cannot produce enough grain to feed the entire population, and imported flour, dried legumes, and preserved vegetables are essential, particularly during harsh winters when local food stores run low.

Textiles — wool, linen, and the occasional luxury fabric — are imported from the human lands to the south. While the Nond produce excellent wool from their sheep, the variety and quality of southern textiles exceed what local production can offer.

Gnomish mechanisms and devices fascinate Nond craftsmen, and small quantities of clockwork tools, navigational instruments, and other gnomish products find their way to the Frozen Isles through trade networks. These items are expensive luxuries, but they are eagerly sought by Nond sailors and shipwrights who appreciate their practical value.

Information — news, maps, scholarly works — is perhaps the most undervalued import. The Frozen Isles' isolation means that events in the wider world can affect the Nond long before they learn about them. Traders who bring reliable news along with their goods are especially valued, and a merchant with good information can negotiate better terms than one with merely good products.
