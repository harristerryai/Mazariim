# Geography of the Horselands: The Central Plains

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

In the center of the continent of Nigal lies the Horselands, a vast dry region of nomads and inhospitable towns and nations of humans surrounding the Lake of Tears, the largest freshwater lake in Nigal. Life is not for the weak in this region of the world, where barter is more common than coinage and the horse is the most prized and traded commodity. Dwarves also have settlements and city-states along the northeastern portion of the Horselands, although most ride ponies rather than full-sized horses.

The humans originating in this area are known as Chak'ti, the name of their people meaning "horse" in their language. Their dialect is known as Chak'atan, a rough and guttural language with harsh consonants and sound effects that can carry across the open steppe when shouted between riders. Common is known to tribal leaders and those who trade with the settled nations to the west and south, but the vast majority of the Chak'ti conduct their lives entirely in Chak'atan.

The Horselands have the equivalent of technology from the Middle Ages, although generally major resources are always scarce and the population is somewhat if not completely poor. Bronze and iron tools are common, but steel is a luxury imported from dwarven or Madronian smiths. Horses, weapons, and armor represent the Chak'ti's most advanced material culture — their cavalry tactics are studied by military commanders across the world, their composite bows are engineering marvels of wood, horn, and sinew, and their horsemanship is unmatched by any people on Nigal. Beyond these martial specialties, life on the steppe is austere. Analogs for portions of Eastern Europe, the Mongol steppe, and Central Asia can be found here.

The Horselands occupy the central interior of Nigal, a vast expanse of grassland, semi-arid steppe, rocky badlands, and seasonal wetlands stretching hundreds of miles in every direction from the Lake of Tears. The terrain is deceptively monotonous to outsiders — rolling grassland under an enormous sky, broken by occasional low hills, dry river valleys, and stretches of scrubland. To the Chak'ti, however, the landscape is richly varied. They read the quality of grass, the direction of animal migration, the condition of water sources, and the patterns of wind and weather with the same fluency that a Madronian scholar reads a printed page. Every fold in the terrain, every seasonal stream, every stand of brush has a name and a history in Chak'ti oral tradition.

The Lake of Tears dominates the region — a massive body of freshwater surrounded by marshland, seasonal flood plains, and the richest grazing land on the steppe. The lake is sacred to the Chak'ti, who believe it was formed from the tears of their ancestors during a great sorrow in the distant past. Its shores provide the closest thing the Horselands have to permanent settlement, with seasonal gathering sites, sacred grounds, and the mobile tent-city of Sharma-Ad all located within a day's ride of the water.

The climate is extreme. Summers are brutally hot, with temperatures that can kill an unprepared traveler. Winters are equally punishing — arctic winds sweep down from the northern mountains, temperatures plummet, and snowstorms can bury entire encampments. Spring and autumn are brief, violent transitional seasons marked by sudden storms, flash floods, and the mass migrations of animal herds that the Chak'ti follow. Only the hardiest peoples can survive here, and the Chak'ti are among the hardiest on Nigal.

The Horselands are bounded by the Dragon Spine mountains to the northeast (beyond which lie the Dwarven Nations), the Merchant Nations and Lands of Chaos to the southwest, the Inner Kingdoms to the far northwest (separated by distance and terrain rather than mountains), and the Ancient South to the far south (across desert and scrubland). These boundaries are more geographic than political — the Chak'ti do not recognize borders in the settled-nation sense. Their territory is wherever their herds graze and their riders patrol, and that territory shifts with the seasons, the weather, and the strength of individual clans.

Unlike every other major region of Nigal, the Horselands have no cities, no permanent buildings, no roads, and no centralized government. The Chak'ti are nomadic by culture, conviction, and necessity — the steppe cannot support permanent agriculture on any significant scale, and the people who live here must move with the herds or starve. Governance is tribal, authority is personal, and the only law that matters is the law of the clan.

**Current Political Organization (842 AW)**

The Horselands are dominated by the Chak'ti nomadic civilization, organized into clans and confederacies with no permanent government:

- **Khonayn** — Ancestor Holy Grounds. The most sacred site in Chak'ti culture, a complex of burial mounds, standing stones, and ritual grounds on the western shore of the Lake of Tears. Not a city or nation but a place of pilgrimage and seasonal gathering.
- **Sharma-Ad** — Nomadic Tent-City. The closest thing the Chak'ti have to a capital — a massive mobile settlement of thousands of yurts that moves seasonally around the Lake of Tears, serving as the gathering point for clan councils, trade, and diplomacy.

Dozens of independent clans, each numbering from a few hundred to several thousand people, roam the steppe in seasonal patterns. Dwarven settlements (mostly Bryndawi) along the northeastern border of the Horselands maintain trade relationships with the Chak'ti clans.

**Total Population (842 AW, estimated)**

- Humans (Chak'ti): ~180,000 across all clans
- Humans (other — Sabonaar traders, Laveri visitors, Madronian merchants): ~5,000
- Dwarves (Bryndawi, in northeastern border settlements): ~25,000
- Other (gnomes, halflings, exotic visitors): ~3,000
- **Total: ~213,000**

## REGIONAL HISTORY

The Horselands' history is not recorded in books or inscribed on monuments. It lives in the oral traditions of the Chak'ti — in songs sung around campfires, in genealogies recited by elders, in the names given to every hill, stream, and grazing ground across the vast steppe. To the Chak'ti, history is not something that happened in the past; it is something that is happening now, carried forward in every word spoken and every story told.

### The Madronian Migration (~2,500 BW)

The Chak'ti people trace their origins to the Madronian peoples of the Inner Kingdoms. Around 2,500 BW, a wave of Madronian humans migrated east from the settled lands of the northwest, drawn by the open grasslands and the great herds of wild horses that roamed the central plains. The reasons for this migration are debated by scholars — population pressure, spiritual calling, conflict with neighboring peoples, or simply the human desire to see what lies beyond the horizon.

What is certain is that the migrants who entered the central plains underwent a profound cultural transformation. Within a few generations, they abandoned the settled, agricultural life of their Madronian ancestors and adopted a nomadic existence centered on the horse. The domestication and breeding of horses — a skill the Madronians had developed to a modest degree — became the defining achievement of the new culture. The Chak'ti did not merely ride horses; they built their entire civilization around them. Horses provided transportation, warfare capability, social status, economic wealth, and spiritual meaning. A Chak'ti without a horse was not merely poor — they were, in a cultural sense, incomplete.

The language shifted as well. Chak'atan, derived from Madronian Madra, evolved into a rough, guttural tongue suited to the open steppe — a language of harsh consonants and emphatic sound effects that could be shouted across distances between riders, that could mimic the sounds of hooves and wind and animal calls. The flowing vowels and silent letters of Madra were stripped away, replaced by a practical, forceful dialect as different from its parent language as the steppe is from the rolling farmlands of Beneshire.

### The Establishment of Khonayn (~2,500 BW)

Almost immediately after settling the central plains, the Chak'ti established Khonayn — a sacred site on the western shore of the Lake of Tears. Khonayn is not a city or a settlement but a complex of burial mounds, standing stones, and ritual grounds where the earliest Chak'ti ancestors were interred. The site served, and continues to serve, as the spiritual heart of Chak'ti civilization — a place where the living come to honor the dead, where clans gather for seasonal ceremonies, and where the most important decisions affecting the entire people are debated and decided.

The establishment of Khonayn so early in Chak'ti history reflects the centrality of ancestor worship to their culture. The Chak'ti believe that the spirits of deceased clan members watch over the living, offering guidance, protection, and warnings. The burial mounds of Khonayn are not merely graves — they are points of connection between the physical world and the spirit world, places where the boundary between living and dead grows thin enough for communication.

### The Rebellion of Broken Spears (~2,400 BW)

Around 2,400 BW, a Chak'ti leader attempted to unite the scattered clans against the elemental cults that had gained influence among some groups. The unnamed leader developed the "Spear-Breaker" tactic — a coordinated cavalry charge designed to overwhelm and scatter magic-wielding opponents before they could complete their spells. The tactic became a staple of Chak'ti warfare against magical enemies, demonstrating the nomads' pragmatic approach to threats they could not match in arcane power.

The rebellion itself was only partially successful — some cults were driven out, others simply retreated deeper into the wilderness — but it established an important cultural precedent: the Chak'ti would not submit to magical domination. This principle would prove relevant again and again throughout their history, shaping their relationships with the settled nations whose power derived largely from magical and technological superiority.

### The Formalization of Sharma-Ad (~400 BW)

For over two thousand years after their arrival on the plains, the Chak'ti remained a loose collection of independent clans with no central authority beyond the seasonal gatherings at Khonayn. Around 400 BW, increasing pressure from neighboring peoples — Sabonaar traders pushing into the southern plains, dwarven settlements expanding along the northeastern mountains, and the persistent threat of monsters in the less-patrolled interior — drove the clans to formalize their cooperation.

The result was Sharma-Ad, a mobile tent-city that served as a permanent (if constantly relocating) center for inter-clan governance, trade, and diplomacy. Sharma-Ad is not a fixed settlement — it is a vast encampment of thousands of yurts that moves seasonally around the Lake of Tears, following traditional routes that optimize access to grazing land, water, and trade routes. At its peak during seasonal gatherings, Sharma-Ad can contain ten thousand or more people, making it temporarily one of the largest human settlements on Nigal. At its smallest, during the harsh winter months when clans disperse to their own territories, it contracts to a core population of perhaps a thousand.

The Great Khan — the title given to the leader of the Sharma-Ad council — is chosen by the clan leaders through a combination of combat skill, wisdom, and political acumen. The position is not hereditary, though certain clans have produced disproportionate numbers of Great Khans through their tradition of producing exceptional leaders. The Great Khan's authority is limited — each clan retains its autonomy, and the Khan can only compel cooperation in matters of collective defense or inter-clan disputes that both parties have agreed to submit to arbitration.

### Wars with Outside Powers

The Chak'ti have fought numerous conflicts with neighboring peoples throughout their history, though their nomadic lifestyle and vast territory make conventional conquest nearly impossible. Enemies who invade the Horselands find themselves chasing shadows across an endless steppe, their supply lines stretching to breaking while Chak'ti raiders harry their flanks and starve their horses.

The Great Stallion Drift of 205 BW was one of the most unusual events in Chak'ti history. A magical phenomenon caused all horses in the central plains to migrate westward simultaneously, forcing the Chak'ti tribes to follow. The mass movement brought the nomads into conflict with Sabonaar settlers on the western edges of the steppe, resulting in a series of skirmishes and territorial disputes that took decades to resolve. The cause of the Drift was never determined — some scholars attribute it to a ley-line disruption, others to the influence of an unknown entity, and the Chak'ti themselves believe it was a message from the ancestors.

The First Tiovinian Crusade of 172 AW was the most traumatic external military incursion the Horselands have experienced. The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo, flush with religious fervor, launched a crusade into the Horselands to convert the "heathen horse-worshipers" to militant Tiovinism. The crusade was a catastrophe for Tentigo. Chak'ti cavalry tactics — hit-and-run archery, feigned retreats, encirclement, and the systematic destruction of supply trains — devastated the crusading army. The Tiovinian forces, accustomed to set-piece battles on the Sabon Peninsula, could not adapt to the fluid, mobile warfare of the steppe. The remnants of the crusade withdrew after suffering devastating losses, and the Chak'ti gained a lasting hatred of Tiovinian missionaries that persists to the present day.

### The War to End All Wars (4 BW – 1 AW)

The War to End All Wars affected the Horselands less severely than the settled regions of Nigal, though it was far from untouched. Orc raiding parties penetrated the steppe from the eastern mountains, attacking Chak'ti clans and disrupting the migration patterns of the herds. The nomads' mobility proved both a strength and a vulnerability — they could flee from threats that would overwhelm a fixed settlement, but they could not protect their grazing lands or water sources from occupation by enemy forces.

The Chak'ti did not participate in the Concord of Unity or the alliance that formed at Fort Hope. The distance was too great, the cultural gap too wide, and the Chak'ti's independent temperament too fierce to accept subordination to a council of settled peoples. Individual clans fought their own wars against the orcs, using the same cavalry tactics they had employed against the Tiovinian crusaders — and with similar success. The orcs, like the crusaders before them, found the steppe a hostile environment where their numerical superiority counted for little against mounted archers who could strike and vanish across the endless grassland.

After the War ended, the Chak'ti resumed their traditional life with remarkable speed. The steppe healed quickly — grass grew back over battle sites, herds replenished themselves, and the nomads' lack of permanent infrastructure meant there was little to rebuild. The War's most lasting impact was demographic: some clans had been destroyed entirely, their survivors absorbed into other groups, altering the political balance between the remaining clans.

### The Modern Horselands (842 AW)

As of 842 AW, the Horselands remain one of the most unchanged regions on Nigal. The Chak'ti continue to live as they have for over four thousand years — following the herds, gathering at Khonayn for ceremonies, trading at Sharma-Ad, raiding and being raided by neighboring peoples, and measuring wealth in horses rather than gold.

The wider world's increasing interest in the Horselands has created new pressures, however. Merchants from the Merchant Nations and the Federation of Man seek access to Chak'ti horses — the finest in the world — and offer increasingly attractive trade goods in exchange. Scholars from Saerrus and the Astral Circle have begun studying the Lake of Tears, which they believe may sit atop a significant ley-line convergence point. The ley-line reawakening of 815 AW has produced subtle effects in the region — the lake's water level has risen slightly, the grass grows taller in certain areas, and some Chak'ti shamans report unusually vivid visions during ancestor-communion rituals.

The Chak'ti view these developments with characteristic wariness. Trade is welcome — they need iron, textiles, and other goods the steppe cannot produce — but cultural contamination is not. The settled peoples who arrive seeking trade often also bring their religions, their political ideologies, and their assumption that settled life is superior to nomadic existence. The Chak'ti have heard these arguments before. They remain unconvinced.

## THE CHAK'TI PEOPLE

**"The horse carries the warrior. The warrior carries the clan. The clan carries the people. The people carry the ancestors."**
— Chak'ti proverb

### Overview

**Alignment:** N (with chaotic tendencies)
**Government:** Tribal Confederacy (clan-based, with the Great Khan as nominal leader)
**Capital:** Sharma-Ad (mobile tent-city, pop. ~1,000 – 10,000 depending on season)
**Population:** ~180,000 across all clans
**Languages:** Chak'atan, Common (limited, among traders and leaders)
**Religions:** Ancestor spirits (dominant), Garnak (the hunt), Vuleala (weather and wind), Tiovin (minor, among those with western contact), elemental spirits
**Primary Exports:** Horses, leather goods, furs, dried meat, animal products, mercenary cavalry services
**Real-World Analog:** Mongol steppe nomads, Scythians, Huns, Central Asian horse cultures

The Chak'ti are a horse-centric culture from central Nigal, comparable to the Asiatic races of the Mongol steppe and Central Asia. They have a wide range of skin tones from pale to tan, with straight black or brown hair and dark eyes. Their build tends toward lean and wiry — the product of a lifetime in the saddle and a diet heavy on meat, fermented mare's milk, and whatever the steppe provides.

The Chak'ti are fearsome warriors and skilled archers, with their cavalry tactics studied by military commanders across the world. A Chak'ti rider can fire a composite bow with deadly accuracy at full gallop, control a horse with knees alone while wielding a sword in each hand, and cover distances that would exhaust a horse bred by any other people. Their mounts — bred over two millennia for speed, endurance, and intelligence — are the finest in the world, and Chak'ti horses command extraordinary prices in the markets of the Merchant Nations and the Inner Kingdoms.

Honor and loyalty to one's clan are paramount in Chak'ti culture. A Chak'ti who betrays their clan is not merely punished — they are erased from the oral genealogies, their name never spoken again, their existence denied by the living and (the Chak'ti believe) by the ancestral spirits as well. This is a fate considered worse than death, and it ensures a level of clan loyalty that settled peoples — with their written laws, prisons, and courts — rarely achieve.

### Government Structure

The Chak'ti are organized into dozens of independent clans, each led by a Khan — a leader chosen through a combination of martial prowess, wisdom, and the respect of the clan's warriors and elders. The position of Khan is not hereditary, though certain families produce Khans more frequently due to their traditions of leadership training and martial excellence.

Clans range in size from a few hundred to several thousand people. Each clan controls a traditional territory — a seasonal circuit of grazing lands, water sources, and camping sites that the clan follows year after year. These territories are not owned in the settled-nation sense but are defended against encroachment by other clans. Boundary disputes are among the most common causes of inter-clan conflict, resolved sometimes through negotiation, sometimes through raiding, and occasionally through formal combat between champions.

The Great Khan of Sharma-Ad serves as the nominal leader of all the Chak'ti, but the position carries limited authority. The Great Khan can convene inter-clan councils, arbitrate disputes that both parties agree to submit, coordinate collective defense against external threats, and represent the Chak'ti in dealings with foreign powers. The Great Khan cannot compel individual clans to act against their will, levy taxes, or enforce laws beyond the boundaries of the Sharma-Ad gathering itself. Real power remains with the individual clan Khans, and the Great Khan's influence depends entirely on their personal reputation and ability to build consensus.

### Culture

Chak'ti culture revolves around three pillars: the horse, the clan, and the ancestors.

The horse is the foundation of everything. Chak'ti children learn to ride before they can walk steadily on their own feet. A child's first horse — given at age five or six — is as significant a milestone as a first word or first step in other cultures. Horses provide transportation across the vast steppe, military capability that has defeated every army sent against the Chak'ti, wealth measured in herds rather than coins, food in the form of mare's milk (fermented into the mildly alcoholic kumiss that is the Chak'ti's staple beverage) and occasionally horse blood mixed with milk during lean times, and spiritual connection, as horses are believed to carry their riders' prayers to the ancestors. The bond between a Chak'ti and their primary mount is sacred. When a warrior dies, their horse is sacrificed and buried with them so that they might ride together in the spirit world.

The clan is the basic unit of Chak'ti society — a group of related families that travels, hunts, fights, and celebrates together. Clan identity is paramount, superseding all other loyalties. Marriage between clans is one of the few mechanisms for building inter-clan alliances, and the negotiations surrounding a marriage can take months, involving complex exchanges of horses, weapons, and pledges of mutual support. Within the clan, authority flows from the Khan through a council of senior warriors and elders, with important decisions debated openly before the entire clan at regular gatherings.

Ancestor worship forms the spiritual core of Chak'ti life. Every Chak'ti can recite their genealogy back at least ten generations — a feat of memory that serves both spiritual and practical purposes. Ancestral spirits are consulted before major decisions, honored at every meal with a portion of food set aside for the dead, and invoked in battle through war cries that call upon the names of famous warriors. The burial mounds of Khonayn are the physical anchor of this tradition — the place where the oldest and most honored ancestors rest and where the boundary between the living and the spirit world is thinnest.

Chak'ti material culture is practical and portable. Everything the Chak'ti own must be carried on horseback or by pack animal. Yurts — circular felt tents supported by collapsible wooden frames — are the standard dwelling, capable of being assembled or disassembled in less than an hour by a practiced crew. Furnishings are minimal: rugs, cushions, a central hearth, and storage chests for clothing and valuables. Art takes portable forms — embroidered textiles, carved bone ornaments, decorated weapons, and elaborate horse tack that serves as both functional equipment and displays of wealth and status.

Music and storytelling are the Chak'ti's primary artistic traditions. Epic songs recounting the deeds of ancestors, the history of clans, and the great hunts and battles of the past are performed at every gathering. These oral epics can last for hours, their performers — called "voice-carriers" — trained from childhood to memorize vast quantities of verse and to improvise new stanzas in response to audience requests. The voice-carriers are the Chak'ti's historians, entertainers, and diplomats, their art form serving as the primary mechanism for preserving and transmitting culture across generations.

### Religion

Religious life among the Chak'ti is animistic, shamanistic, and deeply tied to the ancestral tradition. There is no organized priesthood, no formal theology, and no temples. Instead, each clan maintains one or more shamans — spiritual practitioners who serve as intermediaries between the physical world and the spirit world, interpreting omens, communicating with ancestors, healing the sick through spiritual as well as herbal means, and performing the rituals that mark birth, coming of age, marriage, and death.

Ancestor worship is the dominant spiritual practice. The spirits of deceased clan members are believed to inhabit the wind, the grass, the water, and the sky — present everywhere on the steppe, watching over their living descendants. Offerings of food, kumiss, and horse blood are made at every meal. Before major decisions, shamans enter trance states to consult ancestral spirits, their visions interpreted as guidance from the dead. The burial mounds of Khonayn are the holiest sites in this tradition — the places where the most powerful ancestral spirits dwell and where the most important consultations are conducted.

Garnak, the entity of beasts, savagery, and the hunt, receives significant veneration among the Chak'ti. The hunt is central to steppe life — the Chak'ti hunt wolves, wild horses, antelope, and the dangerous steppe predators that threaten their herds. Garnak's philosophy — hunt or be hunted, strength is earned not given, respect your prey — resonates deeply with a people whose survival depends on their ability to hunt and fight. Chak'ti hunters offer the hearts of their kills to Garnak, and the First Kill ritual — a young hunter's solo slaying of a dangerous beast — is one of the most important coming-of-age ceremonies in Chak'ti culture. Garnak's Huntmasters are found among the Chak'ti, though they serve as hunters and wilderness guides rather than formal priests.

Vuleala, the entity of air and weather, is honored by the Chak'ti as the Skysingers honor her elsewhere — through prayer flags, offerings scattered to the wind, and the reading of weather signs. On the open steppe, weather is a matter of life and death. A sudden storm can scatter a herd, collapse yurts, and kill the unprepared. The Skysingers — Vuleala's wandering clergy — are welcomed in Chak'ti camps, where their weather predictions and storm warnings are valued highly. Some Chak'ti shamans incorporate Vuleala's teachings into their own practice, blending weather-reading with ancestral communion.

Tiovin has a minor presence among those Chak'ti who have had significant contact with the settled nations to the west. A few clan leaders who have traded extensively with the Merchant Nations or the Inner Kingdoms have adopted elements of Tiovinian worship, attracted by the promise of an ordered afterlife and the moral framework that Tiovin provides. These converts are viewed with suspicion by traditionalists, who regard settled-nation religions as attempts to domesticate the Chak'ti spirit.

The elemental Old Gods — Pyrantor (the sun), Iquinias (the rare and precious water), Dirgin (the earth beneath the steppe) — receive informal acknowledgment through daily practices that predate any organized worship. The sun is greeted at dawn. Water is never wasted. The earth is thanked before digging. These are not religious rituals in the formal sense but ingrained habits of respect for the forces that sustain life on the steppe.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** The Bryndawi dwarves of the northeastern border settlements (trade partners, mutual respect based on shared values of clan loyalty and martial honor), individual Merchant Nations traders (pragmatic commercial relationships).

**Enemies:** The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo (enduring hatred since the First Tiovinian Crusade), orc remnants in the eastern mountains (persistent raiding threat), monsters of the deep steppe (various predators and magical creatures), any settled power that attempts to claim Chak'ti grazing lands.

**Threats:** Encroachment by settled civilizations seeking to claim steppe territory for farming or mining, the persistent danger of harsh weather and drought, inter-clan conflicts that occasionally escalate beyond raiding into genuine warfare, and the subtle cultural pressure exerted by increasing trade contact with the wider world.

### Key NPCs

- **Great Khan Bataar Ironmane** (CN male Chak'ti Barbarian/Fighter) — The current Great Khan of Sharma-Ad. A massive, scarred warrior in his fifties who united three feuding clans through a combination of personal combat victories and shrewd political marriages. Bataar is suspicious of outsiders, fiercely protective of Chak'ti independence, and possessed of a tactical brilliance that has earned him the respect of even the most independent clan Khans. He rides a legendary black stallion called Thunderhoof, said to be descended from the horses of the First Riders.

## KHONAYN — ANCESTOR HOLY GROUNDS

**"Here the dead speak. Here the living listen."**
— Chak'ti saying about Khonayn

### Overview

Khonayn is the most sacred site in Chak'ti culture — a complex of burial mounds, standing stones, and ritual grounds on the western shore of the Lake of Tears. It is not a city, not a settlement, and not a fortress. It is a place of the dead and for the dead, maintained by a small permanent population of caretakers and shamans but visited by the entire Chak'ti people during seasonal gatherings.

The site covers several square miles of gently rolling terrain along the lakeshore. Hundreds of burial mounds — some dating back to the earliest Chak'ti settlement around 2,500 BW — dot the landscape, each containing the remains of a clan leader, great warrior, or honored elder. The oldest mounds are low, weathered humps barely distinguishable from the natural terrain; the newest are freshly raised earth, their offerings still visible. Standing stones mark the boundaries of the sacred precinct, carved with genealogical records in a pictographic script that predates Chak'atan's adoption of written forms.

The site's spiritual significance derives from the Chak'ti belief that the boundary between the living and the spirit world is thinnest here — that the concentration of honored dead creates a permanent thinning of the veil that separates the physical world from the realm of ancestors. Shamans who enter trance at Khonayn report stronger, clearer visions than anywhere else on the steppe. Some scholars from the Astral Circle who have studied the site (with reluctant Chak'ti permission) speculate that Khonayn may sit atop a ley-line convergence point, and that the "thinning of the veil" is actually the presence of concentrated magical energy flowing through the earth beneath the burial mounds. The Chak'ti find this explanation insulting and irrelevant — the ancestors speak here because the ancestors are buried here, and no amount of foreign magical theory changes that fact.

Khonayn is protected by an unwritten but universally respected prohibition against violence. Clans that are at war with each other may visit Khonayn simultaneously without conflict — the sacred ground enforces a peace that no Khan or warrior would dare violate. This makes Khonayn the only truly neutral ground in the Horselands, and inter-clan negotiations that cannot be conducted anywhere else are often held here, under the watchful gaze of the ancestors.

**Key Locations:**
- **The Mound of First Riders** — The oldest burial mound at Khonayn, believed to contain the remains of the original Madronian migrants who became the first Chak'ti. The mound has been added to over millennia and now rises nearly twenty feet above the surrounding terrain.
- **The Speaking Stones** — A circle of standing stones at the center of the sacred precinct where shamans conduct the most important ancestor-communion rituals. The stones are carved with names and symbols stretching back over four thousand years.
- **The Shore of Tears** — The stretch of lakeshore closest to the burial mounds, where the Chak'ti believe the Lake of Tears was born. Offerings are made here during seasonal ceremonies — horses sacrificed, weapons cast into the water, food and kumiss poured onto the ground.
- **The Caretaker's Camp** — A small permanent encampment of shamans and their families who maintain the site year-round, tending the mounds, replacing weathered standing stones, and guarding the sacred ground against desecration.

## SHARMA-AD — THE MOVING CITY

**"We do not build cities. We are the city."**
— Chak'ti saying about Sharma-Ad

### Overview

Sharma-Ad is the closest thing the Chak'ti have to a capital — a massive mobile tent-city that moves seasonally around the Lake of Tears. Formalized around 400 BW as a permanent gathering point for inter-clan governance and trade, Sharma-Ad expands and contracts with the seasons, reaching its peak population during the summer and autumn gatherings when tens of thousands of Chak'ti converge for councils, trade, celebrations, and the great communal hunts that stockpile food for winter.

At its peak, Sharma-Ad is a staggering sight. Thousands of yurts — circular felt tents decorated with clan symbols, animal motifs, and geometric patterns — spread across the steppe in concentric rings around the Great Khan's pavilion. The camp hums with activity: horses being traded, weapons being repaired, children racing between tents, elders debating in the shade of awnings, smiths hammering at portable forges, and voice-carriers performing epic songs for crowds of listeners. The air smells of horse, cooking meat, kumiss, and the smoke of a thousand hearth fires.

During winter, Sharma-Ad contracts dramatically. Most clans disperse to their own territories, following their herds to sheltered winter pastures. The Great Khan's household and a core population of perhaps a thousand people — retainers, guards, permanent traders, shamans, and diplomats — maintain the reduced camp, keeping the institution of Sharma-Ad alive through the coldest months.

The camp moves on a traditional circuit around the Lake of Tears, following routes that have been used for centuries. Each seasonal campsite is chosen for access to grazing land, fresh water, shelter from prevailing winds, and proximity to trade routes. The move itself is a logistical operation of impressive scale — thousands of yurts disassembled, loaded onto pack animals, transported to the new site, and reassembled in the traditional layout, all within a few days.

**Key Locations:**
- **The Great Khan's Pavilion** — An enormous yurt, many times the size of a standard dwelling, serving as the Great Khan's residence, audience hall, and the meeting place for the inter-clan council. The pavilion is decorated with the trophies of the Great Khan's hunts and the banners of allied clans.
- **The Trading Circle** — A designated area within the camp where foreign merchants are permitted to conduct business. Sabonaar traders, Bryndawi dwarven smiths, and occasionally Madronian merchants set up temporary stalls here, exchanging iron tools, textiles, weapons, and luxury goods for Chak'ti horses, leather, and animal products.
- **The Horse Market** — The largest and most important commercial activity in Sharma-Ad. Chak'ti horses are traded between clans, sold to foreign buyers, and displayed in competitions that determine the finest breeding stock. A single exceptional horse can command a price equivalent to a small fortune in any settled nation's currency.
- **The Voice-Carrier's Fire** — A central gathering point where voice-carriers perform epic songs, recount histories, and entertain the gathered clans. During peak season, performances continue from dusk to dawn, with voice-carriers competing for the honor of being named the gathering's finest performer.
- **The Shaman's Quarter** — An area set aside for shamanic practice, where the camp's shamans conduct healing rituals, interpret omens, and perform ancestor-communion ceremonies. During major gatherings, shamans from dozens of clans converge here, sharing knowledge and conducting the most powerful rituals their tradition allows.

**Key NPCs:**
- **Great Khan Bataar Ironmane** (CN male Chak'ti Barbarian/Fighter) — See above.
- **Voice-Carrier Sarnai Windsung** (N female Chak'ti Bard) — The most celebrated voice-carrier of the current generation. Sarnai can recite the complete genealogy of every major clan and perform epic songs lasting twelve hours or more without pause. Her voice is said to carry on the wind itself, reaching listeners a mile away. She serves as the Great Khan's informal ambassador, her status as a voice-carrier granting her safe passage through even hostile clan territories.
- **Shaman Temujin Bonecaller** (N male Chak'ti Druid/Shaman) — The eldest and most respected shaman at Sharma-Ad. Temujin claims to have spoken directly with the First Riders — the ancestral spirits in the oldest mound at Khonayn — and his visions are treated as near-prophetic by the gathered clans. He is deeply suspicious of the Astral Circle scholars who have been studying the Lake of Tears and has warned the Great Khan that foreign meddling with the sacred waters will anger the ancestors.

## THE BRYNDAWI BORDER SETTLEMENTS

Along the northeastern edge of the Horselands, where the steppe gives way to the foothills of the Dragon Spine mountains, a string of Bryndawi dwarven settlements maintains a complex relationship with the Chak'ti clans. These communities — mining outposts, trading posts, and small holds — are technically part of the broader Southern Dwarven Nations but operate with significant independence, their remote location placing them beyond the effective authority of the Dwarven Empire.

The Bryndawi and the Chak'ti share a history of conflict that has, over centuries, evolved into grudging mutual respect. Early encounters between the expanding Chak'ti and the dwarven settlements were violent — the nomads saw the dwarves as interlopers on their grazing lands, and the dwarves saw the nomads as barbarian raiders. Over time, both peoples recognized the other's strengths. The dwarves provided metalwork — weapons, tools, and armor — that the Chak'ti could not produce on the steppe. The Chak'ti provided horses (or, more often, ponies bred to dwarven proportions), leather goods, and animal products that the mountainous dwarven settlements could not easily obtain.

Trade between the two peoples now forms an essential economic relationship. Chak'ti clans that winter near the dwarven border settlements exchange horses, furs, and dried meat for iron tools, bronze fittings, worked metal, and the occasional luxury item. The transactions are conducted with elaborate courtesy on both sides — the Chak'ti respect the dwarves' craftsmanship and clan loyalty, and the Bryndawi respect the Chak'ti's martial prowess and honor traditions.

The Bryndawi of this region have been influenced by Chak'ti culture to a degree that their northern Gormdawi cousins would find disturbing. Some Bryndawi settlements have adopted elements of Chak'ti ancestor worship alongside their veneration of Mordrion. Bryndawi tattoo magic — a tradition with both dwarven and Chak'ti origins — flourishes in the border communities, blending dwarven runic symbolism with Chak'ti spiritual imagery. The border Bryndawi ride ponies with a skill that, while not matching the Chak'ti, is far beyond what most dwarves achieve.

## HORSELANDS ORGANIZATIONS

### The Clan System

The Chak'ti clan system is the only organizational structure that matters in the Horselands. Dozens of independent clans, each consisting of several hundred to several thousand people, roam the steppe in seasonal patterns. Each clan is led by a Khan chosen for martial prowess and wisdom, advised by a council of senior warriors and elders, and served by shamans who maintain the spiritual connection to the ancestors.

Inter-clan relationships are complex, shifting, and frequently violent. Alliances form through marriage, trade agreements, and shared threats; they dissolve through insults, border disputes, and competition for resources. Raiding between clans is common and not necessarily considered dishonorable — a successful raid that steals horses without killing anyone is viewed as a demonstration of skill rather than an act of war. Only raids that result in death, the destruction of yurts, or the despoiling of burial sites cross the line into genuine conflict.

### The Voice-Carriers

The voice-carriers are the Chak'ti's historians, entertainers, diplomats, and cultural custodians. Trained from childhood in the arts of memorization, performance, and improvisation, they carry the entire oral history of their people in their minds — genealogies stretching back dozens of generations, epic songs recounting great hunts and battles, practical knowledge encoded in verse about grazing patterns, water sources, and seasonal routes.

Voice-carriers enjoy unique privileges in Chak'ti society. They are granted safe passage through all clan territories, even those at war. They may speak freely at council meetings, criticizing Khans and warriors without fear of reprisal. They serve as neutral arbiters in disputes, their knowledge of clan histories and precedents providing the closest thing the Chak'ti have to a legal code. The loss of a voice-carrier is mourned by the entire people, not just their clan, as the knowledge they carry dies with them.

### The Skysingers of the Steppe

Vuleala's wandering Skysingers maintain a significant presence on the steppe, where weather prediction is a matter of life and death. These itinerant weather-priests travel between Chak'ti camps, offering forecasts, performing storm-calming rituals, and teaching interested shamans the art of reading wind and cloud. The Skysingers are among the few outsiders who are genuinely welcome in Chak'ti society — their practical skills transcend cultural boundaries, and their wandering lifestyle resonates with Chak'ti values.

Some Chak'ti shamans have incorporated Skysinger techniques into their own practice, blending Vuleala's weather-reading with ancestral communion to produce a uniquely steppe-adapted spiritual discipline. These hybrid practitioners can predict weather days in advance, communicate with ancestral spirits through wind-patterns, and (some claim) ask the ancestors to influence the weather itself — though whether the ancestors actually comply is another matter entirely.

### The Horse Breeders

While every Chak'ti is a competent horseman, certain clans have developed horse breeding into a science. These breeders maintain detailed oral genealogies of their horses stretching back centuries, tracking bloodlines with the same care that voice-carriers track human ancestry. The finest Chak'ti horses — bred for speed, endurance, intelligence, and temperament — are the most valuable animals in the world, commanding prices that would purchase a small estate in the Inner Kingdoms.

The breeders guard their knowledge jealously. Breeding techniques, bloodline information, and the locations of the best pastures are clan secrets, passed from parent to child and never shared with outsiders. Foreign buyers can purchase horses, but they cannot purchase the knowledge that produced them — ensuring that the Chak'ti maintain their monopoly on the world's finest cavalry mounts.

## GEOGRAPHICAL FEATURES

### The Lake of Tears

The Lake of Tears is the largest freshwater lake in Nigal — a vast body of water surrounded by marshland, seasonal flood plains, and the richest grazing land on the steppe. The lake is fed by snowmelt from the northeastern mountains and seasonal rains, and its water level fluctuates significantly between wet and dry years. During particularly wet years, the lake expands to flood surrounding lowlands, creating temporary wetlands that support enormous concentrations of waterfowl and fish. During dry years, the lake contracts, exposing mudflats and creating water-access tensions between clans that depend on the shoreline.

The lake is sacred to the Chak'ti, who believe it was formed from the tears of their ancestors during a great sorrow in the distant past. Scholars have offered more prosaic explanations — the lake occupies a natural geological depression created by ancient tectonic activity — but the Chak'ti's creation story carries more weight on the steppe than any scholar's theory.

Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the Lake of Tears has exhibited subtle changes. Water levels have risen slightly even in dry years. Certain areas of the shoreline produce unusually lush grass. Shamans report stronger visions during communion rituals conducted near the water. Astral Circle researchers who have studied the lake (with limited and grudging Chak'ti cooperation) believe it may sit atop a significant ley-line convergence point — a possibility that, if confirmed, could attract unwanted attention from powers that the Chak'ti would prefer to keep at a comfortable distance.

### The Great Steppe

The vast grassland that stretches in every direction from the Lake of Tears constitutes the Great Steppe — the defining geographical feature of the Horselands. The steppe is not a single uniform landscape but a mosaic of grass types, soil conditions, and microclimates that the Chak'ti read with expert precision. Short grass indicates poor soil or overgrazing. Tall grass signals rich pasture and good water access. Certain grasses bloom only at specific times of year, serving as natural calendars. Others are toxic to horses if consumed in quantity, requiring riders to steer their herds carefully.

The steppe supports vast herds of wild and semi-domesticated animals — horses, antelope, deer, wild cattle, and the massive steppe aurochs that the Chak'ti hunt for meat and hide. Predators include wolves (in large, coordinated packs), steppe lions, and various magical creatures that make their lairs in the few rocky outcroppings and ravines that break the grassland's surface.

### The Dragon Spine Foothills

The northeastern edge of the Horselands rises gradually into the foothills of the Dragon Spine mountains — the massive range that divides Nigal into western and eastern halves. These foothills are rougher, rockier, and more wooded than the open steppe, providing shelter and resources that the grassland lacks. Bryndawi dwarven settlements cluster in these foothills, mining precious metals and gems from the mountain's lower slopes.

The foothills also serve as a buffer zone between the Horselands and the Dwarven Nations beyond. The terrain is difficult for cavalry — limiting the Chak'ti's military effectiveness — and easier for dwarven infantry to defend. This geographic reality has helped maintain the uneasy peace between the two peoples, as neither can project overwhelming force into the other's preferred terrain.
