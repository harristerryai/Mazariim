# Ruins and Lost Places of Akovar

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

The continent of Akovar is, in its entirety, a ruin. Every hill may conceal a collapsed granary. Every jungle-choked valley may shelter the geometric foundations of a city that housed tens of thousands when the elves of Vand'ardhon were still consecrating their first ceremonial forests. The most advanced civilization in Mazariim's history rose here, flourished for over six thousand years, and was annihilated in a single day — and the continent has spent the seven millennia since then being slowly consumed by jungle, reshaped by volcanism, and populated by the descendants of the catastrophe that destroyed everything the Akovarians built. The ruins of Akovar are not relics of a forgotten past. They are the skeleton of the present, the bones upon which dragonborn civilization, dragon territories, and the fragile outpost of New Sabonaa all rest.

What distinguishes Akovar's ruins from those of Nigal or Vand'ardhon is not their age — the elven cities of Vand'ardhon are older — but the sheer concentration of magical infrastructure they contain and the violence of the event that created them. The Akovarian civilization did not decline gradually, was not conquered by invaders, and did not succumb to internal corruption. It was killed in an instant by the detonation of its own greatest achievement: the ley-line network that Asti's avatar had powered for over a thousand years. When the priests transferred Asti's consciousness into the Mask of the Pharaoh and the ley-lines lost their stabilizing presence, the accumulated magical energy of the entire network discharged simultaneously. Cities were vaporized. The landscape was reshaped. Surviving humans were genetically transformed by the surging mana, their blood merging with dragon blood and animal blood in ways that no natural process could produce. The ley-line channels themselves — rivers of magical energy that had flowed between pyramid cities according to precise geometric principles — exploded, leaving scars in the fabric of reality that persist to this day as wild magic zones, dead magic zones, and the erratic portals to other planes that make Akovar the most magically hazardous continent on Mazariim.

The ley-line reawakening of 815 AW — when heroes freed Asti from the Mask of the Pharaoh at the Grand Temple in Amaunet and the ancient network blazed back to life — transformed the continent's relationship with its ruins in ways that are still unfolding twenty-seven years later. Pyramid temples that had been inert stone for millennia began to glow with golden light. Crystalline matrices in underground chambers started humming with stored power. Warding circles carved into floors seven thousand years ago flickered to life, their enchantments waiting patiently for the energy source that had finally returned. The restored ley-line flows have not reached their ancient strength — the network is partially active, with major channels carrying energy that Line-Watchers describe as strong but unstable, prone to surges and fluctuations — but even this partial restoration has made every significant Akovarian ruin on the continent a site of active magical power rather than a dormant monument to the past.

This reawakening has also made the ruins more dangerous. Wild magic zones near active ley-lines have become less predictable rather than less severe, their chaotic energies excited by the ordered flows passing through them. Dead magic zones at the edges of the restored network have begun to flicker — moments of mana flow followed by abrupt cessation, creating conditions where a spellcaster's protections may function one moment and fail the next. Undead sustained by ambient magical energy have grown more active, particularly in the catacombs beneath Amaunet where the lich Thz'zar Ja'laao stirs with renewed purpose. Creatures drawn by ley-line energy arrive through the portals in greater numbers, and the interaction between restored flows and existing wild magic produces effects that make travel across the continent hazardous even for experienced spellcasters.

The ruins documented in this file represent what is known — or at least what has been reported with enough consistency to warrant documentation — as of 842 AW. Akovar's interior remains largely unexplored by outsiders. The dragonborn who inhabit the continent possess oral traditions describing hundreds of sites that no foreign scholar has visited, and the dragons who claim territories across the archipelago guard ruins in their domains with lethal territorial instinct. What follows is drawn from the accounts of the 815 AW heroes who freed Asti, from Line-Watcher and Astral Circle expeditions launched from New Sabonaa since the reawakening, from dragonborn oral histories shared with outside scholars by the Eastern Shore Confederation, and from the research notes of the wizard Vorthax — preserved in his tower on Nigal for 3,500 years and recovered in 800 AW — which document Akovarian sites he visited through a cross-continental portal during the Yrugas era. New discoveries are reported with every expedition that returns to New Sabonaa. Many expeditions do not return at all.

## THE RUINED CAPITAL

### Amaunet

**Common Name:** The Ruined Capital / The Dragon's Grave / Ground Zero
**Akovarian Name:** Amaunet-Ka-Sehel (City of the Golden Light)
**Type:** Ruined City and Pyramid-Temple Complex
**Original Function:** Capital of the Akovarian civilization; seat of Asti's avatar; hub of the ley-line network
**Location:** Eastern coast of the main continent, at the widest point of the crescent
**Era Built:** ~12,000 BW (earliest structures); expanded continuously until 6,830 BW
**Era Destroyed:** 6,830 BW (Dragon War; ley-line detonation)
**Current Claimant:** The Red Wardens — a clan of red dragons led by the ancient great wyrm Kharazzax
**Current State:** Partially destroyed; lower city structurally intact but buried under debris and jungle; Grand Temple complex occupied by red dragons; deepest catacombs haunted by the lich Thz'zar Ja'laao; ley-line convergence point reactivated since 815 AW

#### Historical Background

Amaunet was the first city of the Akovarian civilization and its last. When Asti's avatar descended to the islands approximately twelve thousand years before the War to End All Wars, the seafaring humans who had settled the archipelago gathered in a small valley on the eastern coast — drawn by the mass of energy and light that hovered above a clearing like a small sun that gave off no heat. They built the first stone pyramid beneath the avatar, a modest structure that would be expanded, rebuilt, and eventually absorbed into the largest temple complex on the continent. Around this first temple, the city of Amaunet grew over millennia from a village of worshipers into the capital of the most advanced human civilization in Mazariim's history.

At its height around 7,000 BW, Amaunet was home to an estimated sixty thousand inhabitants — priests, scholars, engineers, artisans, farmers, and the administrators of a civilization that stretched across the entire archipelago. The city was organized according to geometric principles derived from Asti's teachings, its streets and buildings aligned with ley-line flows in patterns that enhanced magical efficiency and promoted the health and longevity of its residents. Seven major ley-lines converged at the Grand Temple, making Amaunet the hub of the entire network — the point where the greatest concentration of magical energy on the planet was gathered, focused, and distributed to the pyramid cities across the islands.

The city was divided into concentric districts radiating outward from the Grand Temple. The innermost district — the Sacred Precinct — housed the temple complex itself, the residences of the senior clergy, and the administrative buildings of the Akovarian government. The second ring contained the scholarly institutions: libraries, observatories, laboratories where arcane and divine magic were studied and combined, and the training facilities where young Akovarians learned the geometric principles of ley-line engineering. The third ring held the artisan quarters, markets, and workshops that sustained the city's material needs. The outermost districts were residential, with the agricultural terraces and irrigation systems that fed the population extending beyond the city walls into the surrounding coastal plain.

The Grand Temple was the spiritual and functional heart of the entire civilization. A seven-tiered pyramid rising approximately three hundred feet above the Sacred Precinct, it was constructed from white limestone quarried from the highlands and faced with polished marble that blazed in the tropical sun. Each tier corresponded to one of the seven major ley-lines that converged beneath the structure, and the engineering of each tier was tuned to the specific frequency of its corresponding line — a feat of combined arcane and engineering precision that modern scholars are only beginning to understand from recovered texts. At the pyramid's apex, in a chamber of crystalline walls designed to focus and amplify Asti's healing energy, the entity's avatar resided — an unmoving, unspeaking presence of pure positive energy that had watched over the Akovarians for over five thousand years.

Beneath the Grand Temple, the catacombs extended deep into the bedrock. The upper catacomb levels housed the temple's administrative functions — record-keeping, supply storage, the mundane logistics of maintaining a massive religious and governmental complex. The middle levels contained the ley-line management infrastructure: monitoring stations, flow regulators, and the crystalline matrices that allowed the temple's engineers to adjust the network's output across the entire archipelago. The deepest levels — accessible only to the highest-ranking clergy — held the most sacred spaces: the Chamber of Convergence where all seven ley-lines met in a single nexus point of extraordinary power, the vaults containing the civilization's most precious knowledge, and the ritual spaces where the most powerful healing ceremonies were performed.

#### The Fall

The Dragon War reached Amaunet in approximately 6,850 BW, when the first chromatic dragon flights attacked the capital. The city's defenses — enhanced by the ley-line nexus that amplified the magic of every defending priest and mage — repelled the initial assaults. But the dragons kept coming. Flights of chromatics from across Mazariim converged on Akovar, drawn by the ley-line energy that creatures attuned to mana could feel across the globe. Over approximately twenty years, the garrison was gradually worn down by attrition as the constant attacks depleted resources, killed defenders, and damaged the outer districts.

By 6,830 BW, the Akovarian clergy recognized that the capital could not hold. The high priest Thz'zar Ja'laao and the senior clergy devised a desperate plan: they would transfer Asti's essence from the avatar into an artifact — the Mask of the Pharaoh, constructed from priceless jewels and exotic metals specifically designed to contain divine consciousness — and simultaneously cast a mass transportation ritual to evacuate the civilian population to a distant land. The transfer and the evacuation would deplete the ley-line network of its power source, and the resulting energy discharge would destroy the attacking dragons along with the capital.

The plan worked — at catastrophic cost. During the final battle, as powerful great wyrms assaulted the Sacred Precinct, the priests performed both rituals simultaneously. Asti's consciousness was drawn from the avatar into the Mask. The transportation spell seized the majority of the civilian population and hurled them across the world to what would become the Ancient South. And the ley-lines, deprived of the entity that had stabilized them for over a thousand years, surged with uncontrolled energy and detonated.

The explosion at Amaunet was the epicenter of a continent-wide catastrophe. The Grand Temple's upper tiers were vaporized. The Sacred Precinct was flattened. The surrounding districts were shattered by the shockwave, their buildings reduced to rubble that the blast wave scattered across the coastal plain. The detonation killed most of the attacking dragons — their bodies, fused with stone and metal by the magical energy, can still be found in the ruins as grotesque geological formations. It also killed thousands of Akovarians who had not been evacuated — soldiers, clergy who remained to complete the rituals, and civilians in the outlying districts who were beyond the transportation spell's range.

Thz'zar Ja'laao survived. Whether by accident, by the ritual's imprecision, or by his own madness-fueled intention, the high priest was not transported with the refugees. He was at the center of the detonation, channeling the Mask's power, and the trauma of the event shattered his mind without diminishing his magical ability. Over the following centuries, driven by obsession, grief, and insanity, Thz'zar used the necromantic energies saturating the ruined capital to transform himself into a lich — and he has existed in the deepest catacombs beneath Amaunet for approximately 7,672 years as of 842 AW.

#### The Dragon Occupation

In the millennia following the detonation, the ruins of Amaunet attracted the most territorial and aggressive of Akovar's dragon population. The site's concentration of magical energy — even in its damaged, chaotic state — made it an irresistible lair for creatures that feed on mana. Over centuries, a clan of red dragons established dominance over the ruins, driving out or killing competitors and claiming the shattered Grand Temple complex as their nesting ground.

The Red Wardens, as the clan is known, are led by the ancient great wyrm Kharazzax — a red dragon of immense age and power whose personal hoard occupies the ruins of the Sacred Precinct's former treasury vaults. Kharazzax's clan includes approximately a dozen adult red dragons and an unknown number of younger specimens, making the Amaunet territory one of the most heavily defended dragon claims on the continent. The clan tolerates no trespass. Dragonborn who approach the ruins are driven off with fire and claw. The lich in the catacombs below is given wide berth — an arrangement of mutual avoidance that has persisted for millennia, the dragons preferring the surface and the lich preferring the depths, neither willing to expend the resources necessary to dislodge the other.

The 815 AW heroes who freed Asti were forced to fight through the Red Wardens' defenses to reach the Grand Temple — an assault that Kharazzax regards as an invasion and a humiliation demanding eventual retribution. The reawakening of the ley-lines beneath the Temple has only intensified the great wyrm's territorial fury. The convergence point that once powered the entire Akovarian network now pulses with restored energy that enhances the power of any creature that lairs near it, and Kharazzax has no intention of sharing this resource with anyone — mortal, dragon, or divine.

#### Current State

The ruins of Amaunet sprawl across approximately four square miles of the eastern coastal plain, though jungle encroachment has blurred the city's original boundaries. The condition of the ruins varies dramatically by district and depth.

The outermost districts are the most accessible and the least rewarding. The residential and agricultural areas beyond the second ring were built of lighter materials — wood, mudbrick, and unenchanted stone — that have not survived seven millennia of tropical weathering. What remains are foundation outlines, fragments of irrigation channels, and the occasional stone wall that resisted the jungle's advance. These outer ruins are inhabited by wildlife — including several species that have evolved in the wild magic environment and exhibit unusual abilities — and by small dragonborn communities of the Eastern Shore Confederation who scavenge the periphery for artifacts while respecting an unspoken boundary that keeps them well clear of the Red Wardens' claimed territory.

The inner districts — the artisan quarters and scholarly institutions of the second and third rings — survive in better condition. The Akovarians built these structures from enchanted limestone blocks fitted with the precision that characterizes all their construction, and the enchantments that were intended to preserve the buildings against weather and time have partially succeeded despite seven millennia without maintenance. Walls stand to their original height in some areas. Roofless chambers still bear carved inscriptions on their interior surfaces. The scholarly district retains the foundations and lower walls of what were once libraries and laboratories, their contents long since destroyed by the detonation, the weather, or both — but the architectural geometry of these buildings, designed to harmonize with ley-line flows, provides valuable data for researchers studying Akovarian engineering principles.

The Sacred Precinct is simultaneously the most important and the most dangerous section of the ruins. The Grand Temple's upper tiers were destroyed in the detonation, but the massive stone core of the lower tiers survived — a truncated pyramid approximately one hundred and fifty feet tall, its limestone faces scarred and blackened but structurally sound. The seven subsidiary temples that surrounded the Grand Temple in the original layout are in varying states of ruin, from near-complete collapse to surprisingly intact structures whose enchanted stone resisted the blast that destroyed their neighbors. The Sacred Precinct is Kharazzax's claimed territory. The great wyrm lairs in the ruins of the Grand Temple itself, and the dragon clan's nesting areas, treasure hoards, and hunting grounds occupy the surrounding subsidiary temples and the open spaces between them. Approaching the Sacred Precinct without the ability to fight or evade a dozen adult red dragons is suicidal.

Beneath the Sacred Precinct, the catacombs extend at least five levels into the bedrock — and possibly more, as the deepest levels have never been fully explored by anyone who returned to report. The upper catacomb levels are accessible through entrances in the Grand Temple's base and through secondary passages that open into the surrounding ruins. These upper levels are dangerous but navigable: structural collapse has blocked some corridors and flooded others, but the Akovarian builders' engineering ensures that the basic framework remains sound. The hazards are magical rather than structural — ambient mana from the reactivated convergence point creates unpredictable effects, and the undead that form spontaneously in the catacombs' negative-energy-saturated depths occasionally wander to the upper levels.

The middle catacomb levels contain the ley-line management infrastructure that once controlled the network across the entire archipelago. The monitoring stations, flow regulators, and crystalline matrices are intact — preserved by the same bedrock that shielded them from the surface detonation — but they have been inert for 7,645 years. Since the reawakening of 815 AW, some of this infrastructure has begun to respond to the restored ley-line energy. Flow regulators hum with activity. Monitoring crystals glow with data that no one alive can interpret. The crystalline matrices — massive structures of faceted stone designed to focus and redirect ley-line energy — vibrate at frequencies that produce effects ranging from mild nausea to spatial distortion in those who approach them. The middle levels are of extraordinary interest to Line-Watchers and Astral Circle researchers, who believe they contain the operational controls for the entire Akovarian network — the equivalent of finding the control room of a machine that spans a continent. Accessing them requires passing through the Red Wardens' territory above and the lich's domain below.

The deepest catacomb levels belong to Thz'zar Ja'laao. The lich lairs in the Chamber of Convergence — the nexus point where all seven major ley-lines meet — surrounded by undead servants created from the remains of Akovarians who died in the detonation. His domain extends through an unknown number of sub-levels carved into the bedrock during the millennia of his undead existence, an excavation conducted with the patience of a being for whom time has no meaning and the magical power of a wizard-priest who has practiced his art for nearly eight thousand years.

Thz'zar Ja'laao's mind oscillates between periods of lucid brilliance and episodes of raving madness. During his lucid phases, he conducts magical research of extraordinary sophistication — his knowledge spans the full breadth of Akovarian arcane and divine tradition, augmented by millennia of undeath. During his mad phases, he believes himself still the high priest of a living civilization, issuing orders to courtiers who have been dust for thousands of years, conducting rituals for a deity who is no longer imprisoned, and raging against a catastrophe that happened so long ago the world has forgotten it. He is aware of the ley-line reawakening and regards it with a tortured mixture of hope — that Asti's return might restore what was lost — and fury — that outsiders dare to touch what he considers his domain, the legacy he has guarded alone for seventy-seven centuries. He is one of the most dangerous individual beings on the continent, and no one who has ventured into his domain has survived to describe it.

#### Key Contents

The Grand Temple core: surviving lower tiers containing the architectural geometry of the original seven-tier pyramid, the entrances to the catacomb system, and the convergence point where Asti's avatar once resided and where the release ritual was performed in 815 AW. The seven subsidiary temples in various states of ruin, several containing intact interior chambers with carved inscriptions, astronomical alignments, and geometric patterns of ley-line engineering significance. The middle catacomb levels containing the ley-line management infrastructure — monitoring stations, flow regulators, and crystalline matrices — that once controlled the network across the entire archipelago. The deepest catacombs containing the Chamber of Convergence, the lich's domain, and an unknown quantity of Akovarian knowledge preserved in vaults that Thz'zar Ja'laao has maintained for millennia. The Red Wardens' hoards — accumulated over centuries of territorial dominance — which almost certainly contain Akovarian artifacts of significant value mixed with the more conventional treasures that dragons accumulate.

#### Hazards

Red dragon clan (approximately a dozen adults plus younger specimens) controlling the Sacred Precinct and surrounding ruins. The lich Thz'zar Ja'laao in the deepest catacombs — a being of wizard-lord power with nearly eight thousand years of magical experience. Undead of various types throughout the catacomb levels, sustained by ambient negative energy and the proximity of the lich's domain. Wild magic zones in and around the ruins, particularly intense near the detonation epicenter. Reactivated ley-line energy producing unpredictable effects on spellcasters, magical items, and creatures with innate magical abilities. Structural instability in the upper ruins where seven millennia of weathering have weakened buildings that the detonation damaged. Tropical environment hazards — heat, humidity, venomous wildlife, disease.

## THE PYRAMID-TEMPLE COMPLEXES

The Akovarian civilization built pyramid temples at every major ley-line convergence point across the archipelago, creating a network of religious, administrative, and magical-engineering centers that managed the ley-line flows between them. At the network's height around 7,000 BW, dozens of pyramid complexes dotted the islands — from the Grand Temple at Amaunet where seven lines converged to smaller installations at secondary and tertiary nodes where two or three lines intersected. The detonation of 6,830 BW destroyed many of these complexes outright, and the subsequent millennia of jungle, volcanism, and dragon occupation have reduced others to rubble. But the Akovarians built to last. Their enchanted limestone construction, geometric precision, and deep foundations ensured that a significant number of pyramid temples survived in recognizable form — their upper structures damaged or destroyed, but their lower levels and underground chambers shielded by the bedrock into which they were carved.

The pyramid temples share common architectural features. Each consists of a stepped pyramid of white limestone — the number of tiers corresponding to the number of ley-lines converging at the site — surrounded by subsidiary structures including residential quarters for the temple staff, administrative buildings, storage facilities, and the smaller pyramids that housed the three priestly orders: the Luminaries (religious leaders and healers), the Geometers (technical specialists in ley-line engineering), and the Archivists (record-keepers and scholars). Underground, each temple complex extends into a catacomb system containing the ley-line management infrastructure specific to its node — crystalline matrices, flow regulators, monitoring equipment, and archive vaults storing the engineering documentation for that section of the network.

### The Temple of the Seventh Convergence

**Akovarian Name:** Aset-Khemu-Sekhept (Temple of the Seventh Sacred Joining)
**Common Name:** The Temple of the Seventh Convergence
**Type:** Akovarian Pyramid-Temple Complex
**Original Function:** Major ley-line nexus management center; religious center; knowledge repository
**Location:** Eastern coast of the main continent, on a large island in the central archipelago
**Era Built:** ~8,100 BW (construction spanned approximately a century)
**Era Destroyed:** ~6,830 BW (Dragon War; ley-line detonation)
**Current State:** Partially collapsed; deep chambers intact; claimed by jungle; portal to Nigal active
**Environment:** Tropical; uninhabited by intelligent beings for millennia
**Access:** Via portal from the Geometrist's Tower in Beneshire (Nigal); or overland/coastal from New Sabonaa with significant difficulty

#### Historical Background

The Temple of the Seventh Convergence was the last of seven great ley-line nodes constructed by the Akovarian civilization, completed around 8,000 BW after approximately a century of construction. Built on a large island in the center of the archipelago, the temple served as a critical hub in the network — receiving energy from the capital at Amaunet and distributing it to the southern islands. Its location made it a natural waypoint for ley-line flows, and the Akovarian engineers exploited this geography to create a node of extraordinary capacity.

The completed pyramid rose three hundred feet above the jungle floor, its white limestone casing blazing in the tropical sun. Seven smaller subsidiary pyramids surrounded the main structure, each housing one of the temple's three priestly orders and the administrative and support functions necessary for a complex that served as simultaneously a religious center, an engineering facility, and a repository of knowledge. The complex housed over two thousand permanent residents — priests, engineers, scholars, support staff, and their families — and served as a pilgrimage site for tens of thousands more during holy days and ceremonial occasions.

The temple was served by the three orders common to all major Akovarian religious centers. The Luminaries were the religious leaders — healers, teachers, and intermediaries between the people and Asti's divine presence, their magic purely divine, granted directly by Asti through the ley-line network. The Geometers were the technical specialists — engineers, mathematicians, and artificers who understood how the ley-line network actually functioned, many combining divine and arcane traditions in ways unique to Akovarian culture. The Archivists were the record-keepers — scholars who maintained the temple's extensive libraries and preserved knowledge for future generations, their work considered a sacred duty equal in importance to the Luminaries' healing and the Geometers' engineering.

#### The Fall

The Temple of the Seventh Convergence was attacked early in the Dragon War, around 6,850 BW, when a flight of chromatic dragons struck without warning. The temple's defenders fought back effectively — the ley-line nexus amplified their magic, and the combined divine and arcane capabilities of the Luminaries and Geometers made them formidable opponents. The first assault was repelled with significant casualties among the attackers. But the dragons adapted their tactics, and over the twenty years of the war the garrison was gradually worn down by attrition — constant attacks interspersed with periods of siege that depleted food stores, consumed magical reserves, and killed irreplaceable personnel.

When the Akovarian priests at Amaunet transferred Asti's essence into the Mask and initiated the mass teleportation in 6,830 BW, the ley-line network convulsed. At the Temple of the Seventh Convergence, the terminus ruptured — releasing millennia of accumulated magical energy in seconds. The upper levels of the pyramid were vaporized. The subsidiary pyramids were shattered. The surrounding complex was destroyed. Only the deepest chambers, carved from bedrock and shielded by the earth itself, survived the detonation intact.

The priests who survived longest were those who embraced a terrible adaptation. The wild magic flooding the deep chambers — chaotic mana released by the network's destruction — offered a form of preservation. Not life, not death, not undeath, but suspension — a state between all three that required no food, no water, and no hope. Some accepted this fate willingly, becoming guardians of knowledge they could not bear to see lost. They remain in the Guardian Chamber to this day — if "remain" is the right word for beings who have existed in stasis for over seven millennia, their consciousness presumably frozen at the moment they chose preservation over death.

#### Current State

The Temple of the Seventh Convergence exists in three distinct zones, each presenting different conditions and different dangers.

The surface ruins are what seven thousand years of tropical jungle can do to a three-hundred-foot pyramid. The upper levels were destroyed in the detonation, and the jungle has claimed everything above ground level with the patient thoroughness that characterizes tropical vegetation. The main pyramid survives as a massive mound — its stone core too large for the forest to fully consume, it rises above the surrounding terrain as a tree-covered hill whose geometric regularity is apparent only from a distance or from above. The seven subsidiary pyramids are in varying states of ruin: two are completely collapsed, their stone absorbed into the jungle floor; three survive as overgrown mounds similar to the main pyramid but smaller; and two retain enough structural integrity that their stepped profiles are visible beneath the vegetation, their interior chambers accessible through gaps in the stonework.

The observation platform at the pyramid's summit — where Akovarian astronomers tracked celestial movements relevant to ley-line operation — is a flat area of exposed stone at the mound's peak, cleared of major vegetation by the altitude and wind exposure. The platform retains carved astronomical markings — star maps, solstice alignments, and mathematical notations — that are weathered but readable, providing data that correlates with the engineering documentation in the archive below. The inscription walls on the exterior surfaces of the subsidiary pyramids bear religious texts and historical records in Akovarian script, protected in areas where the stone faces inward or where overhanging vegetation has shielded them from direct weathering. These inscriptions represent one of the most accessible sources of Akovarian text on the continent, legible without excavation to anyone who can read the script — or, more practically, anyone who can make rubbings or transcriptions to carry back to scholars who can.

The administrative levels occupy the middle depths of the temple complex — below the destroyed upper structure but above the engineering core. These levels suffered more damage from the detonation than the deepest chambers but less than the surface, creating a zone of partial ruin where intact chambers alternate with collapsed corridors and flooded passages. The secondary archive on this level contained copies of important documents from the primary archive below — a redundancy measure that proved partly effective, as some chambers survived while others did not. The training halls where young Geometers learned their craft occupy a series of connected rooms with instructional carvings on the walls — a complete educational curriculum, from basic mathematical principles through advanced ley-line theory, presented in the systematic visual format the Akovarians used for technical education. These training materials are of extraordinary value to modern researchers, as they present ley-line engineering concepts in a pedagogical sequence designed for students rather than the dense technical notation of the engineering archives — making them potentially more accessible to scholars who are still learning to read Akovarian mathematical conventions.

The communication center on the administrative level contains equipment that was designed to facilitate coordination between the Temple and other nodes in the network. The equipment is non-functional — it relied on ley-line energy to operate, and even the restored flows have not been sufficient to reactivate devices that have been dormant for over seven millennia — but the physical apparatus and any surviving operational records may contain information about how the Akovarians coordinated their network management across the archipelago.

The engineering core is the deepest level of the complex, accessible from the administrative levels above through heavily reinforced stairways designed to survive exactly the kind of catastrophic event that eventually occurred. The core contains the temple's most critical facilities, all carved from the bedrock of the island itself and shielded by both stone and enchantment.

The Flow Chamber is the engineering core's centerpiece — a vaulted hall containing a crystalline focusing matrix approximately fifty feet tall. The matrix is a structure of faceted crystalline stone, its geometry precisely calculated to receive ley-line energy from the seven convergence channels, process it according to the temple's operational parameters, and distribute it to the subsidiary nodes in the southern network. The matrix survived the detonation intact — its crystalline structure was designed to handle enormous energy loads, and while the sudden surge exceeded its operational parameters, the overload was absorbed rather than causing structural failure. Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the matrix has begun to respond to the restored energy flows. It hums at frequencies that produce spatial distortion in the surrounding chamber, and its facets glow with a pale golden light that intensifies during equinox periods when the ley-line network's energy output peaks. The matrix represents the most significant piece of surviving Akovarian ley-line engineering hardware on the continent outside of Amaunet — physical proof of the principles that modern scholars are attempting to reconstruct from textual sources.

The monitoring station adjacent to the Flow Chamber contains devices designed to track ley-line flows throughout the temple's section of the network. These devices — arrays of smaller crystals mounted in geometric frames, connected by channels carved into the floor and walls — retain residual function. Since the reawakening, some have begun to display what appear to be readings — fluctuations in crystal brightness, color shifts, and harmonic changes that correlate with known ley-line activity in the surrounding region. Interpreting these readings requires understanding Akovarian measurement conventions that modern scholars do not yet fully possess, but the devices' partial activation provides both data and a key to the conventions themselves.

The archive vault is the most valuable single room in the temple complex — and arguably the most valuable collection of ley-line knowledge in existence outside of whatever Thz'zar Ja'laao has preserved in Amaunet's deepest catacombs. The vault contains the temple's primary collection of engineering documentation, religious texts, historical records, astronomical observations, and administrative archives, stored on papyrus scrolls, clay tablets, and carved stone panels in climate-controlled conditions that the vault's enchantments have maintained — imperfectly but adequately — for over seven thousand years. Approximately two-thirds of the collection has survived. The lost third was destroyed by water infiltration in the sections of the vault closest to the surface, where the detonation cracked the bedrock ceiling and allowed millennia of tropical rainfall to seep through. The surviving two-thirds includes the complete engineering documentation for the Temple's section of the ley-line network — specifications, maintenance records, calibration procedures, and the mathematical models that the Geometers used to manage energy flows. This documentation represents the single most comprehensive source of practical ley-line engineering knowledge recovered from any Akovarian site.

The Guardian Chamber is the engineering core's most unsettling feature. In a sealed room adjacent to the archive vault, the priests who chose preservation over death at the moment of the catastrophe remain in their stasis — suspended between life and death by wild magic that the ley-line detonation saturated into the bedrock. The stasis fields have been maintained by this ambient wild magic for over seven millennia, and the beings within them are neither alive, dead, nor undead in any conventional sense. Their bodies are visible through the translucent energy fields — robed figures frozen in attitudes of prayer, meditation, or simple resignation, their features preserved with a clarity that suggests the stasis halted not just biological processes but time itself within the field boundaries.

The guardian priests represent an extraordinary potential resource — and an extraordinary ethical dilemma. If they could be revived, they would be living witnesses to Akovarian civilization, bearers of knowledge that no text could fully convey, practitioners of magical traditions that have been lost for millennia. But revival may be impossible — the stasis fields are sustained by wild magic whose behavior is inherently unpredictable, and disrupting them could kill the occupants, release them in states of profound disorientation or madness, or produce effects that no one can predict. The 815 AW heroes who explored the deepest accessible portions of the temple complex during their campaign reported the Guardian Chamber's existence but did not attempt to interact with the stasis fields, and no subsequent expedition has reached the engineering core. The Line-Watchers and Astral Circle researchers who are aware of the Chamber's existence regard it as a question for future generations — when the understanding of Akovarian stasis technology and the stabilization of the local wild magic environment make interaction possible without unacceptable risk.

#### The Portal

The Temple of the Seventh Convergence contains the Akovar-side terminus of a cross-continental portal that connects to the Geometrist's Tower in eastern Beneshire on the continent of Nigal. This portal — a permanent wound in reality created by the ley-line detonation of 6,830 BW — manifests as a vertical disc of shimmering energy approximately ten feet in diameter, located in a chamber below the engineering core.

The portal was discovered and stabilized around 2,780 BW by the Yrugas wizard Vorthax, who spent a decade learning to control the gateway and then made dozens of expeditions through it over fifty years, recovering texts, artifacts, and observations that validated his theories about the ley-line network. The portal remained active after Vorthax's transformation into a greater wraith during the Yrugas collapse, maintained by forces that are not fully understood — possibly residual ley-line energy flowing through the scar intersection that the portal occupies, possibly some property of the detonation damage itself that makes the wound self-sustaining.

The portal is the only known permanent gateway between the continents of Nigal and Akovar that does not require ocean passage through the magical headwind barrier. Its existence is a closely guarded secret, known to the Astral Circle, senior Line-Watchers, and a small number of scholars and officials. The security implications of an uncontrolled cross-continental gateway — particularly one whose Nigal terminus is haunted by a wraith of wizard-lord power — are significant. The Astral Circle has used the portal for limited scholarly expeditions, but the dangers of the Geometrist's Tower on the Nigal side and the wild terrain surrounding the Temple on the Akovar side have limited regular traffic.

Since 815 AW, the portal has remained stable despite the ley-line energy now flowing through the scar intersection that anchors it. Whether the restored energy will strengthen the portal, destabilize it, or eventually cause it to close is unknown.

#### Key Contents

The crystalline focusing matrix in the Flow Chamber — the most significant piece of surviving Akovarian ley-line engineering hardware outside Amaunet. Monitoring station devices retaining residual function and displaying readings that correlate with modern ley-line activity. The archive vault containing approximately two-thirds of the temple's original engineering documentation — the most comprehensive source of practical ley-line knowledge recovered from any Akovarian site. The training halls containing a complete Geometer educational curriculum in visual format. The Guardian Chamber containing an unknown number of Akovarian priests in stasis. The cross-continental portal to the Geometrist's Tower on Nigal. Religious texts, astronomical observations, and historical records distributed throughout the administrative levels and archive vault.

#### Hazards

Wild magic saturation throughout the complex, particularly intense in the engineering core where the ley-line detonation concentrated chaotic energy. Tropical environment — heat, humidity, venomous insects, disease, and wildlife evolved in a wild magic environment with potentially unusual abilities. Dense jungle vegetation making surface navigation extremely difficult. Structural instability in the administrative levels where the detonation damaged but did not destroy the intermediate chambers. Spatial anomalies in the vicinity of the crystalline focusing matrix, intensified since the ley-line reawakening. The stasis fields in the Guardian Chamber — unknown consequences of interaction or disruption. The portal chamber's proximity to both the wild magic environment and the cross-continental gateway creates unique risks for spellcasters. Potential dragon territorial claims — the temple's location on the eastern coast places it within or adjacent to territories claimed by metallic dragon clans of the Eastern Shore region, whose attitude toward outsiders at ley-line convergence points varies from cooperative to territorial.

### Ptei — The Mountain Repository

**Akovarian Name:** Ptei-Ka-Netjer (House of the High Records)
**Common Name:** Ptei / The Mountain Repository / The Dragon Library
**Type:** Akovarian Repository and Pyramid-Temple
**Original Function:** Storage of knowledge deemed too important or too dangerous for the lowland cities; secondary ley-line node
**Location:** High in the Spine of Fire, the volcanic mountain range running along the inner curve of the main continent
**Era Built:** ~9,500 BW (one of the oldest Akovarian constructions outside Amaunet)
**Era Destroyed:** Partially damaged 6,830 BW; never fully destroyed
**Current Claimant:** A coalition of silver and white dragons who guard the repository and its contents
**Current State:** Remarkably well preserved due to high-altitude location; deep chambers intact; dragon guardians permit limited scholarly access under strict conditions

#### Historical Background

Ptei was built early in the Akovarian civilization's development — approximately 9,500 BW, over a millennium before the ley-line network was energized — as a repository for knowledge and materials that the Akovarian clergy considered too valuable or too sensitive to store in the accessible lowland cities. The site was chosen for its remoteness: high in the Spine of Fire, accessible only through treacherous mountain passes that are snowbound for half the year and impassable during volcanic activity, the repository was designed to survive any catastrophe that might befall the coastal civilization below.

The original construction was modest — a small pyramid and a series of excavated chambers carved into the mountain's volcanic rock, connected to the surface by a single defended passage. Over the following millennia, the repository was expanded repeatedly as the Akovarian civilization produced more knowledge worth preserving. By the time of the ley-line detonation, Ptei's underground chambers extended through multiple levels and contained copies of the most critical texts from every major library on the continent, along with artifacts, experimental apparatus, and materials classified as dangerous — magical devices whose effects were not fully understood, substances of unknown origin recovered from planar portals, and records of experiments that had produced results the clergy wished to study further but not replicate in populated areas.

The repository's high-altitude location and deep excavation protected it from the worst effects of the ley-line detonation. The upper pyramid was damaged — its limestone casing cracked by the shockwave, its decorative elements scattered by the blast wind — but the mountain itself absorbed the energy, and the underground chambers survived essentially intact. The repository's contents, sealed in climate-controlled vaults designed for indefinite preservation, remained undisturbed.

#### The Dragon Guardians

In the centuries following the detonation, metallic dragons — who had served as allies and teachers of the Akovarians and as guardians of ley-line intersections during the golden age — gravitated toward the sites they had once protected. At Ptei, a coalition of silver and white dragons established a territorial claim that they have maintained for millennia. The silver dragons, who valued the Akovarian knowledge preserved within and possessed the intelligence and temperament to understand its importance, became the repository's de facto custodians. The white dragons, whose affinity for cold mountain environments made the Spine of Fire's highest elevations their natural habitat, served as the coalition's territorial defenders — less interested in the knowledge within than in the territory itself, but reliable guardians nonetheless.

The arrangement has evolved over the millennia into something approaching an institution. The senior silver dragon of each generation assumes responsibility for the repository's security and decides who, if anyone, is permitted access. These decisions are conservative by nature — dragons are protective of their possessions, and knowledge is the most valuable possession a silver dragon can claim. Outsiders who approach Ptei are evaluated individually: scholars with genuine purpose and respectful demeanor may be granted limited access under supervision; treasure-seekers, looters, and those who approach without adequate explanation are driven off; and those who attempt to take anything without permission are killed.

Since 815 AW, the dragon guardians' attitude toward outside contact has shifted — cautiously and incompletely. The ley-line reawakening has restored purpose to the guardians' vigil, confirming that the knowledge they protect is not merely historical but practically relevant to a restored network. Line-Watchers who have made the difficult journey to Ptei report that the senior silver dragon — an ancient female known as Ithilmere — has permitted them to study certain texts and artifacts under her direct supervision, on the condition that nothing is removed from the repository. This access has produced significant advances in ley-line research, as Ptei's collection includes engineering texts that supplement and in some cases exceed the documentation preserved at the Temple of the Seventh Convergence.

#### Current State

The surface pyramid is a stepped structure of approximately one hundred feet in height, its original white limestone casing cracked and weathered by altitude and volcanic exposure but structurally sound. The high-altitude environment — cold, dry, and above the treeline — has prevented the jungle encroachment that characterizes lowland ruins, leaving the pyramid exposed on the mountainside in a state of preservation that would be remarkable at any Akovarian site but is extraordinary given the structure's age. Snow covers the upper tiers for much of the year, and volcanic ash from periodic eruptions has deposited layers of mineral material on the surfaces, giving the pyramid a gray-white appearance that blends with the mountain.

The entrance passage descends steeply from the pyramid's base into the mountain, widening after the first hundred feet into a defended vestibule where the dragon guardians can intercept visitors. The passage is carved from volcanic basalt with the precision that characterizes all Akovarian construction, its walls bearing inscriptions that identify the repository and warn against unauthorized entry in Akovarian script, Draconic, and a third script that scholars have tentatively identified as an early form of Celestial — suggesting that the Akovarians communicated with entities beyond Asti.

The underground levels extend through at least four documented depths. The upper levels contain administrative spaces, residential quarters for the priests who staffed the repository, and reception chambers where visiting scholars were hosted. The middle levels house the primary collection — vaults of papyrus scrolls, clay tablets, and stone panels organized by subject and date, maintained in conditions that the repository's enchantments have kept stable for millennia. The lower levels contain the materials classified as sensitive or dangerous — sealed chambers whose wards remain active and whose contents are known only to the dragon guardians and, presumably, whatever inventory records survive within. The deepest documented level contains a small ley-line nexus chamber — Ptei sat at a secondary convergence point in the original network, and the nexus infrastructure here, while less sophisticated than the Temple of the Seventh Convergence's Flow Chamber, provides additional data on Akovarian engineering at different scales of the network.

Since 815 AW, the repository's enchantments have responded to the restored ley-line energy with increased activity. Climate-control systems that had been operating at minimal function are now maintaining conditions closer to their original parameters. Warding circles on vault doors glow more brightly. The small nexus chamber at the deepest level hums with energy that was not present before the reawakening. Ithilmere has expressed measured satisfaction at these developments, interpreting them as confirmation that the guardians' millennia of vigilance was not in vain.

#### Key Contents

The primary collection: engineering texts, religious records, historical chronicles, astronomical observations, and administrative archives spanning the Akovarian civilization's history from approximately 9,500 BW to 6,830 BW — one of the most comprehensive repositories of Akovarian knowledge in existence. Sensitive materials in sealed lower vaults: contents unknown to outsiders; the dragon guardians have not disclosed the nature of these materials and have shown no inclination to do so. The secondary ley-line nexus chamber: infrastructure providing data on network engineering at the secondary node scale. Artifacts and experimental apparatus: materials that the Akovarian clergy classified as requiring isolation from populated areas.

#### Hazards

Dragon guardians — silver and white dragons of significant power whose cooperation is required for any access and whose territorial instincts can override their scholarly temperament. Mountain environment — altitude, cold, volcanic activity, and treacherous terrain that makes approach difficult even in favorable conditions. Active wards on sensitive vaults whose triggering conditions and effects are unknown. Ley-line energy in the nexus chamber producing effects that intensify as the reawakening continues. The repository's isolation makes rescue or reinforcement impractical if an expedition encounters difficulties.

## THE COASTAL AND LOWLAND RUINS

### Iseueri — The Swamp Archive

**Akovarian Name:** Iseueri-Ka-Medu (House of the Written Word)
**Common Name:** Iseueri / The Swamp Archive / The Drowned Library
**Type:** Akovarian Archive and Research Complex
**Original Function:** Centralized archive and research facility; secondary ley-line node
**Location:** Southeastern lowlands of the main continent, where jungle gives way to coastal marshland
**Era Built:** ~8,000 BW
**Era Destroyed:** ~6,830 BW (ley-line detonation); subsequently submerged by geological subsidence and rising water levels
**Current Claimant:** Contested territory — black and bronze dragons maintain an uneasy arrangement over the ruins
**Current State:** Half-submerged in warm, stagnant water; upper structures collapsed or sinking; lower archive vaults potentially intact beneath the waterline

#### Historical Background

Iseueri was built during the period of the Akovarian civilization's greatest expansion, around 8,000 BW, to serve as a centralized archive for the southern network — a facility where copies of important documents from across the archipelago were collected, cataloged, and preserved. The site was chosen for its proximity to the southern ley-line channels and for the alluvial clay deposits in the surrounding marshland, which provided raw material for the clay tablets that the Akovarians used alongside papyrus for their most important records. Clay, properly fired and stored, is effectively eternal — and the Akovarian Archivists who designed Iseueri understood that knowledge preserved on clay would outlast anything written on organic material.

The complex consisted of a central pyramid of moderate size — approximately eighty feet tall — surrounded by a network of archive buildings, workshops, and residential structures built on raised platforms to accommodate the marshy terrain. The archive buildings were designed with their most critical storage spaces below ground, excavated into the clay substrate and sealed against water infiltration with enchanted stone lining — a necessary precaution in a location where the water table was never far below the surface.

#### Current State

Iseueri's post-detonation history has been shaped by geology as much as by the catastrophe itself. The ley-line detonation damaged the complex's upper structures, but the buildings' ruin was completed by a more mundane process: the southeastern lowlands have been subsiding for millennia, sinking relative to sea level at a rate that has progressively submerged the ruins. Today, the site occupies a region of coastal marsh where warm, stagnant water covers the terrain to a depth of three to eight feet, with the ruins rising from the water as a jumble of broken stone, submerged walls, and the central pyramid's upper courses — a flat-topped mound approximately twenty feet above the waterline, its limestone green with algae and colonized by nesting birds.

The archive buildings are entirely submerged. Their raised platforms, which once kept them above the marshy ground, now lie beneath several feet of turbid water that reduces visibility to arm's length. The upper stories have collapsed, creating a debris field of stone blocks, wooden beams (mostly rotted), and the scattered remnants of the archivists' equipment. But the underground vaults — the sealed, enchanted chambers where the most important records were stored — may remain intact beneath the waterline. The enchanted stone lining was designed to resist water infiltration, and if the seals held, the vaults could contain clay tablets preserved in the anaerobic conditions of a sealed underwater chamber — conditions that, ironically, are ideal for the preservation of fired clay.

Accessing the vaults is an extraordinary challenge. The murky water, the structural instability of the collapsed buildings above, and the hostile wildlife of the coastal marsh — including several species of aggressive aquatic predators that have made the ruins their habitat — make conventional diving approaches dangerous. Magical approaches are complicated by the site's status as a contested dragon territory: a clan of black dragons claims the marsh as hunting ground, while a smaller group of bronze dragons — who value the archive's historical significance — occupies the central pyramid mound and discourages both looters and the black dragons from disturbing the submerged vaults. This arrangement is tense and periodically violent, with territorial skirmishes between the dragon groups occurring every few years.

Artifacts recovered from the accessible portions of the ruins — the debris field above the vault level — include corroded bronze implements, fragments of coral-encrusted statuary, and clay tablet shards bearing text in Akovarian script. These fragments, recovered by dragonborn divers from the Eastern Shore Confederation who navigate the marsh with local knowledge that outsiders lack, have been traded to scholars at New Sabonaa and have contributed to the ongoing effort to reconstruct the Akovarian archive catalog. The shards suggest that Iseueri held copies of engineering documentation, religious texts, and administrative records from across the network — consistent with its function as a centralized archive.

Since 815 AW, the bronze dragons at Iseueri have reported that the submerged ruins occasionally produce a faint golden luminescence visible through the water at night — the same ley-line activation phenomenon observed at other Akovarian pyramid sites. The luminescence emanates from below the debris field, consistent with the sealed vaults responding to the restored ley-line energy. Whether this activation is affecting the vault contents — and whether the enchanted seals are holding — is unknown. The Line-Watchers have identified Iseueri as a high-priority site for investigation, but the logistical challenges of underwater archaeology in a dragon-contested marsh have prevented any organized expedition.

#### Key Contents (Probable)

Sealed archive vaults containing clay tablets and potentially papyrus documents from across the Akovarian network — copies of engineering specifications, religious texts, historical chronicles, and administrative records. The centralized nature of the archive means that Iseueri may contain copies of documents from sites whose originals were destroyed in the detonation, making its collection potentially irreplaceable. Bronze implements, religious artifacts, and the Archivists' working equipment in the debris field above the vaults.

#### Hazards

Contested dragon territory — black dragons (hostile) and bronze dragons (conditionally cooperative). Submerged environment with poor visibility, strong currents, and structural instability. Aggressive aquatic wildlife adapted to the wild-magic-saturated marsh. Tropical disease vectors concentrated in the stagnant water. The sealed vault enchantments may respond unpredictably to forced entry — particularly if the ley-line reawakening has restored power to their defensive functions.

### Mibampes — The Planar Breach

**Akovarian Name:** Mibampes-Ka-Akh (House of the Spirits Between)
**Common Name:** Mibampes / The Planar Breach / The Screaming Ruin
**Type:** Akovarian Ruins in Active Planar Instability Zone
**Original Function:** Unknown — possibly a research facility or a standard settlement; its current condition is the result of catastrophic planar damage
**Location:** Interior highlands of the main continent, in a region of intense wild magic
**Era Built:** Unknown (pre-detonation Akovarian construction)
**Era Destroyed:** ~6,830 BW (ley-line detonation)
**Current State:** Surrounded by the most intense area of planar instability on the continent; reality actively unstable; inhabited by aberrations and extraplanar creatures; uninhabitable for most mortal beings

#### Description

Mibampes is less a ruin and more a wound — a location where the ley-line detonation tore reality so severely that the damage has never healed and may be unhealable. The settlement that once occupied this highland site was presumably similar to other secondary Akovarian communities — a small pyramid, supporting structures, agricultural terraces — but whatever it was before the detonation has been thoroughly obliterated by the planar instability that has characterized the site for over seven millennia.

The ruins occupy the center of the most intense wild magic zone on the continent — an area approximately three miles in diameter where the boundary between the material plane and multiple extraplanar dimensions is so thin that it barely functions as a boundary at all. Portals open and close without warning, depositing creatures and materials from the Elemental Planes, the Abyss, the Feywild, and stranger dimensions into the ruins and surrounding terrain. The portals are not stable — they appear as shimmering tears in the air, persist for seconds to hours, and vanish without pattern or prediction. A visitor standing in one spot might experience the blast of heat from a portal to the Plane of Fire, followed seconds later by a gust of icy wind from the Plane of Air, followed by a moment of silence during which the air smells of sulfur and the shadows move in wrong directions.

Within the zone, magic is profoundly unreliable. Spells produce random effects — a healing spell might produce fire, a light cantrip might open a momentary portal, a protective ward might attract rather than repel the creatures it was designed to guard against. Magical items activate spontaneously, their functions warping in response to the chaotic mana field. Creatures with innate magical abilities — which includes all spellcasters, all dragons, and many of the aberrations that inhabit the zone — find their powers amplified, diminished, or redirected unpredictably.

The aberrations that inhabit Mibampes are the zone's most dangerous feature. The constant portal activity has deposited creatures from the Far Realm, the Abyss, and other hostile planes into the ruins over the course of millennia, and some of these creatures have established permanent or semi-permanent residence. The wild magic environment suits aberrations — creatures whose biology and cognition operate by rules incompatible with the material plane find the chaotic conditions at Mibampes more comfortable than the ordered reality elsewhere on the continent. The zone's population includes beholders, minor aberrations of types that defy classification, and the periodic appearance of more powerful extraplanar entities whose visits are fortunately brief.

The Akovarian ruins themselves are barely recognizable. Stone walls have been warped, melted, rebuilt, and warped again by millennia of wild magic exposure. Geometric foundations that once followed precise mathematical relationships have been distorted into curves, spirals, and angles that should not exist in three-dimensional space. The remains of the pyramid — if a pyramid ever stood here — are a mound of partially fused stone that shifts slightly in shape depending on the viewer's position, as though the structure exists in more spatial dimensions than the eye can process.

Dragonborn avoid Mibampes absolutely. Their oral traditions include a category of place called "the hollows" — locations where reality is broken and the world's rules do not apply — and Mibampes is the archetype of the category. The Eastern Shore Confederation's maps mark the site with a symbol that translates as "no path" — not merely a warning but a statement that no route through the area can be relied upon, because the terrain itself changes.

Since 815 AW, the planar instability at Mibampes has not improved — if anything, the ley-line reawakening has complicated the situation by introducing ordered magical energy into a chaotic environment, creating interactions that produce novel and unpredictable effects. Line-Watchers who have observed the zone from a safe distance report that the boundary of the instability area has not expanded, which they interpret as encouraging, but that the intensity within the boundary has increased. The Astral Circle has classified Mibampes as a Priority One observation site — not because anyone intends to enter it, but because the zone represents the worst-case scenario for what can happen when ley-line energy interacts with catastrophically damaged reality, and understanding that interaction is essential for preventing similar damage elsewhere.

#### Key Contents

Unknown. Whatever the original settlement contained has been transformed beyond recognition by seven millennia of planar instability. The site's value is primarily as a cautionary example and a research subject — a living demonstration of the destructive potential of uncontrolled ley-line energy.

#### Hazards

Total planar instability — reality cannot be relied upon within the zone. Wild magic of extreme intensity affecting all spellcasting, magical items, and innate magical abilities. Constant portal activity depositing hostile extraplanar creatures. Aberrations of various types inhabiting the ruins. Spatial distortion making navigation unreliable. The possibility that the zone's effects are worsening as ley-line energy interacts with the existing instability.

## MINOR AND SCATTERED RUINS

### Subsidiary Pyramid Sites

Beyond the major temple complexes documented above, the Akovarian civilization built pyramid structures at dozens of locations across the archipelago — smaller installations that managed tertiary ley-line nodes, served as regional administrative centers, or provided agricultural and logistical support for the larger cities. The ley-line detonation destroyed many of these sites completely, particularly those closest to the major channels where the energy discharge was most violent. But others survived in varying states of ruin, scattered across the islands in locations that range from easily accessible coastal sites to nearly impenetrable interior jungle.

These subsidiary sites share common characteristics. Each features a small pyramid — typically between twenty and fifty feet tall — with associated buildings and underground chambers scaled to the installation's purpose. The pyramids' geometric construction follows the same principles as the larger temples, with the number of tiers corresponding to the number of ley-line connections at the site. Most subsidiary pyramids connected to only one or two lines and are correspondingly simple in design — two-or three-tiered structures whose engineering was competent but not remarkable by Akovarian standards.

The subsidiary sites' value lies not in individual significance but in aggregate. Each site provides additional data points for reconstructing the ley-line network's geometry, and the engineering documentation preserved in their underground chambers — while less comprehensive than the major temples' archives — often includes local observations and maintenance records that document how the network behaved in specific geographic conditions. Line-Watchers mapping the restored network across Akovar have identified subsidiary sites as priority survey targets, as their distribution across the archipelago provides the spatial data necessary to understand the network's continental-scale geometry.

Since 815 AW, subsidiary pyramids across the continent have exhibited the faint golden glow characteristic of ley-line activation in Akovarian structures. The glow is weakest at sites farthest from the restored major channels and strongest at sites nearest the reactivated convergence points, consistent with a network gradually propagating energy outward from its restored core. Some subsidiary sites in the most remote locations — particularly on the smaller outer islands — have shown no activation at all, suggesting that the network's restoration has not yet reached the full extent of its original range.

### Dragonborn Heritage Sites

Over the millennia of their habitation, the dragonborn have developed their own relationship with the Akovarian ruins — one that predates outside contact and operates according to cultural principles that scholars from Nigal are still learning to understand. Many dragonborn clans have adopted specific Akovarian ruins as heritage sites — locations tied to clan identity, used for ceremonies, and claimed as ancestral territory regardless of their original Akovarian function.

Heritage sites are not archaeological preserves. The dragonborn have modified, repurposed, and in some cases dismantled Akovarian structures to serve their own needs — using carved stone blocks as building material, converting underground chambers into storage or shelter, and incorporating Akovarian architectural elements into their own construction. This repurposing has preserved some features while destroying others, and the relationship between dragonborn heritage claims and scholarly access is a source of ongoing tension. Clans that welcome outside researchers permit documentation of their heritage sites under supervision. Clans that regard their sites as sacred or proprietary do not, and no amount of diplomatic or financial inducement has changed this — a fact that some outside scholars have difficulty accepting and that dragonborn leaders regard as non-negotiable.

The heritage sites' significance for ley-line research lies in the dragonborn's oral traditions, which in some cases preserve information about the sites' original functions that the physical remains alone would not reveal. Clan genealogies that trace lineage to specific dragons often include references to the dragons' roles during the Akovarian golden age — guardian of a ley-line intersection, teacher at a specific temple, ally of a particular priestly order — and these references, when cross-referenced with archaeological evidence and surviving Akovarian texts, have helped researchers identify the original purposes of ruins whose documentation was lost in the detonation.

### Volcanic Ruins of the Spine of Fire

The Spine of Fire — the volcanic mountain range running along the inner curve of the crescent-shaped main island — has created and destroyed Akovarian sites through the natural process of volcanic activity over seven millennia. Eruptions have buried some ruins under lava flows and volcanic ash, preserving them in conditions similar to the famous preserved cities of our own world — structures sealed in an anaerobic environment where organic materials can survive indefinitely. At the same time, volcanic activity has exposed previously buried ruins through erosion, landslides, and the geological upheaval that accompanies major eruptions.

The volcanic ruins are among the most difficult sites on the continent to access. They are located at altitude, in terrain that is unstable, toxic (volcanic gases), and claimed by white and silver dragon clans whose territories overlap with the Spine of Fire's most significant geological features. The few volcanic ruins that have been documented — primarily by Ptei's dragon guardians, who share limited information with trusted outsiders — include structures preserved under lava flows in conditions of extraordinary completeness, with organic materials, textiles, and even food remains surviving in a state that the coastal ruins, with their tropical weathering, could never match.

The volcanic environment also creates unique magical conditions. The interaction between geothermal energy, volcanic minerals, and the wild magic that pervades the continent produces effects that are distinct from those found elsewhere on Akovar — localized areas where fire magic is enhanced, where earth-based spells produce unexpected results, and where the boundary between the material plane and the Plane of Fire is thin enough to produce spontaneous thermal phenomena. These conditions make volcanic ruins both more dangerous and more interesting than their lowland counterparts, and the artifacts recovered from volcanic sites — items that have been exposed to geothermal-magical interactions for millennia — sometimes exhibit properties that no scholar can explain.

## THE SHATTERED COAST

The southern portion of the main continent — the region where the ley-line detonation did the most severe physical damage to the landscape — is documented in the Akovar geography file as an area of extreme danger and extraordinary magical richness. In the context of ruins, the Shattered Coast represents the most complete category of destruction the detonation produced: areas where the physical landscape was so thoroughly reshaped that the distinction between "ruin" and "terrain" has ceased to be meaningful.

The Shattered Coast's ruins are embedded in the landscape itself. Portions of the original coastline collapsed into the sea during the detonation, creating a maze of inlets, sea caves, and rocky islets that extends for miles offshore. Within this maze, fragments of Akovarian construction — walls, foundations, carved blocks, sections of pyramid facing — are visible in cliff faces, embedded in sea cave walls, and scattered across the ocean floor. These fragments are the remains of coastal settlements that were physically torn apart by the detonation and redistributed across the restructured terrain.

The Shattered Coast has the highest concentration of wild magic zones and active portals on the continent, making it simultaneously the most dangerous and the most magically rich area of Akovar. Black and green dragon clans claim most of the coast, defending their territories with the aggressive territorial instinct characteristic of chromatic dragons. The combination of dragon hostility, wild magic, active portals, treacherous maritime conditions, and the absence of any safe haven makes the Shattered Coast the least explored region of the continent and the one most likely to contain undiscovered Akovarian ruins of significance.

Dragonborn of the Shattered Coast Tribes — chromatic-heritage clans who are openly hostile to outsiders — possess oral traditions describing underwater ruins of considerable size in the waters off the coast. These traditions include references to "the drowned temples" — submerged pyramid structures visible in the shallows during extreme low tides — and "the glass gardens" — formations of crystallized mana similar to those found in impact craters on Nigal, produced by the ley-line detonation's interaction with coastal sand and seawater. The Shattered Coast Tribes do not share these traditions willingly, and the few accounts that have reached outside scholars come from dragonborn traders who heard them secondhand and may not have the details correct.

## NOTE ON THE NEPHERITES PYRAMID

While geographically located in the Ancient South of the continent of Nigal — thousands of miles from the Akovarian archipelago — the Nepherites Pyramid is architecturally and culturally Akovarian. Built by the Varkonan refugees who were transported from Akovar during the 6,830 BW evacuation, the pyramid at Nepherites represents the continuation of Akovarian architectural and engineering traditions on a foreign continent by a people who had lost most of their memories of the civilization they came from.

The Nepherites Pyramid housed the Mask of the Pharaoh — the artifact containing Asti's imprisoned consciousness — for approximately 7,645 years, from the evacuation in 6,830 BW until the 815 AW heroes recovered it and returned it to Amaunet for the release ritual. The pyramid's construction followed Akovarian geometric principles that the refugees retained even as they lost the understanding of why those principles mattered, building a structure that unknowingly channeled ley-line energy according to patterns established by a civilization the builders no longer remembered.

The Nepherites Pyramid is documented in the Nigal ruins file as a Varkonan cultural site, but its Akovarian heritage makes it a critical reference point for understanding the continuity of Akovarian engineering knowledge across cultures and continents. The geometric relationships embedded in its construction — relationships that the Varkonan builders may have preserved through religious tradition rather than technical understanding — provide data that supplements the engineering documentation recovered from Akovarian sites on the continent itself. The Azala temple-pyramids of the Mespoian Peninsula, built by Varkonan migrants who traveled northeast from the Ancient South around 2,200 BW, continue this chain of architectural inheritance — a tradition of pyramid-building that stretches unbroken from the Akovarian golden age of 8,200 BW through the Varkonan settlement of 6,830 BW to the Azala Classical Period of 1,900 BW. This eleven-thousand-year continuity of geometric architectural tradition is unique in Mazariim's history.

## ORGANIZATIONS ACTIVE IN AKOVAR'S RUINS

### Line-Watchers of Akovar

The Line-Watchers of Asti have established a growing presence on the continent since 815 AW, with approximately forty clergy and affiliated researchers operating from camps at major ley-line convergence points and a permanent way-station at the Asti Temple in New Sabonaa. Their mission on Akovar is twofold: to study and map the restored ley-line network that the Akovarians built alongside Asti eight thousand years ago, and to stabilize the network's operation as it returns to function after millennia of dormancy. The Line-Watchers' work on Akovar provides the theoretical foundation for ley-line research across all of Mazariim — the Akovarian network is the original model from which every subsequent mortal-made ley-line draws its principles.

The Line-Watchers coordinate their efforts with the Astral Circle's research station at New Sabonaa and maintain cautious relationships with the metallic dragon clans who guard some of the most significant convergence points. Access to Ptei, facilitated by the silver dragon Ithilmere, has been the Line-Watchers' most significant scholarly achievement on the continent, providing primary source engineering documentation that has advanced understanding of the network's design by decades.

### The Astral Circle Research Station

The Astral Circle maintains a permanent research station at New Sabonaa staffed by a rotating team of six to ten scholars. Their focus on Akovar includes the study of planar instability, wild and dead magic zones, and the ancient Akovarian magical traditions preserved in the ruins. The station serves as a clearinghouse for information gathered by independent expeditions and as a coordination point for the Circle's own research missions to the main continent. The station's head researcher files regular reports with the Circle's leadership, though the distance and difficulty of communication mean that the Akovar team operates with considerable autonomy.

The Astral Circle's particular interest in Akovar's ruins centers on the relationship between ley-line energy and planar stability. The detonation of 6,830 BW created the continent's wild magic zones, dead magic zones, and erratic portals, and the ley-line reawakening is altering these phenomena in ways that the Circle monitors closely. Mibampes, as the most extreme example of planar instability, is a Priority One observation site for the Circle's instruments at New Sabonaa.

### Dragon Preservationists

A loose coalition of metallic dragons — primarily gold and bronze — who view the ley-line reawakening as an opportunity to restore the cooperative relationship between dragons and mortal races that existed during the Akovarian golden age. These dragons actively assist Line-Watchers and scholars, share knowledge of Akovarian history preserved in their own memories and hoards, and work to prevent chromatic dragon clans from seizing or destroying ley-line convergence points. The preservationists are not formally organized — dragons are too individualistic for institutional structure — but they communicate and coordinate through ancient draconic networks of alliance and obligation.

The preservationists' most significant contribution to ruin research is access. Metallic dragon territories contain some of the most important Akovarian sites on the continent, and the preservationists' willingness to permit scholarly investigation — under conditions they define — has opened sites that would otherwise be inaccessible. The arrangement at Ptei, where the silver dragon Ithilmere permits supervised study of the repository's collection, is the model that Line-Watchers hope to replicate at other dragon-guarded sites.

### The Red Wardens of Amaunet

Kharazzax's red dragon clan controls the most important Akovarian ruin on the continent and has no intention of sharing it. The Red Wardens' hostility to outsiders — intensified by the humiliation of the 815 AW heroes' assault — makes the ruins of Amaunet effectively inaccessible for scholarly investigation. The clan's control of the Grand Temple convergence point, the middle catacomb infrastructure, and the surface ruins of the Sacred Precinct means that the single most important concentration of Akovarian architectural, engineering, and religious material on the continent is behind a wall of draconic fire that no research expedition can breach.

The Wardens' territory represents the most significant obstacle to comprehensive study of Akovar's ruins. Without access to Amaunet — the hub of the original network, the site of the Grand Temple, and the location of the ley-line management infrastructure that controlled the entire continental system — any reconstruction of the Akovarian network must rely on peripheral data from secondary sites. The Line-Watchers and the Astral Circle have explored diplomatic options — including the possibility that metallic dragon intermediaries might negotiate limited access — but Kharazzax's fury at the 815 AW intrusion has not diminished, and no approach has been attempted.

## HAZARDS COMMON TO ALL AKOVARIAN RUINS

The following hazards are present at most or all Akovarian sites on the continent and should be assumed by any expedition:

Wild magic is pervasive across the continent, its intensity varying by location but present everywhere outside the dead magic zones. Spells may produce unintended effects. Magical items may activate spontaneously or fail to function. Creatures with innate magical abilities are affected unpredictably. Wild magic severity is greatest at the detonation epicenters (Amaunet, the Shattered Coast, Mibampes) and diminishes with distance, but no location on the continent is entirely free of it.

Dead magic zones — areas where mana has been completely drained — are less common but more dangerous in practice. A spellcaster who enters a dead magic zone loses all magical protection and capability instantly. A dragon that flies over one may lose flight. Dead magic zones are concentrated in the interior highlands and along the western coast but can occur unpredictably elsewhere, particularly at the edges of restored ley-line channels where the flow's boundary creates a sharp transition between enhanced and absent mana.

Dragon territories cover virtually the entire continent. With an estimated one thousand to two thousand dragons inhabiting the archipelago, no significant location on Akovar is unclaimed. Metallic dragon claimants may permit access to scholars under conditions they define. Chromatic dragon claimants will not. Entering a dragon's territory without permission is an act of trespass that the claimant is likely to punish with lethal force.

Tropical environment hazards — heat, humidity, venomous wildlife, disease, and the logistical challenges of operating in dense jungle — affect all lowland sites. The volcanic highlands present different but equally serious environmental challenges: altitude, cold, toxic volcanic gases, and unstable terrain.

The ley-line reawakening has made all Akovarian ruins more magically active than they have been at any point since the detonation. Enchantments are reactivating. Wards are powering up. Infrastructure is responding to energy flows it was designed to channel but has not received for over seven millennia. This reactivation is unpredictable — some systems are returning to normal function, others are malfunctioning, and the distinction between the two is not always apparent until someone triggers a response. Any expedition entering an Akovarian ruin should assume that the site's magical defenses may be partially operational and that their behavior cannot be reliably predicted.
