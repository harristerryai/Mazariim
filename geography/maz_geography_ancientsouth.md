# Geography of the Ancient South: The Varkonan Lands

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

Far from the majority of the civilized world, separated by barren deserts and rocky steppes, the Ancient South holds some of the oldest city-states and nations of humankind. Large pyramids that can be seen for miles have lasted thousands of years, and the peoples of this region rely on rivers and prayers to see them through year to year. This is a land of extremes — scorching days, freezing nights, fertile river valleys surrounded by merciless wasteland — where civilization clings to the narrow ribbon of the River of Life and the scattered oases that dot the interior.

The humans originating in this part of the world are known as the Varkonan, the term for "god-king" in their ancient language. Originally made of pictograms, the language of Varkonae eventually transitioned into its own alphabet, which greatly expanded the accessibility of the written form to its people. Common is spoken only in trade districts and by those who deal regularly with foreign merchants; most Varkonans conduct daily life entirely in Varkonae.

The Ancient South exists at a technology level between the Bronze Age and the Middle Ages, with remarkable exceptions. While most inhabitants rely on pre-medieval technology for daily life — bronze tools, animal-drawn plows, simple irrigation — the Varkonans have achieved extraordinary advancement in engineering, sciences, cosmology, and magic. Their pyramids are feats of construction that rival anything built by dwarves or gnomes. Their astronomical observations are the most precise in the world. Their understanding of mathematics, geometry, and magical theory dates back to traditions inherited — though they do not fully remember this — from the lost continent of Akovar, the most advanced civilization in Mazariim's history.

The Ancient South is dominated by two major nations: the Pharaonic Empire of Nepherites, the older and more powerful of the two, and the Geniecity of Uadjit to the south, known for its decadence and mastery of genie-binding magic. Between, around, and beyond these two powers lie scattered oasis settlements, desert nomad tribes, ancient ruins from civilizations even older than Nepherites, and vast stretches of uninhabitable wasteland where only the hardiest — or most desperate — dare to travel.

The region is defined by the River of Life, a massive waterway that flows from highland lakes in the east down through a broad valley before emptying into the southern sea. Nearly all permanent settlement in the Ancient South exists within a few miles of this river or its tributaries. The Varkonan Canal, constructed around 2,100 BW using slave labor and powerful magic, extends the river's reach westward into the arid interior, doubling food production for the region and creating a secondary band of habitable land. Beyond the river valleys, the landscape is unforgiving — the Great Desert stretches northward toward the Horselands, rocky steppes rise to the east toward the Dragon Spine mountains, and arid scrubland extends south toward the coast.

Unlike the Inner Kingdoms with their Federation or the Merchant Nations with their trade networks, the Ancient South has no unifying political structure. Nepherites and Uadjit maintain an uneasy coexistence defined by the river — Nepherites controls the northern and central portions of the River of Life valley, while Uadjit claims the southern reaches and the coastal delta. Between them, independent oasis settlements and nomadic tribes owe allegiance to whichever power can project force to their location at any given time. Desert raiders, ancient curses, and the ever-present threat of the wasteland itself keep both nations focused more on survival than expansion.

The Ancient South's isolation from the rest of Nigal is both geographic and cultural. The Great Desert to the north separates it from the Horselands and, beyond them, the Merchant Nations and Inner Kingdoms. The Dragon Spine mountains to the east form a near-impenetrable barrier. Sea routes along the southern coast provide the primary connection to the wider world, but these are long, dangerous, and expensive. As a result, the Varkonans have developed largely in isolation, preserving traditions and knowledge stretching back over six thousand years while remaining largely unknown to the bustling civilizations of the north and west.

**Current Political Organization (842 AW)**

The Ancient South consists of two major nations and numerous independent settlements with no overarching government:

- **Nepherites** — Ancient Pharaonic Empire. The oldest continuous human civilization, ruled by a divine Pharaoh who claims descent from the god-kings of legend. Center of Ra (Pyrantor) worship and pyramid construction.
- **Uadjit** — Decadent Geniecity. A southern city-state known for its genie-binding traditions, exotic luxuries, and moral permissiveness. Powerful but decadent.

Numerous oasis settlements, nomadic desert tribes, independent villages along the River of Life, and the ruins of forgotten civilizations exist outside the direct control of either major power.

**Total Population (842 AW, estimated)**

- Humans (Varkonan): ~350,000 across all settlements
- Humans (other — Sabonaar traders, Chak'ti nomads, Laveri visitors): ~15,000
- Dwarves: ~5,000 (mostly Bryndawi traders and smiths)
- Elves: ~1,000 (mostly scholars and diplomats)
- Gnomes: ~2,000
- Other (genasi, half-elementals, exotic races drawn by genie magic): ~8,000
- **Total: ~381,000**

## REGIONAL HISTORY

The history of the Ancient South is among the oldest of any human civilization on Mazariim — and its deepest roots stretch back far further than even the Varkonans themselves remember.

### The Akovarian Legacy (~6,830 BW)

The Varkonan people do not know their own origin. Their oldest traditions speak of a "First Dawn" when the ancestors emerged from a great light and found themselves in a strange land, confused and suffering. Scholars from the Inner Kingdoms and the Astral Circle have pieced together the truth: the Varkonans are the descendants of the Akovar civilization, the most advanced human society in Mazariim's history, which flourished on the distant continent of Akovar from approximately 13,000 to 6,830 BW.

When chromatic dragons attacked the Akovarian homeland in 6,830 BW, the priests and arcanists of the entity Asti enacted a desperate ritual. They transferred Asti's consciousness into the Mask of the Pharaoh, a legendary artifact, and simultaneously teleported the majority of the population to a distant land. The ley-lines that powered Akovarian civilization exploded, destroying the cities and killing most of the attacking dragons.

The transported Akovarians arrived in what is now the Ancient South — but the ritual had catastrophic side effects. The high priest Thz'zar Ja'laao went mad from channeling the Mask's power. Worse, the population suffered near-total memory loss, their minds wiped by the magical trauma. Few survived the first year. Those who did found themselves in a harsh desert landscape with no memory of their advanced civilization, no understanding of their sophisticated magical traditions, and only fragments of instinct and knowledge to guide them.

What they retained, unconsciously, was remarkable. They remembered how to build pyramids. They remembered the importance of rivers and irrigation. They remembered that the sun was sacred and that geometry held divine significance. These fragmentary memories, passed down through generations as sacred traditions rather than conscious knowledge, would shape Varkonan civilization for the next six thousand years.

The Mask of the Pharaoh — containing the imprisoned consciousness of Asti — was preserved as the most sacred object in Varkonan culture. Generation after generation of priests guarded it without understanding what it truly was, knowing only that it held immense power and must never be lost. It was eventually sealed within the Nepherites Pyramid, where it remained hidden for nearly seven thousand years until its recovery in 815 AW.

### The Founding of Nepherites (~6,500 BW)

Approximately three hundred years after the Akovarian refugees arrived, the survivors had stabilized enough to establish their first permanent settlement. Nepherites was founded along the banks of the River of Life, where fertile soil and reliable water allowed agriculture to take hold. The city was built around the first great pyramid — a structure that the founders could not fully explain why they felt compelled to construct, but which they knew in their bones was essential.

The early Pharaohs of Nepherites were likely powerful priests or warriors who claimed divine authority. Over the centuries, the institution of the god-king solidified: the Pharaoh was not merely a ruler but a living deity, an intermediary between the mortal world and the cosmic forces that governed existence. This tradition of divine rulership — unique among human civilizations on Mazariim — may be an unconscious echo of the Akovarian priests who had served as intermediaries between Asti and the common people.

Nepherites grew slowly but steadily, expanding along the River of Life and establishing satellite settlements at major oases. Pyramid construction became central to Varkonan culture — each Pharaoh commissioned at least one during their reign, and the great pyramids of Nepherites became the largest structures built by human hands on Nigal.

### The Rise of Uadjit (~5,800 BW)

As Nepherites expanded, some Varkonans pushed southward along the River of Life toward the coastal delta. Around 5,800 BW, the city of Uadjit was established at the river's mouth, where the River of Life meets the southern sea. Uadjit developed a character distinct from Nepherites almost immediately — its position on the coast exposed it to maritime influences, and its distance from the Pharaonic court allowed greater independence.

Most significantly, the people of Uadjit discovered — or were discovered by — genies. The elemental spirits that inhabited the desert winds, the deep waters, and the burning sands had always been present, but the Uadjitians developed sophisticated techniques for contacting, bargaining with, and eventually binding these powerful beings. Genie-binding magic became Uadjit's defining tradition, granting the city access to elemental powers that supplemented and in some ways surpassed conventional magic.

Uadjit's relationship with Nepherites has oscillated between cooperation and rivalry for nearly six thousand years. The Pharaonic Empire claims sovereignty over all Varkonan lands, including Uadjit, but the Geniecity's independent power base — its genie-binders, its coastal trade, its exotic wealth — has always made this claim more theoretical than practical.

### Migration Waves Out of the Ancient South

The Varkonan homeland served as the origin point for two major human migration waves that would shape civilizations across Nigal.

Around 3,500 BW, Varkonan migrants began moving eastward along the southern coast of Nigal. Over centuries of travel and cultural adaptation, these migrants eventually reached the Yashaki Islands off the southeastern coast, where they founded the civilization that became the Ejiin people. The Ejiin abandoned Varkonan pharaonic traditions and developed an entirely new elemental religion (worshiping water, earth, air, and fire under names distinct from the western Old Gods), a shogunate governmental system, and the honor-based culture that defines the Lands of Honor today. By 3,200 BW, the Miyasaki Shogunate was formally established. The connection between Varkonans and Ejiin was forgotten by both cultures within a few generations of separation.

Around 2,200 BW, a second wave of Varkonan migrants moved northeast into the tropical jungles of what is now the Mespoian Peninsula. These migrants adapted their pyramid-building traditions to the rainforest environment, constructing temple-pyramids that blended Varkonan architectural heritage with jungle materials and designs. By 1,900 BW, the Azala kingdom was formally established. The Azala represent the most direct continuation of Varkonan pyramid-building traditions outside the Ancient South itself, creating an unbroken chain of pyramid architecture stretching from Akovar (13,000 BW) through Nepherites (6,500 BW) to Azala (1,900 BW) — over eleven thousand years of continuous architectural tradition.

A third, later migration around 1,500 BW saw Varkonan peoples moving north into the Lavantine Peninsula, where they mixed with the native Laveri peoples. These migrants fragmented into isolated tribal groups and eventually formed the Ubyra swamp tribes, losing their connection to Varkonan pyramid-building culture entirely.

### The Varkonan Canal (~2,100 BW)

One of the greatest engineering achievements in the Ancient South — and one of the most morally troubling — was the construction of the Varkonan Canal around 2,100 BW. Using slave labor and powerful magic, the Pharaonic government constructed a massive irrigation canal connecting the River of Life to the arid western deserts. The canal doubled food production for the region, transforming barren wasteland into arable farmland and creating a secondary band of habitable land extending westward from the river valley.

The human cost was enormous. Thousands of slaves — many of them prisoners of war, criminals, or debtors — died during the decades of construction. The canal's completion cemented both the Pharaonic Empire's power and the institution of slavery that persists in Varkonan society to the present day.

### Contact with the Wider World

For most of its history, the Ancient South developed in near-total isolation. The Great Desert, the Dragon Spine mountains, and the vast distances separating the region from other human civilizations meant that contact was rare and often accidental.

Around 2,500 BW, early trade contact was established between the Ancient South and the Sabonaar peoples of the Merchant Nations to the northwest. These connections remained sporadic for centuries. By 1,800 BW, Sabonaar merchant networks had established more regular — though still infrequent — trade routes connecting Varkonan ports to the wider world. Varkonan gold, gems, exotic spices, and magical artifacts flowed north; iron tools, textiles, and dwarven metalwork flowed south.

The Dukhan Empire's expansion (1,001 BW onward) in the Merchant Nations increased awareness of the Ancient South, though the Dukhan theocracy's Zebulba worship and the Varkonans' Ra (Pyrantor) worship created theological friction that limited formal relations.

During the War to End All Wars (4 BW – 1 AW), the Ancient South was affected primarily through disruption of trade routes and the arrival of orc raiding parties that penetrated the desert from the north and east. The region's isolation provided some protection — the orcs' main thrust was directed at the more densely populated Inner Kingdoms and Dwarven Nations — but Varkonan settlements along the northern frontier suffered raids, and the disruption of maritime trade caused economic hardship.

In the post-War era, the Ancient South has maintained its character as an isolated, ancient, and somewhat mysterious region. Trade with the Merchant Nations and (to a lesser extent) the Inner Kingdoms has increased, bringing new ideas and technologies, but the Varkonan people remain deeply traditional, their society shaped by six thousand years of continuous pharaonic rule and religious tradition.

### The Second Tiovinian Crusade (219 AW)

The most significant external military threat the Ancient South has faced in the post-War era came not from orcs but from fellow humans. In 219 AW, the Holy Theocracy of Tentigo launched its Second Tiovinian Crusade into the Ancient South, seeking to convert the "heathen sun-worshipers" to militant Tiovinism.

The crusade was a disaster for both sides. Tentigo's armies, accustomed to the temperate Sabon Peninsula, were devastated by desert heat, sandstorms, and supply shortages. Varkonan defenders, though technologically inferior in some respects, fought with the fanaticism of people defending their sacred homeland against religious invaders. The crusade penetrated as far as the northern oasis settlements before being turned back, leaving a legacy of mutual hatred that persists to this day.

### The Recovery of the Mask (815 AW)

In 815 AW, a group of adventurers who had explored the ancient ruins on the continent of Akovar pieced together the true history of the Varkonan people — their origin as Akovarian refugees, the imprisonment of the entity Asti within the Mask of the Pharaoh, and the location of the Mask within the Nepherites Pyramid. Journeying to the Ancient South, the heroes navigated Varkonan politics, consulted the Shi'ars (scholar-priests) of Uadjit, and ultimately delved into the Nepherites Pyramid to recover the Mask.

The recovery was controversial. Varkonan nationalists viewed the Mask as their sacred heritage and resisted its removal. The heroes ultimately transported the Mask back to Akovar, where they released Asti's consciousness at the ruined Grand Temple. The entity's awakening sent ripples of magical energy across Mazariim, reawakening ancient ley-lines and stirring long-dormant powers.

For the Varkonans, the recovery of the Mask was both a revelation and a crisis. For the first time, their true history was laid bare — they were not the divinely chosen people of Ra, sprung from the desert itself, but refugees from a distant land, their civilization built on fragmentary memories of a greater culture they had forgotten. This knowledge has been suppressed by the Pharaonic court, which views it as a threat to the divine legitimacy that underpins their rule. Among scholars and reformers, however, the truth circulates quietly, raising uncomfortable questions about the foundations of Varkonan society.

### The Gods of the Fall (839 AW)

In 839 AW, a cloud of mana that had spent tens of thousands of years coalescing in interstellar space separated into five distinct proto-entities and crashed into the Varkonan desert like meteors. The impact sites were scattered across the wasteland, each creating a massive crater and releasing enormous amounts of magical energy.

The five proto-entities — Zonth (order), Khemset (hunger), Menot (change), Nakar (destruction), and Anumata (harmony) — awakened in the desert, confused, powerful, and dangerous. Khemset, the entity of hunger and acquisition, discovered knowledge of the ancient Machine of Gyru — a device that, if reassembled, could drain mana from other entities and transfer it to the operator. Khemset believed the Machine was the key to achieving true godhood and began searching for its scattered parts across Mazariim.

The village of Osymunhotep, a small Varkonan settlement near one of the impact sites, became the epicenter of the crisis. Heroes who happened to be present during the crash began a globe-spanning race to collect the Machine's five parts — scattered in the Frozen Isles, the Mespoian Peninsula, the Merchant Nations, and the Dwarven Nations — before Khemset could assemble them.

The crisis also drew attention to the Yak-Men, brutal raiders and slavers who control portions of the Varkonan desert. The Yak-Men, drawn by the magical energy of the crash sites, allied with the proto-entity Nakar and attempted to seize machine parts for themselves.

The campaign's resolution prevented Khemset's ascension, and the five proto-entities have since begun integrating into Mazariim's existing cosmological landscape — their ultimate roles in the divine hierarchy still taking shape.

### The Modern Ancient South (842 AW)

As of 842 AW, the Ancient South is a region defined by the tension between its ancient traditions and the changes being forced upon it by the wider world. The recovery of the Mask of the Pharaoh and the revelation of the Varkonan people's true origins have created an undercurrent of intellectual ferment that the Pharaonic court struggles to contain. The crash of the five proto-entities has drawn unprecedented outside attention to a region that preferred its isolation. Trade with the Merchant Nations and the Federation of Man continues to increase, bringing new ideas, technologies, and — some traditionalists fear — cultural contamination.

The ley-line reawakening triggered by Asti's liberation in 815 AW has had subtle but growing effects on the Ancient South. Ancient pyramid temples that had been inert for millennia have begun to pulse with faint magical energy. Line-Watchers — clergy of Asti — have arrived from other regions, seeking to study and restore ley-line nodes that the Varkonans had maintained for thousands of years without understanding their true purpose. The Ra-Priests of Nepherites view these newcomers with suspicion, seeing them as competitors for spiritual authority over the sacred pyramids.

The desert itself remains as dangerous as ever. Yak-Men raiders threaten trade routes. Ancient tombs and ruins hold treasures and terrors in equal measure. The five proto-entities, still finding their place in the world, occasionally cause disruptions as their power interacts with the ancient magical infrastructure of the region. And beneath it all, the River of Life flows as it has for millennia — the source of everything the Varkonans have built and the only thing standing between their civilization and the merciless desert that surrounds it.

## NEPHERITES

**"The sun rises eternal. The Pharaoh endures."**
— Inscription above the gates of the Great Pyramid

### Overview

**Alignment:** LN (with evil tendencies among the priestly elite)
**Government:** Pharaonic Theocracy
**Capital:** Ra'khet (pop. ~45,000)
**Population:** ~280,000 (Varkonan humans, with small populations of Bryndawi dwarves, gnomes, and foreign traders)
**Languages:** Varkonae, Common (trade districts only)
**Religions:** Pyrantor (as Ra — dominant state religion), Iquinias (River of Life), Zebulba (forbidden but present in tomb-keeper traditions), Asti (re-emerging since 815 AW)
**Primary Exports:** Gold, gems, exotic spices, papyrus, magical artifacts, astronomical instruments, preserved foods
**Real-World Analog:** Ancient Egypt, pharaonic period

The Pharaonic Empire of Nepherites is the oldest continuous human civilization on Mazariim, founded approximately 6,500 BW by refugees from the lost continent of Akovar — though the Varkonan people themselves did not know this until 815 AW, and the Pharaonic court has actively suppressed this knowledge since its revelation. For over six thousand years, Nepherites has endured along the banks of the River of Life, governed by a succession of divine Pharaohs who claim to be living gods, intermediaries between the mortal world and the cosmic forces that sustain existence.

The nation's landscape is defined by the River of Life, a massive waterway that flows from highland lakes in the east through a broad, fertile valley before emptying into the southern sea. Nearly all of Nepherites' population lives within a few miles of this river. The valley is lush and green — irrigated farmland, date palm groves, papyrus marshes, and thriving settlements line both banks. Beyond the valley, the desert begins with brutal immediacy. There is no gradual transition; one can stand with a foot in irrigated cropland and a foot on baked sand.

The great pyramids of Nepherites dominate the landscape. Dozens of these massive structures rise from the desert along the western bank of the River of Life, their peaks precisely aligned to catch the first and last rays of the sun each day. The largest — the Great Pyramid of Ra'khet — is the most massive structure built by human hands on Nigal, visible from miles in every direction. These pyramids serve multiple functions: temples to Ra (Pyrantor), tombs for the Pharaohs, astronomical observatories, and — though the Varkonans do not fully understand this — ley-line convergence points that focus magical energy according to geometric principles inherited from Akovarian tradition.

Nepherites is a rigidly stratified society. The Pharaoh stands at the apex, a divine being whose word is cosmic law. Below the Pharaoh, a powerful priestly class — the Ra-Priests — controls religious life, education, and much of the economy. A warrior nobility maintains the army and governs provincial territories. Scribes and administrators manage the vast bureaucracy that keeps the irrigation systems, granaries, and trade networks functioning. At the base, a massive population of farmers, laborers, and slaves produces the food and labor that sustains everything above them.

### History

Nepherites was founded approximately 6,500 BW by Akovarian refugees who had arrived in the Ancient South around 6,830 BW. The three centuries between arrival and formal founding represent a period of desperate survival — the amnesiac refugees struggling to feed themselves, establish shelter, and make sense of a harsh desert environment utterly unlike their lost homeland.

The instinct to build pyramids — an unconscious echo of the Akovarian pyramid temples that had served as ley-line convergence points — drove the earliest construction efforts. The first pyramid at Nepherites was crude by later standards, but it established the architectural tradition that would define Varkonan civilization. Around this pyramid, the city grew.

The institution of the Pharaoh emerged early. Whether the first Pharaohs were genuinely powerful priests who retained fragments of Akovarian magical knowledge or simply charismatic leaders who claimed divine authority is debated by historians. What is certain is that within a few generations of the founding, the Pharaoh was established as a living deity — a god-king whose authority derived not from military conquest or political maneuvering but from an inherent divine nature. This tradition has persisted, unbroken, for over six thousand years.

Worship of Pyrantor as Ra — the Solar Pharaoh — became the state religion of Nepherites within its first millennium. The brutal desert sun, which dominated every aspect of Varkonan life, was a natural focus for worship. The Ra-Priests developed elaborate theology claiming that Ra travels the sky by day in a solar barge, descends into the underworld at night to battle darkness, and emerges victorious each dawn. The truth — that Pyrantor is a mindless elemental force, not a conscious deity — did not diminish their devotion. If anything, the Varkonan theological framework gave shape to shapeless flame, creating meaning where none existed.

The expansion of Nepherites along the River of Life proceeded gradually over millennia. Satellite settlements were established at major oases and along the river's tributaries. The construction of the Varkonan Canal around 2,100 BW represented the empire's greatest engineering achievement, extending arable land westward into the desert at enormous human cost. Each Pharaoh commissioned at least one pyramid during their reign, and the necropolis along the western bank grew into a vast city of the dead rivaling the living capital in scope.

The Second Tiovinian Crusade of 219 AW was the most traumatic external event in Nepherites' post-War history. Tentigo's invasion of the northern frontier — driven by militant Tiovinian zeal to convert "sun-worshiping heathens" — was repelled, but the experience reinforced Varkonan isolationism and deepened suspicion of northern religions.

The recovery of the Mask of the Pharaoh in 815 AW created a theological and political crisis that continues to reverberate. The revelation that the Varkonan people are descended from Akovarian refugees — not divinely created from the desert itself as their mythology holds — threatens the legitimacy of the Pharaonic institution. The court has suppressed this knowledge, but it circulates among scholars, reformers, and the growing community of Asti's Line-Watchers who have arrived to study the ancient pyramid-ley-line connection.

The crash of five proto-entities into the Varkonan desert in 839 AW further disrupted the region's stability. The impact sites released enormous magical energy, drew the attention of Yak-Men raiders, and forced the Pharaonic court to contend with cosmic forces it was ill-equipped to understand.

### Government Structure

Nepherites is governed as a pharaonic theocracy — a system in which divine authority and political power are one and the same. The Pharaoh is the supreme ruler, considered a living god whose decrees carry the weight of cosmic law. The Pharaoh's authority is absolute in theory, though in practice it is constrained by the power of the Ra-Priests, the warrior nobility, and the sheer logistical challenge of governing a civilization spread across hundreds of miles of river valley.

Below the Pharaoh, the Ra-Priests form the most powerful institutional force in the nation. The High Priest of Ra serves as the Pharaoh's chief advisor and, in some reigns, the true power behind the throne. The Ra-Priests control the temples, manage the astronomical observatories, oversee mummification and funerary rites, educate the scribal class, and collect temple tithes that constitute a significant portion of the national economy. Their political influence is immense, and succession crises — when a Pharaoh dies without a clear heir — are often resolved by the Ra-Priests selecting and legitimizing the new ruler.

The warrior nobility, known as the Medjay, governs the provinces and commands the army. Medjay lords administer territories along the River of Life, collect taxes, maintain local militia, and defend borders. Their power derives from military strength and land ownership rather than divine mandate, creating an occasional tension with the priestly class.

Scribes and administrators form the bureaucratic backbone of the empire. Literacy in Varkonae is limited to the educated classes — scribes, priests, and nobility — while the majority of the population is functionally illiterate. The scribal class maintains records of irrigation schedules, grain storage, labor assignments, tax collections, and astronomical observations that are essential for the empire's functioning.

Slavery is an established institution in Nepherites. Slaves constitute a significant portion of the labor force, particularly in construction, mining, and canal maintenance. Sources of slaves include prisoners of war, criminals, debtors, and those captured by desert raiders and sold to Varkonan slavers. The institution is viewed as natural and divinely ordained by Varkonan culture, though foreign visitors — particularly those from Saerrus or the more progressive Inner Kingdom nations — find it abhorrent.

### Culture

Varkonan culture is defined by three pillars: the sun, the river, and the dead.

The sun dominates every aspect of life. The daily cycle of Ra's journey across the sky — dawn as resurrection, noon as triumph, sunset as descent into the underworld, midnight as battle with darkness — provides the metaphorical framework for understanding birth, life, death, and the afterlife. Buildings are oriented to solar alignments. Festivals follow solar cycles. The Pharaoh's authority derives from identification with Ra. Even time itself is measured by the sun's movement, with Ra-Priests maintaining solar clocks atop the pyramids that track the hours with remarkable precision.

The River of Life is the source of physical survival. The annual flood cycle — which deposits fertile silt across the valley floor — dictates the agricultural calendar. Farming, fishing, and river trade sustain the economy. Iquinias, the water entity, receives offerings and prayers along the riverbanks, though never with the institutional fervor directed at Ra. To the Varkonans, the river is a gift that sustains the body, while the sun is a god that sustains the soul.

Death and the afterlife occupy an extraordinary place in Varkonan culture. The Varkonans believe that the soul persists after death and that the body must be preserved through mummification to ensure the soul's continued existence. This belief drives one of the most elaborate funerary traditions in the world. Pharaohs and nobles are mummified using techniques perfected over millennia, their organs preserved in canopic jars, their tombs stocked with food, tools, weapons, and treasures for use in the afterlife. Even common people receive some form of preservation, though the quality varies with wealth.

This intense focus on death and preservation has created an uncomfortable proximity to the teachings of Zebulba, the entity of entropy and undeath. While Zebulba worship is officially forbidden in Nepherites, the line between "preserving the dead for the afterlife" and "defying natural death through necromantic practice" is thinner than the Ra-Priests would admit. Some tomb-keepers — the priests responsible for maintaining the vast necropolises — practice traditions that are, functionally, necromantic in nature. The Ra-Priests tolerate this as long as it remains confined to funerary practice and does not cross into the creation of intelligent undead.

Art and architecture in Nepherites are monumental in scale and deeply symbolic. Every surface of every significant building is covered in hieroglyphic inscriptions and painted scenes depicting the deeds of Pharaohs, the blessings of Ra, and the cosmological stories that underpin Varkonan theology. Sculpture is massive and idealized — colossal statues of Pharaohs guard temple entrances, their features perfect and ageless. Music and poetry accompany every religious ceremony, with a tradition of hymn-singing that dates back to the earliest days of the civilization.

Mathematics, astronomy, and geometry are the most advanced fields of Varkonan knowledge, surpassing even the Inner Kingdoms in some respects. The astronomical observations made from atop the pyramids are the most precise in the world. Varkonan mathematicians developed concepts of geometry, measurement, and calculation that other civilizations would not achieve for millennia. These intellectual achievements are remnants of the Akovarian legacy — knowledge preserved in fragmentary form through tradition and instinct even after the refugees lost their conscious memories.

### Religion

The worship of Pyrantor as Ra — the Solar Pharaoh — dominates religious life in Nepherites to such a degree that all other faiths exist in its shadow. Ra is not merely the most important deity; Ra is the organizing principle of Varkonan civilization. The Pharaoh rules as Ra's earthly representative. The pyramids are Ra's temples. The calendar follows Ra's journey. The moral framework of Varkonan society — order, duty, hierarchy, preservation — derives from Ra's eternal cycle of death and resurrection.

Ra-Priests maintain political power as advisors to the Pharaoh and administrators of the temple economy. Every major decision is made with consultation from the clergy, and the largest pyramid in Ra'khet serves as both temple and government center. The Dawn Vigil — a daily ceremony in which Ra-Priests gather at pyramid peaks before dawn to greet the sun's return — is performed without exception, every single day, in every significant settlement across Nepherites. Missing a Dawn Vigil is considered grave disrespect to Ra.

Iquinias, known by local names along the River of Life, receives significant but secondary worship. River priests tend shrines along the banks, perform blessing ceremonies before fishing seasons and flood cycles, and manage the sacred crocodiles that inhabit certain stretches of the river. The relationship between Ra-worship and river-worship is generally harmonious — the sun and the river are understood as complementary divine forces, one sustaining the soul and the other sustaining the body.

Zebulba worship is forbidden in Nepherites, but the line between sanctioned funerary practice and prohibited necromancy is ambiguous. The tomb-keeper traditions — which involve preserving bodies, warding tombs against desecration, and maintaining spiritual connections between the living and the dead — share uncomfortable similarities with Zebulban practice. The Ra-Priests maintain that their funerary magic serves to honor and preserve the dead for the afterlife, not to defy death itself. Critics — including some Line-Watchers who have studied the practices — note that the distinction is more semantic than substantive.

Since 815 AW, the worship of Asti has begun to re-emerge in the Ancient South. Line-Watchers from other regions have arrived to study the ancient pyramids, which they recognize as ley-line convergence points built according to Akovarian principles. Some Varkonan scholars, learning of the Akovarian connection, have privately begun to study Asti's teachings alongside Ra's theology. The Ra-Priests view this development with deep suspicion, seeing Asti worship as both a foreign intrusion and a threat to the theological framework that legitimizes Pharaonic rule.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** Uadjit (uneasy coexistence, cultural kinship despite rivalry), Sabonaar and Laveri merchants (trade partners), the Line-Watchers of Asti (cautious acceptance by some scholars, rejection by the Ra-Priests).

**Enemies:** The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo (mutual hatred since the Second Tiovinian Crusade), Yak-Men raiders (persistent desert threat, especially since their alliance with the proto-entity Nakar in 839 AW), tomb robbers and relic hunters from the Merchant Nations and Inner Kingdoms, Zebulban cults (officially suppressed, persistently present).

**Threats:** Desert raiders (Yak-Men and others), tomb curses and ancient magical traps, the political implications of the Akovarian origin revelation, increasing foreign interest in Varkonan artifacts and ruins, the five proto-entities still settling into the region, and the persistent tension between the Ra-Priests and the growing Line-Walker presence.

### Major Cities

**Ra'khet** — Metropolis (Pop. ~45,000)

The capital of Nepherites and the largest city in the Ancient South, Ra'khet sprawls along both banks of the River of Life at the point where the valley is broadest and most fertile. The city is dominated by the Great Pyramid of Ra — the largest structure built by human hands on Nigal — which rises from the western bank like a manufactured mountain, its limestone casing gleaming white in the sunlight, its golden capstone flashing with reflected light visible for miles in every direction.

Ra'khet is divided by the River of Life into the Living City (eastern bank) and the Necropolis (western bank). The Living City contains the Pharaoh's palace, the administrative center, the great market, residential districts, and the harbor. The Necropolis is the city of the dead — an enormous complex of pyramids, mortuary temples, tombs, and embalming workshops where the dead of Ra'khet have been interred for over six thousand years. The Necropolis is larger in area than the Living City, testament to the vast number of dead accumulated over millennia.

The Living City is organized around the Palace of the Sun, a sprawling complex of courtyards, audience halls, throne rooms, and private quarters where the Pharaoh holds court. Adjacent to the palace stands the Great Temple of Ra, where the High Priest conducts daily Dawn Vigils and seasonal ceremonies. The Grand Market extends along the riverfront, a bustling trade center where Varkonan goods are exchanged alongside imports from the Merchant Nations, the Dwarven lands, and occasionally the Inner Kingdoms.

The Scribal Quarter houses the administrative bureaucracy and the great libraries where Varkonan knowledge is preserved on papyrus scrolls. The Harbor District handles river and canal traffic, with shallow-draft boats carrying goods and passengers up and down the River of Life. The Workers' Quarter, home to laborers, artisans, and the free poor, spreads across the eastern portions of the city. Slave quarters and construction camps for pyramid-building projects occupy the southern outskirts.

The Necropolis is sacred ground, accessible to the living only through designated gates guarded by tomb-keepers. The Great Pyramid of Ra stands at the center — enormous, ancient, and pulsing with faint magical energy since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW. Surrounding it are dozens of lesser pyramids, each the final resting place of a Pharaoh, arranged in a pattern that — scholars now realize — corresponds to ley-line geometry. Mortuary temples provide space for ongoing funerary rites, and embalming workshops prepare the dead for their journey to the afterlife. Deep beneath the Necropolis, labyrinthine catacombs hold the remains of millions of common dead, their tombs humble but carefully tended.

**Key NPCs:**
- **Pharaoh Amenhotep VII** (LN male Varkonan Cleric of Pyrantor/Aristocrat) — The current Pharaoh of Nepherites. A traditionalist who regards his divine authority as absolute and views the Akovarian origin revelation as a dangerous lie spread by foreigners. Amenhotep rules with the confidence of a man who believes himself a living god, tempered by the pragmatism necessary to manage a vast empire in a harsh environment.
- **High Priest Kha'eset** (LN male Varkonan Cleric of Pyrantor) — Leader of the Ra-Priests and the Pharaoh's chief advisor. An elderly, brilliant, and deeply conservative theologian who has dedicated his life to maintaining the purity of Ra worship. He views the Line-Watchers as dangerous heretics and the Akovarian origin story as blasphemy that must be suppressed for the stability of the empire.
- **Shi'ar Nebtawi** (N female Varkonan Wizard/Scholar) — A prominent Shi'ar (scholar-sage) who has privately accepted the Akovarian origin theory and studies the connection between the pyramids and the ley-line network. She walks a dangerous line between scholarly inquiry and political heresy, maintaining a public persona of loyalty to Ra while secretly corresponding with Line-Watchers and Astral Circle researchers.

**Osymunhotep** — Small Town (Pop. ~2,500)

A modest Varkonan settlement in the desert northeast of Ra'khet, Osymunhotep gained sudden and unwanted fame in 839 AW when five proto-entities crashed into the surrounding desert. The village sits at the intersection of two minor trade routes connecting the River of Life valley to eastern oasis settlements, making it a way station for caravans and desert travelers.

Before the proto-entity crash, Osymunhotep was a quiet agricultural community, its fields irrigated by a minor canal branching from the Varkonan Canal system. The settlement's modest temple to Ra, a small marketplace, and a fortified caravanserai constituted its only notable features. The crash changed everything. The impact craters — visible from the town walls — released enormous magical energy that continues to affect the surrounding desert. Strange creatures have emerged from the disrupted wasteland. Yak-Men raiders, drawn by the magical energy, have increased their activity in the region. Scholars, treasure-seekers, and the curious have arrived from across Nigal, transforming the quiet village into an impromptu frontier outpost.

The local priesthood is overwhelmed. The proto-entities — cosmic beings of immense power — are utterly beyond the theological framework of Ra worship. The Pharaonic court has sent representatives to investigate, but the remoteness of the location and the incomprehensible nature of the threat have limited the government's response.

**Key NPCs:**

He prays to Ra for guidance and receives, as always, only the silent heat of the sun.

## UADJIT

**"Name your desire. Everything has a price."**
— Uadjitian merchant proverb

### Overview

**Alignment:** CN (with evil tendencies among the genie-binding elite)
**Government:** Oligarchic Theocracy (Council of Binders)
**Capital:** Uadjit (pop. ~30,000)
**Population:** ~80,000 (Varkonan humans, with significant populations of genasi, half-elementals, foreign traders, and bound genies)
**Languages:** Varkonae, Common (widely spoken in trade districts), Aquan, Auran, Ignan, Terran (elemental languages used in genie-binding)
**Religions:** Pyrantor (as Ra — culturally significant but less dominant than in Nepherites), Iquinias (strong, given coastal and river location), elemental spirits (genies venerated as intermediaries), Zebulba (hidden but more prevalent than in Nepherites)
**Primary Exports:** Exotic luxuries, genie-crafted goods, perfumes, alchemical products, slaves, elemental magic services
**Real-World Analog:** Ancient Egypt meets Arabian Nights, decadent late-period

Uadjit is a city of wonders and excess, founded approximately 5,800 BW at the mouth of the River of Life where it meets the southern sea. Where Nepherites is ancient, austere, and bound by tradition, Uadjit is sensual, cosmopolitan, and morally flexible. The city's wealth derives from two sources: its position as the primary port connecting the Ancient South to maritime trade routes, and its mastery of genie-binding magic — the art of summoning, bargaining with, and compelling elemental spirits to perform extraordinary feats.

Uadjit's skyline is a fantastical blend of Varkonan pyramid architecture and structures that could only exist through elemental magic. Towers of crystallized water that never melts rise beside traditional limestone temples. Gardens suspended in mid-air, held aloft by bound air elementals, float above the city's wealthiest districts. Bridges of solidified flame span canals, producing warmth without burning. The streets shimmer with permanent illusions, and the air carries the scent of exotic perfumes created by alchemists whose ingredients are harvested from elemental planes.

The city is governed by the Council of Binders — a ruling body of the most powerful genie-binders in Uadjit. Unlike Nepherites' divine monarchy, Uadjit's power structure is based on magical ability and the number and power of the genies one controls. The wealthiest Binders maintain stables of bound genies the way northern lords maintain stables of horses, using elemental servitors to construct buildings, guard estates, produce luxury goods, and project military power.

This system creates both extraordinary beauty and profound exploitation. The genies themselves — djinn, efreet, marid, and dao — are sentient beings bound against their will, their freedom traded for magical power. Some Binders treat their genies with a measure of respect; others are brutal taskmasters who view elemental spirits as tools to be used and discarded. The ethical implications of genie-binding are a source of ongoing tension between Uadjit and the more morally conscious civilizations of the north.

### History

Uadjit was founded around 5,800 BW as Varkonan settlers pushed southward along the River of Life from Nepherites. The river's delta — where fresh water meets the sea — provided a rich environment for agriculture and fishing, while the natural harbor offered access to maritime trade routes.

The discovery of genie-binding magic around 5,500 BW transformed the settlement from a simple fishing village into something unprecedented. The exact origins of the practice are debated — some scholars believe the Varkonans developed it independently through experimentation with elemental magic, while others suggest that genies themselves initiated contact, seeking mortal partners for their own inscrutable purposes. Whatever the case, by 5,000 BW, genie-binding was an established practice and Uadjit's defining cultural tradition.

Uadjit's relationship with Nepherites has been defined by interdependence and rivalry. The Pharaonic Empire claims sovereignty over all Varkonan lands, and Uadjit nominally acknowledges this claim, sending tribute and participating in imperial ceremonies. In practice, however, Uadjit's independent power base — its genie-binders, its coastal trade, its exotic wealth — has always made it effectively autonomous. Multiple Pharaohs have attempted to bring Uadjit under tighter control; all have eventually been forced to accept the city's de facto independence.

The city's cosmopolitan character increased after the War to End All Wars, as maritime trade routes reopened and merchants from the Merchant Nations, the Frozen Isles, and even distant Vand'ardhon began calling at Uadjit's port. Foreign influences have diluted the austere Varkonan traditionalism that characterizes Nepherites, creating a more open, diverse, and morally flexible society.

The Shi'ars of Uadjit — a class of scholar-priests distinct from Nepherites' Ra-Priests — played a crucial role in the recovery of the Mask of the Pharaoh in 815 AW. When adventurers arrived seeking information about the Mask's location, it was the Shi'ars who provided the historical and magical knowledge necessary to find it within the Nepherites Pyramid. This willingness to assist outsiders — which the Ra-Priests of Nepherites viewed as treachery — reflects Uadjit's more pragmatic approach to foreign relations and the preservation of knowledge.

### Government Structure

Uadjit is governed by the Council of Binders, a body of the twelve most powerful genie-binders in the city. Seats on the Council are not hereditary or elected — they are held by those who command the most powerful genies and the greatest magical resources. When a Binder's power wanes — through the loss of bound genies, the depletion of magical resources, or simple old age — they are replaced by a rising competitor. This creates a system where political power is in constant flux, driven by magical ability rather than bloodline or popular mandate.

The Council makes decisions by majority vote, with each Binder casting one vote regardless of the number of genies they control. In practice, the most powerful Binders wield disproportionate influence, as they can use their genies to reward allies and threaten rivals. The position of First Binder — the Council's presiding officer — rotates annually among the twelve members, preventing any single Binder from consolidating too much formal authority.

Below the Council, a complex network of lesser binders, merchants, guild leaders, and neighborhood chiefs manages the city's daily affairs. Justice is administered by appointed magistrates whose rulings can be appealed to the Council. The city maintains a professional guard force supplemented by elemental servitors — bound genies who patrol the streets, guard the harbor, and defend the walls.

### Culture

Uadjit's culture is a heady blend of Varkonan tradition and exotic cosmopolitanism. The city embraces pleasure, beauty, and excess in ways that would scandalize the austere Ra-Priests of Nepherites. Perfumed baths, elaborate feasts, musical performances, and theatrical spectacles fill the lives of the wealthy, while the common people enjoy a standard of living higher than most Varkonans — genie-crafted infrastructure provides clean water, waste removal, and magical lighting that other cities lack.

The genie-binding tradition shapes every aspect of Uadjitian life. The most prestigious profession is that of the Binder — one who can summon and control elemental spirits. Young Varkonans with magical talent are recruited into binding academies, where they spend years learning the complex rituals, sigils, and bargaining techniques necessary to interact with genies. Successful Binders accumulate wealth and power; failures suffer the consequences of displeasing an elemental spirit, which can range from humiliation to death.

The ethical dimension of genie-binding is the city's most controversial feature. Genies are sentient, intelligent beings — djinn with their own cultures, desires, and lives. Binding them against their will is, fundamentally, a form of slavery. Some Binders acknowledge this and treat their genies with respect, negotiating contracts rather than compelling servitude. Others view genies as resources to be exploited, binding them with painful wards and forcing compliance through magical torture. The debate between these approaches is ongoing and occasionally violent.

Trade is Uadjit's lifeblood. The city's port handles goods from across the southern coast and beyond — spices from the Merchant Nations, metals from the Dwarven lands, textiles from the Inner Kingdoms, exotic creatures from the Mespoian jungles. Uadjitian merchants are renowned as shrewd negotiators, and the city's markets offer goods available nowhere else in the world — genie-crafted jewelry, elemental essences, perfumes that can alter emotions, and alchemical products of extraordinary potency.

### Religion

Religious life in Uadjit is more diverse and less rigidly controlled than in Nepherites. Ra (Pyrantor) worship is culturally important — the city maintains sun pyramids and observes the Dawn Vigil — but the Ra-Priests do not hold the same political dominance they enjoy in the capital. Iquinias receives strong worship given the city's coastal and river location, with wave-speakers maintaining shrines throughout the harbor district and blessing fishing boats and merchant vessels.

The elemental spirits themselves — the genies — occupy a unique place in Uadjitian spirituality. They are not gods, but they are more powerful than mortals, and the relationship between Binder and genie has quasi-religious dimensions. Some Binders develop genuine devotion to the elemental planes and the spirits that inhabit them, viewing genie-binding not as exploitation but as a form of communion with cosmic forces.

Zebulba worship is more prevalent in Uadjit than in Nepherites, though still officially forbidden. The city's moral flexibility and cosmopolitan atmosphere provide cover for necromantic practice, and some Binders have attempted to bind undead spirits alongside elemental ones — experiments that rarely end well but continue to attract the foolish and the ambitious.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** Nepherites (uneasy cultural kinship), Sabonaar and Laveri merchants (trade partners), the Shi'ars (scholarly class that bridges both nations), Amara (growing trade relationship).

**Enemies:** The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo (shares Nepherites' hatred of the Crusaders), pirates raiding coastal trade, rogue genies who have escaped binding and seek revenge, Yak-Men raiders threatening overland trade routes.

**Threats:** Political instability within the Council of Binders, the ethical crisis of genie-binding attracting outside criticism, growing Zebulban influence among the city's underworld, the increasing power of foreign merchants who may eventually challenge Uadjitian commercial dominance, and the unpredictable effects of the ley-line reawakening on the city's genie-binding traditions.

### Key NPCs

- **First Binder Nefertari Khonshu** (CN female Varkonan Sorcerer/Genie-Binder) — The most powerful genie-binder in Uadjit, currently serving as First Binder on the Council. Nefertari commands a retinue of seven bound genies — three djinn, two efreet, a marid, and a dao — making her the most formidable magical force in the Ancient South outside the Pharaoh's court. She is ambitious, pragmatic, and views genie-binding as a partnership rather than slavery, though her definition of "partnership" is more generous than most Binders' but would still horrify a free genie.
- **Shi'ar Ankhet** (NG male Varkonan Wizard/Scholar) — The eldest and most respected of the Shi'ars of Uadjit. It was Ankhet who provided the crucial information that allowed adventurers to locate the Mask of the Pharaoh within the Nepherites Pyramid in 815 AW. Now ancient and frail, Ankhet continues to study the connection between Varkonan pyramid architecture and Akovarian ley-line geometry, believing that the two civilizations' shared heritage holds the key to unlocking lost magical knowledge.

## ANCIENT SOUTH ORGANIZATIONS

### Ra-Priests of Nepherites

The Ra-Priests constitute the most powerful religious institution in the Ancient South and the dominant political force in Nepherites after the Pharaoh himself. They maintain the great pyramids, conduct the daily Dawn Vigil, oversee mummification and funerary rites, manage the temple economy, and advise the Pharaoh on all matters of governance. Their authority derives from their role as intermediaries between Ra (Pyrantor) and the mortal world — a role that parallels, unknowingly, the function of the Akovarian priests who served as intermediaries between Asti and the common people.

The Ra-Priests are deeply conservative and view any challenge to traditional theology — including the Akovarian origin revelation and the re-emergence of Asti worship — as existential threats to the social order they maintain. Their opposition to the Line-Watchers is theological, political, and personal: if the pyramids are ley-line convergence points built by Asti worshipers, then the Ra-Priests' claim to spiritual authority over these structures is fundamentally compromised.

### The Shi'ars

The Shi'ars are a class of scholar-priests found in both Nepherites and Uadjit, though they are more numerous and influential in the latter. Unlike the Ra-Priests, who are primarily religious and political figures, the Shi'ars are scholars, researchers, and keepers of esoteric knowledge. They study astronomy, mathematics, history, and magical theory, maintaining libraries and archives that preserve Varkonan intellectual traditions stretching back millennia.

The Shi'ars played a crucial role in the recovery of the Mask of the Pharaoh in 815 AW, providing the historical and magical knowledge that allowed adventurers to locate the artifact. This willingness to share knowledge with outsiders has made the Shi'ars controversial in Nepherites, where the Ra-Priests view their openness as treachery. In Uadjit, the Shi'ars are respected as essential contributors to the city's intellectual and magical life.

Since 815 AW, some Shi'ars have begun studying the connection between Varkonan pyramid architecture and Akovarian ley-line geometry, collaborating quietly with Line-Watchers from other regions. This research is the most significant intellectual development in the Ancient South in centuries, with the potential to transform understanding of both Varkonan history and the nature of magical energy.

### The Tomb-Keepers

The Tomb-Keepers are the priests responsible for maintaining the vast necropolises of the Ancient South — the cities of the dead where mummified Pharaohs, nobles, and common people rest in their tombs. Their duties include performing ongoing funerary rites, maintaining tomb wards against desecration, managing the embalming and mummification process, and guarding against tomb robbers.

The Tomb-Keepers occupy an uncomfortable position in Varkonan religious life. Their work requires intimate familiarity with death, preservation magic, and spiritual practices that blur the line between sanctioned funerary tradition and forbidden necromancy. Some Tomb-Keepers maintain traditions that are, functionally, necromantic — communicating with the dead, binding spirits to guard tombs, and performing rituals that keep mummified bodies in a state that could, with the right provocation, animate them.

The Ra-Priests tolerate the Tomb-Keepers because their work is essential to the funerary traditions that underpin Varkonan culture, but the relationship is uneasy. Periodic purges of Tomb-Keepers suspected of crossing into Zebulban practice occur every few generations, reinforcing the boundary between acceptable and forbidden death magic without ever fully resolving the inherent ambiguity.

### The Yak-Men

The Yak-Men are brutal raiders and slavers who control portions of the Varkonan desert, particularly the eastern and northeastern wasteland between the River of Life valley and the Dragon Spine mountains. They are not human — they are a monstrous humanoid race with bovine features, standing seven to eight feet tall, with thick hides, curving horns, and a predatory intelligence that makes them far more dangerous than simple bandits.

The Yak-Men worship destruction in various forms and have historically raided Varkonan settlements for slaves, livestock, and goods. Their alliance with the proto-entity Nakar during the Gods of the Fall crisis (839 AW) elevated them from a persistent nuisance to a genuine strategic threat. While the immediate crisis has passed, the Yak-Men retain some of the enhanced power and organization that Nakar's patronage provided, and they continue to threaten trade routes, oasis settlements, and the desert frontier.

The Pharaonic army and the Medjay warrior nobility conduct periodic campaigns against the Yak-Men, but the desert is vast and the raiders are skilled at disappearing into the wasteland. The Yak-Men are not a problem that can be solved by force alone — they are a permanent feature of the Ancient South's geography, as much a part of the desert as the sand and the heat.

### Line-Watchers of the Ancient South

Since 815 AW and the reawakening of ley-lines across Mazariim, Line-Watchers — clergy of the entity Asti — have begun arriving in the Ancient South to study the ancient pyramids. They have recognized what the Varkonans never fully understood: that the pyramids are ley-line convergence points, built according to geometric principles inherited from the Akovarian civilization that the Varkonans descended from.

The Line-Watchers' presence is small but growing. They number perhaps two dozen in the Ancient South, operating from temporary camps near major pyramid sites and a small permanent presence in Uadjit (where the Shi'ars are more welcoming than the Ra-Priests of Nepherites). Their research into the pyramid-ley-line connection is the most significant development in the region's magical understanding in millennia.

The Ra-Priests of Nepherites view the Line-Watchers with deep suspicion and have barred them from accessing the great pyramids without explicit Pharaonic permission — which has not been granted. The Line-Watchers have responded by studying lesser pyramids and ruins in the desert, building relationships with sympathetic Shi'ars, and waiting patiently for political circumstances to change. Asti, having waited nearly eight thousand years for liberation, can wait a little longer for the Varkonans to accept the truth of their own history.

### Desert Nomads

Beyond the settled lands of the River of Life valley, scattered bands of desert nomads traverse the vast wasteland. These groups — some Varkonan in origin, others of mixed heritage including Chak'ti influence from the Horselands to the north — follow ancient routes between oases, trading, herding goats and camels, and surviving in conditions that would kill settled peoples within days.

The nomads maintain their own religious traditions, often blending Ra worship with older elemental practices and shamanic traditions that predate the Pharaonic civilization. They serve as guides, traders, and occasionally raiders, existing in a complex relationship with the settled Varkonan population that involves both mutual dependence and mutual distrust.

## GEOGRAPHICAL FEATURES

### The River of Life

The River of Life is the defining geographical feature of the Ancient South — a massive waterway that flows from highland lakes in the east, through a broad fertile valley, and empties into the southern sea at the delta where Uadjit stands. Nearly all permanent settlement in the region exists within a few miles of this river or its tributaries. The annual flood cycle deposits fertile silt across the valley floor, enabling agriculture in an otherwise arid landscape.

The river is more than a water source; it is a transportation network, a trade route, and a spiritual symbol. Flat-bottomed boats carry goods and passengers between settlements. Fishing provides a significant portion of the region's protein. The river's annual rise and fall dictates the agricultural calendar and the rhythms of daily life. To the Varkonans, the River of Life is a divine gift — evidence that the gods have not abandoned them to the desert.

### The Great Desert

North and east of the River of Life valley stretches the Great Desert — a vast, merciless expanse of sand, rock, and heat that separates the Ancient South from the rest of Nigal. Daytime temperatures can kill an unprepared traveler within hours. Sandstorms can bury entire caravans. Water sources are rare, unreliable, and often contested.

The Great Desert is not empty. Yak-Men raiders, desert nomads, ancient ruins, scattered oases, and strange creatures — some natural, some magical — inhabit the wasteland. The crash sites of the five proto-entities (839 AW) are located in the northeastern desert, their impact craters still radiating magical energy that attracts and disturbs the desert's inhabitants.

Trade routes across the desert exist but are dangerous, expensive, and seasonal. Caravans traveling north toward the Horselands or the Merchant Nations require experienced guides, armed guards, and substantial water supplies. Most trade to and from the Ancient South travels by sea along the southern coast — a longer but safer route.

### The Varkonan Canal

The Varkonan Canal, constructed around 2,100 BW, extends from the River of Life westward into the arid interior. This massive engineering project — built with slave labor and magical assistance — channels water into the western desert, creating a secondary band of arable land that roughly doubled the region's food production.

The canal requires constant maintenance. Without regular dredging, repair of retaining walls, and management of sluice gates, the desert would reclaim the canal within a generation. This maintenance is one of the primary labor demands of the Pharaonic government, employing thousands of workers — many of them slaves — year-round.

### The Necropolis of Ra'khet

The Necropolis occupies the western bank of the River of Life opposite the living city of Ra'khet. It is the largest cemetery complex in the world — over six thousand years of continuous burial have created a city of the dead that exceeds the living city in area. Dozens of pyramids, hundreds of mortuary temples, thousands of noble tombs, and uncounted common graves fill the landscape, their monuments weathered by millennia of wind and sand.

The Necropolis is sacred ground, patrolled by Tomb-Keepers and accessible to the living only through designated gates. Beneath the surface, labyrinthine catacombs extend in all directions, some mapped and maintained, others sealed and forgotten. The dangers within — tomb traps, undead guardians, residual curses, and the sheer complexity of the underground network — make the Necropolis one of the most dangerous places in the Ancient South for the unprepared.

Since the ley-line reawakening of 815 AW, the Necropolis has exhibited strange phenomena. Faint golden light pulses from the depths of the oldest pyramids. Tomb-Keepers report hearing sounds — not voices, but geometric harmonies, mathematical rhythms — emanating from deep underground. Whether these are signs of ley-line energy flowing through ancient convergence points or something more unsettling remains unclear.
