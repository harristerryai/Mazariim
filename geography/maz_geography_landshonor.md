# Geography of the Lands of Honor: The Yashaki Islands

**As of 842 AW**

---

## INTRODUCTION

A large string of islands known as the Yashaki, or the "Warlord's Pearls," lies to the southeast of the main continent of Nigal. There one can find the Lands of Honor, a group of city-states with different values and cultures than the rest of the world. Samurai are the paladins of these lands filled with warlords, ninjas, and monks.

The humans originating from this part of the world are known as the Ejiin, which in their dialect simply means "person." Their language, known as Ejao, is a flowing language with a wide variety of sounds that emphasize different parts of the same words for different meanings. The written form consists of thousands of pictograms that can be written in an artistic form, an Ejiin art form said to take decades to truly master.

The Lands of Honor generally have the technology of Asia in the 15th century, including gunpowder and advanced ships, although this too can vary wildly within the islands themselves. Character classes such as samurai, ninja, and wu-jen are appropriate for this area of the world. Analogs for Japan, China, Korea, and Vietnam can be found here.

The Yashaki Islands are volcanic in origin, with dramatic mountainous terrain, dense forests, terraced rice paddies, and a coastline of sheltered harbors, rocky cliffs, and black sand beaches. The climate ranges from subtropical in the southern islands to temperate in the north, with heavy monsoon rains in summer and cold, dry winters. Typhoons strike the eastern coasts regularly, and earthquakes are a constant threat in a region of active volcanism.

**Current Political Organization (842 AW)**

The Lands of Honor are divided between two major powers and numerous smaller clans, temples, and independent settlements:

- **Tansudo** (Miyasaki Shogunate) — The dominant nation, known for its samurai warriors and strict code of honor. An ancient shogunate tracing its origins to ~3,200 BW.
- **Veppankarai** — A complex network of clans, both major and minor, that vie for power and influence through politics, trade, and occasionally warfare. A ninja-run autocracy established ~2,800 BW.

Numerous smaller island domains, independent monasteries, temple complexes, and fishing villages exist outside the direct control of either major power, though all are influenced by the broader Ejiin culture.

**Total Population (842 AW, estimated)**

- Humans (Ejiin): ~600,000 across all islands
- Spirit folk, hengeyokai, and other native races: ~15,000
- Other (foreign traders, half-elves, exotic visitors): ~5,000
- **Total: ~620,000**

## RELIGION OF THE LANDS OF HONOR

Religion in the Yashaki Islands is a layered system unlike anything found elsewhere on Mazariim. Rather than the single-deity or competing-faith models common on Nigal and Vand'ardhon, the Ejiin practice multiple religious traditions simultaneously, seeing them as complementary rather than contradictory. A single household might honor the local spirits through a shaman, worship the elemental forces through a shugenja, and revere Ashura through a samurai's code of honor — all without any sense of conflict.

### Shamanism and the Spirit World

All people in the Ejiin lands believe in and honor the spirits that inhabit the natural world around them. These spirits — called kami — are believed to inhabit everything from trees and rocks to rivers and mountains. Every village, grove, waterfall, and mountain pass has its resident spirits, and maintaining harmonious relationships with them is considered essential for community wellbeing.

The local shaman serves as the intermediary between citizens and the spirits of nature. Shamans are highly respected community leaders who perform blessings, divinations, healing rituals, and ceremonies to appease angry spirits or thank benevolent ones. They read omens in natural phenomena — the flight of birds, the pattern of clouds, the behavior of animals — and advise communities on planting, travel, and important decisions.

Evil shamans exist who worship the Oni Lords, demonic entities that corrupt the natural order. These dark shamans draw power from Jigoku (the realm of Khan) rather than the natural spirit world, spreading Taint — a corrupting supernatural force that warps both body and soul. Communities that discover a dark shaman in their midst react with horror and swift violence.

### Shugenja and Elementalism

The old religion of the Lands of Honor worships the four elemental forces that govern the cycle of life: water, earth, air, and fire. Shugenja are the priests of this religion, channeling the power of the elements through prayer and ritual. They believe that balance between the elements is essential for harmony in the world.

Shugenja temples are found across the Yashaki Islands, often built at sites of elemental significance — volcanic vents for fire, waterfalls for water, mountain peaks for air, and deep caves for earth. The four elemental forces are personified as entities known by their Ejiin names:

- **Ryran** (known elsewhere as Pyrantor) — The entity of Fire, representing passion, destruction, and renewal.
- **Nas** (known elsewhere as Iquinias) — The entity of Water, representing flow, adaptability, and life.
- **Vulu** (known elsewhere as Vuleala) — The entity of Air, representing freedom, thought, and change.
- **Ginto** (known elsewhere as Dirgin) — The entity of Earth, representing strength, stability, and endurance.

The shugenja tradition predates contact with the western faiths and represents one of the oldest formalized worship systems in the Lands of Honor. Unlike western elemental cults, which often focus on a single element, Ejiin elementalism emphasizes the interplay and balance of all four forces.

### Ashura and the Realm of Tengoku

Ashura is an androgynous entity, almost always depicted dressed in full samurai plate mail, representing honor, justice, and goodness. Ashura awakened around 2,900 BW when the first samurai swore oaths of honor, and carved the realm of Tengoku from a portion of Heaven with Tiovin's blessing.

Tengoku is the bright and radiant portion of the Positive Energy Plane where the Shijin (Kami spirits) hail from. Those who live honorable lives and die with dignity are said to ascend to Tengoku to serve Ashura in eternal glory. Tengoku is described as an endless realm of perfect beauty — cherry blossoms that never fall, mountains that touch the stars, and fields where honored ancestors feast and train for eternity.

Ashura's worship is inseparable from the code of Bushido — the warrior's way — that governs samurai behavior. To follow Bushido is to worship Ashura through action rather than prayer. Temples to Ashura are austere and martial, featuring training grounds alongside prayer halls. Priests of Ashura are often warrior-monks who combine divine magic with martial skill.

### Khan and the Realm of Jigoku

A dark and shadowy entity, Khan is the antithesis of Ashura, representing pure evil, dishonor, and corruption. When the first samurai broke an oath of honor around 2,900 BW, Khan stirred as Ashura's dark reflection, claiming territory within Hell with Taovan's permission.

Jigoku is a dark and sinister section of the Negative Energy Plane, the source of Taint — a corrupting supernatural force that warps both body and soul. The Oni, demonic fiends that serve Khan, seek to spread Taint throughout the mortal world. Those corrupted by Taint undergo physical and spiritual transformation, becoming monstrous servants of Khan's will.

Khan is not openly worshiped — to do so would be the ultimate dishonor. Instead, Khan's influence spreads through corruption, temptation, and the slow erosion of honor. Fallen samurai, corrupt officials, and those consumed by revenge or ambition become unwitting servants. The Oni actively seek to corrupt the honorable, finding particular pleasure in the fall of great warriors and noble lords.

Ashura and Khan battle eternally, their conflict most visible during lunar eclipses when the boundaries between Tengoku and Jigoku thin and spirits from both realms walk the mortal world.

## TANSUDO (MIYASAKI SHOGUNATE)

**"The blade serves honor. Honor serves the people. The people serve the land."**
— Samurai oath of the Miyasaki Shogunate

### Overview

**Alignment:** LN
**Government:** Shogunate (military government under hereditary Shogun)
**Capital:** (not yet detailed)
**Population:** ~400,000 (Ejiin humans, with small populations of spirit folk and hengeyokai)
**Languages:** Ejao, Common (limited, among traders)
**Religions:** Ashura, Shamanism, Elementalism (Shugenja tradition)
**Primary Exports:** Silk, lacquerware, ceramics, steel, tea, rice
**Real-World Analog:** Feudal Japan, 15th century

Tansudo, formally known as the Miyasaki Shogunate, is the dominant nation of the Lands of Honor and one of the oldest continuous governments on Mazariim. Founded around 3,200 BW by Ejiin migrants who had traveled east from the Ancient South, the Shogunate has endured for over four millennia through a combination of military strength, cultural cohesion, and rigid social order.

The Shogunate controls the largest and most fertile islands of the Yashaki chain, including the Great Island (the largest landmass in the archipelago) and several major surrounding islands. The terrain is mountainous and heavily forested, with arable land concentrated in river valleys and coastal plains where terraced rice paddies climb hillsides in spectacular stepped formations. Volcanic peaks — some still active — dominate the interior, and hot springs are found throughout the islands.

Tansudo's society is rigidly stratified. The Shogun rules as military dictator, theoretically serving a largely ceremonial Emperor whose authority derives from divine mandate. Below the Shogun, a hierarchy of daimyo (regional lords) govern provinces, supported by samurai (warrior-nobles), monks (spiritual leaders), merchants, artisans, and peasant farmers. This hierarchy is considered natural and divinely ordained — to challenge one's station is to challenge the order of the universe itself.

### History

The Ejiin people migrated east from the Ancient South around 3,500 BW, eventually settling the Yashaki Islands. The Miyasaki Shogunate was established around 3,200 BW when the first Shogun united warring island clans under a single military government. The new nation adopted an elemental religion distinct from the faiths of western Nigal, drawing on both the Varkonan traditions the migrants carried and the shamanic practices they developed in their new island home.

The awakening of Ashura around 2,900 BW transformed Ejiin culture. The samurai code of Bushido — honor, courage, self-sacrifice — became the foundation of social order. Ashura's counterpart Khan awakened simultaneously, establishing the cosmic duality that defines Ejiin spirituality to this day.

For millennia, Tansudo developed in relative isolation from the rest of Nigal. The vast ocean separating the Yashaki Islands from the mainland limited contact, and the Ejiin showed little interest in western affairs. Internal conflicts — succession wars, clan rivalries, peasant rebellions — occupied the nation's attention.

The War to End All Wars (4 BW – 1 AW) reached the Lands of Honor only indirectly. Orc raiders and sea daemons attacked coastal settlements, but the Yashaki Islands' isolation and strong naval defenses limited the damage. Tansudo contributed no forces to the Concord of Unity — the Shogunate considered western affairs irrelevant to Ejiin honor.

The Orceri Incursion of 811 AW brought the first serious external threat when orcs from an alternate timeline attacked Kashi, a settlement on the Shogunate's frontier. During the defense, an Oni — a demon-spirit servant of Khan — mysteriously aided the defenders, an event with dark repercussions that are still unfolding. In 832 AW, a shaman who had been banished twenty years earlier for attempting to harness Oni power unleashed a zombie plague on Kashi, killing thousands before he was destroyed.

By 842 AW, Tansudo faces growing threats both internal (Khan's corruption spreading through the Oni) and external (increasing contact with western powers disrupting traditional isolation).

### Government Structure

The Miyasaki Shogunate is a military government in which the Shogun holds supreme executive authority. The Shogun is theoretically appointed by the Emperor, but in practice the position is hereditary within the ruling clan, and the Emperor's role is ceremonial and spiritual.

Below the Shogun, the nation is divided into provinces governed by daimyo — powerful regional lords who maintain their own samurai retinues, collect taxes, administer justice, and defend their territories. Daimyo owe fealty to the Shogun and provide military forces when called upon, but enjoy considerable autonomy within their provinces. The balance of power between Shogun and daimyo shifts constantly — a strong Shogun can dominate, while a weak one faces rebellion.

The samurai class serves as both the military and the administrative elite. Samurai govern villages, command armies, adjudicate disputes, and enforce the law. Their authority derives from Bushido and the feudal hierarchy. Below them, monks provide spiritual guidance, merchants facilitate trade (despite low social status), artisans produce goods, and peasant farmers work the land that sustains everything.

### Culture

Tansudo's culture is defined by the code of Bushido — the warrior's way — that governs all social interactions, not just those of the samurai. Bushido emphasizes honor (maintaining personal and family reputation through righteous action), courage (facing danger without hesitation, including death), loyalty (absolute devotion to one's lord, family, and nation), self-discipline (controlling desires and emotions through rigorous training), and respect (proper behavior toward superiors, equals, and inferiors according to station).

The code of Bushido is not merely a military doctrine but a complete social philosophy. It determines how people greet each other, how disputes are resolved, how marriages are arranged, how business is conducted, and how the dead are honored. Violations of Bushido — cowardice, betrayal, dishonor — carry severe consequences, including ritual suicide (seppuku) for samurai who have failed their duty.

Ancestor worship is deeply integrated into daily life. Every household maintains a shrine to deceased family members, offering prayers, incense, and food to honor their spirits. Ancestors are believed to watch over and protect their living descendants, and consulting ancestral spirits before major decisions is common practice.

The arts flourish alongside martial culture. Calligraphy, poetry, flower arrangement, tea ceremony, and garden design are considered essential skills for cultured individuals. Samurai are expected to be poets as well as warriors — the ideal is the warrior-scholar who can compose a death poem moments before battle.

Gunpowder technology exists alongside traditional weapons. "Thunder-dust" was accidentally discovered by Ejiin alchemists around 350 BW while seeking immortality, initially used for fireworks. Military applications developed gradually, and by 842 AW, firearms supplement (but have not replaced) traditional samurai weaponry.

### Religion

Tansudo practices the layered religious system common to all Ejiin lands. Shamanism (honoring local kami spirits through village shamans) forms the foundation of daily spiritual life. Elementalism (the shugenja tradition worshiping the four elemental forces) provides formal priestly hierarchy and temple infrastructure. Ashura worship (through the code of Bushido and warrior-monk temples) defines the martial-spiritual ideal. These three traditions coexist without conflict, and most Ejiin participate in all three to varying degrees.

Khan's influence is the great spiritual threat. The Oni — demonic servants of Khan — work to corrupt the honorable and spread Taint through the mortal world. Combating Oni influence is a primary concern of shamans, shugenja, and warrior-monks alike. The mysterious Oni involvement in the defense of Kashi (811 AW) and the subsequent zombie plague (832 AW) have intensified fears that Khan's power is growing.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** Few formal alliances exist outside the Yashaki Islands. Tansudo maintains limited trade relationships with Naarlam and Amara through intermediary merchants. The Veppankarai Syndicate is a rival but also a cultural cousin — open war between the two powers is rare, as shared Ejiin identity restrains conflict.

**Enemies:** Khan and the Oni (the primary spiritual threat), pirates raiding coastal settlements, and increasingly the corrupting influence of western contact that threatens traditional social order.

**Threats:** Oni corruption spreading through compromised shamans and fallen samurai, succession disputes among daimyo, natural disasters (typhoons, earthquakes, volcanic eruptions), and the slow erosion of traditional values as contact with the wider world increases.

## VEPPANKARAI

**"The shadow serves the light it conceals."**
— Veppankarai Syndicate saying

### Overview

**Alignment:** LN (with chaotic and evil tendencies among some clans)
**Government:** Clan-based Autocracy (the Veppankarai Syndicate)
**Capital:** (not yet detailed)
**Population:** ~180,000 (Ejiin humans)
**Languages:** Ejao, Common (limited, among traders)
**Religions:** Khan (some clans, secretly), Ashura, Shamanism, Elementalism
**Primary Exports:** Intelligence, assassination services, exotic goods, poisons, silk
**Real-World Analog:** Ninja clans of feudal Japan, with elements of Southeast Asian island cultures

The Veppankarai Islands form the other major power in the Lands of Honor. Established around 2,800 BW — slightly younger than the Miyasaki Shogunate — Veppankarai developed a radically different culture despite shared Ejiin heritage. Where Tansudo prizes open honor and martial valor, Veppankarai values subtlety, information, and the strategic application of power from the shadows.

The Veppankarai Syndicate is not a single government but a complex network of clans — both major and minor — that vie for power and influence through politics, trade, espionage, and occasionally warfare. The Syndicate's internal politics are labyrinthine, with alliances shifting constantly and every clan maintaining networks of spies, assassins, and agents throughout the Yashaki Islands and beyond.

The islands of Veppankarai are smaller and more scattered than Tansudo's territories, with dense tropical jungles, hidden coves, and a coastline of natural harbors perfect for the secretive maritime culture the clans have developed. The terrain favors stealth and concealment — fog-shrouded valleys, dense bamboo forests, and cave networks riddle the islands, providing natural training grounds for the ninja traditions that define Veppankarai culture.

### History

Veppankarai was settled around 2,800 BW by Ejiin peoples who had broken away from (or been expelled by) the emerging Miyasaki Shogunate. The founders — warriors, outcasts, and dissidents who rejected the Shogunate's rigid hierarchy — established a society based on clan loyalty rather than feudal obligation, and on pragmatic effectiveness rather than rigid honor codes.

Over millennia, the Veppankarai clans developed the ninja traditions for which they are feared across the Yashaki Islands. Where samurai fight openly with honor, Veppankarai operatives strike from shadows, gather intelligence through infiltration, and eliminate targets through poison, deception, and assassination. This approach is considered dishonorable by Tansudo's samurai but devastatingly effective.

The relationship between Tansudo and Veppankarai has oscillated between cold war and uneasy coexistence for over three thousand years. Open warfare is rare — both powers recognize that the cost of conquest would be catastrophic — but espionage, sabotage, and proxy conflicts are constant. Veppankarai clans sell their services to Tansudo's daimyo (who publicly denounce ninja while privately employing them), and Tansudo's samurai hunt Veppankarai agents who operate within Shogunate territory.

### Government Structure

The Veppankarai Syndicate is governed by a council of major clan leaders who meet irregularly to coordinate on matters affecting all clans — external threats, territorial disputes, and major contracts. The council has no formal leader; influence depends on a clan's current strength, wealth, and reputation. Minor clans align with major ones in shifting patron-client relationships, creating a fluid political landscape where today's ally may be tomorrow's target.

Within each clan, authority is typically held by a single leader (clan master) who governs through a combination of personal skill, loyal lieutenants, and fear. Succession is determined by each clan's traditions — some are hereditary, others meritocratic, and a few resolve succession through assassination (the ultimate test of worthiness).

### Culture

Veppankarai culture values pragmatism, adaptability, loyalty to clan, and effectiveness over honor in the Tansudo sense. The ninja code — different from Bushido but no less demanding — emphasizes mission completion (failure is unacceptable regardless of obstacles), clan loyalty (betraying one's clan is the ultimate sin, punishable by death), adaptability (rigid thinking gets you killed; the successful operative adjusts to any situation), and secrecy (what is unknown cannot be defended against).

Despite their fearsome reputation, most Veppankarai are not assassins. The clans include farmers, fishermen, merchants, craftsmen, and scholars alongside their shadowy operatives. Ninja skills — stealth, intelligence gathering, poison craft, disguise — are taught as practical survival skills rather than exclusively martial disciplines. A Veppankarai farmer who can disappear into a bamboo forest when raiders approach is simply sensible, not sinister.

The arts in Veppankarai tend toward the practical and deceptive. Origami (folded paper art) doubles as coded message system. Tea ceremony includes knowledge of poisons and their antidotes. Garden design incorporates defensive architecture. Music training develops the acute hearing needed for surveillance. Even apparently peaceful crafts serve dual purposes.

### Religion

Veppankarai shares the layered religious system of all Ejiin lands but with darker undertones. Shamanism and elementalism are practiced as in Tansudo, but some clans have made pacts with Oni Lords — demonic entities serving Khan — in exchange for supernatural power. These dark pacts are Veppankarai's most dangerous secret, as Oni corruption threatens to consume the clans that embrace it.

Ashura is respected but followed less rigorously than in Tansudo. Veppankarai operatives argue that Ashura values results — protecting the innocent, defeating evil — more than methods. A ninja who assassinates a tyrant serves Ashura's purpose even if the method lacks samurai honor. This theological interpretation is rejected by Tansudo's warrior-monks but has genuine philosophical merit that even some Ashura priests acknowledge privately.

### Allies and Enemies

**Allies:** Various Tansudo daimyo (secretly hiring Veppankarai services), foreign merchants (who value Veppankarai intelligence networks), individual clans form temporary alliances as situations demand.

**Enemies:** Tansudo's government (officially considers Veppankarai a criminal organization), rival clans within the Syndicate itself, Oni-corrupted elements within their own ranks, and anyone who threatens clan interests.

**Threats:** Internal Oni corruption (some clans have embraced Khan's power and risk consuming the Syndicate from within), Tansudo military campaigns against Veppankarai-held islands, natural disasters, and the constant danger inherent in a society where assassination is a political tool.

## CULTURAL NOTES FOR CAMPAIGNS

The Lands of Honor offer campaign opportunities fundamentally different from the western regions of Nigal. The following elements define adventures in the Yashaki Islands:

**Bushido and Honor:** The code of Bushido governs all social interactions in Tansudo and influences even Veppankarai society. Player characters must navigate honor obligations, face consequences for dishonorable actions, and understand that reputation is as important as combat ability.

**The Samurai-Ninja Duality:** Tansudo's open honor and Veppankarai's shadow pragmatism create natural tension. Campaigns can explore both perspectives, forcing characters to choose between honor and effectiveness.

**Layered Religion:** The simultaneous practice of shamanism, elementalism, and Ashura/Khan worship creates rich spiritual landscape. Characters may serve multiple religious traditions without conflict — or discover that competing spiritual loyalties create impossible choices.

**Taint and Corruption:** Khan's Taint is a constant supernatural threat. Characters may encounter corrupted individuals, Oni infiltrators, or cursed locations where the boundary between the mortal world and Jigoku has thinned.

**Isolation and Contact:** The Lands of Honor's long isolation from western Nigal is ending. Western traders, missionaries, and adventurers bring new ideas, technologies, and threats. Characters may navigate cultural clashes between Ejiin traditions and western values.

**Appropriate Character Classes:** Samurai (paladin variants), ninja (rogue/monk variants), wu-jen (wizard variants with elemental focus), shugenja (cleric variants channeling elemental forces), monks (traditional and martial), and shamans (druid/cleric variants communicating with kami spirits).
