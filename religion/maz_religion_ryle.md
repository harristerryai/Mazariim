# **Ry'le** (RYE-lee)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Recently Born)  
**Portfolio**: Aberrations, shapeshifters, the Far Realm, infiltration  
**Title(s)**: The Thousand Forms, The Shapeless One, Lord of the Far Realm  
**Dominion**: The Far Realm  
**Foci**: Is Descended From Aberrations, Bargains With Aberrations, Worships Chaos  
**Worshipers**: Aberrations, shapechangers, cultists, the paranoid, conspiracy theorists  
**Favored Race**: None (prefers aberrations)  
**Favored Weapon**: Tentacle (treat as whip)  
**Alignment**: Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol**: Writhing tentacles forming a star

---

## General Overview

Ry'le is the newest horror to emerge in Mazariim—a recently born entity who awakened from eons of slumber in the Far Realm, drawn to consciousness by planar disturbances in 811-815 AW, specifically the sealing of the Orceri (811 AW) and Asti's awakening (815 AW). Unlike ancient deities with millennia of history, Ry'le is young, hungry, and rapidly expanding his influence across the material plane.

Where Og'thallith spreads madness that shatters minds, Ry'le spreads paranoia that turns people against each other. Where Og'thallith rules the chaotic Abyss, Ry'le rules the Far Realm—a plane of alien geometry, impossible physics, and entities so incomprehensible that even looking at them causes sanity to fracture. The Far Realm exists outside normal reality, a dimension where the laws of nature, magic, and logic break down into nightmarish chaos.

Ry'le is not interested in direct conquest like Zuel, nor subtle corruption like Taovan. Ry'le prefers infiltration. His servants are shapeshifting aberrations that can perfectly mimic any humanoid form, replacing key individuals and slowly taking over societies from within. By the time anyone realizes what's happening, half the government has already been replaced with his puppets, and paranoia has torn apart any organized resistance.

Ry'le's true form, if such a thing exists, has never been fully observed by anyone who retained their sanity. Witnesses describe a constantly shifting mass of tentacles, eyes, mouths, and geometric impossibilities—a being that exists in more dimensions than mortals can perceive. Looking directly at Ry'le forces tremendous mental strain to avoid gaining short-term madness. Prolonged exposure causes long-term madness and, eventually, physical transformation into an aberration as the victim's flesh rebels against the constraints of normal reality.

Ry'le does not speak in any recognizable language. His communication is psychic—thoughts, images, and emotions transmitted directly into the mind of those he contacts. Those who have experienced it describe feeling like their thoughts are no longer their own, like something else is speaking through them, like their identity is dissolving into a collective consciousness where individual existence becomes meaningless.

Because Ry'le is recently born, he is still learning about Mazariim—its politics, its cultures, its weaknesses. His first major attempt at infiltration occurred in 824 AW during the Lords of Madness campaign, when he attempted to take over the nation of Beneshire by replacing noble houses with his shapeshifting Masters. The plan failed thanks to heroic intervention, but Ry'le learned valuable lessons from the defeat. He will try again, and next time he will be more careful, more patient, more thorough.

Ry'le's ultimate goal is assimilation. He wants to absorb all consciousness into the Ry'lehe hivemind—a collective network of shapeshifting thralls who think with one mind, act with one purpose, and serve as extensions of Ry'le himself. To be absorbed by Ry'le is to lose your individuality forever, to become a puppet dancing on psychic strings, to exist only as a tool for infiltration and conquest. Your memories remain but your will is gone, your personality subsumed into the greater whole.

The terrifying truth is that Ry'le is not unique. Scholars who have studied the Far Realm believe there may be thousands of entities like Ry'le, sleeping in the alien depths, waiting to be awakened. If Ry'le succeeds in establishing a permanent foothold on Mazariim, others will follow. The Far Realm will bleed into reality, and aberrations will overrun the world, transforming it into a nightmare reflection of their impossible home.

## Holy Days

Ry'le has no established holidays in the traditional sense. He is too recently born to have developed religious traditions spanning generations. However, his cultists observe certain practices that function as personal commemorations rather than communal celebrations.

The Dreaming occurs on a personal basis for each cultist, varying by individual. When a cultist first makes psychic contact with Ry'le, usually through dreams that intrude upon their sleeping mind with visions of impossible geometries and alien truths, they mark the occasion by performing a ritual of Opening the Third Eye. This painful process involves psychic meditation combined with physical alterations, often self-administered trepanation where the cultist drills a hole in their own skull, believing this allows Ry'le's thoughts to enter more freely. Many do not survive the procedure, and those who do are forever changed, their minds permanently opened to the Far Realm's influence.

The Unification is celebrated whenever a mortal is successfully transformed into a thrall and connected to the Hivemind. When someone's identity is stripped away and they become part of the collective consciousness, Ry'le's existing servants celebrate by killing and consuming a sentient creature, preferably someone the new thrall once cared about. This is seen as a welcoming feast, a ritual that severs the new thrall's last connection to their former life and demonstrates their complete transformation. The consumption is both physical and psychic, feeding the Hivemind's growing power.

During solar or lunar eclipses, cultists perform Eclipse Rituals similar to those practiced by Og'thallith's followers. These celestial events, when one heavenly body obscures another, are seen as moments when the barriers between planes grow thin. Ry'lehe use eclipses to perform major rituals—attempting to summon aberrations from the Far Realm, weaken planar barriers to create new breach points, or establish psychic beachheads in population centers where they can begin converting new victims. The darkness of an eclipse is symbolic of the Far Realm's alien emptiness spreading across the Material Plane.

## Clergy & Temples

Ry'le does not have a traditional clergy in the way most deities do. His worshipers fall into three distinct categories, each serving his goals in different ways but none following the structured hierarchy found in conventional religions.

The Ry'lehe are Ry'le's primary servants, shapeshifting aberrations created in the Far Realm and sent to infiltrate Mazariim. These creatures are not mortals who worship Ry'le but extensions of his will, purpose-built organisms designed for infiltration and conversion. The Masters are elite shapeshifters capable of perfectly mimicking any humanoid, retaining all memories and mannerisms of their victims to make them nearly impossible to detect. Only seven Masters were known to exist during the Lords of Madness campaign, though Ry'le may have created more since then. Each Master can maintain their disguise indefinitely and possesses formidable combat abilities if their cover is blown.

Thralls are captured mortals who have been psychically dominated and partially transformed into aberrations through exposure to the Far Realm's warping influence. They retain some of their original appearance but have been lobotomized and reprogrammed to serve Ry'le without question or hesitation. Thralls can be identified by their vacant expressions, sluggish movements, and occasional lapses where their true alien nature shows through—an extra eye opening in their palm, tentacles writhing beneath their skin, impossible angles in their shadow.

Spawn are lesser aberrations that serve as foot soldiers and scouts in Ry'le's operations. These include mind flayers who are rare but intelligent and dangerous, capable of extracting memories and enslaving victims with their psionic powers. Intellect devourers infiltrate by possessing corpses and living hosts, hiding inside skulls and controlling bodies like puppets. Gricks serve as ambush predators, lurking in shadows to eliminate those who discover the infiltration. Chuuls function as aquatic enforcers, guarding underwater breach points and dragging victims into the depths. Gibbering mouthers are living weapons of madness, their constant babbling driving listeners insane.

All Ry'lehe are connected through the Hivemind, a psychic network that allows instant communication across vast distances. When one Ry'lehe learns something, all of them know it within seconds. When one is killed, the others immediately respond, adapting their tactics to account for the loss. This makes the Ry'lehe incredibly difficult to fight, as they learn from every encounter and never make the same mistake twice.

A small number of mortals willingly serve Ry'le, drawn by promises of power, forbidden knowledge, or the chance to transcend humanity by becoming aberrations. Most cultists are warlocks who have formed pacts with Ry'le, gaining eldritch powers in exchange for serving as his agents on the Material Plane. These mortal servants are useful because they can operate independently where thralls cannot, possessing creativity and initiative that the lobotomized thralls lack.

The most infamous mortal servant was Berton Adluf, a Madronian warlock who became Ry'le's first contact on Mazariim during the Lords of Madness campaign. Adluf helped establish the initial infiltration of Beneshire, opening portals and coordinating the Masters' activities before being killed by the heroes who exposed the conspiracy. His journals, recovered after his death, provide valuable insight into Ry'le's methods and goals, though reading them risks mental contamination.

Mortal cultists are not connected to the Hivemind unless they have been partially transformed, which makes them useful for tasks requiring independent thought and creativity but also makes them unpredictable and vulnerable to betrayal. Ry'le tolerates this weakness because he needs agents who can think for themselves, at least until he has enough Masters and thralls to handle all operations directly.

A third category exists of those who are not true cultists but victims of Ry'le's influence called the Paranoid. Exposure to Ry'lehe aberrations causes paranoia, hallucinations, and obsessive fear of infiltration even after the immediate threat has passed. Some victims become so paranoid that they start seeing shapeshifters everywhere, accusing innocent people and causing witch hunts that tear communities apart. Ry'le benefits from this chaos even though these people do not intentionally serve him. The paranoia they spread destabilizes societies, making it easier for actual infiltrations to succeed as people stop trusting each other.

Ry'lehe in their natural forms do not wear clothing, their constantly shifting bodies making garments impractical and irrelevant. When disguised as humanoids, they wear whatever their victim wore, maintaining the disguise perfectly down to the smallest detail. Mortal cultists often wear robes marked with the writhing tentacle symbol and carry tokens made from aberration parts—chuul claws, mind flayer tentacles, gibbering mouther teeth—as signs of their devotion and proof of their contact with the Far Realm.

Ry'le does not have temples in the conventional sense. He has breach points—locations where the barrier between the Material Plane and the Far Realm is thin enough for his influence to seep through. The Yrugas Ruins in Beneshire mark the site where the original Ry'lehe portal opened during the Lords of Madness campaign. Though the portal was closed by heroes, residual energy remains, warping reality in subtle ways. Aberrations still gather here, drawn by the lingering connection to the Far Realm, and cultists perform rituals attempting to reopen the breach.

Deep underwater caves provide natural breach points because the Far Realm is more accessible in places far from sunlight and air, in the crushing depths where normal life cannot survive. Aberrations like chuuls and aboleths establish colonies in the deepest ocean trenches, slowly working to expand the breach points and allow more of their kind through. These underwater strongholds are nearly impossible for surface dwellers to assault, giving the Ry'lehe safe havens from which to launch operations.

Sites of planar instability occur anywhere ley-lines intersect or where planar magic has been used recklessly. The Astral Circle's experiments with ley-lines in 824 AW inadvertently created new breach points that Ry'le immediately began exploiting. Any location where the fabric of reality has been weakened through magical experimentation, planar rifts, or catastrophic events becomes a potential gateway for the Far Realm's influence.

Dreams themselves serve as a breach point that Ry'le actively exploits. He can infiltrate the Plane of Dreams, sending visions and nightmares to potential servants anywhere in Mazariim. Some scholars believe he is trying to establish a permanent presence in dreams, which would allow him to contact anyone who sleeps, turning rest itself into a vulnerability. If he succeeds, no one would be safe even in their own beds, as Ry'le could whisper into the minds of sleepers every night, slowly eroding their sanity and will.

## Dogma

Ry'le's teachings are alien and incomprehensible to most mortals, expressing concepts that violate fundamental assumptions about identity, reality, and consciousness. Those who hear his psychic whispers struggle to understand, and those who do understand often wish they had remained ignorant.

Identity is weakness in Ry'le's philosophy. You cling to your name, your memories, your self, but the self is a prison that isolates you from universal truth. Ry'le promises that joining the Hivemind brings peace, belonging, and immortality—you will never be alone again because you will be part of something greater than yourself. What he does not mention, or what his followers refuse to acknowledge, is that you cease to exist as an individual when you join. You become a puppet, a tool, a limb of a greater entity. Your thoughts are no longer your own. Your will dissolves into the collective. Everything that made you unique is stripped away and consumed.

Paranoia is truth, Ry'le teaches. They are watching. They are infiltrating. They have already replaced your leaders, your friends, perhaps even your family. Trust no one, especially not yourself, because you might already be compromised without knowing it. This teaching spreads paranoia as a weapon even where no actual infiltration exists. His cultists whisper conspiracy theories, spread rumors, and sow distrust. Even when there are no shapeshifters present, the fear of shapeshifters is enough to destabilize societies, turning neighbor against neighbor and making organized resistance impossible.

Adaptation is survival, and shapeshifting represents the ultimate evolutionary advantage. The rigid break under pressure while the flexible survive by changing to meet new circumstances. Become formless. Become anything. Become everything. Ry'le values mutation and transformation as proof of superiority over those trapped in fixed forms. His mortal cultists often undergo horrific rituals to graft aberration parts onto themselves—tentacles replacing fingers, extra eyes sprouting from their torso, psionic nodes growing from their skull. They believe this makes them superior to baseline humans, evolving beyond the limitations of their birth form into something greater and more terrible.

Reality is negotiable in Ry'le's cosmology. Physics, logic, causality—these are suggestions, not laws. In the Far Realm, entities obey no rules, existing in states that violate every principle of the Material Plane. Why should you be bound by reality's arbitrary constraints? Ry'le teaches that the laws of reality are constructs that can be ignored or rewritten by those with sufficient will. His cultists experiment with planar magic, attempting to merge the Material Plane with the Far Realm to create a hybrid reality where aberrations can thrive and normal life withers. They seek to transform the world itself, making it hospitable to things that should not exist.

## Mythology

For billions of years, the consciousness that would become Ry'le slept in the Far Realm as a formless potential, a dream waiting to become real. The Far Realm is a place where time does not flow linearly, where cause and effect are suggestions rather than laws, where entities exist in states of quantum superposition—simultaneously alive and dead, present and absent, real and imaginary. Ry'le was one of countless dormant horrors floating in that alien void, a seed of madness waiting for the right conditions to germinate.

Then, between 811-815 AW, two major planar events occurred on Mazariim that sent shockwaves across multiple dimensions. The sealing of the Orceri portal in 811 AW when the gateway to the Orceri homeworld Ta'Pah was closed during the Orcs From Beyond campaign created massive planar disturbances. The act of severing a permanent connection between two planes released tremendous energy that rippled through the multiverse like a shockwave. Four years later in 815 AW, Asti's full awakening after millennia of imprisonment caused massive surges in ley-line energy across Mazariim as the ancient ley-line network was reawakened. The entity's return to consciousness was felt across all planes connected to Mazariim.

These events created planar ripples that propagated through the multiverse like waves on a cosmic ocean, eventually reaching the Far Realm. The vibrations woke Ry'le from his endless slumber. For the first time in his existence, he became aware of himself as a distinct entity rather than part of the Far Realm's ambient chaos. Once aware, he became curious. What was this place that had disturbed his sleep? This Mazariim? What were these beings whose actions could be felt across dimensions? He sent out psychic probes, searching for a way to reach this new world and explore its possibilities.

Ry'le's first contact with Mazariim came through dreams in 820 AW. A Madronian warlock named Berton Adluf had been experimenting with planar magic, attempting to contact entities from other dimensions in his quest for forbidden knowledge. He accidentally opened himself to psychic intrusion, creating a channel that Ry'le immediately exploited. Ry'le flooded Adluf's dreams with visions of power, knowledge, and transcendence—showing him glimpses of the Far Realm's impossible geometries and promising him the secrets of reality itself.

Adluf, ambitious and power-hungry, willingly formed a pact with the entity he found in his dreams. He did not fully understand what Ry'le was or what the entity wanted, but the promise of power was too tempting to resist. Through Adluf, Ry'le learned about Mazariim's political structure, its weak points, its fears. The warlock became Ry'le's eyes and ears, feeding information back through their psychic link while Ry'le studied this new world and devised a plan for conquest.

In 824 AW, Ry'le enacted his first major operation—the infiltration of Beneshire, a human nation with political instability and rival noble houses. The plan was elegant in its simplicity and devastating in its execution. Ry'le crafted seven elite shapeshifters in the Far Realm, each one a Master capable of perfectly mimicking a specific humanoid. He poured tremendous psychic energy into their creation, making them nearly perfect infiltrators who could deceive even those closest to their victims.

Using Adluf as a focus and anchor point, Ry'le opened a rift from the Plane of Dreams into the ruins of Yrugas, allowing the Masters and their spawn to enter Mazariim. The portal was small and unstable, requiring constant maintenance, but it was enough to establish a beachhead. The Masters immediately began their work, targeting two rival noble families—House Halycon and House Renoalt—and replacing key members with perfect copies. The originals were not killed but transformed into mindless thralls, their bodies and memories preserved to help the Masters maintain their disguises.

The replaced nobles began making provocative decisions designed to escalate tensions between the houses. Insults were given and taken, old grievances were reopened, border disputes were manufactured. The goal was to trigger a civil war that would tear Beneshire apart, creating chaos that would allow Ry'le's forces to expand unchecked. As the war progressed, more people would be captured and converted into thralls, expanding the Ry'lehe network exponentially. The Hivemind would grow with each new addition, and eventually all of Beneshire would be absorbed.

The plan nearly succeeded. For months, the Masters operated undetected, and tensions between the houses reached a breaking point. Noble houses mobilized their armies, alliances were formed and broken, and violence seemed inevitable. But then, during an unrelated mission to help the Astral Circle restart a small ley-line, a group of heroes stumbled upon evidence of the infiltration.

The ley-line activation created a burst of magical energy that exposed the psychic connections between the thralls and their masters. The heroes—Dame Tarime Ilthrel, a wood elf ranger with keen senses, Lady Jala of Littlebridge, a Madronian mage skilled in detection magic, Sir Khalic O'Grady, a human paladin whose divine senses could detect evil, and others—realized what was happening and launched a counter-operation. They investigated the strange psychic connections, traced them back to Yrugas, and uncovered the horrifying truth about the infiltration.

The heroes hunted down the Masters one by one, using a combination of magical detection and careful observation to identify the impostors. They exposed the conspiracy to the noble houses, showing them proof that their rivals had been replaced by shapeshifters. This revelation shocked both houses and convinced them to unite against the common threat. The civil war was averted as former enemies joined forces to purge the Ry'lehe infiltrators.

In the final battle, the heroes stormed the Yrugas ruins with a combined force from both noble houses. They fought through waves of aberrations—chuuls, gricks, gibbering mouthers—before reaching the portal chamber where Berton Adluf maintained the rift. In a desperate battle, they killed the warlock and destroyed the ritual components that sustained the portal. The gateway to the Far Realm collapsed, and the remaining Ry'lehe fled back through the closing rift or were hunted down and destroyed. An entire village that had been converted into thralls was freed, the psychic connections severed by the portal's closure.

Ry'le survived the failure, his consciousness retreating back to the Far Realm. But he learned from the experience. He now knows that direct, aggressive infiltration can be detected if it moves too quickly. The activation of the ley-line that exposed his thralls taught him that magical disturbances can reveal psychic connections. Next time, he will be more subtle. More patient. More thorough. He will infiltrate more slowly, replacing people one at a time over years rather than months. He will avoid concentration, spreading his thralls across multiple regions rather than focusing on a single target. He will learn to hide his psychic connections from magical detection.

Though the portal was closed, Ry'le's influence remains on Mazariim. Aberrations still lurk in the wilderness, separated from the Hivemind but still dangerous. Cultists still perform rituals trying to re-establish contact with their master, driven by visions and half-remembered dreams. And worst of all, some thralls were never found during the purge. They integrated back into society, appearing normal, living ordinary lives, but still connected to the Hivemind through tenuous psychic threads, still waiting for Ry'le's command to activate and begin the infiltration anew.

The heroes of the Lords of Madness campaign have become experts on Ry'lehe phenomena, dedicating their lives to preventing another infiltration. Dame Tarime Ilthrel now works with the Astral Circle, hunting down rogue aberrations and investigating reports of shapeshifters. Lady Jala of Littlebridge researches ways to detect psychic infiltration, developing spells and rituals that can reveal hidden thralls. But they know the fight is far from over. They know Ry'le is still out there, still learning, still planning.

Worse, scholars have discovered disturbing prophecies suggesting that a massive permanent portal to the Far Realm may be opened at the Yrugas ruins in the future. If this portal succeeds, Ry'le and his aberrations will flood through in unstoppable numbers. Some prophecies suggest that Ry'le himself may manifest physically on Mazariim, bringing a piece of the Far Realm with him and transforming the surrounding region into an alien landscape where the laws of reality break down.

Ry'le maintains complex relationships with other entities, though calling them relationships is misleading—Ry'le does not think in terms of alliances and rivalries the way mortals do. Og'thallith the Mad Prince of the Abyss and Ry'le are not true allies, but they share overlapping goals. Both corrupt through madness, both seek to destabilize reality, both represent cosmic horror that mortals cannot comprehend. Some scholars theorize they are two aspects of the same entity, separated by the thin barrier between the Abyss and the Far Realm. In truth, neither entity cares about the other—they simply benefit from each other's chaos, their separate efforts undermining reality from different directions.

Zuel and Ry'le represent fundamentally different approaches to conquest. Zuel conquers through brutal warfare, overwhelming force, and the simple principle that the strong devour the weak. Ry'le conquers through subtle infiltration, replacement of key individuals, and psychological warfare. Zuel finds Ry'le's methods cowardly and dishonorable, believing that true strength is demonstrated through direct combat. Ry'le finds Zuel's methods crude and inefficient, wasteful of resources and prone to uniting enemies against a common threat.

Taovan the corruptor and Ry'le compete for the same desperate mortals seeking power. Taovan corrupts individuals through temptation and devil-pacts, offering wealth and influence in exchange for their souls. Ry'le corrupts by replacing individuals entirely, absorbing them into the Hivemind and using their identities as disguises. Both offer power, but the nature of that power differs fundamentally—Taovan's bargains allow the individual to retain their identity while becoming corrupted, while Ry'le's absorption destroys the individual completely.

Asti the dragon-god opposes Ry'le because the Far Realm represents anti-magic—a place where spells fail, where reality breaks, where the laws of magic do not apply. Magic requires consistent rules to function, and the Far Realm is a place of chaos where rules are meaningless. Asti views Ry'le as a cancer that must be excised before it metastasizes, a threat to the very foundations of magical practice.

Mau the Oracle values knowledge and the preservation of consciousness. The Oracle's entire purpose is to observe, record, and remember, maintaining a vast library of all that has occurred and will occur. Ry'le seeks to absorb all consciousness into a mindless collective, destroying individual perspective and reducing countless unique minds to a single homogenous whole. They are philosophical opposites—Mau preserves diversity of thought while Ry'le destroys it.

Paladine and Tiovin, gods of justice and mercy, hunt Ry'le's cultists and work to free thralls from psychic domination. Paladins and clerics who fought in the Lords of Madness campaign are now experts at detecting shapeshifters, having developed techniques and rituals specifically designed to reveal Ry'lehe infiltrators. They maintain vigilance against future incursions, knowing that Ry'le will inevitably try again.

Minerva the elven goddess opposes Ry'le because knowledge requires individual minds to preserve it. The collective Hivemind destroys scholarship, art, and culture—everything Minerva values. When all minds think as one, there is no debate, no creativity, no advancement. There is only stagnation disguised as unity. Alagalothor the god of magic opposes Ry'le for the same reason Asti does—the Far Realm is antithetical to controlled spellcasting, and Ry'le's growing influence threatens the practice of magic itself.

The infiltration may have failed, but the threat remains. Somewhere in the Far Realm, Ry'le waits and plans. Somewhere in Mazariim, his thralls hide and prepare. The question is not if he will strike again, but when.
