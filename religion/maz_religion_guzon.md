# Guzon

**Power Level:** Greater Entity (Native)  
**Portfolio:** Enlightenment, meditation, discipline, inner peace, cosmic law  
**Title:** The Serene One, Master of the Still Mind  
**Dominion:** Utopia (The Garden of Perfect Contemplation)  
**Foci:** Worships Knowledge, Worships Order, Worships Pacifism  
**Worshipers:** Monks, philosophers, scholars, pacifists, gnomes  
**Favored Race:** Gnome  
**Favored Weapon:** Unarmed strike  
**Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** Lotus flower in perfect symmetry

---


## Origin

**Year of Awakening:** ~3,200 BW  
**Era:** Era of Awakening

Guzon awakened among early human and gnome communities seeking enlightenment and inner peace during the Era of Awakening (~3,200 BW). As the first philosophers, monks, and contemplatives developed meditation practices and pursued self-perfection, their collective consciousness of discipline and order coalesced into divine form. Guzon created **Utopia**, a great city of eternal perfection served by Inevitables. Later shares this plane with Mordrion (arrived 1,866 BW) and Gyru (ascended 1,111 BW).

---


## General Overview

Guzon is enlightenment through discipline—the belief that understanding the fundamental truths of existence requires quieting the mind, mastering the self, and perceiving reality without distortion. Where Minerva teaches knowledge through study and Mordrion teaches order through law, Guzon teaches wisdom through meditation—direct perception of truth unburdened by ego or emotion.

Guzon appears as an ageless gnome seated in eternal meditation, eyes closed, radiating perfect calm. Those who perceive him describe feeling their thoughts slow, their anxieties quiet, and a profound sense that everything is exactly as it should be. He rarely speaks; when he does, his words are simple yet carry layers of meaning.

Guzon's domain in Utopia, the plane of perfect law, is the **Garden of Perfect Contemplation**—an infinite space of geometric precision where every stone is placed exactly right, every plant grows in mathematical perfection, and time itself seems optional. Here, the **Inevitables**, constructs enforcing cosmic law, serve Guzon, and philosophers from across the multiverse seek enlightenment.

Guzon created the Inevitables to enforce fundamental laws—not mortal laws, which is Mordrion's domain, but cosmic laws: cause and effect, entropy and order, death following life. The Inevitables are neither good nor evil; they are necessity made manifest. They correct cosmic imbalances, hunt those who cheat death unjustly, and maintain reality's structure.

## Holy Days

The **Day of Stillness** occurs on the spring equinox, when followers observe twenty-four hours of absolute silence and meditation. The **Awakening** takes place on the summer solstice to celebrate achieving enlightenment insights, with monks sharing their breakthrough moments and guiding others toward similar revelations. The **Discipline Trial** falls on the autumn equinox as a test of endurance and self-control, where monks undergo physical and mental challenges to prove their mastery over body and mind. The **Long Night** marks the winter solstice, when monks meditate from dusk to dawn without sleep, demonstrating their ability to transcend physical limitations and maintain perfect focus through the darkest hours.

## Clergy & Temples

The **Still Brothers and Sisters** are monks who have achieved inner peace through meditation and serve as Guzon's clergy. The hierarchy begins with the **Silent Master**, the highest rank who speaks only once per year. Currently this position is held by Master Korin Whisperstone, a gnome and fifteenth-level monk whose rare pronouncements are studied for years afterward. Below them serve the **Contemplatives**, senior monks of tenth level or higher who guide others toward enlightenment and maintain the philosophical teachings of the faith. The **Still Brothers and Sisters** proper are full monks of fifth level or higher who maintain temples and teach meditation to students and visitors. **Seekers** represent students at first through fifth level who are learning discipline and meditation, following rigorous training regimens. **Aspirants** are initiates testing their commitment to the path, spending their first months in complete silence to learn if they can endure the discipline required.

Guzon's temples are called **Stillness Halls**—sparse structures of stone and wood with meditation gardens, simple sleeping quarters, and training halls. No decoration, no excess. The architecture emphasizes empty space, clean lines, and perfect symmetry. Each hall contains meditation chambers where monks can retreat for days or weeks of uninterrupted contemplation. Training halls allow monks to practice their martial arts in disciplined silence, perfecting forms that embody both combat skill and meditative focus. The Grand Temple in Utopia is a single perfect stone room containing nothing but space for contemplation, where the most advanced monks journey to experience pure meditation untainted by material distraction.

## Dogma

Guzon's core teaching proclaims that to understand the universe, first understand yourself, and to master reality, first master your mind. The **Seven Steps to Enlightenment** form the foundation of his philosophy. The first step is **Discipline**, learning to control body and mind through rigorous training and meditation. The second step is **Detachment**, releasing attachment to outcomes and accepting that all things are transient. The third step, **Perception**, teaches one to see reality without filters, biases, or preconceptions clouding truth. **Understanding**, the fourth step, brings comprehension of cosmic law and the fundamental patterns that govern existence. The fifth step is **Acceptance**, meaning embracing what is rather than fighting against the nature of reality. **Transcendence**, the sixth step, moves one beyond ego and the illusion of separate self. Finally, **Enlightenment** itself represents direct perception of truth, where one sees reality as it truly is without any mental distortion.

Guzon teaches absolute non-violence when possible. His monks train in martial arts for self-defense only—never to kill. The goal is to defeat enemies without harming them, or better, to avoid conflict entirely through wisdom. A true master of Guzon's teachings can walk through a battlefield and emerge untouched, not through combat but through perfect awareness and positioning. Monks learn to redirect attacks, use minimal force, and seek diplomatic solutions before resorting to physical intervention. Those who kill, even in self-defense, are considered to have failed in their discipline and must undergo extensive meditation and penance to regain spiritual balance.

## Mythology

Guzon emerged approximately 5,000 BW during an era when gnomish civilization was flourishing across Mazariim. Unlike Old Gods who arose from elemental forces or entities who arrived from other universes, Guzon manifested from the collective gnomish pursuit of philosophical understanding. Gnome scholars and thinkers gathered in the first great libraries, debating the nature of reality, consciousness, and existence. From countless hours of meditation, philosophical discourse, and the gnomish drive to understand fundamental truths, a consciousness began to form—something born from pure contemplation itself.

When Guzon first awakened, he did not announce himself with miracles or demand worship. Instead, he simply appeared in meditation halls across gnome cities, sitting in perfect stillness. Those who meditated near him felt their minds clarify, their understanding deepen, their questions begin to answer themselves. Word spread slowly through gnomish communities that something profound had emerged—not a god demanding tribute, but a teacher offering wisdom to those patient enough to listen.

Guzon established his domain in Utopia, drawn to that plane's perfect mathematical order and lawful nature. There he created the **Garden of Perfect Contemplation**, a space where reality itself operates according to pure logical principles. Here, Guzon meditated on the fundamental laws governing existence: cause and effect, entropy and negentropy, the balance between chaos and order, the relationship between consciousness and reality.

From these meditations, Guzon birthed the **Inevitables**—constructs designed to enforce cosmic law. These beings are not servants in the traditional sense but rather extensions of Guzon's understanding that certain rules cannot be broken without consequences. The **Maruts** hunt those who cheat death unjustly, maintaining the fundamental law that mortality exists for a reason. The **Kolyaruts** enforce contracts and oaths, upholding the principle that words have power and meaning. The **Zelekhuts** pursue fugitives from cosmic justice, ensuring that actions have consequences that cannot be escaped. The Inevitables serve Guzon but act independently when cosmic law is violated, following logical imperatives rather than emotional judgments.

Guzon's worship spread among gnomes first, as many saw in him the culmination of their philosophical traditions. Monasteries arose where scholars could study, meditate, and seek enlightenment. Unlike other faiths that demanded specific rituals or offerings, Guzon's path required only dedication to understanding oneself and reality. This appealed to gnomes' intellectual nature while also attracting humans, elves, and other races seeking wisdom beyond traditional religious structures.

When the dwarf Gyru attempted to achieve godhood through a mechanical device around 3,000 BW, Guzon's avatar intervened. The device worked—it absorbed massive amounts of magical energy and began transforming Gyru into an entity. But Alagalothor the Dragon Mage destroyed the machine before the transformation completed. Guzon, recognizing that Gyru's motivation was not power-hunger but genuine desire for understanding, transported the partially-transformed dwarf to Utopia. There, Gyru spent centuries in meditation under Guzon's guidance, eventually achieving enlightenment through discipline rather than machinery. Gyru later became a lesser entity in his own right, dwelling in Utopia alongside Guzon and focusing on invention and artifice, but he never forgot that true power comes from within.

Guzon did not participate in the War to End All Wars. When asked by both sides to support their cause, he simply stated that war is the absence of understanding, and those who wage it have failed the first step toward enlightenment. His monks maintained neutrality throughout the conflict, offering sanctuary to refugees from both sides and providing medical care without judgment. Some criticized this as cowardice or moral failure, but Guzon's followers maintained that taking sides in a war of egos would betray everything their path represents.

In the modern era, Guzon's influence extends beyond his relatively small number of clergy. His teachings on meditation and inner peace have influenced countless philosophical schools across Mazariim. Rulers consult with Still Brothers when facing difficult decisions, seeking the clarity that comes from detached analysis. Warriors recovering from trauma seek healing in Stillness Halls, learning to quiet minds haunted by violence. Scholars of all faiths study Guzon's philosophical writings, finding in them insights that complement their own traditions.

Guzon maintains respectful relationships with other knowledge-focused entities. Minerva and Guzon share mutual respect—she represents external knowledge while he represents internal wisdom, and both recognize these as complementary paths. Mordrion's emphasis on law and structure aligns with Guzon's cosmic order, though Mordrion focuses on societal law while Guzon contemplates universal principles. Gyru, once Guzon's student, now serves as a peer entity dwelling in Utopia, their relationship demonstrating that enlightenment can be achieved through multiple paths.

Some find Guzon's philosophy frustrating or even dangerous. Critics argue that absolute pacifism enables evil by refusing to stop aggressors. Others claim that detachment from outcomes makes his followers useless in crises requiring passionate commitment. Guzon's response to such criticism is typically silence—those who understand need no explanation, and those who don't understand won't be convinced by words. His followers provide counsel to rulers seeking wisdom, meditation training for warriors dealing with stress and trauma, monastery refuges for those seeking peace away from worldly concerns, and diplomatic mediation from a perspective of complete neutrality and pacifism.

The **Inevitables** continue their eternal work, maintaining cosmic balance regardless of mortal opinion. A Marut might hunt a beloved king who used dark magic to extend his life unjustly, or a Kolyarut might enforce a contract that seems morally wrong but was freely entered. These actions remind mortals that cosmic law operates on principles beyond good and evil, that the universe has structure and rules that transcend individual desires. Guzon created them knowing they would be feared and sometimes hated, but understanding that reality needs enforcement mechanisms or chaos would consume all.

Guzon remains in eternal meditation, speaking once per year through his Silent Master, offering cryptic pronouncements that philosophers debate for months. He watches as civilizations rise and fall, as mortals struggle with existence, as reality continues its vast operation. And in his perfect stillness, he understands that everything—even suffering, even chaos, even the endless cycle of confusion and enlightenment—is exactly as it should be.
