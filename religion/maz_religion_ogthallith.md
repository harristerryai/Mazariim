# Og'thallith

- Power Level: Greater Entity
- Portfolio: Madness, demons, corruption, the Abyss
- Title(s): The Mad Prince, Lord of the Infinite Layers, The Whisperer in Darkness
- Dominion: The Abyss (Founder and Primary Ruler)
- Foci: Bargains With Fiends, Worships Chaos, Worships Destruction
- Worshipers: Cultists, demons, the insane, nihilists, those seeking forbidden power
- Favored Race: None (despises all mortals equally)
- Favored Weapon: Flail
- Alignment: Chaotic Evil
- Symbol: Twisted spiral descending into darkness

---


## Origin

**Year of Awakening:** ~18,000 BW  
**Era:** Late Timeless Era

Og'thallith awakened in the later Timeless Era (~18,000 BW) as mortal civilizations began experimenting with dangerous magics and forbidden knowledge. Born from the collective consciousness of madness, insanity, and corrupted minds seeking power beyond comprehension, this entity feeds on the psychological fractures of sentient minds. Unlike the elemental Old Gods who formed from natural forces, Og'thallith emerged from sapient suffering. The entity merged with the **Abyss**, establishing a realm of gibbering chaos and demonic corruption.

---


## General Overview

Og'thallith is madness given divine form—the embodiment of entropy, chaos, and the slow unraveling of sanity. He is not merely evil; he is the antithesis of order, reason, and reality itself. Where other deities rule their domains, Og'thallith infects his. Where others build, he unmakes. Where others inspire devotion, he drives his followers insane.

He is the Founder of the Abyss, the infinite plane of chaotic evil that exists as a wound in the fabric of reality. The Abyss is not a place he created through intentional design—it is a byproduct of his existence, a cosmic scar that formed when his consciousness first coalesced in the primordial darkness between planes. The Abyss is Og'thallith's realm, his prison, and his playground—a plane of infinite layers, each more twisted and nightmarish than the last, where traditional demons serve as his legion of corruption.

Unlike Taovan (who corrupts through temptation and devil-pacts) or Zuel (who destroys through conquest and war), Og'thallith corrupts through madness. His whispers erode the mind. His visions shatter sanity. His influence turns rational beings into gibbering wrecks who no longer understand the difference between dream and reality. Those who worship him do not do so out of ambition or greed—they worship because they have lost the capacity for rational thought.

Og'thallith's form (when he manifests, which is blessedly rare) is impossible to fully perceive. Witnesses describe different things: a towering figure made of living shadows with too many limbs, a swirling vortex of screaming faces, a geometric impossibility that hurts to look at, a whisper that sounds like laughter and sobbing at once. No two accounts agree, because Og'thallith exists partially outside normal reality, and mortal minds cannot process what they see. Those who look directly at him risk gaining permanent madness.

His voice is worse. It does not sound like speech—it sounds like the absence of speech, like the silence between thoughts, like the moment before you realize you've forgotten something important. It bypasses ears entirely and manifests directly in the mind. Og'thallith does not argue or persuade. He simply suggests, and those suggestions burrow into the subconscious like parasites.

Og'thallith's ultimate goal is simple and terrifying: the complete dissolution of reality. He does not want to rule the cosmos—he wants to unmake it. He believes that existence itself is a mistake, that consciousness is suffering, and that true peace can only come through the annihilation of all things. His cultists work tirelessly to weaken the barriers between planes, to summon demons, to spread madness, to erode the foundations of reality layer by layer until everything collapses into the Abyss.

Fortunately, Og'thallith is not omnipotent. His power is vast but constrained—he is bound to the Abyss, unable to manifest on the Material Plane except through avatars, summoned demons, or the actions of his insane cultists. The cosmic laws that govern the multiverse prevent him from directly unmaking reality. But he is patient. Infinite, unending patience. He has eternity to whisper, to corrupt, to erode. And one day, he believes, the final wall will crumble.

## Holy Days

Og'thallith's cultists do not have holidays in any traditional sense, as their worship is illegal and hidden. However, certain events trigger gatherings and acts of terrible devotion. **The Night of Unraveling** occurs during lunar eclipses when the barrier between planes is thin. Og'thallith's cultists gather in secret to perform rituals designed to summon minor demons or induce mass hallucinations. These gatherings often end in violence as cultists turn on each other or descend into gibbering madness. The observances include secret gatherings in abandoned places, self-mutilation, speaking in tongues, attempting to summon demons, and mass hallucinations that sometimes spill out into nearby communities.

**The Spiral Awakening** takes place on the first of Zebul (December 1), marking the anniversary of the Spiral Plague that occurred in 4,200 BW. Cultists believe this day is especially auspicious for spreading madness. They distribute cursed items to unsuspecting victims, poison water supplies with hallucinogens, or perform rituals to weaken the barrier between the Material Plane and the Abyss. Acts of terrorism, distribution of cursed items, poisoning, arson, and random violence characterize this dark celebration. Authorities in major cities increase patrols around this date, having learned from past tragedies.

**The Whisper** is not a public holiday but a personal milestone that occurs when an individual first hears Og'thallith's voice. When someone first hears Og'thallith's voice in their mind—whether through direct contact or gradual descent into madness—they often mark the occasion by performing a violent act or self-harm. This is seen as their "true awakening," the moment they shed the illusion of sanity and embrace what they believe is enlightenment. Personal acts of violence or self-mutilation, carving spirals into flesh or walls, and abandoning their previous life mark this terrible transformation.

## Clergy & Temples

Og'thallith's clergy are not organized in any traditional sense. There is no hierarchy, no training, no ordination. His followers are broken minds who have been touched by his madness and can no longer function in normal society. They do not worship out of devotion—they worship because they cannot stop.

That said, certain patterns emerge among the mad. The most powerful cultist in a region is called the Whisperer, usually the first person to be directly contacted by Og'thallith. Whisperers are often high-level warlocks, clerics, or sorcerers (10th level and above) who have bargained with Og'thallith or been driven insane by direct contact. They receive visions, hear the Mad Prince's voice, and translate his incomprehensible desires into actionable plans. Whisperers rarely live longer than a year—the strain of channeling Og'thallith's will eventually destroys their minds completely.

Harbingers are mid-level cultists (5th through 10th level) who lead smaller cells and perform rituals. They are recognizable by their erratic behavior, self-mutilation, and tendency to speak in riddles or prophecies. Harbingers are often former scholars, mages, or priests who delved too deep into forbidden knowledge and could not escape. Their descent was gradual, beginning with curiosity and ending in complete corruption.

The Broken are low-level cultists (1st through 5th level) who are in the early stages of madness. They may still appear functional in society, holding jobs and maintaining relationships, but they secretly serve Og'thallith by spreading chaos, performing minor rituals, and recruiting others. Many Broken do not even realize they serve a deity—they believe the voices are their own thoughts, that their increasingly dark impulses are personal choices rather than external corruption.

The Lost have descended so far into madness that they are no longer recognizable as people. The Lost wander the wilderness or hide in dungeons, attacking anyone they encounter. They are technically cultists, but they lack the cognitive function to perform rituals or follow commands. They are simply weapons Og'thallith discards when they break, dangerous remnants that continue to spread violence and fear long after their minds are gone.

Og'thallith's cultists wear whatever they were wearing when madness took them, usually filthy and torn. Some decorate themselves with spirals carved into their flesh, paint themselves with blood or ash, or wear masks made from the faces of their victims. There is no uniform—only the shared mark of insanity.

Og'thallith has no temples in the traditional sense. His worship occurs in abandoned asylums where the walls still echo with screams, in deep caves where reality is thin and whispers seep through, in ruined buildings in forgotten corners of cities marked with spirals and incomprehensible sigils, and at sites of great tragedies such as battlefields, mass graves, and places where sanity shattered en masse.

The most infamous "temple" is the Screaming Tower, a ruined asylum in the Blighted Wastes where Og'thallith's influence is so strong that merely entering the building forces constant mental assaults against madness. Adventurers who have survived the Tower describe walls that shift when not directly observed, corridors that loop impossibly, and doors that open into places that should not exist. Many who enter seeking to destroy the cult presence there never emerge. Those who do are forever changed.

Another horrific site is the Spiral, a massive geological formation in the Northern Wastes that resembles a spiral descending into the earth. Scholars believe it formed naturally over millennia, but locals claim it appeared overnight 200 years ago. Those who descend into the Spiral often do not return. Those who do return are changed in fundamental ways—they speak of things that should not be, see connections in random events, and slowly descend into the same madness that claimed those who never returned.

## Dogma

Og'thallith's teachings are not coherent philosophy—they are infectious ideas that spread like disease. The first teaching holds that reality is a lie, whispering that what mortals call truth is merely consensus and what they call sanity is merely limitation. The doctrine promises that breaking free from these constraints allows one to see what lies beneath the surface of perceived reality. Og'thallith teaches that the reality mortals perceive is an illusion, a prison constructed by the so-called "good" gods to keep mortals docile. He promises that madness is enlightenment, that insanity allows one to perceive the true nature of existence. His followers believe they are the only sane ones, and that everyone else is trapped in delusion.

The doctrine teaches to embrace the void, suggesting that mortals fear the darkness because they cling to light, but the darkness was here first and it will be here last, making resistance to the inevitable futile. Og'thallith teaches nihilism in its purest form. Life is suffering. Consciousness is pain. The only true peace is oblivion. His cultists do not fear death—they seek it, and they seek to bring it to others as an act of mercy, believing they are freeing people from the torment of existence.

Followers are instructed to destroy in order to create, or rather to destroy without the necessity of creation following. The whispered teaching declares that the old must be torn down before the new can rise, and if nothing rises after, that proves existence was never necessary in the first place. Og'thallith's followers are anarchists and terrorists. They burn libraries to destroy accumulated knowledge, sabotage infrastructure to weaken civilization's foundations, assassinate leaders to create power vacuums and chaos, and sow discord wherever they go. They believe that civilization is a house of cards waiting to collapse, and they are eager to pull the final card.

The teaching declares that the whispers are truth, commanding followers not to ignore the voices but to recognize them not as madness but as revelation. Og'thallith teaches that the voices in one's head, the hallucinations, the paranoia—these are not symptoms of illness but divine communication. His cultists follow every stray thought, every irrational impulse, believing it to be the Mad Prince guiding them toward some incomprehensible purpose. This teaching is perhaps the most dangerous, as it turns the symptoms of mental corruption into validation and encouragement, creating a feedback loop that accelerates the descent into complete madness.

## Mythology

Og'thallith's origin is unknown even to the oldest entities like Mau the Oracle. He was not awakened like the Old Gods, nor did he arrive from another universe like the immigrant deities. Most scholars believe he always existed, lurking in the spaces between planes, a fundamental force of entropy and chaos that predates the current multiverse. Some theorize he is not an entity at all but rather a phenomenon—the inevitable heat death of consciousness, given form and will through the collective fear of all thinking beings.

The Abyss formed when Og'thallith's consciousness first coalesced in unknown prehistory. Reality could not contain him, and so it tore—a wound opened between planes, and through that wound poured infinite layers of chaos. The Abyss is not a place Og'thallith consciously designed; it is a reflection of his nature, a plane where logic does not apply, where physical laws are suggestions, where sanity goes to die. Each layer of the Abyss is different and equally horrific—some are endless wastelands of screaming wind, others are forests of flesh and bone, still others are impossible geometric constructions that hurt to perceive.

Demons—traditional demons, not devils—were born in the Abyss. They are not creations of Og'thallith, but rather byproducts of his presence, embodiments of chaotic evil that emerged spontaneously from the primordial chaos. Some demons serve Og'thallith willingly, acting as his agents in spreading madness and corruption. Others ignore him entirely, pursuing their own chaotic goals. A few even oppose him, recognizing that his ultimate goal of unmaking reality would destroy them as well. But all are tied to the Abyss, and thus tied to him whether they acknowledge it or not.

The first mortals to contact Og'thallith were likely primitive shamans around 10,000 BW who delved too deep into the spirit world and stumbled into the Abyss. What they saw there shattered them. When they returned (if they returned), they were no longer themselves. They spoke in tongues, performed horrific rituals, and eventually gathered followers who believed their madness was divine truth.

These early cults were small and isolated—a handful of madmen in remote villages, easily dealt with by local authorities who simply executed the afflicted and burned their writings. But Og'thallith is patient. Every cult that was destroyed left behind texts, symbols, and whispers. The knowledge spread slowly, like a virus, passing from village to village, generation to generation, never quite dying out no matter how many were killed.

The first major outbreak of Og'thallith's influence occurred in 4,200 BW in what became known as the Spiral Plague. A virulent form of madness swept through the human kingdoms, causing mass hallucinations, paranoia, and violence. Entire villages descended into chaos as neighbors turned on each other, convinced everyone else was a demon or imposter. Scholars called it the Spiral Plague because victims drew spirals everywhere—on walls, on their skin, in the dirt. They claimed the spirals were "pathways to truth" and that following them would lead to enlightenment.

Tiovin's clerics and Minerva's healers worked tirelessly to contain the outbreak. They discovered that the madness originated from a cursed artifact—a stone tablet inscribed with Og'thallith's symbol, unearthed by archaeologists who had no idea what they were unleashing. The tablet was destroyed through divine intervention, and the plague subsided, but not before killing thousands. The incident taught the gods and their followers that Og'thallith's influence could be physically contained in objects, making such artifacts among the most dangerous items in existence.

Unlike Taovan (who works through secret cults of the ambitious) or Zuel (who wages open war), Og'thallith's influence is nearly invisible in what some call the Silent War. His cultists do not wear uniforms or gather in temples. They hide in plain sight—the scholar who spends too long reading forbidden texts and begins to see patterns where none exist, the soldier who starts hearing voices after a traumatic battle and believes they are receiving divine guidance, the merchant who becomes convinced of impossible conspiracies and begins acting on paranoid delusions.

Og'thallith's war is a war of attrition against sanity itself. Every person driven mad, every act of senseless violence, every descent into paranoia and despair—these are his victories. And they are slow, insidious victories that no army can fight, no spell can permanently prevent, no treaty can stop. Entities like Paladine, Tiovin, and Minerva oppose Og'thallith vigorously, but they are fighting a nearly invisible enemy. How do you stop whispers? How do you prevent madness? How do you protect people from their own minds turning against them?

In 824 AW, Og'thallith's influence was indirectly responsible for the events of the Lords of Madness campaign. While the primary antagonist was Ry'le (an aberration deity from the Far Realm), Og'thallith's presence in the Abyss had weakened planar barriers, making it easier for Ry'le's aberrations to infiltrate Mazariim. Some scholars believe Og'thallith and Ry'le may have a symbiotic relationship—Og'thallith spreads madness that makes mortals vulnerable to Ry'le's shapeshifting infiltrators, while Ry'le's aberrations create chaos that weakens reality, bringing it closer to Og'thallith's Abyss. Neither deity has confirmed or denied this theory, assuming they could even be asked questions in any meaningful way.

Og'thallith's relationship with other entities is defined entirely by opposition and uneasy coexistence. Ry'le, the aberration deity, and Og'thallith are not true allies, but they have overlapping goals. Both seek to destabilize reality. Both corrupt through madness. Some scholars believe they are two aspects of the same fundamental cosmic horror, separated by the thin barrier between the Abyss and the Far Realm.

Sheeva, the poison deity, shares the Abyss with Og'thallith, but they are not friends. Sheeva values calculated revenge and the precision of poison; Og'thallith values random chaos and the dissolution of all order. They tolerate each other's presence in the Abyss but do not cooperate, each ruling their own layers and occasionally coming into conflict when their followers clash.

Taovan corrupts through temptation and devil-pacts, offering power in exchange for souls with clear terms and contracts. Og'thallith corrupts through madness, offering nothing but dissolution and the destruction of identity. They compete for the same pool of desperate mortals, and their cultists sometimes clash when both target the same individual or community.

Zuel represents brutal, straightforward destruction through conquest and warfare. Og'thallith represents slow, insidious unraveling through madness and corruption. Zuel finds Og'thallith's methods cowardly and indirect; Og'thallith finds Zuel's methods crude and inefficient. Both are chaotic evil, but their approaches could not be more different.

Mau the Oracle values knowledge, prophecy, and the preservation of consciousness after death. Og'thallith seeks to destroy consciousness itself. Mau actively opposes Og'thallith's cults and teaches mortals how to resist his whispers, providing meditation techniques and mental disciplines that can ward off the early stages of corruption.

Tiovin and Paladine, the gods of mercy and justice, despise Og'thallith with an intensity matched by few other divine oppositions. Their clerics and paladins hunt his cultists relentlessly and work to cure those afflicted by his madness, though success rates are tragically low once corruption reaches advanced stages.

Minerva, the elven goddess of knowledge, is Og'thallith's polar opposite. She values reason, beauty, and enlightenment; he values madness, chaos, and oblivion. Minerva's scholars study ways to ward against his influence, developing protective spells and identifying warning signs of corruption.

Alagalothor, the god of magic, opposes Og'thallith because madness is the enemy of controlled spellcasting. Mages who succumb to Og'thallith's madness become dangerous, unpredictable threats who cast spells with disastrous results, creating wild magic zones and destabilizing the fabric of magic itself.
