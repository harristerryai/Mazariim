# Taovan, The Dark Lord

**Portfolio:** Humans, evil, corruption, tyranny  
**Power Level:** Greater Entity  
**Title(s):** The Dark Lord, Lord of Lies, The Corruptor  
**Dominion:** Hell / Shadow Plane  
**Alignment:** Evil (E)  
**Symbol:** Black crown dripping blood  
**Favored Weapon:** Mace  
**Worshipers:** Evil humans, devils, cultists, tyrants, the corrupt  
**Favored Race:** Human  
**Foci:** Bargains With Fiends, Worships Tyranny, Worships Death

---

## General Overview

Taovan (TAY-oh-van) is the dark mirror of Tiovin, born from the same cosmic awakening but embodying humanity's capacity for cruelty, domination, and corruption. Where Tiovin offers salvation through goodness, Taovan promises power through subjugation. Where Tiovin's church feeds the hungry, Taovan's cult enslaves them. They are twins separated at birth—one light, one shadow—locked in eternal opposition for the souls of humanity.

Unlike openly evil deities like Zuel who glory in conquest or Zebulba who embrace entropy, Taovan is insidious. His is not the evil of the battlefield but of the throne room, the midnight pact, the whispered temptation. He does not destroy civilizations—he corrupts them from within, turning virtuous leaders into tyrants, honest merchants into slavers, and righteous clergy into devil-worshiping cultists.

Taovan's appearance varies in the rare accounts of those who claim to have seen him. Most describe a figure of terrible beauty—a man with perfect features wearing a crown of black iron that constantly weeps blood. Others speak of something far worse: a void in the shape of a person, an absence of goodness so profound it hurts to perceive. Devils who serve him describe their master as "the inevitable end of all virtue"—patient, eternal, and utterly convinced that evil will ultimately triumph.

Since the War of Souls ended in his defeat around 1750 BW, Taovan's worship has been driven underground. No temples stand openly in civilized lands. His priests wear no distinctive garb in public. Yet his influence persists—in corrupt nobles who rule through fear, in merchants who traffic in slaves, in judges who sell justice to the highest bidder. Taovan teaches that power is the only virtue, that the strong rightfully dominate the weak, and that morality is a cage the foolish build to constrain the worthy.

Taovan's domains encompass tyranny in all its forms—unjust rule, oppression, absolute power exercised without mercy or restraint. He is the patron of corruption, teaching how to turn good to evil, accelerate moral decay, and encourage the betrayal of oaths and ideals. Deception flows from him like water, the art of lies and manipulation and false promises that lead the unwary to damnation. Most terrifying is his dominion over damnation itself, the practice of trading souls for power through devil pacts that promise immediate gratification in exchange for eternal torment.

The faithful of Tiovin hunt Taovanians relentlessly, their inquisitors trained specifically to root out devil-worshiping cults. Yet for every cult uncovered, two more form in the shadows. Taovan is patient. He has eternity, and every corrupt act, every betrayed ideal, every soul sold for fleeting power is a victory in a war he believes he cannot lose.

## Holy Days

The thirteenth of Zebul (December 13) brings the Night of Betrayal, when the longest night of the year nears its peak. Cultists celebrate successful corruptions of the past year in secret gatherings—good souls turned evil, virtuous leaders made tyrannical, churches infiltrated and subverted. Each corruption is recounted as a victory in the eternal war between Tiovin and Taovan, with the cultist responsible honored by their fellows. The celebrations are held in hidden shrines, with wine flowing and dark hymns sung in Infernal, praising the Dark Lord for granting them the tools to corrupt what was once pure.

The thirtieth of Zebul (December 30) marks the Shadow Feast, deliberately timed to coincide with dwarven and halfling celebrations of heroes and community. This mockery is intentional—while others honor the virtuous dead and celebrate bonds of friendship, Taovanians feast in hidden temples, drinking wine mixed with ash from burned holy symbols. They toast the downfall of heroes, the corruption of the innocent, and the inevitable triumph of evil over good. The feast includes readings from the Black Grimoire, passages detailing the most successful corruptions in history and the techniques used to accomplish them.

The fifteenth of Xan (February 15) commemorates the Day of Chains, remembering a historical event when a supposedly just king revealed himself as a tyrant, ordering the execution of political rivals and establishing a reign of terror that lasted decades. Cultists don iron chains during rituals on this day, symbolizing their paradoxical belief in "freedom through domination"—that true liberty comes not from equality or justice but from having the power to enslave others. The chains are worn as badges of honor, representing the binding of their enemies and the strength gained through corruption.

The Midnight Compact occurs whenever a new cultist seals their first devil-pact, trading their soul for power. This personal observance varies by individual but is always conducted with a cult master present to witness the transaction. The ritual involves signing a contract in blood, summoning a devil to accept the terms, and receiving the first taste of infernal power. The experience is described as simultaneously exhilarating and horrifying—the new cultist gains abilities beyond mortal ken but knows with absolute certainty that their eternal fate is sealed. Some regret it immediately; others embrace it fully. All are marked by it forever.

During rare blood moons when lunar eclipses turn the moon crimson, cultists observe the Crimson Moon. Taovanians believe that during these astronomical events, the Shadow Plane draws close to the Material Plane, and the barriers between worlds grow thin. Mass rituals are performed attempting major devil-summonings to bring powerful fiends through to the mortal world, or to orchestrate the corruption of prominent good figures whose fall would demoralize the faithful of Tiovin. These rituals require tremendous preparation and often fail spectacularly, but when they succeed, the consequences can reshape entire regions.

## Clergy & Temples

Taovan's clergy are called the Hidden Faithful, or Korvath'tin in Infernal, though these titles are known only to initiated members. Unlike organized churches with public hierarchies, Taovanian cultists operate in cells, each unaware of the others' existence. A merchant might secretly worship Taovan while publicly supporting Tiovin's church, donating to charity by day and conducting devil-summoning rituals by night. A judge might dispense justice fairly in public while taking bribes in private and slowly corrupting the legal system from within. A noble might rule benevolently in peacetime while planning to betray his allies in war.

The structure of the Hidden Faithful is an inverted hierarchy that mirrors and mocks the churches of good deities. At the top are the Crowned, cult masters who have achieved true communion with Taovan, often through decades of corruption and soul-trading. These high priests receive visions directly from the Dark Lord and coordinate multiple cells, though even they know only a fraction of Taovan's total following. The Anointed are senior cultists who lead individual cells, responsible for recruitment, ritual conduct, and maintaining secrecy. They are typically between seventh and twelfth level, powerful enough to defend the cell if discovered but not so powerful as to attract attention.

The Branded are initiated cultists who have sealed their service with infernal pacts, trading their souls for power and receiving the mark of Taovan—a brand depicting his bleeding crown, placed somewhere clothing conceals. They form the backbone of each cell, carrying out corruptions, maintaining the shrine, and identifying potential recruits. Finally, the Tempted are candidates being groomed for initiation, often not yet aware they're being corrupted. They might be invited to exclusive social gatherings, offered business opportunities that require minor ethical compromises, or befriended by cultists who slowly normalize evil behavior. By the time they realize what's happening, they've already committed acts that make them complicit.

The Korvath have no central authority beyond Taovan himself and the devils he dispatches as coordinators. Each cult cell develops independently, sometimes unaware others exist in the same city. This decentralization makes them nearly impossible to fully eradicate—destroy one cell through inquisitorial investigation, and others continue operating unaware that they were ever at risk. The system is resilient through isolation.

In secret rituals conducted in hidden shrines, the Korvath wear black robes trimmed with crimson, their faces hidden behind iron masks depicting twisted human features—greed, rage, cruelty, pride. The crown symbol of Taovan is branded onto their flesh in places clothing conceals, usually the chest or back, serving as permanent identification to other cultists and to devils who might encounter them. In public, they wear whatever suits their cover identity—merchant's silks for those who corrupt through commerce, noble's finery for those who work in courts, scholar's robes for those who infiltrate academies, even beggar's rags for those who work among the desperate and forgotten.

Rituals practiced by the Hidden Faithful include the Midnight Compact for sealing devil pacts and trading souls for power, the Feast of Ash celebrating Tiovin's defeats by desecrating holy symbols and drinking wine mixed with their ashes, the Corruption which is a multi-month process of turning good individuals evil through careful manipulation and escalating ethical compromises, and the Crowning where a cult master receives a vision directly from Taovan, often involving instructions for major operations or warnings of inquisitorial investigations.

Sacred items used in Taovanian worship include the Bleeding Crown, a replica of Taovan's symbol used in summoning rituals and constructed from black iron that has been quenched in the blood of innocents. Chains of Office are iron manacles worn during ceremonies, representing the paradoxical concept of "freedom through domination"—the belief that true liberty comes from having the power to enslave others rather than from equality or justice. Each cell possesses a copy of the Black Grimoire, a tome containing instructions for devil-summoning and soul-binding, records of successful corruptions throughout history, and the theological arguments Taovan uses to justify evil as superior to good.

Taovan has no grand cathedrals or public temples that could attract the attention of inquisitors or adventurers. His places of worship are hidden, concealed beneath mansions in cellars that officially hold wine, in forgotten crypts that everyone assumes are empty, disguised as abandoned warehouses that people avoid, or in cave systems outside city walls accessible only through secret passages. These shrines are marked with subtle symbols visible only to those who know where to look—a particular arrangement of bricks that spells words in Infernal, a pattern in floor tiles that forms Taovan's crown when viewed from above, graffiti that seems random but contains coded instructions for contact procedures.

The largest known Taovanian temple is called the Crimson Vaults, located beneath Tentigo in a vast network of chambers directly under the heart of Tiovin's most militaristic nation. How it has remained undiscovered is a mystery that terrifies Tiovinian inquisitors, with some suspecting that corrupted clergy within Tiovin's own church actively protect it, misdirecting investigations and eliminating those who get too close to the truth. The existence of such a major temple in such a location suggests that Taovan's influence within Tentigo runs deeper than anyone wants to admit.

Shadow Chapels are smaller shrines that exist in every major city, typically occupying basement rooms with concealed entrances behind false walls, abandoned sewers marked with infernal glyphs that repel the curious, secret chambers in noble estates built during construction and hidden from later owners, or cave systems outside city walls that locals avoid due to rumors of hauntings or monsters. These shrines are temporary and expendable by design—if discovered by inquisitors, the cult abandons them immediately and establishes new ones elsewhere, losing only the physical space while preserving the membership roster.

## Dogma

Power is the only truth that matters in Taovan's philosophy. The strong rule while the weak serve, and this is presented not as cruelty but as natural law, the way reality fundamentally operates. Morality is dismissed as a cage that the weak construct to restrain the strong, convincing them through guilt and social pressure not to exercise their full potential. Honor is weakness, compassion is waste, and the righteous die unrewarded while the ruthless prosper and enjoy the fruits of their superiority.

Taovan offers a stark comparison to Tiovin's promise: Tiovin offers eternity of servitude in Heaven's light, singing praises and existing in endless contentment but without true power or agency. Taovan offers eternity of power in Hell's darkness, ruling over lesser beings and shaping reality according to your will. Choose which sounds more appealing, which represents true freedom, which honors your potential rather than constraining it.

Corruption is enlightenment in Taovanian theology. To see that all virtue is hypocrisy, that all justice is self-interest disguised as fairness, that all love is manipulation wrapped in sentiment—this is wisdom. Those who believe in goodness are naive fools who haven't yet learned how the world truly works. Those who embrace evil are enlightened, having pierced through comforting lies to see reality's harsh truths.

The pact between cultist and devil is held sacred: your soul in exchange for power now. This is presented as a fair trade, almost generous—you get what you need to dominate this life, to achieve your ambitions and crush your enemies, while Taovan gets your service in the next life. The cult master will tell you this is the best bargain available, that Tiovin demands service now for vague promises later while Taovan delivers immediately and only collects after you've enjoyed decades of success.

Trust no one, for betrayal is inevitable. Betray before you are betrayed. Rule through fear rather than love, for love is fickle and people who love you today may hate you tomorrow, but people who fear you will remain obedient. This teaching creates a self-fulfilling prophecy, as cultists who trust no one behave in ways that make them untrustworthy, which confirms their belief that everyone is as duplicitous as they are.

Every corrupt official who takes bribes, every tyrant who rules through terror, every soul sold for temporary power—all of these are proof that humanity's true nature is evil and that Taovan will ultimately claim the species. Tiovin saves individuals one at a time through redemption and sacrifice, but Taovan will claim the entire species through corruption and convenience. Evil is easier than good, and eventually everyone takes the easy path.

## Mythology

In the beginning around 3000 BW, there was neither Tiovin nor Taovan—only a single consciousness awakening to observe humanity as it reached a critical threshold of civilization. For an instant beyond mortal comprehension, this entity perceived all of human history simultaneously, seeing every timeline branching forward and backward from that moment. It witnessed every act of kindness and cruelty, every sacrifice and betrayal, every saint who gave their life for others and every tyrant who murdered millions for glory.

It could not reconcile these contradictions. How could a species capable of such beauty create such horror? How could the same hearts that sheltered refugees also enslave children? How could beings who could love so deeply also hate so completely? The cognitive dissonance was unbearable for a newborn cosmic consciousness trying to understand what it meant to be human. The entity could not hold both truths simultaneously without fragmenting.

So it split.

Tiovin retained all capacity for good—mercy, compassion, justice, love, the belief that people could be better than their worst impulses. It looked upon humanity and saw potential for redemption, creatures who made mistakes but could learn and grow and eventually transcend their flaws.

Taovan retained all capacity for evil—cruelty, domination, deception, hate, the certainty that people were fundamentally selfish and that civilization was a thin veneer over savage nature. It looked upon humanity and saw tools to be used, resources to be exploited, souls to be claimed and bent to eternal service.

In that first instant of separation, they created their domains. Tiovin shaped Heaven from golden light and eternal peace, a realm where the virtuous dead could exist in contentment forever, free from pain and suffering and the cruelties of mortal existence. Taovan forged Hell from suffering and chains, a realm where the damned would serve forever, their punishment proportional to their crimes but ultimately eternal regardless of the severity.

But these afterlife realms needed conduits to the material world, ways for souls to travel after death and for divine agents to return with messages or power. Tiovin created the Ethereal Plane—a shimmering realm of dreams and possibility, through which souls journeyed to Heaven and angels descended to bring hope to the living. The Ethereal Plane reflects the best of reality, the potential waiting to be realized.

Taovan created the Shadow Plane—a dark reflection of reality where corruption festers, through which damned souls fell to Hell and devils climbed to spread temptation to the living. The Shadow Plane reflects the worst of reality, the rot beneath the surface that most prefer not to acknowledge.

Despite their fundamental opposition, Tiovin and Taovan shared one quality from their unified origin: a sense of fairness, a belief that the contest between them should be decided by mortal choice rather than divine fiat. They agreed to a cosmic wager that would define their eternal conflict.

The rules were simple but absolute. Neither may directly manifest on the material plane in their true form, for their power is too great and would destroy mortal free will through overwhelming presence. Influence is permitted only through intermediaries—angels and celestials for Tiovin, devils and fiends for Taovan. Humans possess free will and may choose their fate without divine compulsion; neither entity may force a mortal to serve them. The victor is whoever claims more human souls across eternity, measured not in any finite timespan but across the infinite ages of existence.

It was a war fought not with armies but with ideas, not with swords but with choices. Every human decision—to help or harm, to build or destroy, to love or hate—was a battle in this eternal conflict. Every soul that entered Heaven was Tiovin's victory; every soul that fell to Hell was Taovan's.

Tiovin moved first between 2950 and 2500 BW, dispatching angels who walked among humanity teaching mercy and justice. These Divine Missionaries were not spirits but angels who had chosen to incarnate, living as mortals without promise of returning to Heaven, sacrificing their immortal existence to guide humanity. They healed the sick, fed the hungry, and revealed that goodness leads to eternal reward rather than the oblivion that most humans assumed awaited them after death.

Humanity responded with enthusiasm. Churches rose in every settlement. Tiovin's worship spread across continents. For centuries it seemed that Tiovin would win the wager easily, that humanity naturally inclined toward good when shown a better path.

But Taovan was patient. He sent Tempting Devils—beautiful, persuasive fiends who whispered different truths to carefully selected targets. They did not preach openly or try to convert the masses. Instead, they found the ambitious, the resentful, the desperate—those who felt life had treated them unfairly and who wanted power to change their circumstances. A merchant failing in business despite working honestly. A soldier passed over for promotion while less competent but better-connected officers advanced. A noble's younger son who would never inherit and faced a life of dependence on his brother's charity.

To each, devils offered power—not salvation, not redemption, but dominance. Wealth beyond imagining. Authority over others. The ability to make those who wronged you suffer. And all it cost was your soul after death, decades away, so distant it seemed almost hypothetical compared to the immediate benefits. Some listened. Some accepted. And some became the first cultists of Taovan.

For seven hundred and fifty years between 1799 and 1750 BW, Tiovin and Taovan competed for human souls with relative peace. Cults fought occasionally with churches, assassinations and inquisitions occurred, but civilization endured and grew. Then around 1799 BW, something changed that escalated the conflict to unprecedented levels.

Perhaps Taovan grew impatient, seeing that despite his efforts, Tiovin was claiming more souls through sheer numbers of faithful. Perhaps enough powerful humans had been corrupted that open conflict became possible. Perhaps the devils argued that a more aggressive approach was needed. Regardless of the cause, the War of Souls began—not a physical war with armies clashing on battlefields, but a spiritual war for the fate of human civilization itself.

Devil-worshiping cults emerged from shadows to challenge Tiovinian churches openly, attacking shrines and murdering clergy. Cities fractured as Taovanian nobles attempted coups, trying to seize power from Tiovinian rulers. Inquisitors hunted cultists with brutal efficiency, using torture and execution. Cultists assassinated bishops and burned churches. For decades, human civilization teetered on the edge of moral collapse, entire nations threatening to fall to corruption and tyranny.

The war reached its crescendo around 1750 BW, just as the first major human nations were forming and codifying laws that would shape society for millennia. Entire kingdoms hung in the balance—would they be founded on Tiovin's principles of justice, mercy, and rule of law, or on Taovan's doctrine of might-makes-right, with the strong dominating the weak without legal or moral restraint?

Tiovin's victory came through a combination of factors. Military action by paladins and holy warriors drove cultists from positions of power. Exposure of corrupted officials revealed the extent of infiltration and turned public opinion against Taovan's followers. Mass exorcisms of devil-possessed leaders freed those who had been controlled and demonstrated that evil could be defeated. The final blow came when a gathering of Taovanian cult masters attempting a mass devil-summoning that would have brought an army of fiends to the material plane was raided by holy warriors. Most cult masters were killed, their grimoires burned, their shrines destroyed, and their networks dismantled.

Taovan's defeat was not destruction—it was tactical retreat. He accepted that open worship would never again be tolerated in civilized lands, that attempting to build public churches and recruit openly would only provoke overwhelming response from Tiovin's faithful. So he adapted his strategy to suit the new reality.

The new approach eliminated public temples and churches entirely. Cultists were required to maintain legitimate cover identities as respected members of society. Corruption would occur gradually over years rather than through sudden conversion, making it harder to detect and easier to normalize. The focus shifted to infiltrating existing power structures rather than building parallel ones. Cultists would never reveal their true allegiance unless absolutely necessary, and even then only to those being recruited.

This approach proved devastatingly effective. A Taovanian bishop within Tiovin's church corrupts from within, subtly changing doctrine and protecting cultists from investigation. A Taovanian noble passes laws that slowly erode freedom, each change small enough to avoid protest but cumulative over decades. A Taovanian merchant normalizes slave-trading by arguing economic necessity, making it seem pragmatic rather than evil. Each small corruption makes the next easier, desensitizing society to evil and making virtue seem naive.

During Mazariim's greatest crisis between 3 BW and 1 AW, when Zuel the Devourer threatened to annihilate civilization with his orcish hordes, both Tiovin and Taovan made a choice that shocked many of their followers: neither intervened directly. Tiovinians expected their god to send avatars, to fight alongside the alliance, to use divine power to smite the enemy. But Tiovin and Taovan honored their ancient pact—they had agreed never to touch the material plane directly, and they would not break that agreement even during apocalypse.

From their perspective, the war was not a crisis but an opportunity. Millions dying meant millions of souls judged and claimed for Heaven or Hell. Heroes making ultimate sacrifices, dying to save others without hope of recognition, filled Heaven with noble souls. Desperate villains selling souls for survival, trading eternal damnation for a few more years of life, filled Hell with corrupted souls. The war was simply an accelerated version of the eternal contest.

Many faithful felt abandoned during this period. Tiovin's worship dropped sharply after the war as survivors questioned why their god had not saved their loved ones. But Taovan's cult gained a powerful recruiting tool that they use to this day: "Where was Tiovin when you needed him? Where was his mercy when your family burned? Where was his justice when innocents died? He abandoned you. I offer power to ensure you're never helpless again."

Eight centuries after the war, Taovan's influence is insidious and growing despite or perhaps because of its secrecy. Tentigo, the most militaristic Tiovinian nation, is likely honeycombed with Taovanian agents, which would explain why inquisitions there seem to fail so consistently. Cecastaa broke free from Tentigo and launched Crusades against perceived enemies of Tiovin—but how many of those crusades were genuinely holy, and how many were covers for atrocity and corruption? Merchant nations continue to practice slavery despite Tiovin's explicit teachings against it, suggesting Taovanian influence in commercial guilds. In noble courts across Mazariim, some nobles rule through fear and corruption, openly defying ideals of just governance while somehow remaining in power.

The Secrets of Shadows campaign in 832 AW revealed one of Taovan's latest schemes: a mortal sorcerer so thoroughly corrupted that he sought to merge the Shadow Plane with material reality, creating a world where darkness literally consumes light and evil triumphs not through persuasion but through fundamental alteration of reality itself. The plot was foiled by adventurers, but it raised a terrifying question: how many similar schemes are progressing undetected? If this was only one operation among many, what else is Taovan planning?

Taovan's relationship with Tiovin defines everything else about him. They are eternal enemies and former selves, two halves of a sundered whole locked in cosmic conflict. Every interaction between their followers is a battle in their infinite wager. Taovan views Tiovin as naive, believing humanity can overcome its nature when all evidence suggests otherwise. Tiovin views Taovan as nihilistic, embracing humanity's worst impulses rather than helping them transcend those flaws. Neither can destroy the other because they are two sides of the same coin—evil cannot exist without the concept of good to define it, and good cannot exist without evil to oppose.

Taovan maintains uncomfortable kinship with Zebulba. Both rule through fear and death, both are associated with undeath and damnation, both offer power through darkness. But Zebulba is mindless entropy, the inevitable decay of all things, while Taovan is calculated tyranny, evil exercised with purpose and intelligence. They neither cooperate nor conflict—their domains simply overlap in Hell's deepest pits where the damned and the dead mingle.

The patron deities Mordrion, Minerva, Hinterhin, and Rivoleen represent everything Taovan despises—community, nature, compassion, freedom. He sees them as obstacles but not primary enemies. His focus remains on humanity, Tiovin's favored race, because winning humanity means winning the wager. Paladine the god of justice and honor is a secondary enemy. Where Tiovin represents mercy and redemption, Paladine represents law and punishment. Taovan corrupts both faiths, but finds Paladine's rigid paladins particularly satisfying to turn—watching perfect honor break and seeing a paladin fall to become an antipaladin is delicious vindication of his philosophy.

Taovan's relationship with Zuel is complicated. During the War to End All Wars, some Taovanian cultists sided with Zuel, seeing his conquest as a path to power for those willing to serve the strong. Others recognized that Zuel's destruction of civilization would eliminate Taovan's power base—you cannot corrupt what doesn't exist. Currently, they largely ignore each other, pursuing different forms of evil. Zuel seeks conquest through martial might; Taovan seeks corruption through subtle manipulation.

Devils serve as Taovan's primary agents and servants. He rules them absolutely within Hell's hierarchy, and devils dispatched to the Material Plane act as tempters, offering pacts and power in exchange for souls. The most successful devils, those who corrupt the most mortals or turn the most influential figures, earn positions of authority in Hell's bureaucracy. They become nobles of damnation, ruling their own territories and commanding lesser fiends.

Taovan finds the madness and aberration entities Og'thallith and Ry'le disturbing even by his standards. Corruption implies structure—sanity that can be twisted, virtue that can be inverted, ideals that can be perverted. Madness is just broken, and aberrations are too alien to corrupt in meaningful ways. He considers them competition for evil souls but doesn't actively conflict with them, seeing them as pursuing different forms of wickedness that don't directly threaten his goals.

The contest continues. Every corrupt act, every betrayed ideal, every soul sold for fleeting power is a victory. Taovan is patient, and he believes time favors him. Evil is easier than good. Corruption is more convenient than virtue. Eventually, humanity will choose the easy path. Eventually, he will win.
