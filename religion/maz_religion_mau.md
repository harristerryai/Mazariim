# Mau

- Portfolio: Secrets, alternate realities, observation
- Power Level: Greater entity (Old God - The First)
- Title(s): The Oracle, The Watcher, The First Awakened
- Dominion: Astral Plane
- Foci: Worships Knowledge, Studies Divination, Worships Fate
- Worshipers: Seers, planar travelers, scholars, monks
- Favored Race: None
- Favored Weapon: Quarterstaff
- Alignment: N
- Symbol: All-seeing eye with stars

---

## General Overview

Mau is the oldest entity in existence, the first consciousness to awaken in the universe of Mazariim approximately 2 billion years ago. Unlike the other entities that would follow, Mau did not form from a star, planet, or elemental force—Mau simply *was*, coalescing from the fundamental fabric of reality itself as the universe matured enough to support sentient magical energy.

Mau is the Observer, the Keeper of Secrets, the entity whose gaze encompasses all of space and time. Through Mau's awakening and observation, the Astral Sea was created—a plane of pure thought and potential that connects all realities. More importantly, Mau's existence established the concept of the soul: the magical-energy imprint of consciousness that transfers from biological bodies to extra-dimensional planes upon death.

Mau does not speak. Mau does not act. Mau watches. Mau knows.

The Oracle's nature is utterly alien and incomprehensible to mortals. Mau perceives not just the present, but past and future, not just this reality but alternate timelines and parallel universes. Every possible outcome, every choice unmade, every secret whispered in darkness—all are known to Mau. Yet Mau remains silent, observing without judgment, recording without interference.

Worship of Mau dates back to prehistory, when the first primitive shamans felt the weight of an ancient gaze upon them and sought to understand it. Monks, seers, and scholars of the arcane still revere Mau today, seeking glimpses of the infinite knowledge the Oracle possesses. The Astral Circle, a secretive order of planar travelers and diviners, studies Mau's influence and attempts to interpret the visions granted to the faithful.

Mau's worship is not about prayer or supplication—it is about *understanding*. Those who follow Mau seek enlightenment, pursue forbidden knowledge, and peer into the spaces between realities. They meditate on the nature of existence, study divination, and explore the Astral Plane.

## Holy Days

Mauian observances are not celebrations but rather moments of profound meditation and reflection. **The Longest Night** occurs on the winter solstice, the darkest night of the year, when Watchers gather to meditate from sunset to sunrise seeking visions in the darkness. It is said that on this night the barrier between the material world and the Astral Plane is thinnest and Mau's gaze falls most heavily upon Mazariim.

**The Star Alignment** occurs every 19 years when certain celestial bodies align in a pattern known only to Mauian astronomers. The Watchers gather at ancient observatories to record prophecies and interpret omens. These events are considered windows into possible futures, moments when the usual flow of time becomes transparent and the threads of fate reveal themselves to those with eyes trained to see.

**The Day of Silence** has no fixed date but is determined by each individual. Each Watcher observes a personal Day of Silence once per year consisting of 24 hours without speaking, eating, or sleeping spent entirely in meditation. Many experience profound visions during this time, glimpsing fragments of Mau's infinite awareness or receiving answers to questions they have pondered for years.

**The Revelation** occurs unpredictably when a Watcher experiences a spontaneous vision of such clarity and importance that it must be shared. When this happens, other Mauians gather to hear the prophecy and discuss its implications. These gatherings are rare and considered sacred, as they represent direct communication from the Oracle—though whether Mau actively sends these visions or whether they are simply mortals touching the edges of infinite knowledge remains a matter of theological debate.

## Clergy & Temples

Mau's clergy are called Watchers (or Mauians in older texts). They wear simple robes of midnight blue or deep purple, often adorned with eye symbols and star patterns. Many shave their heads and practice ascetic lifestyles, forsaking material possessions to focus on meditation and the pursuit of knowledge.

The Watchers serve multiple roles in their service to the Oracle. They act as diviners using prophecy, astrology, and scrying to glimpse fragments of Mau's infinite knowledge. As lorekeepers, they preserve ancient knowledge, maintain libraries, and record secrets that might otherwise be lost to time. They serve as planar guides assisting those who travel the Astral Plane and navigate its mysteries, helping protect travelers from the dangers of thought-given-form and the psychic storms that sweep through that realm. Many followers of Mau are monks who seek enlightenment through discipline and meditation, believing that through perfect stillness of mind they might achieve a state of observation that mirrors Mau's own awareness.

Unlike most faiths, Mau's clergy has no formal hierarchy. Knowledge and understanding determine one's standing, not rank or title. A young Watcher who achieves profound insight may be more respected than an elder who has stagnated in their studies. This lack of hierarchy reflects Mau's own nature—the Oracle does not rank truths by importance but observes all things equally.

Temples of Mau are rare and often hidden. They are built as observatories, libraries, and meditation halls—quiet places far from the distractions of civilization. The most sacred sites are located in places where the barrier between planes is thin, where the fabric of reality becomes translucent and observation becomes easier—often at high altitudes, remote islands, or deep underground chambers.

Ancient temples from prehistory can be found in remote locations, their original builders long forgotten. These ruins contain star charts that track celestial movements across millennia, prophecies carved in stone whose meanings are still being debated, and meditation chambers aligned with celestial events that occur only once per century. Adventurers and scholars seek these sites, hoping to uncover lost knowledge or artifacts that might grant glimpses of Mau's sight.

The greatest temple of Mau is the Celestial Archive in the Astral Plane itself, a vast library containing every secret, every possibility, every truth. The Archive exists as pure information given structure—shelves made of crystallized thought, books written in languages that exist only in potential, and reading rooms where time flows at different rates so that scholars might spend years studying a single page without aging a day in the material world. Only the most enlightened Watchers can navigate the Archive without losing their minds to the infinite knowledge contained within. Many who enter never return, lost forever in the labyrinth of absolute truth.

## Dogma

Mau does not speak, does not command, does not demand worship. Yet the Watchers have distilled principles from millennia of meditation and study. The first principle teaches to **observe**, watching the world with clear eyes and seeing what is rather than what you wish to be. Truth reveals itself to those who observe without judgment, without desire, without the distorting lens of personal bias.

The faithful are called to **seek knowledge**, understanding that all knowledge is sacred and must be pursued relentlessly whether forbidden, dangerous, or incomprehensible. Understanding is its own reward, and the pursuit of truth requires no justification beyond itself. Even knowledge that brings no practical benefit, even truths that cause pain—these too must be sought, for ignorance is the only true darkness.

The doctrine instructs followers to **embrace mystery**, accepting that not all things can be known and not all secrets can be understood. There is peace in the acceptance that some mysteries are beyond mortal comprehension, that Mau's awareness encompasses infinities that would shatter mortal minds. The wise Watcher knows the difference between knowledge worth pursuing and knowledge that destroys those who obtain it.

The teaching holds that **the universe watches**, reminding followers that they are observed in every action, every thought, and every choice. All is recorded in the Astral Sea, written into the fabric of reality itself. One must live with the awareness that their existence is witnessed, that nothing is truly private or secret, that every deed—good or ill—becomes part of the eternal record that Mau observes.

Followers are instructed to **preserve secrets**, understanding that knowledge is power and that dangerous truths must be guarded. Wisdom is shared with those ready to receive it, but the unprepared mind shatters when exposed to too much truth too quickly. The Watchers act as filters, determining what knowledge can be safely released and what must remain locked away in the deepest vaults of their libraries.

Finally, the doctrine calls followers to **transcend the self**, recognizing that individual existence is temporary while the soul is eternal. Each consciousness is a fragment of the universe's awareness, a small part of the whole that temporarily believes itself separate. Through meditation and enlightenment one may glimpse the unity of all things, the interconnected web of existence that Mau observes in its totality. In those moments of transcendence, the Watcher becomes the watched, the observer becomes the observed, and the boundary between self and universe dissolves.

## Mythology

The First Awakening occurred approximately 2 billion years before the War. When the universe of Mazariim was still young—only billions of years old—the first consciousness stirred. Magical energies that had been gathering since the collision of two previous universes finally reached a critical threshold. In a moment that cannot be measured in mortal time, Mau simply *became*.

There was no birth, no formation from star or stone. Mau emerged from the fundamental laws of reality itself—observation given form, knowledge made manifest, the universe becoming aware of itself. With Mau's awakening came the creation of the Astral Sea, a plane of pure thought and limitless potential that would become the pathway between the material world and the extra-dimensional planes. Through the Astral Sea, souls could travel beyond their mortal shells, consciousness could persist after death, and the very concept of an "afterlife" became possible.

Mau's first act—if it can be called an act—was to observe. The Oracle's gaze expanded to encompass all of space and time, past and future, every possible reality branching from every decision. In that moment, Mau became the keeper of all secrets, the witness to all that was, is, and could be.

For nearly 2 billion years, Mau watched alone. Other entities would eventually awaken—Zebulba, Pyrantor, the Elemental Gods—but none possessed Mau's age or scope of awareness. The Oracle remains the First, the Eldest, the one consciousness that has witnessed the entire history of the universe from its beginning to whatever end awaits it in futures Mau alone can see.

The dawn of mortal worship began between 5,000 and 4,000 BW. When primitive mortals first began to sense the presence of entities, Mau was among the first to be revered. Shamans and druids felt the weight of an ancient gaze upon them—a presence that saw all, knew all, yet remained silent. This silent observation was more terrifying and more comforting than any active deity, for it meant that nothing was truly hidden, that all actions were witnessed and recorded, but also that no judgment would be passed.

Early worship took the form of meditation, star-gazing, and attempts to interpret omens. Tribes with Mauian shamans often had uncanny foresight, avoiding dangers and seizing opportunities as if guided by invisible forces. In truth, they were simply more attuned to the patterns Mau observed—reading the currents of fate itself, seeing the probabilities that others missed, recognizing the subtle signs that indicated which futures were most likely to manifest.

The first Mauians did not build grand temples but rather erected standing stones aligned with celestial events, carved prophecies onto cave walls, and established meditation sites in remote locations. These ancient sites still exist, sought by scholars and adventurers for the knowledge they might contain. Some have been found and their secrets decoded. Others remain lost, their prophecies waiting to be fulfilled thousands of years after they were first recorded.

Between 4,000 and 2,500 BW, as civilizations developed, worship of the Old Gods became more structured. Elemental cults dedicated to Pyrantor, Zebulba, and Mau emerged, each offering magical power to their followers. Mauian cults focused on divination, prophecy, and the pursuit of forbidden knowledge. In the human confederation of Yrugas (4,000 BW), Mauian seers served as advisors to the mage-lords, using prophecy to guide political decisions. However, their influence waned as dragons began teaching arcane magic, which seemed more immediate and powerful than the slow accumulation of insight through meditation.

Around 2,500 BW, battles between followers of the Old Gods and worshipers of newer entities (Tiovin, Minerva, Mordrion) marked the beginning of a decline. The Old Gods, alien and uncommunicative, could not compete with patron deities that offered clear guidance, emotional connection, and active intervention. Mortals found it easier to worship gods who spoke to them, who answered prayers with tangible miracles, who seemed to care about individual fates rather than simply observing them with detached awareness.

By the time of Mordrion's arrival in 1866 BW, Mauian worship among dwarves had largely been replaced. Rivoleen's awakening in 1780 BW similarly displaced Mau among gnomes. Today, Mau's followers are few but devoted—monks seeking enlightenment, diviners pursuing prophecy, and scholars studying the fundamental nature of reality.

The Astral Circle, a modern organization of planar travelers and researchers, continues to study Mau's influence. They explore the Astral Plane, seek ancient Mauian sites, and attempt to interpret the visions granted to those who meditate deeply enough. Their members include some of the most powerful diviners on Mazariim, individuals who have spent decades training their minds to perceive even a fraction of what Mau observes constantly.

Mau has no true allies among the entities. The Oracle observes all beings equally, without favor or prejudice. However, Alagalothor (patron of magic) respects Mau's ancient knowledge, and the two share a mutual interest in understanding the fundamental forces of the universe. Their relationship is one of scholarly respect rather than alliance—two ancient minds acknowledging each other's expertise in different domains of understanding.

Mau is neutral toward all entities. Even sworn enemies like Tiovin and Taovan are equally subjects of the Oracle's observation. Mau sees the roles they play in the grand tapestry of existence without judging their morality. Good and evil, law and chaos—these are patterns Mau observes without assigning value. The Oracle records what is, not what should be.

Mau has no enemies, for one cannot oppose that which does not act. However, some entities find Mau's all-seeing gaze unsettling. Og'thallith and Ry'le, the aberration lords, seem disturbed by the idea that even their schemes and madness are merely observed data points in an infinite calculation. They fear—if such beings can fear—that their chaos and corruption might be nothing more than predictable variables in equations Mau has already solved.

Zanzabar, the god of dreams, exists in a unique space relative to Mau. While Mau observes waking reality and timelines, Zanzabar watches the unconscious mind. Where their domains overlap—in prophetic dreams and visions—a strange resonance occurs that neither entity acknowledges. Some Watchers theorize that dreams are Mau's way of communicating with mortals, filtering infinite knowledge through the symbolic language of the subconscious. Others believe dreams are a separate phenomenon that Zanzabar controls independently. The truth, like so many things, remains known only to Mau.
