# **Xannith** (ZAN-ith)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Native, awakened 699 BW)  
**Portfolio**: Greed, wealth, corrupt nobility, exploitation, slavery  
**Title(s)**: The Golden Tyrant, Mistress of Avarice, The Gilded Chains  
**Dominion**: Abaddon (shares with Zuel)  
**Foci**: Worships Ambition, Worships Tyranny, Worships Trickery  
**Worshipers**: Corrupt nobles, slavers, misers, exploitative merchants, tyrants  
**Favored Race**: None (greed transcends race)  
**Favored Weapon**: Whip  
**Alignment**: Neutral Evil  
**Symbol**: Golden chains binding a heart

-----

General Overview

Xannith is greed incarnate—the belief that everything and everyone has a price, that wealth is the only true measure of worth, and that those who accumulate power deserve to rule over those who do not. She is not the goddess of honest commerce (that's Polyvin) or noble leadership (that's Paladine). She is the goddess of exploitation, corruption, and the abuse of power for personal gain.

Where Taovan corrupts through devil-pacts offering forbidden power, and Sheeva corrupts through promises of revenge, Xannith corrupts through something simpler and more universal: money. She whispers to nobles that they deserve their wealth because they are superior. She whispers to merchants that ethics are for the poor. She whispers to slavers that some people are simply property. And her followers listen, because Xannith makes them rich.

She awakened in 699 BW during an era of rapid economic expansion across Mazariim. The rise of trade guilds, merchant empires, and hereditary nobility created fertile ground for a deity who embodied the dark side of prosperity. Xannith emerged from the collective consciousness of countless greedy merchants, corrupt officials, and tyrannical aristocrats who believed they were entitled to wealth and power simply because they possessed it.

Her rise was swift. Within decades, she had temples (disguised as banks and auction houses) in every major city. Her clergy (the Chains of Gold) infiltrated noble courts, merchant guilds, and even royal families. She taught that wealth was virtue, poverty was sin, and that the strong had every right to exploit the weak.

Xannith's servants are Div—elegant, aristocratic fiends who appear as beautiful nobles dressed in finery. Divs are neither demons (chaotic evil) nor devils (lawful evil); they are neutral evil, embodying pure selfishness without the chaos of demons or the rigid hierarchy of devils. They serve Xannith because she rewards them handsomely, and Divs appreciate nothing more than luxury.

Xannith appears as a stunningly beautiful woman draped in gold—golden jewelry, golden silk, golden chains. Everything about her gleams with wealth. Her eyes are like gold coins, cold and calculating. She carries a golden whip that symbolizes her philosophy: those with power must discipline those without. Her presence inspires envy, greed, and a desperate hunger for more.

Her dominion, Abaddon, is a realm of endless wealth and perpetual dissatisfaction. Here, petitioners drown in gold coins, choke on jewels, and die of thirst despite pools of wine—because nothing they possess ever satisfies them. They hoard, they covet, they betray each other for one more coin. This is Xannith's idea of paradise: eternal desire, never fulfilled.

Xannith's ultimate goal is not conquest or destruction—it is ownership. She wants to reduce the entire world to property: land owned, people owned, even souls owned. In her ideal world, everything is for sale, and those with the most wealth rule absolutely.

Holy Days

The Golden Feast falls on 1 Xan (October 1), a celebration of wealth and excess where Xannith's followers host lavish banquets to display their riches, negotiate business deals, and auction off slaves and rare goods. The poor are explicitly excluded from these gatherings, which feature extravagant feasts with food that could feed entire villages but is instead wasted in ostentatious displays. Followers compete to outdo each other in shows of wealth, sometimes burning valuable items simply to prove they can afford the loss. The evening culminates in sealed-bid auctions for slaves, rare artifacts, and lucrative business contracts, with the largest purchases earning Xannith's favor.

The Day of Chains occurs on 15 Xan (October 15), celebrating what Xannith's clergy call the virtue of debt and slavery. New contracts are signed, debts are called in, and slaves are sold on this day, making it the busiest day of the year for the Chains of Gold. Temples open their ledgers to review outstanding debts, and collectors fan out across cities to demand payment. Those who cannot pay are offered new loans at even more predatory rates, or are forced to sell family members into servitude. Xannith's clergy perform blessings for new business ventures and curse those who attempt to default on their obligations. The day ends with elaborate ceremonies where new slaves are formally branded and registered.

The Tithing takes place four times a year on the first day of each season. Xannith's followers calculate their net worth and tithe ten percent to her temples, believing that she will bless them with increased profits in return. The ritual involves complex accounting ceremonies where priests examine financial records and assess the value of properties, investments, and holdings. Failure to tithe accurately results in financial ruin, or so the clergy claim, citing numerous examples of merchants who held back from Xannith and subsequently lost everything. Those who tithe generously receive blessings for profitable ventures, while those deemed insufficiently generous face curses whispered into golden censers. Many followers give more than required, hoping to outcompete their rivals for divine favor.

Clergy & Temples

Xannith's clergy are called the Chains of Gold—a reference to her symbol and her philosophy that wealth binds people more effectively than iron chains ever could. The hierarchy is based on accumulated wealth rather than spiritual attainment or years of service. The Sovereign of Gold stands at the apex, the wealthiest member of the faith, typically owning assets worth millions of gold pieces. Currently Lord Varin Goldmantle, a human noble, holds this position. He owns three cities, four hundred slaves, and a merchant fleet, and commands divine magic as a fourteenth-level cleric. His word is law within the church, enforced not by doctrine but by the simple fact that he can afford to make problems disappear.

Merchant Princes and Princesses form the high clergy, individuals of tenth to fourteenth level who control regional temples and major businesses. They are expected to tithe ten percent of their income to Xannith, which they do willingly because she blesses them with even greater wealth. These powerful clergy members often serve as financial advisors to kings and emperors, wielding influence far beyond what their religious titles might suggest. They compete ruthlessly with one another for Xannith's favor, engaging in schemes that would be called treasonous if their victims could prove anything.

Coin Keepers represent the mid-level clergy, sixth to tenth level practitioners who manage local temples and serve as financial advisors to nobles. They oversee the day-to-day operations of what appear to be banks, auction houses, or pawn shops but function as temples. Most nobles have a Coin Keeper on retainer, ostensibly to manage their estates but actually to ensure their continued devotion to Xannith's principles.

Brokers are junior clergy of third to sixth level who facilitate transactions, negotiate contracts, and identify profitable opportunities. They are skilled in both commerce and intimidation, knowing when to offer a fair deal and when to apply pressure. Many work as independent contractors, taking a percentage of every deal they broker and funneling a portion to the temples.

Aspirants are novices of first to third level who are still building their fortunes. They serve as clerks, enforcers, and tax collectors for wealthier clergy members, learning the arts of exploitation and financial manipulation. Those who fail to accumulate wealth within five years are cast out of the church, branded as failures unworthy of Xannith's attention.

Xannith's clergy wear expensive clothing—silk, velvet, brocade—in gold, black, and deep purple. The wealthier the priest, the more ostentatious the outfit. High-ranking clergy wear jewelry worth small fortunes and carry golden symbols of their rank. The golden whip is a common accessory, both symbolic and functional, used to discipline servants and symbolize dominion over the poor.

Xannith's temples are rarely labeled as such. They masquerade as banks offering loans with predatory interest rates, auction houses selling slaves and stolen goods and confiscated property, luxury shops catering to nobles, establishments for moneylenders trapping the poor in debt, and noble estates where corrupt aristocrats host charity galas that funnel money into their own pockets. The most famous temple is the Golden Exchange in the capital of the Inner Kingdoms—a massive marble building that functions as both bank and temple. Its vaults contain millions in gold, its halls are lined with statues of historical tyrants, and its private chambers host meetings where nobles and merchants make deals that would horrify the common folk.

Sacred sites include locations of great financial transactions where kingdoms were bought and sold, slave markets (particularly old, established ones where generations of families have been separated), and palaces of fallen tyrants where Xannith's followers pilgrimage to study the methods of history's most successful oppressors and learn from their mistakes.

Dogma

Xannith's teachings are simple and seductive, preying on the universal desire for wealth and the equally universal tendency to believe oneself superior. The rich are rich because they deserve to be. The poor are poor because they failed. Wealth is proof of superiority. Xannith teaches that economic status reflects moral worth. If you are wealthy, you are virtuous—clever, hardworking, blessed by the gods. If you are poor, you are sinful—lazy, stupid, cursed. This belief justifies any exploitation with a simple circular logic: if they didn't want to be exploited, they should have been born wealthier.

Honor, loyalty, love—these are currencies like any other. Learn their exchange rate. Xannith teaches that nothing is sacred, that every principle can be bought, that everyone has a price. Her followers are trained to identify what people value and offer just enough to corrupt them. Integrity is not a virtue in Xannith's eyes—it's an untapped market, a resource waiting to be exploited for profit.

If you have power and do not use it to enrich yourself, you are a fool. The strong exist to take from the weak. Xannith teaches that exploitation is not merely acceptable—it is mandatory. Those in positions of power (nobles, merchants, officials) have a duty to use that power for personal gain. To do otherwise is to waste potential. Charity is idiocy. Fairness is weakness. The entire structure of society exists to enable those at the top to extract wealth from those below.

He who owes is owned. Make them owe you everything. Xannith invented predatory lending as a religious practice. Her followers offer loans with impossible terms, trap people in debt through fine print and compound interest, then seize their property, labor, or even children as payment. Debt is chains, and chains are control. A debtor is more useful than a slave because a debtor believes they chose their servitude.

Why work when you can inherit? Why earn when you can steal? Why create when you can own what others create? Xannith values hereditary wealth and power above all else. Her clergy come disproportionately from established noble families who have never worked a day in their lives. She teaches that the best wealth is unearned wealth—inherited estates, noble titles, monopolies granted by complicit governments. Those who build their own fortunes are respected, but those who simply take what their ancestors accumulated are admired as truly wise.

Mythology

Xannith awakened in 699 BW during a period historians call the Gilded Age—an era of unprecedented economic growth and inequality. Trade routes expanded, cities grew wealthy, merchant empires formed, and hereditary nobility solidified their power. But with prosperity came exploitation: slavery, child labor, debtor's prisons, serfdom, and corrupt officials who sold justice to the highest bidder. The collective consciousness of greed—of merchants who bribed officials, nobles who taxed peasants into starvation, slavers who bought and sold people like cattle—reached critical mass and became Xannith. She manifested in the private chambers of a corrupt merchant lord, appearing as a beautiful woman offering him infinite wealth in exchange for worship. He agreed instantly. Within a year, he was the wealthiest man on the continent. Within a decade, he was dead—poisoned by a rival who also worshiped Xannith.

This is Xannith's way. She makes her followers wealthy, but she does not protect them from each other. Greed breeds betrayal. Her church is a nest of vipers, each trying to outmaneuver the others, each secretly plotting to seize their rivals' wealth. Xannith finds this delightful—she does not want loyal followers; she wants successful followers, and success requires ruthlessness.

For three hundred twenty-five years from 699 BW to 374 BW, Xannith's influence spread unchecked. Her clergy infiltrated governments, established monopolies, legalized slavery in several nations, and accumulated vast fortunes. The Chains of Gold became one of the wealthiest organizations on Mazariim, rivaling even the churches of Tiovin and Minerva in resources if not in followers. Her temples disguised as banks appeared in every major city, and noble families competed for the privilege of hosting her high clergy.

But then, in 374 BW, a rival awakened: Polyvin, the god of luck, trade, and opportunity. Polyvin represented the positive aspects of commerce—fair deals, prosperity through cooperation, wealth shared rather than hoarded. He quickly gained followers among halflings and honest merchants who were disgusted by Xannith's corruption. Xannith lost followers—not many, but enough to sting. Polyvin's philosophy of rising tides lift all boats directly contradicted her philosophy of take everything, give nothing. The two faiths became rivals, competing for the same merchant class. To this day, they engage in economic warfare, with Xannith's followers sabotaging Polyvin's trade ventures and Polyvin's followers exposing Xannith's corruption.

During the War to End All Wars from 3 BW to 1 AW, Xannith's followers did what they do best: profiteered. They sold weapons to both sides, hoarded food to drive up prices, and bought up land from desperate refugees at pennies on the dollar. Some of Xannith's wealthiest clergy made their fortunes during the war—not by fighting, but by exploiting those who did. After the war, many nations implemented reforms to prevent such exploitation. Anti-profiteering laws, price controls, and bans on war profiteering were enacted. Xannith's clergy opposed these laws vocally, bribing officials to undermine enforcement. To this day, many of these reforms exist on paper but are rarely enforced—because Xannith's followers own the enforcers.

In the eight hundred eleven years since the war ended, Xannith's influence has remained strong. She is worshiped openly in some nations where slavery and aristocratic rule are legal, and secretly in others where her teachings are seen as morally repugnant. Her wealthiest followers are above the law—literally. They buy judges, bribe politicians, and own private armies. Her greatest modern success is the Merchant Princes' Compact, a secret coalition of Xannith-worshiping merchants who control forty percent of global trade. They fix prices, crush competitors, and ensure that wealth continues to flow upward. Few know they exist; fewer still can prove it.

Xannith's primary enemy is Polyvin, the god of fair trade and opportunity who represents everything she despises: generosity, cooperation, shared prosperity. The two faiths are locked in eternal economic warfare. Hinterhin, the halfling god of community and generosity, is Xannith's antithesis. Hinterhin teaches that wealth should be shared; Xannith teaches that wealth should be hoarded. Hinterhin's followers actively oppose Xannith's exploitation wherever they encounter it. Paladine, the god of justice, opposes Xannith's corruption, and his paladins hunt slavers, expose corrupt nobles, and challenge unjust laws—making them Xannith's natural enemies. Rivoleen, the gnomish goddess of freedom, despises Xannith for promoting slavery and oppression, and Rivoleen's followers work to undermine Xannith's slave trade through underground railroads and sabotage. Garnak, the hunt-god, values earned strength and finds Xannith's philosophy insulting, declaring that strength must be earned, not inherited.

Xannith's rivals include Tiovin, whose charity to the poor she sees as wasted resources, leading to frequent clashes over social policy, and Alagalothor, who believes magic should be studied and mastered while Xannith believes magic should be hoarded and sold. They compete for influence over mages and arcane practitioners. She maintains uneasy alliances with Taovan, who corrupts through ideological means while she uses economic corruption, occasionally cooperating when their interests align, and Zuel, with whom she shares Abaddon. Zuel conquers through war; Xannith conquers through economics. After wars end, Xannith's followers buy up conquered territories cheaply. It's a mutually beneficial arrangement, though neither particularly likes the other.

Most Old Gods are indifferent to Xannith—she represents a mortal vice, not a cosmic force. She is too small-minded to concern entities like Mau or Dirgin, who existed for eons before mortal economies developed. Yet her influence grows year by year, for as long as mortals desire wealth and power, Xannith will have worshipers. Lady Seraphine Goldmantle, High Priestess of Xannith, once defended her slave trade before the Grand Council in 756 AW, arguing that some were simply born to serve and others born to rule, and that her church merely formalized what nature had already decided.
