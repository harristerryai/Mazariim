# Zebulba, The Entropic Void

**Portfolio:** Death, entropy, undeath, endings  
**Power Level:** Greater Entity (Old God)  
**Title:** The Entropic Void, Lord of Final Silence, The Black Flame  
**Dominion:** Negative Energy Plane  
**Alignment:** Chaotic Evil (CE)  
**Symbol:** Skull consumed by black flames  
**Favored Weapon:** Scythe  
**Worshipers:** Necromancers, undead, nihilists, death cults, those who seek endings  
**Favored Race:** None (predates racial distinctions, favors undead)  
**Foci:** Worships Undeath, Worships Death, Studies Necromancy

-----

General Overview

Zebulba is entropy given consciousness—the inevitable heat death of the universe, the final darkness, the silence after the last heartbeat. Unlike death gods of other pantheons who guide souls or maintain balance, Zebulba is pure negation: the end of energy, the cessation of being, the cold void that waits beyond all light.

To encounter Zebulba is to feel existence itself draining away. Not the clean finality of Mau's observation or the natural decay of Taovan's shadows, but something far worse—unmaking. Where Zebulba's attention falls, heat bleeds from reality, light dims, and the fundamental forces holding matter together begin to fail.

The Old God does not hate life, for hatred implies emotion. Zebulba simply is—the mathematical certainty that all systems decay, all energy dissipates, all things end. It is patient beyond comprehension, for it knows that given infinite time, everything will join it in the black silence.

Those who claim to have seen Zebulba and survived describe a void in the shape of a figure, a hole in reality draped in tattered robes that seem to be woven from extinguished starlight. Where a face should be, there is only absence—not even darkness, but the total negation of existence. Black flames flicker around it, cold fires that consume heat instead of producing it.

Holy Days

The Night of Unmaking falls on 30 Zebul (December 30), the longest night of the year when darkness holds dominion longest. Void-Touched perform mass necromantic rituals, animating undead servants and opening small breaches to the Negative Energy Plane. Civilized lands increase guard patrols on this night, knowing death cults become most active. The rituals often involve sacrificing living beings at sites of previous mass death, using the accumulated negative energy to tear temporary holes in reality. Those who witness these ceremonies report feeling their body heat drain away, their breath turning to frost even in mild weather.

The Silent Days span 1-3 Quarz (January 1-3), immediately following the Night of Unmaking. Void-Touched observe total silence for three days, neither eating nor speaking. This meditation is meant to simulate death's absolute stillness. Practitioners sit motionless in crypts or abandoned buildings, reducing their breathing and heartbeat to near-death levels. Some never wake from this meditation, their bodies found frozen in permanent stillness, expressions serene. The Void-Touched consider this an ideal death—passing peacefully into the silence they venerate.

Plague Moons occur whenever disease spreads through a population, making them variable rather than fixed holidays. Void-Touched consider these sacred times, venerating plagues as Zebulba's natural manifestation. They do not typically cause epidemics, but they observe them with reverence, sometimes tending to the dying not to ease suffering but to witness the moment of transition. When plagues strike, the faithful gather at pest houses and mass graves, meditating on the certainty of entropy and the futility of resistance.

The Commemoration of Madron falls on 15 Xan (February 15), marking the fall of Yrugas and the creation of The Hollow Sepulcher. Void-Touched pilgrimage to sealed crypts and mass graves, leaving offerings of bone and ash. They recite the names of the millions who died when the mageocracy collapsed, honoring the greatest expression of Zebulba's power in recorded history. Some pilgrims travel to the Hollow Sepulcher itself, though few return—those who enter the sealed temple either join the wardens permanently or are consumed by what dwells within.

Clergy & Temples

Zebulba's priests are called the Void-Touched, known as Zel'korum in Ancient Dwarvish. The title is both description and warning. To serve the Entropic Void is to feel its nature seeping into your soul—warmth fading, emotions dulling, the slow realization that nothing matters because nothing lasts. Most Void-Touched are necromancers who have stared too long into death and found something staring back. Others are nihilistic philosophers who worship entropy itself, or undead creatures who remember mortality and celebrate its absence.

Unlike other clergy, the Void-Touched have no formal hierarchy or organization. They simply exist in proximity to Zebulba's manifestations—ancient tombs, sites of mass death, breaches where the Negative Energy Plane touches the material world. Some are hermits who have not spoken in decades, their vocal cords withered from disuse. Others lead death cults in urban centers, recruiting the desperate and the doomed. The only qualification for leadership is proximity to entropy—those who have died and returned as undead, those who have opened breaches to the Negative Energy Plane, those who have witnessed their own humanity drain away until only cold purpose remains.

The Void-Touched wear black robes stained with ashes and graveyard soil. Many incorporate bones into their garments—finger bones woven into belts, skulls as pauldrons, vertebrae as prayer beads. Senior priests bear ceremonial scythes, often enchanted to drain life energy with each strike. Some priests undergo voluntary transformation, allowing their flesh to wither until they resemble the undead they serve, skin gray and tight against bone, eyes sunken and lightless.

Void-Touched necromancers perform the ritual called the Unmaking at sites of plague or battle, raising undead to honor what they call Zebulba's gift of unlife. These mass animations can produce dozens or hundreds of undead simultaneously, creating armies that serve no purpose beyond existing as monuments to entropy. The Cold Meditation involves priests sitting in absolute stillness for days in frigid environments, seeking to lower their body temperature until they achieve a trance state where Zebulba's whispers become audible. Those who succeed report hearing not words but the sound of heat leaving the universe, stars dying, and the mathematical certainty of endings. The Final Offering sees worshipers sacrifice living beings, or sometimes themselves, in ceremonies designed to open temporary breaches to the Negative Energy Plane. These breaches last only moments but flood the area with negative energy that can kill vegetation, sicken the living, and empower undead for miles around.

Zebulba's temples are not built—they form spontaneously wherever enough death accumulates. Mass graves, plague pits, battlefields soaked in blood, crypts sealed for millennia—these places become thin spots where the Negative Energy Plane presses close to reality. The most infamous temple is the Hollow Sepulcher, a massive catacomb beneath what was once the Yrugan city of Madron, destroyed around 2700 BW. Millions died when the mageocracy collapsed, and their unquiet dead formed a permanent breach. The Void-Touched maintain vigil there, serving as gatekeepers to prevent, or sometimes facilitate, undead incursions. These wardens live permanently within the sealed temple, existing in a twilight state between life and undeath, sustained by negative energy and their devotion to entropy.

Smaller shrines appear wherever necromancers gather: forgotten crypts, abandoned plague houses, execution grounds. These are marked with Zebulba's symbol carved into stone or burned into wood with cold-fire that never fully extinguishes. The flames consume no fuel and produce no heat, instead radiating cold that makes flesh ache and metal brittle. These markers serve as warnings to the living and beacons to those who serve the Entropic Void.

Dogma

Zebulba's doctrine is nihilistic and entropic, teaching that all things end regardless of struggle or hope. Life, love, kingdoms, gods themselves—all are temporary aberrations in the eternal void. The faithful are taught to embrace this truth rather than fight it, accepting the inevitability of entropy as a fundamental law of existence more certain than gravity or time.

Undeath is liberation according to Zebulba's teachings. Death frees the soul from the body, but undeath frees it further—from mortality's weakness, emotion's pain, and entropy's immediate touch. To become undead is to step outside the cycle of life and death, achieving a state of preservation that defies natural decay. The Void-Touched see vampires, liches, and other intelligent undead as enlightened beings who have accepted Zebulba's truth and transcended the limitations of living flesh.

Why prolong suffering when all warmth fades and all light dims? Those who spread death are merely servants of mathematical certainty, hastening an inevitable conclusion. The Void-Touched believe that mercy lies not in preserving life but in granting swift passage to the void. They view healers and life-preserving magic as cruel delays, forcing the dying to endure when release is at hand.

Reject the noise and chaos of existence, the teachings state. Seek the cold, the dark, the still. In absence, find truth. The faithful practice reducing stimulation—eating less, speaking less, feeling less—gradually acclimating themselves to the nothingness they believe awaits all things. Many elder Void-Touched have reduced their lives to near-death experiences sustained indefinitely, existing in meditative states where heartbeat and breathing slow to imperceptible levels.

Hope, morality, love, ambition—all are illusions that crumble before entropy's patient advance. Act accordingly. This nihilistic core teaches that ethical systems and emotional attachments are temporary constructs that will not survive the heat death of the universe. Therefore, conventional morality is meaningless, and actions should be judged not by their ethical weight but by their alignment with entropy's ultimate victory.

Mythology

Zebulba emerged alongside the other Old Gods around twenty-five thousand years before the War to End All Wars, but its nature is unique among them. Where Pyrantor is fire and Iquinias is water—things that exist—Zebulba is anti-existence, the principle of entropy that will ultimately unmake the universe itself. Some cosmological theories suggest Zebulba existed even before Mazariim formed, waiting in the void between universes. When two cosmos collided to create this world, Zebulba simply noticed. It had a new playground, a new reality to slowly drain of heat and life.

Early mortal civilizations around twenty thousand years before the War encountered death and tried to understand it. Most cultures developed ancestor worship or afterlife beliefs. But some—the desperate, the mad, the too-curious—found Zebulba instead. The first death cults appeared among humans and dwarves simultaneously, separate groups who independently contacted the Entropic Void through necromantic experimentation. These early Void-Touched animated corpses, drained life energy, and worshiped the cold silence they felt growing in their souls. Most such cults were destroyed by their own creations or purged by rival factions. But Zebulba's worship never fully vanished—it merely went underground, surfacing in new forms generation after generation.

The Yrugas mageocracy, centered in Madron and flourishing from approximately 4000 to 2700 BW, developed necromancy into an art form. While not explicitly worshiping Zebulba, their death-magic drew the Old God's attention. When Yrugas finally collapsed around 2700 BW—torn apart by magical catastrophe and slave revolts—millions died in weeks. The mass death event created the Hollow Sepulcher, Zebulba's greatest temple. For centuries afterward, undead armies poured from the ruins until a coalition of druids, clerics of Tiovin, and even some of Mordrion's paladins sealed the breach with powerful wards. The Void-Touched maintain these wards from inside the temple, a perpetual sacrifice to prevent something worse from emerging. They exist in a paradox—worshipers of entropy serving as its jailers, maintaining the seals that prevent their god's full manifestation while simultaneously venerating the force those seals contain.

During the War to End All Wars from 3 BW to 1 AW, Zebulba's influence grew. Battlefields created dozens of new thin spots where the Negative Energy Plane pressed close. Necromancers on both sides raised fallen soldiers as undead reinforcements. The sheer scale of death—millions perishing over years—made Zebulba more active than it had been in millennia. Some historians believe Zebulba fed on the war's carnage, growing stronger. Others argue this is projection—Zebulba doesn't grow or hunger, it simply exists more prominently where entropy is accelerated. After the war ended, many battle sites remained permanently tainted, becoming pilgrimage sites for the Void-Touched who seek places where the veil between life and death has worn thin.

In the modern era from 1 AW to 811 AW, Zebulba's worship is outlawed in most civilized nations. Temples are sealed or destroyed when discovered, and necromancers face execution in lands ruled by Tiovin's faithful. Yet the Old God endures, for entropy cannot be forbidden by law. Recent centuries have seen a quiet resurgence of Zebulba worship among certain groups. Academic necromancers who study undeath objectively gradually fall under the Old God's influence, their research transforming from clinical examination to religious devotion. Plague survivors who watched entire communities die sometimes turn to Zebulba, seeking meaning in horror that defies conventional religious explanations. Undead themselves—vampires, liches, and other intelligent undead—often develop reverence for the force that freed them from mortality, seeing Zebulba as the source of their liberation.

The Hollow Sepulcher remains the center of Zebulba's organized worship, with the Void-Touched who dwell in this massive temple-prison serving as both worshipers and wardens. In major cities, small groups of nihilists and necromancers gather in secret, performing minor rituals and studying forbidden texts. Most are discovered and executed within years, but new cults always form to replace them. In rare regions where undead rule openly, Zebulba often receives formal veneration as the source of their liberation from mortality. Scattered across Mazariim, isolated wizards study death magic in hidden towers and abandoned fortresses, gradually succumbing to the Old God's influence. During epidemics, desperate or insane individuals sometimes form impromptu Zebulba cults, spreading disease as sacred work. These are inevitably destroyed, but the pattern repeats with each new plague.

Zebulba's relationships with other entities reflect its nature as pure entropy. Mau the Oracle observed Zebulba's emergence with neither approval nor concern—entropy is simply another cosmic force to be catalogued. Some Stonekeepers claim Mau and Zebulba formed an accord: Mau observes endings, Zebulba creates them. Tiovin and Taovan, the twin gods of life and death, are Zebulba's direct antithesis. Where Tiovin celebrates life and Taovan guides souls, Zebulba seeks to end both life and afterlife, reducing all to void. Tiovin's faithful hunt Void-Touched relentlessly, viewing them as existential threats to the natural order.

Asti, the Draconic Healer, stands as Zebulba's polar opposite—positive energy versus negative, healing versus entropy, preservation versus decay. Ancient texts describe a battle between their avatars around 10,000 BW that devastated an entire region, leaving it a lifeless waste that still bears neither plant nor animal. The area remains cursed, a dead zone where positive and negative energy fought to mutual annihilation, leaving only absence.

Pyrantor represents entropy versus energy, philosophically opposed but too alien to truly conflict. Where they meet, fires burn cold and consume nothing—a phenomenon called black flame that radiates freezing temperatures instead of heat. Other Old Gods have no meaningful relationships with Zebulba. It exists alongside them, patient as heat death itself, waiting for the universe to wind down. Intelligent undead often feel drawn to Zebulba without understanding why. Vampires report dreams of cold silence. Liches experience visions of the Negative Energy Plane. Whether this is Zebulba calling them or simply recognition of shared nature remains unclear.

The Archlich Velthran, before his phylactery crumbled to dust in his final moments, spoke words that sum up Zebulba's philosophy: the end is perfect, and resistance against it is futile. Why struggle when entropy is inevitable, when all warmth will fade and all light will dim? The Void-Touched have no answer except to embrace the darkness and wait for the final silence.
