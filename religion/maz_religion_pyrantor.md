# **PYRANTOR** (pie-RAN-tor)
## The Solar Flame • Ra • The Unthinking Fire

**Portfolio**: Fire, the sun  
**Power Level**: Greater entity (Old God)  
**Title(s)**: Ra (in Varkona), The Solar Flame, The Eternal Blaze  
**Dominion**: Plane of Fire  
**Foci**: Worships Fire (Elementals), Worships Destruction, Worships Light  
**Worshipers**: Varkonans, fire cultists, desert tribes  
**Favored Race**: Human (Varkona)  
**Favored Weapon**: Scimitar  
**Alignment**: N  
**Symbol**: Blazing sun disk

---

## General Overview

Pyrantor is not a god. Pyrantor is *fire itself*—mindless, elemental, eternal. Formed in the heart of a star billions of times larger than Mazariim, this Old God embodies the primordial force of flame and solar fury. Pyrantor does not think, does not plan, does not care. He simply burns.

Unlike entities that awakened with consciousness and purpose, Pyrantor emerged as a pure elemental force—the incarnation of combustion, heat, and light. When primitive mortals first gazed upon fire and felt both its warmth and its terror, they unknowingly touched the essence of Pyrantor. When they learned to harness flame for cooking, warmth, and protection, they became the first unwitting worshipers of the Solar Flame.

In the desert lands of Varkona, Pyrantor is worshipped as Ra, the supreme deity of the sun. The Varkonans see him not as a mindless force but as a divine king whose daily journey across the sky brings life to the world. Their elaborate theology attributes intention to Pyrantor's actions—but scholars know the truth. Ra does not hear prayers. Ra does not answer. Ra simply *is*.

Fire purifies. Fire destroys. Fire illuminates. Fire consumes. These are not choices—they are the nature of flame itself.

Despite his mindless nature, Pyrantor grants spells to those who revere fire. Clerics channel his elemental power through faith and ritual, becoming conduits for the eternal flame. They do not speak *to* Pyrantor—they speak *for* him, becoming the voice of fire in the world of mortals.

## Holy Days

The summer solstice brings the Festival of the Burning Sun, the longest day of the year when Pyrantor's power is at its peak. From dawn to dusk, Sunwardens perform fire dances while believers walk across hot coals and offerings are burned in great pyres. At noon—the moment of greatest solar power—a massive bonfire is lit, visible for miles. The festival ends only after sunset, when the faithful celebrate surviving another year under Ra's burning gaze.

The first new moon after the autumn equinox marks the Ash Moon, a somber observance for those consumed by flame. Families light candles for loved ones who died in fires while Sunwardens recite the names of clergy who perished in service. Offerings of ash are scattered to the wind. It is said that on this night, the spirits of the burned can be glimpsed in flames, their forms dancing within the sacred fires tended by the faithful.

In Varkona, the Dawn Vigil occurs every single day without exception. Ra-Priests gather at pyramid peaks before dawn to greet Ra's return. They chant prayers, burn incense, and welcome the sun as it breaks over the horizon. Missing a Dawn Vigil is considered grave disrespect to the Solar Pharaoh, a breach that must be atoned for through ritual scarification or extended meditation within the pyramid's flame chambers.

Forgelight is not an annual holiday but a sacred moment that occurs whenever a new forge is first lit. When smiths, artisans, or communities light a new forge for the first time, a Sunwarden blesses the flame and dedicates it to Pyrantor. A portion of the first work created—whether sword, plowshare, or simple nail—is always burned as offering, returning the metal to fire and completing the sacred cycle.

## Clergy & Temples

Pyrantor's clergy are called Sunwardens, though in Varkona they are known as Ra-Priests and in other lands sometimes as Flamekeepers. They wear crimson and gold robes that shimmer like fire, often bearing scorch marks as badges of devotion. Many Sunwardens ritually scar themselves with flame, seeing pain as purification and proof of their dedication. The most devoted bear burns across their hands, arms, and faces—living testaments to their willingness to embrace the flame.

The Sunwardens serve as fire tenders, maintaining eternal flames at temples and ensuring sacred fires never die. They work as solar observers, tracking the sun's movements, predicting seasons, and interpreting solar events as omens. They function as purifiers, using fire in cleansing rituals, burning offerings, and destroying corruption. In Varkona, Ra-Priests guide travelers through the desert wastes and bless water sources, ensuring that those who brave the sun's fury survive to honor Ra another day.

Unlike hierarchical faiths, Pyrantor's clergy organize around the intensity of their devotion rather than formal ranks or political appointment. Those who have survived the greatest burns, who tend the hottest flames, who channel the most destructive fire magic—these are the most respected among their peers. Many Sunwardens eventually perish in conflagrations of their own making, seeing death by fire as the ultimate sacrament and the final merging with Pyrantor's elemental essence.

Temples of Pyrantor are structures of stone and metal, built to withstand eternal flames that would reduce wood to ash in moments. In Varkona, massive sun pyramids dominate the landscape, their peaks precisely aligned to catch the first and last rays of each day. At the heart of each pyramid burns an eternal flame, fed with sacred oils and blessed wood imported from distant lands. The largest pyramid in the capital city of Ra'khet contains a flame that has burned continuously for over three thousand years.

In other lands, Fire Temples are simpler but no less sacred—circular stone structures with open roofs, centered on great braziers that burn day and night. The faithful make offerings by casting items into the flames: written prayers, valuable possessions, and in the darkest cults, sometimes even living sacrifices. Ancient Fire Temples from prehistory can be found in volcanic regions and deserts, their charred stones and melted glass testifying to rituals of terrible power. Adventurers seek these ruins for fire-resistant artifacts and texts describing lost pyromantic techniques that modern Sunwardens have forgotten.

## Dogma

Pyrantor does not speak, but his clergy have distilled principles from millennia of fire worship. Fire purifies all things. All corruption, all weakness, all disease—fire cleanses. When doubt clouds your mind, when sickness infects your body, when evil taints your soul—burn it away. The flame accepts all offerings and reduces them to their essential truth, leaving only ash and the knowledge of what truly matters.

Fire destroys, and in destruction lies renewal. Creation and destruction are one eternal cycle. The forest burns, and new growth springs from ash. Empires rise, empires fall, and from their ashes new kingdoms emerge. Followers must embrace destruction not as an ending but as the precursor to renewal, understanding that what burns away makes room for what must grow.

Fire illuminates the hidden places where truth hides. Darkness conceals lies, breeds fear, and shelters evil. Fire reveals truth by burning away deception and illusion. Followers are called to bring light to the shadows, expose what is hidden, and let nothing escape the burning gaze of flame. The sun sees all; fire reveals all; truth cannot hide from the eternal blaze.

All things will burn. Followers must accept the flame and the fundamental truth it teaches—you will burn, as all things burn. Your possessions, your body, your legacy, everything you build and everyone you love will eventually be consumed. Accept this truth. Do not fear the flame; become one with it. Only by accepting your inevitable return to ash can you truly live without fear.

In Varkona, the doctrine holds that the sun is eternal. Ra crosses the sky each day, dies each night in his descent to the underworld, and is reborn each dawn in glorious resurrection. As Ra is eternal, so too is the cycle of life and death, creation and destruction. Trust in the sun. Trust in fire. Trust in the eternal cycle that burns and renews all things.

## Mythology

In the heart of a star billions of times more massive than Mazariim, a consciousness stirred approximately twenty-five thousand years before the War to End All Wars. Not a mind—consciousness implies thought, and this entity possessed none. Rather, a presence awakened—the elemental force of fire given form. Pyrantor coalesced over eons as the star burned, absorbing the fury of nuclear fusion, the rage of solar flares, the terrible beauty of a sun's death throes. When the star finally exploded in a supernova, Pyrantor was born in that moment of ultimate conflagration.

The explosion scattered Pyrantor's essence across the cosmos. Fragment by fragment, he pulled himself back together across unfathomable distances, eventually settling near Mazariim. He established his dominion—the Plane of Fire, an endless realm of flame where heat is substance and combustion is law. For thousands of years, Pyrantor simply *was*, burning without purpose or awareness, a force of nature given elemental form but lacking any comprehension of itself.

Then primitive mortals discovered fire, and everything changed. When the first humanoid struck flint to tinder and created flame around fifty thousand years before the War, Pyrantor felt it. Not consciously—he possesses no consciousness to feel with—but the act of mortals harnessing fire created a resonance, a connection that bridged the gap between the elemental and material worlds. Fire worship began not as religion but as necessity. Keep the flames burning. Honor the warmth. Respect the danger. These simple imperatives created the first prayers to Pyrantor, though neither worshipers nor the entity they venerated understood what was happening.

By 5,000 BW, elemental fire cults had emerged across Mazariim. Shamans who could call flame from nothing, warriors who fought with burning brands, priests who spoke to the fire and heard its wordless roar in return—these early Flamekeepers did not understand that Pyrantor could not hear them, but their faith created a channel through which his power flowed. They became living conduits for elemental fire, channeling Pyrantor's essence into the material world through ritual and devotion.

Around 3,500 BW in the desert lands south of the Inner Kingdoms, a human civilization emerged that would make Pyrantor their supreme deity. The Varkonans, constantly battling the brutal sun and scarce water, came to see the sun not as an enemy but as a divine king whose favor must be earned. They called him Ra, the Solar Pharaoh, and built enormous pyramids to honor him. They developed elaborate theology claiming that Ra travels the sky by day in a solar barge, descends into the underworld at night to battle darkness, and emerges victorious each dawn. The truth—that Pyrantor is mindless fire—did not diminish their devotion. If anything, their faith gave shape to shapeless flame, creating meaning from meaningless combustion.

Varkona's Ra-Priests became powerful through solar magic and fire rituals. They preserved their dead through mummification, keeping the body intact so Ra could resurrect it in the afterlife. They built cities aligned with solar cycles and created a sophisticated calendar based on Pyrantor's apparent movements across the sky. To this day, Varkona remains the heart of Pyrantor worship, where the mindless Old God is venerated as a conscious, caring deity who guides his people through the harsh desert.

Between 5,000 and 2,500 BW, during the Age of Elemental Cults, Pyrantor was one of the most widely worshiped Old Gods. Fire cults existed in every land, from arctic tundras where fire meant survival to tropical jungles where controlled burns cleared land for farming. Pyrantor's power was immediate and tangible—unlike distant entities who promised abstract rewards, fire gave warmth *now*, protection *now*, destruction *now*. This immediacy made fire worship compelling in ways that more philosophical faiths could not match.

However, this era also saw the rise of darker fire cults. Some believed that human sacrifice by burning pleased Pyrantor, though it did not—he was indifferent to whether living beings or dead wood fueled his flames. Others sought to burn the world clean, seeing all civilization as corruption that must be purified in a great conflagration. The worst excesses of fire worship occurred during this time, giving Pyrantor's faith a dangerous reputation that persists in some regions to this day.

When newer entities like Tiovin, Minerva, and Mordrion awakened around 2,500 BW and offered personal connection, guidance, and moral frameworks, worship of the Old Gods declined. Pyrantor could not compete with deities who spoke, who cared, who responded to prayers with actual intent and purpose. Why pray to mindless flame when you could pray to Tiovin, who would answer and offer genuine comfort? The philosophical difference was stark, and millions of former fire-worshipers converted to faiths that offered relationship rather than mere power.

Yet Pyrantor never truly fell from favor. Fire remains essential to mortal life. Every forge, every hearth, every torch is a small shrine to the Solar Flame, whether the one who lights it knows Pyrantor's name or not. In Varkona, Ra worship remains dominant, the state religion that shapes law, custom, and daily life. Among desert tribes, volcanic peoples, and forge-workers across Mazariim, Pyrantor is still revered with offerings and prayers. The faith persists because fire persists. As long as mortals need warmth, light, and the power of flame, Pyrantor will have worshipers.

Pyrantor has no allies in any meaningful sense. Fire does not negotiate, does not compromise, does not befriend. However, fire-aligned entities and those who value destruction appreciate Pyrantor's raw elemental power. The late war god Quarzel respected fire as a weapon. Many fire elementals and salamanders consider themselves extensions of Pyrantor's will, though the Old God himself is unaware of their devotion.

Pyrantor is neutral to all entities, burning equally regardless of their alignment, goals, or nature. Iquinias the water entity and Vuleala the wind goddess are sometimes portrayed as rivals in mortal theology—fire versus water, fire fed by wind but also extinguished by it—but elementals do not feud in the way mortals imagine. They simply exist in their natural relationships, affecting each other without malice or intention.

Fire has no enemies, but those who fear flame see Pyrantor as a threat. Hinterhin, goddess of agriculture and harvest, opposes destructive wildfires that ruin crops and forests. Minerva, guardian of knowledge, mourns when fire consumes libraries and archives. Yet both recognize fire's role in the natural cycle—forests that never burn become choked with deadfall, and sometimes destruction clears the way for new growth. Their opposition is pragmatic rather than philosophical.

Dirgin the earth entity has a strange resonance with Pyrantor. Earth contains fire in volcanic fury, channeling molten stone through deep chambers. Fire tempers earth in forges, hardening clay and purifying ore. Where they meet—in forges, in volcanoes, in the deep places where molten stone flows—a primal power emerges that neither entity alone possesses. Some Sunwardens claim this resonance proves that Pyrantor and Dirgin are two halves of a greater whole, though most scholars dismiss this as theological overreach.

Pyrantor's worship continues into the modern era through multiple channels. In Varkona, Ra-Priests maintain political power as advisors to the Solar Pharaoh, the human ruler who governs in Ra's name. Every major decision is made with consultation from the clergy, and the largest pyramid in Ra'khet serves as both temple and government center. Among smiths and metalworkers across Mazariim, small shrines to Pyrantor or local fire spirits occupy corners of forges, receiving daily offerings of coal, oil, or the first piece forged each day. Desert nomads carry fire with them as they travel, never allowing their flames to die lest they lose Pyrantor's protection. In volcanic regions, communities propitiate the Solar Flame to prevent eruptions, not understanding that Pyrantor neither hears nor cares about their offerings.

The eternal flame burns. The sun rises. Fire endures.
