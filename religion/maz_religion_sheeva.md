# **Sheeva** (SHEE-vah)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Immigrant from Talernon)  
**Portfolio**: Poison, revenge, spite, assassination  
**Title(s)**: The Poisoner, Lady of Vengeance, The Widow's Blade  
**Dominion**: The Abyss (shares with Og'thallith)  
**Foci**: Worships Death, Worships Trickery, Studies Necromancy  
**Worshipers**: Assassins, the vengeful, scorned lovers, those seeking retribution  
**Favored Race**: None (accepts all who seek revenge)  
**Favored Weapon**: Poisoned dagger  
**Alignment**: Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol**: Serpent coiled around a dagger

---

## General Overview

Sheeva is vengeance incarnate—cold, patient, and absolutely merciless. She is not the goddess of justice or righteous retribution; she is the goddess of revenge for revenge's sake, of spite, of making those who wronged you suffer as you have suffered. Where Taovan offers power through devil-pacts and Og'thallith spreads mindless madness, Sheeva offers something simpler and more personal: the tools to make your enemies pay.

She arrived on Mazariim in 1492 BW from Talernon, a universe that was destroyed by magical hubris. According to the holy texts in the Library of the Ages, Sheeva was locked in an apocalyptic battle with Alagalothor, then a dragon, and Thalaman, a time-traveling archmage. The conflict escalated to cosmic proportions when Thalaman attempted a desperate spell using Sheeva's avatar as a power source. The spell went catastrophically wrong, causing vacuum decay that began unmaking their entire universe.

Alagalothor managed to escape, but the spell's backlash transported all three entities to Mazariim. In the process, a portion of Sheeva's power was siphoned by both Alagalothor and Thalaman, diminishing her but also binding the three together in a complicated web of hatred, grudging respect, and mutual wariness. Sheeva has never forgiven them. She never forgives anyone.

On Talernon, Sheeva was a deity of considerable power—perhaps even a greater force than she is now. But the catastrophic transition cost her dearly. Scholars estimate she lost thirty to forty percent of her divine essence in the planar crossing. This loss only deepened her bitterness. She arrived on Mazariim diminished, enraged, and hungry for revenge against the two entities who cost her so much—and against a universe that dared to exist when hers did not.

Sheeva claimed a domain in the Abyss, sharing that chaotic plane with Og'thallith. The two deities are not allies—Og'thallith values random chaos and madness while Sheeva values calculated revenge and cold spite. They tolerate each other's presence but do not cooperate. Sheeva's domain within the Abyss is called the Gardens of Vitriol—a twisted landscape of poisonous plants, venomous creatures, and monuments to famous acts of revenge throughout history.

Her servants are Demodands—tar-black fiends with claws dripping venom and hearts filled with bitterness. Demodands are neither demons in the chaotic evil sense nor devils in the lawful evil sense; they occupy a middle ground, embodying personal malice and spite. They do not serve abstract concepts like chaos or order—they serve grudges. And Sheeva has an infinite supply of grudges.

Sheeva appears as a beautiful woman cloaked in shadows, her eyes like polished emeralds, her smile like a razor. She carries a dagger that drips with black venom, and wherever she walks, flowers wither and die. Those who see her describe feeling an overwhelming sense of betrayal—as though everyone they ever trusted has let them down. This is Sheeva's gift: she makes you remember every slight, every insult, every moment someone hurt you. And then she whispers: "Don't you want them to pay?"

Her ultimate goal is not conquest or destruction—it is eternal suffering for those who wronged her. She will never stop hunting Alagalothor and Thalaman. She will never stop whispering to mortals who feel betrayed. She will never stop teaching that forgiveness is weakness and revenge is the only true justice. Because Sheeva never forgives, and she never forgets.

## Holy Days

The thirteenth of Taovan (November 13) brings the Night of Knives, the anniversary of Sheeva's arrival on Mazariim in 1492 BW. Her followers spend the night sharpening blades, brewing poisons, and swearing oaths of revenge. This is considered the holiest night of the year for those who worship the Lady of Vengeance. Widows induct new members on this night, bringing them before altars stained with the blood of past betrayers and asking them to swear that they will never forgive, never forget, and never show mercy to those who wronged them. Weapon sharpening rituals are performed with meditative precision, each stroke of the whetstone accompanied by whispered remembrances of grievances. Poison brewing occurs in temple workshops, with master Widows teaching apprentices the proper methods for extracting venom from serpents, distilling nightshade, and preparing hemlock. Stories of successful revenge are shared among the faithful, with particularly impressive acts of vengeance commemorated in the Hall of Remembrance.

Each follower observes a personal Day of Remembrance on the anniversary of their greatest grievance—the day they were betrayed, the day a loved one was killed, the day they lost everything. Unlike the communal Night of Knives, this observance is solitary and intensely personal. Followers spend the day in silent meditation, feeding their hatred, reviewing their plans for revenge, and refusing to let time dull the sharp edge of their anger. They visit Sheeva's altars to leave offerings, usually symbolic items tied to the grievance—a lock of hair from the betrayer, a piece of jewelry that was stolen, a fragment of a broken wedding vow. The Day of Remembrance serves to keep wounds fresh, to prevent healing, to maintain the burning rage that Sheeva teaches is the only appropriate response to being wronged.

The Widow's Feast is not celebrated on a fixed date but occurs whenever a follower successfully enacts revenge. When a disciple of Sheeva achieves their vengeance, they host a feast for fellow Widows, recounting in exquisite detail the planning, execution, and final moments of their target's suffering. The meal always includes at least one dish made with poisonous ingredients, carefully prepared to be non-lethal as a test of the Widow's skill in poison craft. To serve a poisoned dish that actually kills a guest would be a tremendous embarrassment, proving incompetence. To serve a poisoned dish that causes no sensation at all demonstrates mastery. The recounting of the revenge tale follows a ritual structure, with the successful Widow describing first the original grievance, then the years of planning and patience, then the moment of execution, and finally the target's realization that justice had come for them. If the revenge was significant—the assassination of a noble, the destruction of an entire family line, the ruining of a powerful merchant—the successful Widow may be elevated in the clergy hierarchy during this feast.

## Clergy & Temples

Sheeva's clergy are called Widows regardless of gender—those who have lost something precious and seek revenge. The title signifies that they have died to their old lives and now exist only to settle accounts with those who wronged them. The hierarchy is based not on years of service or theological knowledge but on the magnitude of revenge achieved. A Widow who successfully destroys a powerful enemy rises in rank; a Widow who fails or shows mercy is cast out.

The highest-ranking Widow bears the title of Matriarch or Patriarch of Vitriol, typically a cleric or rogue of twelfth level or higher who has achieved a legendary act of revenge that inspires awe and fear among the faithful. Currently this position is held by Matriarch Velenna Shadowsting, a half-elf who poisoned the entire royal family that executed her parents for treason. Her parents were innocent, framed by political rivals, and Velenna spent thirty years infiltrating the royal household as a trusted servant before finally administering the poison that killed the king, queen, and all three heirs in a single night. Her patience and thoroughness are held up as the ideal of Sheeva's teachings.

Venom Speakers are senior clergy between eighth and twelfth level who lead regional temples and train assassins in the arts of poison, stealth, and patience. They are masters of poison craft, able to identify any toxin by taste and create custom venoms for specific targets. They also excel at political manipulation, understanding that revenge often requires destroying not just a person but their reputation, their legacy, and everything they hold dear.

Widows are full clergy members between fourth and eighth level who have completed at least one major revenge—killing the person who wronged them or utterly destroying their lives in some other satisfying manner. They wear black veils that obscure their faces and carry poisoned daggers blessed by Sheeva herself. These daggers are considered holy relics, each one anointed with venom drawn from serpents raised in the Gardens of Vitriol.

The Scorned are junior members between first and fourth level who are still pursuing their first revenge. They serve as apprentices, learning poison craft, stealth techniques, and most importantly, patience. Sheeva teaches that rushed revenge is often failed revenge, and the Scorned spend years preparing for their moment. Many never achieve it, dying before they can settle their accounts, but those who succeed earn their place among the Widows.

The Bitter are lay worshipers who have sworn vengeance but not yet joined the clergy formally. They visit temples for advice, purchasing poisons and receiving blessings for their revenge efforts. Some eventually join the clergy; others remain independent agents who simply share Sheeva's philosophy that forgiveness is weakness and revenge is the only true justice.

Sheeva's clergy wear black and deep green, the colors of poison and shadow. High-ranking Widows wear veils that obscure their faces, symbolizing that they have died to their old identities and now live only for revenge. All clergy carry at least one poisoned weapon and, ironically, a vial of antidote. Sheeva teaches that poisons must be respected—careless poisoners poison themselves, and there is no glory in dying to your own incompetence.

Sheeva's temples are hidden, operating in secret to avoid the attention of authorities who universally outlaw her worship. Temples are often disguised as apothecaries selling medicinal herbs that are actually poisons, perfumeries where venomous concoctions are masked by pleasant scents, orphanages that recruit children who lost parents to injustice and raise them as future assassins, or taverns where the Widows listen for tales of betrayal and offer solutions to the aggrieved.

The largest known temple is the House of the Widow in the undercity of a major human metropolis, its exact location kept secret even from most worshipers. The temple features the Garden of Grievances, a greenhouse containing every poisonous plant known to exist on Mazariim—nightshade, hemlock, wolfsbane, deathcap mushrooms, and dozens of exotic specimens imported from distant lands. The Hall of Remembrance has walls inscribed with the names and descriptions of famous acts of revenge throughout history, serving as inspiration for current Widows. The Venom Vaults house libraries of poison recipes and assassination techniques, scrolls detailing the weaknesses of various political figures, and preserved specimens of rare venomous creatures. The Altar of Spite stands at the temple's heart, where followers pray not for blessings but for the suffering of their enemies.

Sacred sites include locations where famous acts of revenge occurred. Battlefields where betrayers were slain become pilgrimage destinations. Palaces where poisoned kings died are marked with secret symbols known only to the Widows. Execution grounds where innocents were wrongly killed are treated as holy ground, places where Sheeva's presence is strongest because the injustice committed there cries out for vengeance.

## Dogma

Sheeva's teachings are simple and brutal, expressing a philosophy that rejects forgiveness and elevates revenge to the highest virtue. Never forgive and never forget. They wronged you. They hurt you. They took from you. Do not forgive them. Do not move on. Do not let time heal the wounds or dull the anger. Make them pay. Sheeva teaches that forgiveness is weakness, that turning the other cheek is cowardice, that letting go of grudges is a betrayal of the self. Every wrong must be answered. Every slight must be avenged. This is not justice in any legal or moral sense—it is revenge, pure and personal.

Patience is a weapon, perhaps the greatest weapon in the arsenal of the wronged. Revenge served cold is revenge served best. Wait for the perfect moment. Watch your enemy and learn their patterns. Study their weaknesses and their fears. And when they have forgotten their crime against you, when they believe they have escaped consequences, remind them in the most devastating way possible. Sheeva values patience above all other virtues. Her followers are taught to wait years, even decades, for the perfect moment to strike. The longer the wait, the sweeter the revenge. Immediate retaliation is crude and often fails; calculated vengeance is an art form.

Poison is honesty, the great equalizer between the weak and the strong. Swords are for heroes, for those born to privilege and trained in combat from childhood. Poison is for those who understand the world as it truly is: cruel, unfair, and indifferent to justice. Knights fight with swords because they have the luxury of fair combat. The scorned fight with venom because they must use whatever weapons are available. Sheeva teaches that there is no shame in this approach—there is only pragmatism. The powerful will always have advantages in open combat, but poison allows the powerless to strike back effectively. A servant can poison a lord. A commoner can kill a noble. A victim can destroy their oppressor. This is true justice.

Trust no one, for everyone will betray you eventually. Accept this truth. Use it. Sheeva's followers are paranoid and isolated by design. They form alliances out of necessity but never out of genuine trust. Every relationship is transactional, every friendship is temporary, every partnership is a prelude to inevitable betrayal. Better to expect treachery and prepare for it than to be blindsided by someone you foolishly trusted. This teaching creates a self-fulfilling prophecy—Widows who trust no one behave in ways that make them untrustworthy, which confirms their belief that betrayal is universal.

Make them suffer, for death is mercy and mercy must never be granted. Do not simply kill your enemies—that would be too kind. Poison their food slowly so they waste away over months, their bodies failing piece by piece. Ruin their reputations so they become pariahs. Destroy their families so they lose everything they love. Make them watch their world crumble. Make them understand that what is happening to them is punishment for what they did to you. Only when they have suffered as you have suffered, only when they have lost as you have lost, should you grant them the mercy of death. And even then, make that death slow and painful.

## Mythology

On Talernon, Sheeva was a powerful deity of poison and revenge, served by legions of Demodands and worshiped by assassins, the betrayed, and those who valued vengeance above all else. She was locked in an eternal conflict with two rivals who threatened her dominion. Alagalothor was a chromatic dragon of immense power who sought to control all magic, viewing Sheeva's poison-based divine magic as a challenge to his authority. Thalaman was a time-traveling archmage who had ascended to near-godhood through chronomancy, able to manipulate timelines and undo the victories of his enemies.

The three-way conflict escalated for centuries, each entity striking at the others and being struck in turn. Sheeva poisoned Alagalothor's servants, corrupting them from within and turning his own followers against him. Alagalothor responded by burning her temples with dragon fire, reducing centuries of accumulated knowledge to ash. Thalaman manipulated timelines to undo both their victories, creating temporal paradoxes where battles that had been won suddenly had never occurred. The conflict seemed eternal, with no clear victor possible.

The battle reached a breaking point when Sheeva's avatar manifested on the material plane to personally kill Alagalothor. She had grown tired of proxy wars and decided that only direct confrontation would end the stalemate. Thalaman, observing the impending clash and realizing that whoever emerged victorious would then turn their full attention to him, made a desperate decision. He would attempt a spell of unimaginable complexity—a planar teleportation powered by siphoning energy directly from Sheeva's divine essence while she was distracted by combat.

The spell worked, but it worked far too well. Instead of merely teleporting Thalaman to a safe distance, the spell triggered a vacuum decay event—a spreading wave of un-reality that began erasing Talernon from existence. The fundamental laws of physics in that universe began to break down, reality unraveling like a frayed rope. Entire continents disappeared in moments. Civilizations that had stood for thousands of years vanished as though they had never existed. The universe itself began to collapse inward, reality consuming itself.

Alagalothor, in a rare act of self-preservation over pride, tore open a portal to another universe—Mazariim—and fled through it to escape the spreading destruction. But the spell's backlash was still connected to both him and Sheeva through the stolen power. The dimensional rift pulled Thalaman and Sheeva through as well, along with a significant portion of Sheeva's divine essence that Thalaman's spell had already siphoned. All three arrived on Mazariim in 1492 BW, forever linked by the catastrophe they had caused.

Sheeva's arrival on Mazariim was traumatic beyond description. She lost thirty to forty percent of her divine power in the transition, diminished from a major deity whose name inspired terror across an entire universe to a greater entity still rebuilding her strength in an unfamiliar world. She found herself in an alien universe, surrounded by strange gods whose very existence offended her, her home destroyed, her servants scattered across dimensional boundaries.

Her first act was to claim territory in the Abyss, fighting off demons and other entities to carve out the Gardens of Vitriol. Her second act was to summon surviving Demodands from the ruins of Talernon, calling through the dimensional void to those few of her servants who had survived the universe's collapse. Her third act was to begin plotting revenge against Alagalothor and Thalaman for destroying everything she had built.

But she quickly realized that direct confrontation would fail. Both entities had also arrived diminished but were adapting to Mazariim faster than she was. Alagalothor had already begun establishing a power base among mortal wizards, teaching them magic and accepting worship in return. Thalaman, fragmented across multiple timelines due to temporal madness caused by the catastrophe, was unpredictable and dangerous, appearing and disappearing at random intervals.

So Sheeva did what she does best: she waited. She established her faith among assassins and the vengeful, finding mortals who understood that sometimes justice requires a poisoned blade. She spread her teachings, whispering to those who had been wronged that forgiveness was weakness and revenge was strength. She learned about Mazariim's politics, its wars, its endless cycles of betrayal and retribution. And she whispered to those who had been hurt: "I can teach you how to make them pay."

Sheeva did not participate directly in the War to End All Wars that erupted between 3 BW and 1 AW. She views large-scale warfare as crude and wasteful—too impersonal, too chaotic, too unfocused. Revenge must be personal, targeted, intimate. However, her followers worked behind the scenes throughout the conflict, assassinating key generals whose deaths would prolong the suffering, poisoning supply lines to starve armies, and sowing distrust among allies by forging letters and planting false evidence of betrayal.

Some scholars believe Sheeva's Widows prolonged the war by sabotaging peace negotiations, ensuring that hatred remained fresh and wounds remained open. Others argue she shortened it by eliminating particularly bloodthirsty warlords whose removal allowed more reasonable leaders to negotiate. The truth is likely both—Sheeva cares nothing for peace or war in the abstract, only for revenge in the specific. If vengeance required war to continue, she enabled it. If vengeance required war to end, she ended it. The war itself was irrelevant; only the settling of individual grudges mattered.

In the 811 years since the war, Sheeva's influence has grown steadily despite—or perhaps because of—her worship being illegal in most civilized nations. Her temples operate in secret, hidden within legitimate businesses or in the undercities beneath major metropolises. Assassins' guilds pray to her for steady hands and undetectable poisons. Scorned lovers seek her blessings to destroy those who betrayed their trust. Wrongly accused innocents swear oaths in her name, vowing that even if the law will not give them justice, they will take their own revenge.

Her long-term revenge against Alagalothor and Thalaman continues across centuries. She has attempted to assassinate both multiple times, sending her most skilled Widows armed with poisons specifically designed to kill entities of divine power. She has always failed—Alagalothor is too well-protected by his followers, Thalaman too unpredictable in his temporal existence—but she is learning from each failure. She is patient. She has eternity. And unlike her enemies who have built lives and relationships on Mazariim, she has nothing to lose and everything to gain from their eventual deaths.

Sheeva's relationships with other entities are defined almost entirely by how they relate to revenge. Her hatred for Alagalothor is absolute, an all-consuming rage that will never diminish. He stole her power, destroyed her universe, and forced her into diminished exile. She will never stop trying to kill him, and if she cannot kill him, she will settle for making his existence as miserable as possible. Thalaman the time deity is equally hated. His reckless spell caused the Sundering of Talernon, and Sheeva has vowed to poison him across every timeline until he ceases to exist in any reality.

Tiovin the god of mercy earns Sheeva's particular contempt because he teaches forgiveness, the one virtue she finds most obscene. Forgiveness is weakness. Mercy is betrayal of justice. Those who forgive their enemies are spitting on their own pain, declaring that what was done to them did not matter. Paladine the god of honor opposes Sheeva's methods, considering assassination and poison to be dishonorable tactics. Paladins hunt her assassins relentlessly, and she considers them self-righteous fools who care more about looking noble than achieving actual justice. Hinterhin the halfling god of community and generosity is Sheeva's antithesis, teaching that cooperation and forgiveness build stronger societies. She mocks his naive optimism, certain that his followers will eventually be betrayed by those they trusted.

Taovan and Sheeva are rivals competing for the same desperate souls. Both corrupt mortals toward evil, but Taovan uses temptation and devil-pacts while Sheeva uses revenge and poison. When someone seeks power or justice, both entities offer solutions, and the choice between them often comes down to whether the supplicant wants to become powerful or wants their enemy to suffer. Zuel the god of destruction earns Sheeva's professional disdain. He destroys through open warfare, leading armies in brutal combat. She destroys through hidden blades and subtle poison. He considers her methods cowardly; she considers his methods wasteful and inefficient.

Sheeva and Og'thallith share the Abyss but are not allies in any meaningful sense. Og'thallith spreads random madness, breaking minds for the joy of watching them shatter. Sheeva spreads calculated vengeance, breaking lives in carefully planned operations. They tolerate each other's presence because the Abyss is vast enough for both, but they cooperate only when it serves their individual purposes. Zebulba the death-god and Sheeva both value the end of life, but Zebulba is neutral and patient, accepting that all things die eventually. Sheeva is evil and vindictive, demanding that death come as punishment for wrongdoing. They acknowledge each other as professionals in the business of mortality but do not collaborate.

Most other deities are irrelevant to Sheeva unless they interfere with her revenge against Alagalothor and Thalaman. She does not seek to rule Mazariim or to convert all mortals to her faith. She seeks only to make her enemies suffer, and any entity that helps her achieve that goal is temporarily useful while any entity that hinders her becomes another name on her list of grievances.

Never forgive. Never forget. Never show mercy to those who showed you none.
