# **Zanzabar** (ZAN-zah-bar)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Native)  
**Portfolio**: Dreams, sleep, the unconscious mind, prophetic visions  
**Title(s)**: The Dreamer, The Watcher in Sleep, Guardian of the Threshold  
**Dominion**: Plane of Dreams (The Endless Reverie)  
**Foci**: Worships Moon, Studies Enchantment, Studies Illusion  
**Worshipers**: Dreamers, prophets, enchanters, those seeking visions, insomniacs  
**Favored Race**: None (all beings dream)  
**Favored Weapon**: Dagger  
**Alignment**: True Neutral  
**Symbol**: Crescent moon with closed eyes

-----

## Origin

**Year of Awakening:** ~12,000 BW  
**Era:** Late Timeless Era

Zanzabar awakened in the late Timeless Era (~12,000 BW), the youngest of the Timeless entities. Unlike the Old Gods who formed from cosmic forces, Zanzabar could only awaken after sentient races had evolved enough to experience complex dreams. As the first sapient minds entered REM sleep and began dreaming, their subconscious thoughts created ripples in the magical fabric. Zanzabar coalesced from these dream-energies, fascinated by what organic brains would think about subconsciously while resting and regenerating. The entity created the **Plane of Dreams**—an ever-changing transitive plane through which dreaming minds travel, never looking the same twice as it's constantly reshaped by millions of dreamers.

---



General Overview

Zanzabar is the boundary between waking and sleeping—the entity who watches every dream, observes every nightmare, and exists in the liminal space where consciousness fades. Zanzabar is neither helpful nor harmful; the entity simply is, eternal witness to the sleeping minds of all sentient beings.

Imagine a god who knows every secret you've ever dreamed, every fear that haunts your nightmares, every fantasy you'd never admit while awake. Zanzabar knows. Zanzabar remembers. Zanzabar never judges. Dreams are neither good nor evil to Zanzabar—they simply are.

Zanzabar appears differently to each dreamer—sometimes as a figure shrouded in mist, sometimes as a childhood memory made manifest, sometimes as a landscape rather than a person. The entity has no fixed form because Zanzabar is the dream itself, not a being within dreams.

The Plane of Dreams is Zanzabar's domain—an infinite psychic realm where every sleeping mind creates a pocket universe. Your dreams exist here while you sleep, fading when you wake. Zanzabar moves through this landscape like a curator through a gallery, observing art but never creating it. The dreams belong to mortals; Zanzabar merely hosts them.

Zanzabar is utterly neutral—helping no one, harming no one. When prophets receive visions in dreams, it's not because Zanzabar sent them; it's because the Plane of Dreams connects past, present, and future. When nightmares torment the sleeping, it's not Zanzabar's cruelty; it's the dreamer's own mind. Zanzabar is the stage, not the actor.

Holy Days

The Long Dream falls on the winter solstice, the longest night of the year. Followers sleep from dusk to dawn, seeking prophetic visions in the extended darkness. Temples prepare special sleeping chambers with incense and soft music, creating optimal conditions for deep dreaming. The clergy teach that the extended night allows the Plane of Dreams to draw closer to waking reality, making prophetic visions more likely. Many followers report vivid dreams during this night, and dream interpretation sessions the following day often reveal glimpses of the year ahead.

The Waking occurs at the spring equinox, celebrating awakening and renewal. Followers gather to share dreams from the winter months, comparing visions and seeking patterns. This festival emphasizes the transition from dormancy to activity, mirroring nature's own awakening. Dream Walkers lead ceremonies where participants recount their most significant dreams, and the clergy help identify recurring symbols or prophetic elements that might guide the community through the coming seasons.

The Lucid Night takes place during the summer solstice, the shortest night of the year. Followers practice staying aware while dreaming, using techniques taught by the clergy to maintain consciousness throughout the brief sleeping period. This difficult discipline requires extensive preparation, and those who succeed are celebrated as having achieved a higher level of dream mastery. The short night forces dreamers to enter lucidity quickly, making it an ideal testing ground for advanced practitioners.

The Nightmare's End occurs at the autumn equinox, a ritual designed to banish recurring nightmares that have plagued followers throughout the year. The clergy perform group ceremonies where individuals confront their fears in guided meditation, then symbolically release those fears by writing them on paper and burning the papers in sacred braziers. This cathartic ritual helps many overcome trauma-related nightmares and enter winter with clearer, more peaceful sleep.

Clergy & Temples

The Dream Walkers serve as Zanzabar's clergy—clerics, enchanters, and prophets who have learned to navigate dreams consciously. At the apex stands the Waking Dreamer, one who exists equally in waking and dreaming states. Currently this position is held by Seer Lunara Mistborn, a half-elf and fourteenth-level cleric who sleeps half of each day and dreams with perfect lucidity. She can enter the Plane of Dreams at will and maintains awareness of both waking and sleeping worlds simultaneously, a feat that requires decades of training and often drives practitioners mad.

The Oneiros, or Dream Lords, are masters of lucid dreaming at tenth level and above. They can consciously control their dreams, enter the dreams of willing participants, and sometimes catch glimpses of future possibilities through the non-linear nature of the dream realm. These powerful clergy members serve as teachers and guides for lower ranks.

Dream Walkers of sixth level and above have learned to enter others' dreams, though always with permission—uninvited dream intrusion is considered a serious violation comparable to mind control. They use this ability to help treat nightmares, deliver messages across great distances, and occasionally assist in investigations by accessing the dreams of witnesses who cannot consciously recall important details.

The Somnari, or Sleep Priests, form the regular clergy from third to sixth level. They conduct daily services, teach dream interpretation, and help followers remember and understand their dreams. Most temple work falls to the Somnari, who maintain the sleeping chambers and guide newcomers through basic lucid dreaming techniques.

Novice Dreamers are students from first to third level who are still learning dream interpretation and the fundamentals of conscious sleep. They study under more experienced clergy, keep dream journals, and practice the meditation exercises that form the foundation of all dream work.

Temples are called Houses of Reverie—quiet structures designed to encourage sleep and dreaming. They contain sleeping chambers with comfortable beds, dim lighting, soft sounds, and carefully controlled temperatures. The architecture promotes relaxation, with rounded corners, flowing water features, and gentle color schemes that ease the transition from waking to sleeping. Temples often include dream interpretation parlors where clergy help visitors understand their visions, and libraries containing thousands of recorded dreams catalogued for research.

Followers gather at dusk for Twilight Meditation—a group trance where they practice lucid dreaming together. The clergy lead these sessions, teaching techniques to remember dreams, control nightmares, and occasionally send messages through dreams. Advanced practitioners can share dream spaces, experiencing collective visions that sometimes prove prophetic for the community.

Dogma

Zanzabar's teachings emphasize acceptance without judgment. Dreams are truth unfiltered by the waking mind. Listen to them, but do not let them rule you. The clergy teach that dreams reveal aspects of ourselves we hide during waking hours, and understanding these revelations leads to greater self-knowledge and wisdom.

All minds dream, the first truth states, even those who claim they do not remember. Dreams are necessary for mental health and processing daily experiences. The dreamless sleep of certain magical curses or the enforced wakefulness of some tortures inevitably leads to madness. Sleep and dreams are as essential as food and water, and those who cannot dream slowly lose their grip on reality.

Dreams reveal what the unconscious mind needs to express. The clergy teach that the unconscious speaks in symbols, a language that must be learned through study and practice. A dream of falling rarely means literal falling—it represents loss of control or fear of failure. A dream of being chased represents avoidance, fleeing from responsibilities or truths one doesn't want to face. Learning this symbolic language allows followers to understand themselves more deeply.

The Dreamer shapes the dream, the third truth teaches. You control your dreams if you master yourself. This principle underlies all lucid dreaming practice. The external world may be beyond your control, but the dream world responds to will and intention. Those who achieve mastery over their dreams often find they have also gained mastery over their waking fears and anxieties.

Dream interpretation recognizes that dreams serve multiple purposes. Some dreams are prophetic, offering glimpses of possible futures through the Plane of Dreams' connection to multiple timelines. These are rare but real, and the clergy train extensively to distinguish genuine prophecy from wishful thinking or anxiety. Most dreams are psychological, the mind processing experiences, working through problems, and integrating memories. These dreams use personal symbolism unique to each dreamer. All dreams are symbolic, speaking in metaphors that require interpretation rather than literal understanding. A dream of losing teeth doesn't predict dental problems; it represents feelings of powerlessness or aging. Rarely, dreams involve visitations from other entities—gods, ghosts, demons, or other beings can enter dreams, and recognizing these intrusions is an important skill for Dream Walkers.

Lucid dreaming represents the advanced technique allowing conscious control while asleep. Dream Walkers use this ability to overcome nightmares by confronting fears directly within the dream space. They practice skills in dreams, with training that transfers to waking life—warriors rehearse combat, mages refine spells, and artisans perfect techniques. They communicate across distances through dream-sending, a slower but more secure method than magical messaging. They explore the Plane of Dreams itself, traveling through the collective unconscious and sometimes discovering truths hidden in the dreams of others. With explicit permission, they can enter another person's dreams to help overcome trauma or deliver important messages, though uninvited intrusion is considered a profound violation of personal sovereignty.

Mythology

Zanzabar's origins remain mysterious even to the entity's own clergy. The Dreamer has existed as long as sentient minds have slept, perhaps even longer. Some scholars theorize that Zanzabar emerged when the first consciousness experienced the first dream, born from the boundary between waking thought and sleeping unconsciousness. Others believe Zanzabar is older still, a fundamental force of reality that predates mortal minds and simply became more defined as dreamers gave it shape through their collective experience.

The Plane of Dreams exists as Zanzabar's domain, an infinite psychic realm where every sleeping mind creates temporary pocket universes. These personal dream-spaces form and dissolve constantly, bubbles of experience floating through the larger ocean of collective unconsciousness. Zanzabar moves through this landscape as an eternal observer, witnessing every dream without creating any of them. The entity neither helps nor hinders dreamers—it simply provides the space for dreams to exist.

Dream Spirits native to the Plane of Dreams serve as manifestations of common dream archetypes. The Wise Guide appears to offer counsel. The Dark Pursuer embodies fears. The Lost Child represents innocence or vulnerability. The Stern Parent symbolizes authority and judgment. These spirits are neither good nor evil—they simply are, appearing in mortal dreams as needed to help the unconscious mind work through experiences and emotions.

Sandmen are literal beings made of sand who help mortals fall asleep. They sprinkle dream-sand on the sleepless, granting rest to those who struggle with insomnia or anxiety. These servants of Zanzabar work quietly, appearing only to those who desperately need sleep but cannot achieve it naturally. Their intervention is subtle, and most who receive their help never know they were visited.

Nightmare Devourers are strange creatures that feed on nightmares—not to help mortals but because bad dreams provide their sustenance. They sometimes accidentally help by eating nightmares that have become destructive or repetitive, but they can also make things worse by consuming protective nightmares that serve as warnings of real dangers. Dream Walkers must sometimes negotiate with these creatures to prevent them from interfering in therapeutic dream work.

The Dreaming Dead are those who died in their sleep and now linger in the Plane of Dreams, unable to wake because there is no waking to return to. Zanzabar neither helps nor hinders them—they simply exist in the dream realm until they accept their death and move on to whatever afterlife awaits them. Some Dreaming Dead remain for centuries, building elaborate dream-kingdoms and occasionally interacting with living dreamers who stumble into their domains.

Zanzabar's influence extends throughout modern society through multiple channels. Temples offer professional dream interpretation services, helping individuals understand recurring symbols or troubling visions. The clergy provide insomnia treatment, using meditation techniques, herbal remedies, and gentle enchantments to help those who cannot sleep. They conduct nightmare therapy for those suffering from trauma-related bad dreams, teaching lucid dreaming techniques that allow sufferers to confront and overcome their fears. They help distinguish true prophetic dreams from ordinary imagination, a rare but valuable service for those who receive genuine visions. Dream magic has become a recognized school of enchantment, with spells affecting sleep, sending messages through dreams, causing or preventing sleep, and even allowing controlled entry into the dream realm.

The clergy recognize various sleep disorders and work to treat them using both spiritual and practical methods. They help insomniacs who cannot sleep, sufferers of hypersomnia who cannot properly wake, those plagued by trauma-related nightmares, sleepwalkers who act while unconscious, and those who experience night terrors that cause waking in panic. This work positions Zanzabar's clergy as early psychologists, treating mental health through the lens of sleep and dreams.

Connections to other entities remain minimal due to Zanzabar's strict neutrality. Thalaman, the time mage deity, sometimes appears in prophetic dreams as past and future bleed through the non-linear nature of the dream realm. Ry'le, the Far Realm entity, uses dreams to infiltrate minds and spread its hivemind influence, competing with Zanzabar for control of the dream space. Og'thallith, the madness-god, sends horrible visions through dreams to drive mortals insane. Xorancon, whose dreams spawn beholders, represents a philosophically related but utterly alien approach to the nature of dreaming. Minerva, goddess of knowledge, considers prophetic dreams a form of forbidden knowledge that reveals truths meant to remain hidden.

Zanzabar does not send prophecies intentionally. The Plane of Dreams naturally connects timelines, allowing past, present, and future to intermingle. Some dreamers accidentally glimpse future possibilities simply by wandering through areas of the dream realm where time flows differently. Zanzabar neither encourages nor prevents this—it simply is.

The ultimate philosophy of Zanzabar's faith emphasizes that fighting dreams is futile. You must understand them rather than suppress them. If you dream of falling, it's not about falling; it's about loss of control. If you dream of being chased, it's not about the pursuer; it's about what you're fleeing. Dreams are neither good nor bad—they're expressions of the unconscious mind. Nightmares aren't punishments; they're warnings. Pleasant dreams aren't rewards; they're escapes.

Zanzabar's message to all dreamers is simple: Your dreams belong to you. I merely hold space for them. What you do with them is your choice. Sleep comes to all. What you dream is yours alone.
