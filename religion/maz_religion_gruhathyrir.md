# Gruhathyrir, The Wyld Heart

**Portfolio:** Wild nature, primal forces, fey creatures, untamed wilderness  
**Power Level:** Greater Entity (Old God)  
**Title:** The Wyld Heart, Lord of the Untamed, The Green Whisper  
**Dominion:** The Feywild  
**Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral (CN)  
**Symbol:** Oak tree intertwined with wild vines  
**Favored Weapon:** Sickle  
**Worshipers:** Druids, fey creatures, rangers, those who reject civilization  
**Favored Race:** None (predates racial distinctions; favors fey)  
**Foci:** Worships Nature, Descended From Fey, Bargains With Archfey

---

## General Overview

Gruhathyrir is the wild places that resist civilization's advance—the forest that swallows abandoned roads, the storm that demolishes coastal towns, the predator that culls the herd. Where Minerva represents nature in harmony with elvenkind and Hinterhin embodies pastoral agriculture, Gruhathyrir is primal: beautiful, terrible, indifferent, and utterly free.

This Old God does not shepherd or guide. It does not comfort or protect. Gruhathyrir simply is—the living embodiment of wilderness that existed before tool-using races arose and will exist long after they're gone. To encounter it is to remember that humanity, or dwarfhood, or elvenkind, is a thin veneer over animal nature, and that veneer can be stripped away with shocking ease.

Unlike Minerva's serene woodlands or Hinterhin's fertile gardens, Gruhathyrir's domain is the deep forest where sunlight never reaches, the mountain pass where avalanches wait, the swamp where things with too many teeth lurk. Yet there is no malice in this—a thunderstorm doesn't hate the village it destroys any more than a wolf hates the deer it kills. It is simply nature, red in tooth and claw.

Gruhathyrir rarely manifests in fixed form. Witnesses describe a massive oak tree that walks on root-legs, its branches reaching like grasping hands. Others report a stag with antlers that sprout flowering vines, its eyes reflecting centuries of forest growth. Some see a figure woven from living plants, constantly shifting—now humanoid, now bestial, now something beyond category. Often there is simply a presence—the sensation of being watched by the forest itself, every tree an eye, every rustling leaf a whispered judgment. When it speaks, which is rare, its voice comes from everywhere: wind through leaves, rushing water, distant thunder, animal cries woven into syllables.

## Holy Days

Gruhathyrir's followers don't track calendar dates but instead observe natural cycles. The **Green Wrath** occurs when spring thunderstorms are most violent, at which time the Wyld-Bound celebrate nature's destructive power by deliberately exposing themselves to lightning strikes, floods, and tornado winds, trusting Gruhathyrir to protect its own. The **Wild Hunt** takes place at the autumn equinox when prey animals fatten for winter, prompting the Wyld-Bound to embark on ritual hunts lasting weeks using no metal tools but only stone, bone, and wood as they hunt the most dangerous predators such as bears and dire wolves to prove themselves worthy.

The **Long Dark** marks the winter solstice when the Wyld-Bound fast through the longest night, neither eating nor seeking shelter from cold, with survival proving one's right to continue serving the wild. Those who die are left where they fall to feed the forest. The **Blooming** occurs at variable times when rare plants such as century plants or specific orchids bloom, prompting the Wyld-Bound to gather for revelry. These events often involve fey creatures and are dangerous for outsiders, as many tales tell of travelers stumbling into such gatherings and losing decades to a single night's celebration.

## Clergy & Temples

Gruhathyrir's followers are called the **Wyld-Bound**, known as *Gruthak'nor* in Sylvan, druids and rangers who have rejected civilization's constraints to live according to nature's harsh truths. Unlike organized faiths, the Wyld-Bound have no hierarchy, no doctrine, no scripture—only the law of the wild: adapt or die, hunt or be hunted, grow or be overgrown.

Most Wyld-Bound are hermits dwelling deep in untamed wilderness, having spent so long away from civilization that they've half-forgotten human speech. Others are wandering druids who travel forest paths that don't appear on any map, guided by instincts they can't articulate. A few are more sinister—ecoterrorists who burn farms and sabotage roads, believing civilization itself is a disease Gruhathyrir must purge.

The Wyld-Bound wear nothing manufactured—only animal hides, woven plant fibers, and bark. Many incorporate living plants into their clothing, accepting the discomfort of thorns and fungi as connection to Gruhathyrir. Senior druids sometimes appear more plant than person, vines growing through their hair, bark grafting to their skin.

The Wyld-Bound practice several core rituals in service to Gruhathyrir. **The Unmaking** involves destroying symbols of civilization such as a bridge, a road marker, or a fence and watching the wilderness reclaim the space. **The Hunt** requires pursuing prey for days using only primal skills, culminating in a kill performed with gratitude and zero waste. **The Rooting** demands entering deep trance while partially buried in forest soil, allowing fungi and roots to temporarily integrate with one's body. **The Wildshape** recognizes that druids who worship Gruhathyrir claim their wildshape abilities are gifts from the Old God, not mere magical techniques.

Gruhathyrir has no temples built by mortal hands—any structure raised in its name would be anathema to its nature. Instead, sacred sites are naturally occurring places of exceptional wildness. **The Thornheart**, whose exact location is unknown but rumored to be in the Verdant Expanse, is a grove where the trees grow so densely that no light penetrates. Druids who make pilgrimage here report that the forest itself communes with them, though many never return. **Fenwhisper Swamp** in the southern borderlands is a vast wetland where civilization's attempts at drainage have all failed mysteriously. The Wyld-Bound maintain no permanent presence here, but fey creatures are unusually active, and locals report that the swamp actively defends itself against intrusion.

The **Stag-Watch Peaks** in the northern mountains are a range where enormous megafauna—animals that shouldn't still exist in this age—still roam. Dire wolves the size of horses, bears that dwarf mammoths, eagles with thirty-foot wingspans. Wyld-Bound rangers patrol these mountains, ensuring hunters don't threaten the primal ecosystem. Smaller shrines exist at any place the wilderness resists human settlement for generations—ruins swallowed by forest, roads that refuse to stay cleared, fields where crops consistently fail—and may become informal locations where the Wyld-Bound leave offerings of blood and bone.

## Dogma

Gruhathyrir's teachings are less philosophy and more survival instinct. The first principle holds that the **Wild Endures**, recognizing that cities crumble, kingdoms fall, and roads vanish beneath vines, while the wilderness was first and will be last. The teaching declares that **Civilization is Disease**, viewing agriculture, roads, walls, and laws as scars on the world's flesh, understanding that true health is untamed growth with predator and prey in eternal balance. The doctrine affirms that **Death Feeds Life**, seeing that the deer dies so the wolf lives and the tree falls so mushrooms feast, understanding this not as tragedy but as sacred cycle, with denial of death being denial of nature itself.

The harsh truth teaches that one must **Adapt or Perish**, as the forest gives no mercy to the weak, demanding that one struggle, change, and evolve or become mulch for something stronger. Finally, the teaching holds that **Fey Are Kin**, recognizing that the fair folk understand what civilized races have forgotten: that magic is wildness given form, and that trickery and beauty and terror are all facets of nature's truth.

## Mythology

Gruhathyrir emerged around 25,000 BW when Mazariim's first forests reached maturity. Before that, there was only barren stone, primordial oceans, and bacterial life. But when the first trees spread across continents, when the first wolves hunted the first deer, when ecosystems achieved their first complex equilibrium—Gruhathyrir became. Unlike other Old Gods formed from single elements, Gruhathyrir coalesced from the interaction of countless species: predator and prey, parasite and host, decomposer and food source. It is not life itself, which might be Asti's domain, but the wildness of life—the part that resists control, defies prediction, and reclaims all boundaries.

As Gruhathyrir grew in power around 20,000 BW, its consciousness began to warp reality. A parallel plane took shape—the Feywild, where nature operated according to dream-logic and emotion rather than material physics. Trees grew in spirals, seasons changed by whim, and creatures formed from pure wildness given impossible shape. The first fey emerged here: sprites and pixies, dryads and satyrs, archfey lords and ladies. Most had no connection to mortal races—they were Gruhathyrir's thoughts given independent form, each embodying some aspect of wildness. Titania's beauty and caprice, Oberon's hunter's patience, the Cat Lord's predatory grace. The Wyld Heart itself dwells somewhere in the Feywild's deepest reaches, in a place called the Thornheart, though whether this is a location or a state of consciousness is unclear.

Early elves, humans, and other races encountered the Feywild around 15,000 BW through natural thin spots—mushroom circles, ancient groves, mist-shrouded clearings. Some returned changed, carrying knowledge of druidic magic and primal spirits. These became the first shamans, neither quite mortal nor quite fey, serving as bridges between civilization and wilderness. Gruhathyrir neither sought nor rejected these worshipers. Like a wolf might accept a particularly brave human into its pack—not as leader or supplicant, but as fellow hunter—the Old God permitted mortal druids to draw on its power.

When mortals transitioned from hunter-gatherer to agricultural societies around 10,000 BW, Gruhathyrir's influence waned. Forests were cleared, wild animals hunted to extinction, predators killed to protect livestock. The Wyld-Bound resisted—sometimes through violence—but couldn't stop the tide. Some druids abandoned Gruhathyrir for newer nature gods like Minerva (who advocated cooperation with civilization) or Hinterhin (who embodied managed agriculture). Those who remained became increasingly radical, retreating into deep wilderness and sabotaging farms.

When Minerva awakened in 3402 BW and offered elves a gentler relationship with nature—one where forests were tended like gardens and wildlife managed sustainably—many druids converted. Minerva represented nature at peace with civilization, while Gruhathyrir represented nature at war with it. The split created lasting tension. Minerva's druids see the Wyld-Bound as dangerous extremists who would let millions starve to preserve wilderness. The Wyld-Bound see Minerva's followers as traitors who've domesticated themselves into irrelevance.

Gruhathyrir's worship is at its lowest point in recorded history in the modern era from 1 AW to 811 AW. Most druids follow Minerva, Hinterhin, or even Mordrion among dwarves who work with stone rather than against it. The Wyld-Bound are rare—perhaps a few hundred across all of Mazariim—and often viewed as dangerous eccentrics or outright threats. Yet the wilderness endures. Every attempt to drain Fenwhisper Swamp fails. The Thornheart has never been successfully mapped. And in recent decades, some scholars note a troubling pattern: regions that clear too much forest experience sudden ecosystem collapse—plagues of insects, predator population explosions, crops failing despite perfect conditions. Is this natural consequence or Gruhathyrir's active resistance? The Wyld-Bound smile when asked but never answer.

Minerva serves as a rival philosophy. Minerva represents cooperative nature, Gruhathyrir represents untamed nature. Their followers often clash over forest management—Minerva's druids plant and prune, while the Wyld-Bound sabotage such unnatural interference. Gruhathyrir sees Hinterhin the Halfling Father as agriculture's patron and thus an enemy. However, Hinterhin's followers rarely venture into true wilderness, so direct conflict is uncommon. Dwarven mining under Mordrion's guidance destroys underground ecosystems. The Wyld-Bound occasionally collapse mines or trigger cave-ins, which they attribute to Gruhathyrir's wrath. Mordrion's clergy view them as terrorists.

The archfey are Gruhathyrir's children in a sense—independent entities born from its consciousness. Most pay it vague reverence but operate autonomously. Titania and Oberon claim to speak for Gruhathyrir when dealing with mortals, though whether this is true or convenient fiction remains unclear. Some overlap exists with Vuleala (Air) and Iquinias (Water)—wild storms and untamed oceans fall under both their domains and Gruhathyrir's. No conflict arises; they simply coexist in alien parallelism. The god of civilization Tiovin versus the god of wilderness creates fundamentally opposed values, though actual conflict is rare—Tiovin's faithful build cities, the Wyld-Bound avoid them. An odd resonance exists with Zebulba. Both represent forces that civilization fears and cannot control. Some Wyld-Bound claim that death and decay are nature's greatest tools, making Zebulba an unwitting ally. Mainstream Wyld-Bound reject this interpretation.

The Wyld-Bound are scattered and secretive. Perhaps two to three hundred druids live in total isolation as Deep Hermits, some having not seen another person in decades. They emerge only when civilization threatens to encroach too close. A smaller, more radical faction of fifty to one hundred serve as Eco-Warriors who actively sabotage roads, burn farms near protected forests, and sometimes assassinate loggers or miners. They are hunted by authorities across multiple nations. Fey-Touched mortals who have bargained with fey now serve as intermediaries. They are not true Wyld-Bound but share similar values. Some rangers, particularly those who patrol true wilderness, develop reverence for Gruhathyrir without formally joining the clergy as Rangers of the Untamed. They see themselves as guardians of the boundary between civilization and wild. A few scholars study Gruhathyrir as historical curiosity as Academic Druids, attempting to reconcile primal druidism with modern magical theory. The Wyld-Bound consider this laughable.
