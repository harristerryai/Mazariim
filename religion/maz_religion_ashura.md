# **Ashura** (ah-SHOO-rah)

**Power Level**: Lesser Entity (Regional/Cultural — Ejiin)  
**Portfolio**: Honor, light, healing, duty, samurai code  
**Title(s)**: The Golden Blade, Most Honorable Mistress, Guardian of Tengoku  
**Dominion**: Positive Energy Plane (Tengoku — The Realm of Eternal Honor)  
**Foci**: Worships Life, Worships Light, Worships Order, Worships Protection  
**Worshipers**: Samurai, healers, kensai, monks, honorable warriors (Ejiin)  
**Favored Race**: Human (Ejiin)  
**Favored Weapon**: Bo staff  
**Alignment**: Lawful Good  
**Symbol**: Crossed katana and wakizashi within healing energies

---


## Origin

**Year of Awakening:** ~3,100 BW  
**Era:** Era of Awakening

Ashura and Khan awakened as a cosmic duality in the Ejiin civilization during the Era of Awakening (~3,100 BW). As the first samurai swore oaths of honor, duty, and loyalty, the collective consciousness of warrior culture coalesced. **Ashura** awakened first—embodying honor, courage, compassion, and self-sacrifice—and immediately carved **Tengoku** (Realm of Eternal Honor) from the Positive Energy Plane. But when the first samurai broke an oath, **Khan** stirred as the dark reflection, claiming territory in the Negative Energy Plane. Neither can exist without the other; they battle eternally during lunar eclipses, with Ashura always winning at terrible cost.

---


## General Overview

Ashura is honor made divine—the belief that living with integrity, facing death with dignity, and serving others selflessly is the highest calling. In the Ejiin lands (the Lands of Honor, also known as Yashaki or "the Warlord's Pearls"), Ashura represents everything a warrior should aspire to be—courageous in battle, compassionate to the weak, honorable even unto death.

Where Paladine (worshiped in western lands) represents chivalric honor tied to justice and law, Ashura represents bushido honor tied to duty, loyalty, and self-sacrifice. Where Mordrion teaches societal law and structure, Ashura teaches personal honor and the warrior's code. The distinction is subtle but profound. Mordrion asks "What does society require?" while Ashura asks "What does honor demand?"

Ashura is depicted as an androgynous figure in full samurai plate armor—sometimes appearing masculine, sometimes feminine, sometimes neither, always radiant. Their face is typically concealed behind a golden menpō (face mask), revealing only eyes that glow with soft white light. They carry twin swords (katana and wakizashi, the traditional daishō pair) but favor a bō staff in actual combat, believing that the ultimate warrior needs no blade to defend the innocent.

Ashura's existence is intrinsically tied to Khan, their dark counterpart. The two lesser entities are locked in eternal conflict—representing the duality within every Ejiin warrior's soul. Ashura embodies the honorable path through duty, sacrifice, healing, and protection. Khan embodies temptation through betrayal, cowardice, selfish survival, and corruption of honor. Every samurai walks the line between them.

The relationship between Ashura and Khan is not simple antagonism—it is cosmic balance. They are two sides of the same coin, born from the collective consciousness of Ejiin warrior culture. When the first samurai swore an oath of honor, Ashura awakened. When the first samurai broke that oath, Khan stirred. Neither can exist without the other. To destroy Khan would be to deny that dishonor is possible, which would make honor meaningless. To be defeated by Khan would be to admit that honor is futile. So they battle eternally, and Ashura always wins—but at terrible cost.

Ashura's worship is almost exclusively confined to the Ejiin culture—the Japanese-inspired human civilization inhabiting the Yashaki islands southeast of the continent of Nigal. Outside these islands, Ashura is virtually unknown. Travelers from western lands often mistake Ashura for Paladine or confuse their teachings with Mordrion's law-worship. The Ejiin find this insulting—to them, Ashura's bushido is fundamentally different from foreign concepts of honor.

Ashura's ultimate goal is not to eliminate dishonor (impossible and philosophically undesirable) but to inspire every warrior to choose honor when temptation arises. Every samurai who chooses death over dishonor, every healer who tends an enemy's wounds, every soldier who spares a defeated foe—these acts strengthen Ashura. Conversely, every betrayal, every act of cowardice, every corruption of duty strengthens Khan. The eternal battle is not fought with swords but with choices.

## Holy Days

The spring equinox of March 20th brings the Festival of First Light, commemorating Ashura's awakening (though the date itself is mythical rather than historical) and the triumph of light over darkness. Dawn ceremonies mark the beginning of festivities as samurai renew their oaths and new warriors take their first vows before their communities. Sword blessings are performed by senior clergy, imbuing weapons with Ashura's favor. The day continues with tournament competitions where warriors demonstrate their skills in honorable combat. Communities gather to honor local heroes, sharing stories of their brave deeds. Cherry blossom viewings provide moments of reflection on the beauty and transience of life. The festival concludes with communal feasts and storytelling sessions where legendary samurai from history are remembered and their tales passed to the next generation.

Observed during summer on the anniversary of major battle losses, the Day of Fallen Blossoms is a solemn remembrance of samurai who died in battle. This is not a day of mourning but of honoring sacrifice. The names of the fallen are read aloud at temples throughout the Ejiin lands, each name spoken with reverence and respect. Offerings of incense, sake, and flowers are made at ancestor shrines. The stories of final battles and last stands are retold, ensuring that the courage of the fallen is never forgotten. At noon, all activity ceases for a moment of silence. Warriors perform ritual weapon maintenance, carefully tending to the blades of fallen comrades as a sign of continuing honor. Families visit graves to pay their respects and ensure that their ancestors are remembered.

The autumn equinox of September 22nd brings the Trial of Steel, a day of testing when young samurai undergo trials to prove their worth. Clergy demonstrate their skills in both combat and healing arts. Communities test their readiness for hardship through various challenges. The teachings of Ashura are clear—failure is not shameful, but refusing to try dishonors oneself. Combat trials pit students against one another and against their instructors. Endurance tests push warriors to their physical and mental limits. Healing demonstrations show the importance of preserving life as well as taking it. Bushido debates allow scholars to demonstrate their understanding of honor's complexities. Challenges of courage require participants to face their fears, whether standing under a waterfall for hours or confronting personal weaknesses. Those who pass their trials are promoted in elaborate ceremonies that mark their progress on the path of honor.

The winter solstice of December 21st brings the longest night of the year, known as Tengoku's Vigil, when Khan is believed to be at his strongest and temptation greatest. Followers of Ashura hold all-night vigils, resisting the urge to sleep, eat, or rest as a test of their discipline. Throughout the dark hours, they meditate on resisting temptation while readings from the Scrolls of Bushido remind them of honor's demands. By surviving until dawn, they have symbolically defeated Khan for another year. The first light of morning triggers a dawn ceremony celebrating their victory over weakness. A communal breakfast breaks the fast, shared among all who maintained the vigil. The meal is simple but deeply meaningful, representing the reward of discipline and the strength found in community.

During rare lunar eclipses that occur every few years, the Duel of Shadows manifests when the eternal battle between Ashura and Khan becomes visible in the heavens. Followers believe that during these celestial events, the two entities physically duel in Tengoku, and the outcome influences the year ahead. To honor this cosmic battle, samurai throughout the Ejiin lands hold ritual duels drawing first blood but not seeking death. Prayers for Ashura's victory rise from every temple. Offerings are made and anti-Khan wards are renewed to strengthen protection against dishonor. The eclipse is watched in reverent silence, every eye turned skyward. When the moon finally emerges from shadow, it symbolizes Ashura's victory once more. Celebrations erupt as communities rejoice that honor has triumphed over temptation for another cycle.

## Clergy & Temples

Ashura's clergy are called Hikari no Te (Hands of Light)—those who have sworn to embody honor, heal the wounded, and guide warriors on the honorable path. The hierarchy is based on demonstrated virtue rather than age or rank. At the apex stands the Tenshō (Divine General), the highest-ranking cleric, typically a retired samurai or monk of fifteenth level or higher who has lived an exemplary life of honor. Currently this position is held by Tenshō Akane Higashi, an elderly woman who served as a samurai for forty years and never broke an oath. She now oversees Ashura's faith from the Grand Temple in Kashi as a sixteenth-level cleric and master of the bō staff.

Below her serve the Kenshō (Light Generals), regional leaders between eleventh and fifteenth level who oversee temples, train samurai in bushido, and serve as spiritual advisors to daimyōs (feudal lords). They wear white and gold robes with samurai armor accents. The Hikari no Te (Hands of Light) comprise the full clergy between seventh and eleventh level. They serve as battlefield healers, temple guardians, and teachers, functioning as both warriors and priests, equally skilled with healing magic and martial arts. The Shugenja (Students of the Way) are junior clergy between fourth and seventh level who are learning bushido while serving as assistants, messengers, and minor healers. Many are young samurai training to become full Hikari no Te. Finally, the Gakusei (Students) are novices between first and fourth level who have entered Ashura's service but have not yet completed their trials. They train rigorously in both combat and healing arts. Ashura's clergy have no formal gender restrictions—the entity's androgynous nature makes gender irrelevant to spiritual authority. What matters is honor, not anatomy.

Ashura's temples are called Dōjō of Light—places combining martial training halls with healing shrines. They are built in a distinct architectural style featuring wood and paper screens, curved tile roofs, serene gardens, and always a central courtyard for sparring. Each temple features the Hall of Ancestors containing shrines honoring samurai who died honorably, their names inscribed in beautiful calligraphy. The Healing Chambers are where wounded warriors are tended, never turned away regardless of their allegiance. The Sparring Grounds provide open-air courtyards where bushido is taught through practice. The Meditation Garden offers zen gardens for contemplation, tea ceremonies, and spiritual reflection. Finally, the Arsenal of Light serves as a sacred armory where blessed weapons are forged and maintained.

Three temples stand above all others in importance. The Grand Temple of Kashi in the Phoenix Clan capital is the largest temple and home of Tenshō Akane Higashi. The Mountain Dōjō on Mount Tenjiku is a remote monastery where monks train in secret techniques passed down through generations. The Battlefield Shrine on Memorial Plain marks the place where a legendary samurai held a bridge against one hundred enemies, now a sacred pilgrimage site.

Tengoku, Ashura's planar domain within the Positive Energy Plane, is perpetual dawn—the sky always showing the first light of sunrise (symbolizing hope and new beginnings). Cherry blossoms bloom eternally. Honored dead train in perfect dojos, attend tea ceremonies, and serve Ashura as Kami spirits. Those who visit (via plane shift or divine summons) describe feeling an overwhelming sense of peace, purpose, and the desire to be better than they are.

Followers of Ashura maintain a rigorous daily schedule of spiritual and martial discipline. Before dawn, they meditate on the Eight Virtues of Bushido—Honor, Courage, Compassion, Respect, Honesty, Duty, Loyalty, and Self-Control. The morning meditation sets the tone for the entire day and reinforces the warrior's commitment to honor. The tea ceremony holds sacred importance in Ashura's teachings. The entity considers the ritual preparation of tea to be a form of moving meditation that teaches patience, precision, and humility. Even warriors in active campaigns perform abbreviated ceremonies whenever possible. Martial practice occupies at least one hour daily, dedicated to weapons training or martial arts. Combat skill honors Ashura, while neglecting training dishonors the warrior's calling. The body must be maintained as carefully as the spirit. Healing work forms an essential part of daily observance. Followers are expected to heal the injured when possible, regardless of the victim's allegiance. Denying aid to a wounded enemy is considered deeply dishonorable, as it shows that pride outweighs compassion. Each night concludes with the Evening Reflection. Followers review their actions, acknowledge mistakes honestly, and recommit themselves to honor. This nightly examination ensures that lapses in judgment are corrected before they become habits.

## Dogma

Honor is not given. Honor is earned. And once earned, it must be defended—even unto death. Ashura teaches that honor is the foundation of civilization, the measure of a warrior's worth, and the path to enlightenment. A life without honor is a life wasted. A death without honor is a tragedy. But a death with honor is glorious.

The foundation of Ashura's teachings rests upon eight sacred virtues that define the warrior's path. Gi (Righteousness and Justice) demands that followers make the right choice even when it costs them dearly. Yū (Courage) requires warriors to face death without fear, teaching that cowardice is the greatest possible shame. Jin (Compassion) calls for protection of the weak and mercy toward enemies who surrender. Rei (Respect) insists that all people must be honored, from the lowliest peasant to the highest emperor. Makoto (Honesty) forbids lying, deception, or breaking oaths under any circumstances. Meiyo (Honor and Glory) teaches that one should live in such a way that one's name inspires others even after death. Chūgi (Duty and Loyalty) demands service to lord, clan, and people unto death itself. Finally, Jisei (Self-Control) requires mastery over one's emotions, forbidding action driven by rage or fear rather than principle.

Ashura uniquely combines martial prowess and healing arts in a philosophy that sets the faith apart from other warrior traditions. Followers believe that a true warrior protects life rather than merely taking it. The act of healing an enemy honors them by declaring that they are worthy of life despite their opposition. The bō staff symbolizes this philosophy through non-lethal victory, demonstrating that one can defeat an opponent without killing them. Killing should always be a last resort, performed only when absolutely necessary and never enjoyed or celebrated. The greatest warriors are those who can end conflicts without ending lives.

Ejiin culture practices seppuku (ritual suicide) to restore honor after great shame, and Ashura's teachings on this practice are carefully nuanced. Seppuku is considered honorable when it genuinely restores honor lost through betrayal, cowardice, or catastrophic failure. However, seppuku becomes dishonorable when used merely to escape responsibility or consequences rather than to atone. True redemption may actually require living with shame and working to make amends rather than taking the easier path of death. Ashura accepts into Tengoku those who die by seppuku, but only if their intent was genuine atonement rather than escape from the hard work of redemption.

Followers of Ashura understand that Khan (dishonor) is not merely an external enemy but represents internal temptation within every warrior's heart. Every warrior possesses the capacity for betrayal, cowardice, and dishonor. Honor is therefore not a state achieved once and maintained forever, but rather a choice that must be made repeatedly throughout one's life. The eternal battle between Ashura and Khan mirrors the struggle within every soul between the honorable path and the temptation to take easier, dishonorable shortcuts. True victory is not found in destroying temptation entirely, which is impossible, but in consistently choosing honor despite that temptation.

## Mythology

Ashura's awakening occurred when the first samurai swore an oath of honor in ancient Ejiin history, though the exact date remains lost to time. The entity manifested from the collective consciousness of warrior culture—not arriving from another plane or universe, but emerging from the ideals themselves. When warriors began codifying bushido and living by its principles, they gave form to honor itself, and that form became Ashura.

The relationship with Khan emerged simultaneously, as dishonor is honor's shadow. The two entities are cosmic counterparts locked in eternal struggle. This duality defines Ejiin philosophy—honor means nothing without the possibility of dishonor, and every warrior must choose between them daily. The eternal battle between Ashura and Khan is fought not in the heavens but in the hearts of every samurai who faces temptation.

Ashura maintains complex relationships with other entities across Mazariim. Among allies, Paladine of the western lands commands mutual respect despite representing a different cultural expression of honor through chivalry rather than bushido. Mordrion shares philosophical alignment on matters of order and structure, though his focus on societal law differs from Ashura's emphasis on personal honor. Minerva earns respect for her dedication to wisdom and learning. Tiovin shares Ashura's commitment to protecting mortals, creating natural alliance.

Among enemies, Khan stands as the eternal adversary and cosmic counterpart, locked in perpetual battle. Taovan earns enmity because devil-pacts are inherently dishonorable, trading one's integrity for power. Og'thallith represents madness that destroys self-control, attacking one of the cardinal virtues of bushido. Some relationships are neutral or complicated. Zuel, despite being an enemy of civilization, is respected as a worthy adversary because his warriors follow a code, albeit a brutal one. Zebulba generates no particular opposition because Ashura's teachings do not oppose death itself, only dishonorable death.

Ashura's domain Tengoku exists as a carved realm within the Positive Energy Plane. Samurai and healers who die with honor ascend to Tengoku where they train eternally in perfect dojos, always improving. They attend Ashura's tea ceremonies, learning deeper mysteries of bushido, serve as Kami or Tennin guiding the living, and experience eternal sunrise—always the moment of hope, never stagnation. Those who die dishonorably (breaking oaths, betraying lords, dying as cowards) are instead claimed by Khan's servants and dragged to the Negative Energy Plane—a place of eternal darkness, shame, and self-hatred where Oni torment them.

Nature spirits called Shijin (Kami Spirits) serve Ashura, tied to specific places, objects, or concepts. In Ashura's service, Kami embody virtues—Courage Kami, Compassion Kami, Duty Kami, and others. They appear as glowing spirits in animal forms (tigers, cranes, dragons) or as floating orbs of light. They guide honorable warriors, protect temples, and whisper advice during moments of doubt. Kōryū (Light Dragons) are celestial dragons made of pure radiance, extensions of Ashura's will—manifestations of honor given form. They appear during crises to aid samurai, never speaking but communicating through telepathic impressions of bushido principles.

The honored dead who ascended to Tengoku become Tennin—immortal warriors who serve Ashura as guards, messengers, and champions. They can be summoned to aid the living during moments of great need. They appear as they did in life but radiate soft golden light and carry blessed weapons. A unique servant exists in the Golden Blade, Ashura's personal sword, sometimes manifesting as a sentient katana given to exceptional champions. The sword can speak, offers guidance, and refuses to be wielded dishonorably. Only one mortal at a time ever carries it, and the current bearer remains unknown as the sword vanishes when its wielder dies and reappears to the next worthy champion.

Famous miracles throughout Ejiin history demonstrate Ashura's direct intervention. In 433 AW at the Bridge of One Hundred Blades, when enemies attacked a village, a single samurai named Toshiro held a bridge for three days against waves of attackers. Witnesses say he glowed with golden light, his wounds closing as fast as they were inflicted. He killed 97 enemies before finally falling. Ashura personally welcomed him to Tengoku, and the bridge is now a sacred site.

During the Orceri Incursion of 811 AW, a cleric of Ashura named Hanako refused to abandon a field hospital even when Orceri soldiers approached. She stood at the door, bō staff raised, and declared "You will not harm the wounded." The Orceri warriors laughed—until a Kōryū (Light Dragon) materialized beside her. The dragon did not attack but simply stared at them. The Orceri fled. Hanako resumed treating patients, including wounded Orceri soldiers who returned seeking aid.

In the current era, when a corrupt daimyō challenged Ashura's teachings and claimed honor was weakness, a wandering samurai challenged him to a duel. The daimyō brought 20 soldiers. The samurai, a cleric of Ashura, fought all 21 without killing a single one—disarming them through skill alone. At dawn, he stood victorious among unconscious enemies. The daimyō committed seppuku in shame, and the samurai became a legend.

Ashura's teachings define Ejiin culture completely. Bushido is not just religious doctrine—it is law, social custom, and personal identity. Every aspect of Ejiin life is shaped by Ashura's virtues through the legal system with trials by combat and oath-sworn testimonies, social hierarchy where samurai are honored above merchants or farmers because they live by bushido, education where children learn the Eight Virtues before learning to read, diplomacy where oaths between clans are sacred and breaking them is unthinkable, and warfare with its rules of engagement, treatment of prisoners, and ritual duels to settle disputes. The phrase "What would Ashura do?" is common in Ejiin culture—a shorthand for asking "What is the honorable choice?"

The Golden Blade strikes true. Honor endures beyond death.
