# Iquinias

- Portfolio: Water, oceans, sailors
- Power Level: Greater entity (Old God)
- Title(s): The Tide Lord, The Drowned King, Master of Currents
- Dominion: Plane of Water
- Foci: Worships Water (Elementals), Bargains With Sea, Is One With Coast
- Worshipers: Sailors, fishermen, coastal peoples, merfolk
- Favored Race: None
- Favored Weapon: Trident
- Alignment: N
- Symbol: Trident rising from waves

---

## General Overview

Iquinias is the ocean—vast, deep, unknowable, and indifferent. This Old God formed while watching an endless water world for eons, absorbing the rhythm of tides and the fury of storms until consciousness and water became one. Iquinias is not cruel, but neither is he kind. The sea gives life and takes it with equal indifference.

Alien and distant, Iquinias embodies every aspect of water: the gentle rain that nourishes crops, the calm harbor that shelters ships, the raging typhoon that shatters fleets, the crushing depths that swallow the drowned. To pray to Iquinias is to acknowledge your insignificance before the infinite ocean.

Sailors speak his name before voyages, offering coins or rum cast into the waves. They do not expect Iquinias to hear or care—they simply honor the sea itself, hoping that respect might buy them safe passage. More often than not, it doesn't. The ocean takes what it wants.

Coastal peoples and fishermen maintain small shrines to Iquinias, leaving offerings of the first catch, carved driftwood talismans, and prayers written on seaweed. Merfolk and other aquatic races worship him more devoutly, seeing him as the source of their realm and the master of their fate.

Iquinias does not speak. When mortals claim to hear his voice, they hear only the crash of waves, the whisper of currents, the moan of wind across water. Yet patterns emerge—drowned sailors return with messages, strange tides carry lost objects to shore at opportune moments, sea creatures guide ships through fog. Is this Iquinias acting, or simply the random chaos of the ocean?

No one knows. Iquinias keeps his secrets beneath fathoms of dark water.

## Holy Days

**The Offering of First Catch** takes place in spring when fishing season begins and the very first fish caught is returned to the sea with prayers and thanks. Some communities make this a festival with boat races and feasts, but the core ritual remains unchanged as giving the first to Iquinias. 

**Storm's End** follows major storms when communities gather at the shore to honor those lost at sea, with names carved in driftwood and cast into the waves, prayers sung, and offerings made. The ceremony ends when the sun breaks through the clouds as a sign the storm has truly passed.

**Tideturning** occurs twice yearly at the spring and autumn equinoxes when day and night are equal. Tidewatchers perform rituals marking the shift from high-tide season to low-tide season and back, acknowledging the eternal cycle of change. 

**The Drowned Moon** marks rare lunar eclipses visible from the ocean when the moon appears blood-red above the sea and it is said Iquinias rises from the depths. Coastal peoples stay indoors, sailors refuse to launch, and Tidewatchers perform protective rituals. Strange things wash ashore on these nights.

## Clergy & Temples

Iquinias's clergy are called Tidewatchers (or Wave-Speakers among merfolk). They wear robes of blue and green, often decorated with shells, coral, and pearls. Many are sailors, fishermen, or coastal dwellers who have survived storms, shipwrecks, or near-drownings—experiences they interpret as Iquinias claiming them for service.

The Tidewatchers serve multiple roles in coastal communities. They act as harbor priests blessing ships before voyages and performing rituals to ensure safe return. As storm callers, they attempt to predict weather patterns and interpret ocean omens. They serve as death speakers presiding over burial-at-sea ceremonies and speaking for the drowned. Their role as salvage diviners involves using water scrying to locate shipwrecks and lost cargo.

There is no formal hierarchy among Tidewatchers. Those who have survived the worst storms, who can read currents with uncanny accuracy, who return from presumed drownings—these earn respect. Many elderly Tidewatchers eventually walk into the sea and do not return, joining Iquinias in his watery realm.

Temples of Iquinias are built on coastlines, at the end of docks, and on small islands. They are always within sight and sound of the sea. Most are simple structures of driftwood and stone, open to the elements, with altars positioned so waves can wash over them at high tide.

Offerings are never kept—they must be given to the sea. Coins, jewelry, carved figures, written prayers on biodegradable parchment—all are cast into the waves. Some temples maintain offering pools where the faithful can make sacrifices without losing their gifts to the ocean, but purists scorn this practice.

Ancient underwater temples exist in the deep places, accessible only to merfolk or those with magic to breathe beneath the waves. These structures are covered in barnacles and kelp, inhabited by strange sea creatures, and filled with treasures from a thousand shipwrecks. Few surface dwellers have seen them and returned.

## Dogma

The Tidewatchers have learned fundamental truths from the sea. The first teaching is to respect the ocean, understanding that the sea gives life through fish, trade, travel, and rain, while the sea also takes life through storms, drowning, and monsters from the deep. Both are equally the sea's nature, demanding respect and never to be taken for granted. 

The doctrine holds that **the tide turns**, recognizing that nothing is permanent as high tide becomes low tide, calm becomes storm, and storm becomes calm. This teaches that your fortune will change, your sorrows will pass, and your triumphs will fade, requiring one to flow with the tide and not fight the current.

The teaching warns to **never turn your back**, as the ocean is always watching, always moving, and always hungry. The moment you forget to respect it or assume safety is the moment it takes you, demanding vigilance and humility. 

The truth that **what the sea claims, the sea keeps** acknowledges that those who drown belong to Iquinias. Do not mourn them too deeply as they have joined the eternal ocean. Some return as revenants or water spirits forever bound to the waves. 

The principle to **give to receive** instructs that before you take from the sea you must give to the sea. Return the first fish to the water, offer a coin before you sail, and speak a prayer before you dive. The ocean remembers those who are generous and those who are greedy.

## Mythology

In a distant solar system, a world existed covered entirely in water—no land, no islands, just endless ocean from pole to pole. For millions of years, this world spun silently through space, its waves crashing against each other with no shore to break upon. A consciousness formed in that perpetual motion—not in the water itself, but in the concept of water. The rhythm of tides, the cycle of evaporation and rain, the slow dance of currents circling the globe. Iquinias awakened not as a being but as the essence of ocean itself.

When that water world eventually froze or evaporated (the myths are unclear), Iquinias's essence drifted through the cosmos until finding Mazariim—a world with vast oceans and countless shores. Here, he established the Plane of Water, an infinite realm where every drop is part of the whole, where currents flow forever, where the drowned do not die but become one with the eternal sea.

Primitive mortals feared the ocean—too vast to cross, too deep to fathom, too powerful to fight. But necessity eventually drove them onto the waves around 10,000 BW. The first crude boats, the first desperate fishing expeditions, the first voyages from island to island—all unknowingly honored Iquinias.

When the first sailor drowned, Iquinias claimed that soul. When the first ship sank, Iquinias took the wreckage into his depths. When the first storm destroyed a coastal village, Iquinias was blamed. Thus began a relationship of fear, respect, and grudging dependence.

During the age of elemental worship from 5,000 to 2,500 BW, Iquinias was widely worshiped by coastal peoples. Elaborate rituals evolved: ships were blessed before every voyage, storms were appeased with sacrifices, the drowned were given back to the sea in ceremonial burials. Some coastal cities even practiced human sacrifice, casting criminals or volunteers from cliffs into the waves as offerings.

Iquinias neither encouraged nor discouraged these practices. The sea accepted the offerings with the same indifference it accepted shipwrecks and fish. Yet those who performed the rituals seemed to have better fortune—or perhaps they were simply more careful, more respectful, more attuned to the patterns of weather and tide.

As newer entities emerged after 2,500 BW offering guidance and protection, worship of the elemental Old Gods declined. But Iquinias's following persisted stronger than most. Why? Because the ocean remained. Regardless of which god you worshiped, if you sailed, you dealt with Iquinias.

Merchants who followed Tiovin still offered coins to the waves. Elven sailors devoted to Minerva still avoided disrespecting the sea. Even paladins of Paladine learned to honor the ocean's power. Iquinias became a secondary deity for many—not their primary faith, but a force that could not be ignored.

Iquinias has no allies, for water does not befriend—it simply flows. The other elemental Old Gods—Pyrantor of fire, Vuleala of air, and Dirgin of earth—are sometimes portrayed as rivals in mortal stories, but elementals do not feud. They interact as forces of nature interact: water extinguishes fire, air stirs water into waves, earth shapes the shore. These are not battles but natural processes.

Iquinias has no true enemies, but those who seek to control or despoil the ocean earn the sea's wrath. Xannith the god of greed encourages overfishing and pollution—the sea remembers these offenses and eventually exacts payment. Vuleala of air and weather works in tandem with Iquinias to create storms, rain, and ocean winds. Some Tidewatchers also honor Vuleala, recognizing that water and air are eternally intertwined.

Today, Tidewatchers maintain their traditions in ports and coastal villages. Merfolk continue their deeper worship. And every sailor, regardless of faith, knows: the sea takes what the sea wants.
