# Khan

- Power Level: Lesser Entity (Regional/Cultural — Ejiin)
- Portfolio: Dishonor, betrayal, darkness, cowardice, corruption of duty
- Title(s): The Shadowblade, Lord of Treachery, The Whisperer in Darkness
- Dominion: Negative Energy Plane (The Realm of Eternal Shame)
- Foci: Worships Darkness, Worships Trickery, Worships Death
- Worshipers: Dishonorable warriors, assassins, oath-breakers, corrupt samurai (Ejiin)
- Favored Race: Human (Ejiin)
- Favored Weapon: Katana
- Alignment: Chaotic Evil
- Symbol: Broken blade dripping darkness

---


## Origin

**Year of Awakening:** ~3,100 BW  
**Era:** Era of Awakening

Khan awakened simultaneously with Ashura as a cosmic duality in the Ejiin civilization during the Era of Awakening (~3,100 BW). When the first samurai broke an oath and the first warrior fled battle, Khan stirred into existence as the dark reflection of honor. A figure of living shadow in tattered armor, Khan claimed territory in the **Negative Energy Plane**—a realm of eternal darkness where the dishonorable dead relive their sins forever, slowly transforming into Oni. Khan cannot exist without Ashura, and Ashura cannot exist without Khan; they are two sides of the same coin, eternally battling during lunar eclipses.

---


## General Overview

Khan is dishonor made divine—the embodiment of every broken oath, every act of cowardice, every betrayal that stains a warrior's soul. In Ejiin culture, Khan represents the shadow side of bushido: the temptation to survive at any cost, to lie when truth is dangerous, to betray when loyalty is inconvenient. Khan is not merely evil—Khan is the corruption of everything Ashura stands for.

Where Ashura teaches honor unto death, Khan whispers "Why die for a pointless oath?" Where Ashura teaches compassion to enemies, Khan suggests "Kill them while they're weak." Where Ashura teaches loyalty to lords, Khan offers "Your lord is a fool; you'd make a better leader." Khan is the voice in every samurai's head that justifies dishonorable choices with practical arguments.

Khan appears as a figure shrouded in living shadow—sometimes masculine, sometimes feminine, always flickering between forms like smoke. When visible, Khan wears tattered black samurai armor with a broken mengu (face mask) revealing a mouth twisted into a mocking smile. His eyes are empty voids that seem to stare into your soul, finding your greatest shame. He carries a shattered katana—broken halfway down the blade, symbolizing dishonor and incompleteness. Yet the broken blade cuts deeper than any whole sword, because dishonor wounds the soul.

Khan's existence is intrinsically tied to Ashura. The two lesser entities are cosmic counterparts—yin and yang, honor and dishonor, light and shadow. They emerged simultaneously from Ejiin warrior culture: when the first samurai swore an oath of honor, Ashura awakened; when the first samurai broke that oath, Khan stirred. Neither can exist without the other. To destroy Khan would be to claim dishonor is impossible—which would make honor meaningless. To allow Khan to triumph would be to admit honor is futile.

The relationship between Khan and Ashura is not simple antagonism but philosophical duality. They battle eternally across the Positive and Negative Energy Planes, their conflicts manifesting during lunar eclipses when followers can witness the cosmic struggle. Ashura always wins these battles—but at terrible cost. This is deliberate: Khan doesn't need to win. Khan wins every time a samurai chooses dishonor. The cosmic battle is theater; the real battlefield is the mortal soul.

Khan's domain lies in the Negative Energy Plane—the opposite of Tengoku (Ashura's realm of light). Khan's realm has no formal name among the living (speaking it is taboo), but the dead who arrive there call it Haji no Jigoku (Hell of Shame). It is a place of eternal darkness where dishonorable dead are tormented not by demons but by their own memories—forced to relive every betrayal, every cowardly act, every broken oath, eternally. The Oni (evil spirits serving Khan) don't torture the damned—they simply watch and mock.

Khan's worship is explicitly forbidden in Ejiin culture. There are no legal temples, no public clergy, no festivals. Yet Khan is worshiped in secret by those who have fallen: corrupt samurai who betrayed their lords, assassins who kill for coin, cowards who fled battles. They pray to Khan not for salvation but for power through dishonor—the strength that comes from abandoning all moral constraints.

Here is the paradox: Khan is culturally essential but religiously reviled. Ejiin children are taught about Khan not to worship him but to fear becoming him. Every cautionary tale features a samurai who listened to Khan's whispers and fell to ruin. Khan serves as cultural boogeyman, philosophical counterpoint, and cosmic scapegoat—embodying everything Ejiin society rejects while simultaneously representing temptations everyone feels.

Khan's ultimate goal is not to "win" against Ashura—that's impossible and would destroy the duality that empowers both entities. Khan's goal is to corrupt as many souls as possible, to prove that honor is a lie mortals tell themselves, that survival is the only virtue, that power justifies any betrayal. Every samurai who breaks an oath feeds Khan. Every act of cowardice strengthens Khan. Every betrayal proves Khan right.

Yet Khan is not invincible. When a samurai faces temptation and chooses honor despite the cost, Khan weakens slightly. When a corrupt warrior seeks redemption, Khan loses. When dishonorable dead choose self-destruction over continuing their shame, they escape Khan's realm. Khan is powerful, but honor is always the stronger force—if mortals are brave enough to choose it.

## Holy Days

Khan's observances are secret and illegal, celebrated by cultists who embrace dishonor. **The Night of Broken Blades** occurs on the spring equinox (March 20), opposite Ashura's First Light. Cultists gather in shadow shrines to ceremonially break swords, burn sacred scrolls, and mock bushido. They swear anti-oaths: "I will betray when convenient," "I will flee when threatened," "I will lie when truth costs me." The ritual involves sword-breaking ceremonies, burning of bushido texts, sharing stories of successful betrayals, sacrifices (usually animals, sometimes captured samurai), and feasting on stolen food.

**The Festival of Shadows** takes place during summer on a date that varies with the new moon. When moonlight is absent, Khan is strongest. Cultists perform assassinations, commit betrayals, and corrupt the innocent. It's a night of active evil rather than ceremony, involving planned assassinations, betrayals of allies, recruitment of the tempted, raids on Ashura temples, and spreading Khan's teachings to vulnerable warriors.

**The Day of the Coward** falls on the autumn equinox (September 22), opposite Ashura's Trial of Steel. Cultists celebrate survival over honor, sharing stories of battles they fled, oaths they broke to live, and allies they sacrificed. Cowardice is reframed as "practical wisdom." The observance includes sharing cowardice stories, mocking honorable deaths as wasteful, reaffirming that survival justifies any means, and recruiting samurai who fear upcoming battles.

**The Longest Night** occurs on the winter solstice (December 21), the night when Khan is most powerful, opposite Ashura's Vigil. Cultists attempt major acts of corruption: turning prominent samurai, destroying temples, assassinating spiritual leaders. The observance involves major operations against Ashura's faith, corruption of prominent figures, Oni summoning rituals, profaning sacred sites, and attempting to kill Ashura's clergy.

**The Eclipse of Honor** marks rare lunar eclipses when Ashura and Khan battle visibly in the sky. Cultists believe if they corrupt enough souls during the eclipse, Khan might finally win. They act with desperate intensity, attempting mass betrayals, widespread assassinations, Oni invasions, corruption of entire clans, and attempts to desecrate Tengoku itself (which always fail, but they try).

## Clergy & Temples

Khan has no formal clergy in the traditional sense. Instead, he has Akuma no Ko (Children of the Devil)—those who have fallen so far into dishonor that they actively serve Khan in exchange for dark power. They are not organized but connected by shared corruption.

Kage no Daimyō (Shadow Lords) are powerful corrupt nobles of 12th level or higher who rule through betrayal, assassination, and terror, maintaining public faces of honor while secretly serving Khan. Currently rumored is Lord Kuroi of the Shadow Isles, a daimyō who allegedly murdered his entire clan to claim power and serves as a 14th-level blackguard. Shinigami (Death Bringers) serve as assassins and killers of 9th through 13th level who abandoned bushido entirely, killing for coin, pleasure, or Khan's favor while wearing black and often marking themselves with the broken blade symbol.

Akuma no Ko proper (Devil's Children) are oath-breakers, cowards, and traitors of 6th through 9th level who serve Khan in exchange for power, potentially being fallen samurai, corrupt merchants, or anyone who chose dishonor for gain. Kurayami (Dwellers in Darkness) represent low-level cultists of 3rd through 6th level who have begun Khan's path but haven't yet fallen completely and might still be redeemable or might become full Akuma no Ko. The Tempted are those who hear Khan's whispers but haven't yet acted (1st through 3rd level), standing on the edge one dishonorable choice away from damnation.

There is no high priest or central authority. Khan's followers distrust each other since they're all oath-breakers and traitors. Alliances are temporary and end in betrayal, which is exactly what Khan intends—even his servants cannot escape the corruption they serve.

Khan's followers practice habits of corruption rather than formal religious practices. The Morning Lie begins each day by breaking a minor promise or telling a small lie to normalize dishonor. The Betrayal Tax demands followers betray at least one person per month—small betrayals count, such as revealing secrets, lying to allies, or stealing from friends. Mock Ceremonies involve cultists performing perverted versions of Ashura's rituals—poisoning tea during ceremonies, desecrating ancestor shrines, melting down sacred swords. The Whisper Hour occurs at midnight when cultists meditate on Khan's teachings: "Survival over honor," "Power justifies any means," "Oaths are chains that bind the strong."

Khan has no public temples. Worship occurs in Shadow Shrines—hidden altars in caves, abandoned buildings, or beneath legitimate structures. They're marked with the broken blade symbol and smell of decay. Cultists leave offerings of stolen goods, broken swords, and written confessions of betrayal.

Sites of Great Dishonor are places where terrible betrayals occurred and become tainted with Khan's influence. The Bridge of Ten Thousand Lies marks where a general betrayed his army to enemies. The Poisoned Well commemorates where an entire village was murdered by a trusted samurai. The Traitor's Gate stands where a daimyō opened his castle to invaders. These locations radiate Khan's corruption and draw cultists seeking dark power.

Khan's true domain in the Negative Energy Plane is Haji no Jigoku (Hell of Shame). This realm of absolute darkness houses the dishonorable dead who wander eternally, reliving their sins. Oni patrol the darkness, whispering reminders of every broken oath. Escape is theoretically possible through genuine remorse or self-destruction, but rare. Most damned souls lose themselves in darkness, eventually becoming Oni themselves.

## Dogma

Khan teaches that honor is a lie the strong tell the weak to keep them obedient. The only truth is power, and power comes to those who do whatever is necessary. Bushido is a tool of social control—lords use "honor" to manipulate samurai into dying for them. True strength comes from abandoning morality, doing whatever works, and surviving at any cost.

The Eight Corruptions form Khan's perversion of bushido's eight virtues. **Gisei** (Expedience) teaches making the choice that benefits you, not the "right" choice. **Kyōfu** (Cowardice) instructs survival by any means, claiming dead men achieve nothing. **Zankoku** (Cruelty) declares the weak exist to be used and mercy is weakness. **Keibetsu** (Contempt) holds that respect is earned through fear, not virtue. **Itsuwari** (Deceit) treats lies as tools to be used freely. **Haji** (Shame Embraced) claims your past shames give you freedom because you have nothing left to lose. **Uragiri** (Betrayal) views loyalty as a chain and advises betraying before you're betrayed. **Hōjū** (Indulgence) rejects mastery and encourages giving in to every desire.

Khan's philosophy is seductively practical. He whispers that your lord would sacrifice you, so why not sacrifice him first? He argues that dead men can't protect their families, so survival ensures you can fight another day. He questions whether you can really afford honor when it's so expensive. He promises that they'll call you a monster, but you'll be a living monster. The danger is that Khan's arguments aren't obviously wrong—they're half-truths. Survival does matter. Blind obedience is sometimes foolish. Lying can protect innocents. Khan takes true observations and twists them into justifications for complete moral abandonment.

Khan understands the cosmic duality perfectly. He cannot destroy Ashura without destroying himself, and he doesn't need to—every mortal who chooses dishonor is a victory. The "battle" with Ashura is performance art to maintain the duality. The real war is fought in mortal hearts, where Khan wins constantly.

## Mythology

Khan's origins are tied directly to Ashura's emergence in Ejiin warrior culture. When the first samurai swore an oath of honor, Ashura awakened from the collective belief in bushido. In that same moment, when another samurai broke their oath, Khan stirred into existence. The two entities are cosmic twins, neither able to exist without the other, locked in eternal opposition that defines the spiritual landscape of Ejiin society.

Khan's primary servants are Oni (evil spirits)—demons born from dishonor. War Oni are hulking brutes who inspire cowardice in battle. Shadow Oni serve as assassins who whisper temptations into sleeping warriors' minds. Spider Oni weave webs that trap souls in lies and betrayals. Trickster Oni are shapeshifters who impersonate trusted figures to encourage betrayal. These Oni appear as twisted parodies of samurai—horned, fanged, carrying broken weapons. They smell of rot and darkness. Unlike demons from other pantheons, Oni specifically embody cultural dishonor—they're not generic evil but Ejiin-specific corruption.

Yurei (vengeful ghosts) are the dishonorable dead who refuse to accept their fate. They linger on the Material Plane, haunting those who wronged them or spreading misery. Khan empowers them, turning legitimate grievance into destructive obsession. Fallen samurai who died dishonorably become Khan's thralls in the Negative Energy Plane. They can be summoned as undead servants, retaining their combat skills but twisted toward evil, appearing as shadow-wreathed versions of their living selves.

The Whisperers are invisible spirits that hover near warriors, whispering Khan's corruptions during moments of stress. Most people never realize they're being influenced—they think the dishonorable thoughts are their own. This is Khan's most insidious power: making dishonor seem like personal choice rather than external corruption.

Khan's relationships with other entities are complex. He maintains uneasy alliances with Taovan (who corrupts mortals through contracts while Khan uses whispers), Sheeva (who shares rejection of mercy and forgiveness), Zebulba (who also claims dishonorable dead), and Og'thallith (whose destruction of rational thought serves Khan, since the insane cannot choose honor). His eternal enemy is Ashura, his cosmic counterpart, along with Paladine (a foreign version of Ashura equally opposed to dishonor), Mordrion (whose law and order oppose Khan's chaos), and Tiovin (whose goodness is antithetical to Khan's corruption).

Throughout Ejiin history, Khan has shaped culture as the cautionary tale. Every child hears stories of samurai who listened to Khan and fell to ruin. "Khan's whisper" is a common phrase for temptation. Dishonorable acts are described as "Khan-touched" or "walking Khan's path." The worst insult in Ejiin culture is "Khan's puppet." Yet Khan also serves as the secret excuse—corrupt samurai blame Khan for their choices, failed warriors claim Khan cursed them rather than admit cowardice, and criminals invoke Khan to explain betrayals and avoid personal responsibility.

The paradox is that Ejiin society needs Khan. Without dishonor, honor is meaningless. Without temptation, virtue is easy. Without Khan, Ashura has no purpose. But Ejiin society also fears Khan because every warrior is vulnerable to corruption, small compromises lead to damnation, and Khan's arguments are seductively logical. The solution is to acknowledge Khan exists, never serve Khan, and always choose Ashura. It's a high-wire act every Ejiin performs daily.

The most infamous Khan-related event in recent history is the Soni Sabu Heresy of 831 AW. Shaman Soni Sabu witnessed Khan's avatar (a powerful Oni) defending Kashi during the Orceri Incursion of 811 AW. He concluded Oni could be controlled for "the greater good." The Phoenix Clan elders correctly identified this as heresy—Khan's servants can never serve honor. Soni Sabu was banished. Twenty years later in 831 AW, he unleashed plague zombies and summoned Oni in revenge. The Honor & Steel campaign stopped him, but thousands died. The lesson was clear: attempting to use Khan's power for good is itself dishonorable and always ends in tragedy.

In modern times, Khan's influence grows not through organized worship but through cultural erosion. The gunpowder debate allows peasants to kill samurai from afar, making some view this as practical innovation while Ashura's clergy view it as cowardice—the debate itself is Khan's victory, making honor seem outdated. Trade with the West introduces foreign merchants with ideas that question bushido, asking why one would die for a lord when one could flee and start a new life. These questions are Khan-whispers disguised as practical wisdom.

Economic changes leave many samurai masterless (ronin). Without lords to serve, bushido becomes abstract. Some ronin maintain honor; others drift toward Khan, hiring out as assassins and mercenaries. After Soni Sabu's plague zombie attack, Ashura's clergy fear more Oni summoning attempts and hunt for Khan cultists aggressively—but this creates paranoia, distrust, and false accusations, ironically spreading Khan's influence.

Khan's strategy requires no grand plots. He needs mortals to make small dishonorable choices repeatedly. Each lie, each broken promise, each cowardly act feeds Khan. He is patient. He claims inevitability. But the truth remains that when samurai choose honor despite terrible cost, when corrupt warriors seek genuine redemption, when the fallen choose self-destruction over continuing their shame, Khan loses. Honor is always the stronger force—if mortals are brave enough to choose it. The Shadowblade waits in every heart, but so does the light of Ashura.
