# Tiovin

**Power Level**: Greater entity  
**Portfolio**: Good, healing, humans  
**Title(s)**: God, Lord of Heaven and Earth  
**Dominion**: Heaven  
**Foci**: Bargains With Celestials, Worships Life, Worships Protection  
**Worshipers**: Humans, healers  
**Favored Race**: Human  
**Favored Weapon**: Scimitar  
**Alignment**: LG  
**Symbol**: Two hands held in supplication

---

## General Overview

Tiovin (Tee-oh-vin) is the patron deity of humans as well as healers and any who follow a good and honest path through life. Its church embodies mercy for the weak, feeding the poor, healing the sick, and helping thy neighbor, notwithstanding some of the more extreme factions scattered throughout the world. The church seeks to protect the world from the evil of Taovan's followers as well as the other evil races bent on the destruction of humans.

What Tiovin looks like is unknown to the mortals of the world save the very few that have journeyed to Heaven and returned to tell the tale. Its form in its home plane has been said to be a large glowing mass not unlike a miniature sun, impossible to look at directly. It has also been written in legend of a more human form, that of a young man with long flowing brown hair and a beard and milky white skin, though whether this is Tiovin's true appearance or simply how mortal minds interpret divine presence remains unclear.

Tiovin's reputation varies greatly throughout the world. In many parts of the Inner Kingdoms, it is regarded by humans and dwarves as a benevolent and just entity, a source of hope and healing who rewards virtue and punishes wickedness. In places like the Merchant Nations and the militant theocracies, however, Tiovin is known to be an unmerciful and firm deity well versed in the ways of war, whose church pursues crusades against the faithless and executes harsh judgment against those deemed evil. To many of the non-human races, Tiovin is known to be the one greater god that did not send an avatar down to Mazariim to help defend its people during the War to End All Wars, and thus garners expected negative reactions—elves, dwarves, and halflings who fought and died while human souls were judged in Heaven remember this apparent abandonment.

Faithful of Tiovin pray several times each day, usually before each meal and before rest. These prayers range from simple expressions of gratitude to elaborate liturgies depending on the sect and tradition. In addition, the large number of varied Tiovin sects all have small unique rituals or prayers that vary widely—some practice confession and absolution, others emphasize contemplative silence, still others engage in ecstatic worship with music and dance. The diversity of practice within Tiovinian faith is matched only by the diversity of human cultures that embrace it.

Tiovin is venerated by all types of divine casters, with clerics and inquisitors making up most of its ranks of followers. Tiovinian clerics are renowned healers and protectors, their magic flowing from genuine compassion and dedication to preserving life. Tiovinian inquisitors are widely feared in many of the more uncivilized parts of the world, their reputation derived from the many Crusades that the church has pursued over the centuries—expeditions meant to spread the faith but often marked by violence against those who refused conversion.

## Holy Days

All Tiovinians celebrate the New Year on the first of Mor (January 1) with services of renewal and dedication. The faithful attend dawn services where they confess the sins of the past year and commit themselves to living more virtuously in the year ahead. Many sects practice symbolic cleansing rituals—baptisms for converts, washings of hands and feet, or the lighting of candles representing the light of virtue driving out the darkness of sin. The day is spent in reflection and community, with families gathering for meals and churches hosting celebrations that welcome the faithful back after whatever lapses or struggles they experienced in the year past.

During the month of Tiovary, usually around the autumnal equinox, comes the Day of Thanks. This harvest celebration acknowledges Tiovin's blessings throughout the growing season and expresses gratitude for food, health, and community. Churches organize massive feasts where everyone is welcome regardless of wealth or status, with the faithful bringing dishes to share and ensuring that even the poorest members of the community eat well. The day emphasizes generosity and hospitality, with sermons focusing on recognizing blessings and sharing abundance with those in need. Many families make pilgrimages to local temples to offer a portion of their harvest as thanks, and these offerings are then distributed to the poor in the weeks following.

The Eve of Hallows falls during the month of Xan (February) and commemorates those who have died in the service of good. Churches hold evening vigils where the names of faithful departed are read aloud, candles are lit for lost loved ones, and stories are told of saints and heroes who gave their lives protecting others. The observance has a solemn but hopeful tone—mourning the loss while celebrating that the virtuous dead now dwell in Heaven's glory. Some sects believe the barrier between the living and the dead grows thin on this night, allowing brief communion with those who have passed on, and they conduct elaborate rituals attempting to receive messages or blessings from ancestors.

Individual sects celebrate dozens of saints throughout the year, each with their own feast day commemorating their martyrdom or miraculous deeds. Saint Catherine the Healer, who tended plague victims until she herself succumbed, is honored in spring. Saint Marcus the Just, who refused to execute innocent prisoners and was martyred for his defiance, is celebrated in summer. Saint Elara the Pure, who resisted demonic temptation through prayer and fasting, is venerated in autumn. Each region has its own patron saints, and traveling between different Tiovinian communities means encountering unfamiliar observances and traditions, though all share the common thread of celebrating virtue triumphant over evil.

The most militant sects, particularly those in Tentigo and Cecastaa, observe Victory Days commemorating successful crusades and military campaigns against evil. These celebrations involve martial displays, the blessing of weapons and armor, and sermons praising holy warriors who died in battle against devils, demons, undead, or the followers of evil deities. Critics within the broader Tiovinian church argue these celebrations glorify violence and contradict Tiovin's teachings of mercy, but the militant factions counter that righteous warfare against evil is itself an act of mercy—protecting innocents by destroying those who would harm them.

## Clergy & Temples

As one of the oldest entities worshiped in Mazariim, the clergy of Tiovin has some of the widest variances of faith, ethos, and hierarchy of all the so-called gods worshiped by sentients. Numerous sects have grown and developed in all parts of the world, some of them benevolent and generous and some militaristic and unyielding in pursuit of law and justice. This diversity reflects the fundamental nature of human societies themselves—varied, adaptable, and often contradictory.

The Angelics, located throughout the Inner Kingdoms, are one of the most prevalent sects of civilized human lands. Believing that their faith comes from within and that a church is not needed to speak with and pray to God, the Angelics seek to live a life of humility and honesty and to help their fellow man. They emphasize personal relationship with the divine over institutional authority, arguing that clergy should serve as guides and teachers rather than intermediaries between worshiper and god. The churches of Tiovin in these lands have a local pastor, priest, or minister, with no high authority to be reported to other than Tiovin itself. Each congregation governs itself democratically, making decisions through consensus and selecting leaders based on demonstrated virtue rather than formal ordination. The Angelics focus on practical charity—feeding the hungry, sheltering the homeless, healing the sick—viewing these acts as the truest form of worship.

The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo, on the other hand, is the ruling church of a nation in the Sabonaar lands that preaches justice and law with militant fervor. Although the nation does engage in benevolent actions to those in need, their primary mission is twofold—spread the gospel of Tiovin to the heathen lands, and punish the evil, dishonest, or non-worshipper of Tiovin. They accomplish this through their militaristic organization known as the Hand of God, an army of paladins, clerics, and inquisitors who enforce religious law and conduct crusades into territories deemed corrupted by evil. Tentigo has a strict hierarchy of bishops and cardinals ruling from the capital, with over two dozen saints idolized in various ways and their relics kept in grand cathedrals. The Tentigan church claims to be the one true interpretation of Tiovin's will, dismissing other sects as misguided at best and heretical at worst.

Between these extremes exist countless variations. Some sects emphasize contemplative monasticism, with monks and nuns withdrawing from society to dedicate themselves to prayer and study. Others practice active evangelism, sending missionaries to remote regions to spread Tiovin's teachings. Some focus on scholarship and theology, maintaining vast libraries and training clerics in philosophy and ethics. Others prioritize healing and medicine, establishing hospitals and training physicians in both mundane and magical arts of curing disease.

Temples to Tiovin are almost always constructed with very high and vaulted ceilings, reaching toward the heavens as physical expressions of spiritual aspiration. In many towns and cities, the temple is the tallest building, usually adorned with stained glass windows depicting scenes from scripture or carvings and embellishments showing saints and angels. The architectural grandeur serves both practical and symbolic purposes—acoustically, the high ceilings allow hymns and prayers to resonate powerfully, while symbolically, they remind worshipers that heaven awaits above. Often there are representations of various followers of Tiovin or Tiovin itself defeating devils and the lord of the devils, Taovan, visual reminders of the eternal struggle between good and evil.

The garb and vestments of Tiovinians vary widely, although almost always featuring the holy symbol of Tiovin—two hands held in supplication—in a prominent location. In some places, such as Tentigo, many of the robes and outfits have deep reds and yellows, the patterns and shades indicating the rank of the priest in the hierarchy. Cardinals wear crimson trimmed with gold, bishops wear scarlet with silver, and lower ranks wear progressively simpler vestments. In other places, vestments are subtle and unobtrusive, such as the plain peasant robes many Angelics in the north wear, believing that ostentatious display contradicts the humility their faith demands. Some sects incorporate armor into their religious garb, with paladins wearing blessed plate mail bearing Tiovin's symbol, while others forbid their clergy from bearing arms at all.

Humans are by far the race that venerates Tiovin the most, his faith spread across the world to even some of the most remote areas to be found. From arctic settlements to tropical islands, wherever humans establish communities, shrines to Tiovin appear. Small numbers of the other races worship Tiovin as well, although they are mostly to be found in human lands where exposure to the faith is common. Some half-elves raised in human communities adopt Tiovinian faith, and a few dwarves who have close ties to human allies occasionally convert, though their people generally prefer Mordrion. Halflings sometimes worship Tiovin alongside Hinterhin, seeing no contradiction in honoring both the god of human virtue and the god of halfling community.

## Dogma

Lead a good life. This simple commandment forms the foundation of all Tiovinian teaching. Help the needy, feed the hungry, and heal the sick—see suffering and act to alleviate it. Worship your Lord as your God, and your only god. Other gods, while powerful, are not the true God in Tiovinian theology. They are mighty beings worthy of respect but not worship, servants or peers of Tiovin at best, usurpers at worst. Follow the church and its ideals and strive to live through Tiovin, letting divine will guide your actions and choices.

God is everywhere. Tiovin sees all, knows all, judges all. No sin goes unnoticed, no virtue unrewarded. This omnipresent awareness means that morality is not merely a matter of avoiding punishment from earthly authorities but of living righteously because the divine witness never sleeps. Every choice matters, every action has spiritual weight, every moment is an opportunity to serve good or succumb to evil.

Those who do not lead a good life will be cast into Hell to suffer for eternity. Tiovinian theology is clear on this point—virtue leads to Heaven, vice leads to Hell, and death fixes one's fate permanently. There is no reincarnation, no second chance after judgment. The stakes could not be higher. This teaching drives some followers to lives of genuine compassion and service, while it drives others to paranoid obsession with avoiding sin and enforcing virtue in others through any means necessary.

The flesh is only temporary, Heaven is eternal. Material existence is brief and ultimately insignificant compared to the infinite duration of the afterlife. Suffering in this world is bearable when one knows eternal joy awaits. Sacrifice in this life is rewarded infinitely in the next. This teaching can inspire tremendous courage and selflessness—martyrs who die protecting others, healers who risk infection to tend the sick, missionaries who travel to dangerous lands. It can also be twisted to justify exploitation and passivity—telling the poor and oppressed to endure injustice patiently because heaven will compensate them, or encouraging reckless martyrdom that accomplishes nothing except creating more suffering.

## Mythology

The history of Tiovin is inextricably intertwined with that of Taovan, his polar opposite. It was around 3000 BW that the dual entity of Tiovin and Taovan awoke, one mind and semblance of thought formed from the collective consciousness of humanity reaching a critical threshold. Within the first fraction of a second of its existence, it had trouble reconciling the good and evil in the world it had observed for so long while in its slumber. The entity perceived simultaneously every act of compassion and every act of cruelty, every sacrifice and every betrayal, every moment of love and every moment of hate across all of human history past and future.

In that instant before it formed its extra-dimensional plane, unable to hold these contradictions in a single consciousness, the entity split itself into two separate parts, two new entities named Tiovin and Taovan. The division was clean and absolute—all capacity for good flowed into Tiovin, all capacity for evil into Taovan. They almost instantly formed their realms, known as Heaven and Hell, dimensions shaped by their fundamental natures. Heaven became a place of eternal peace and joy, Hell a place of eternal suffering and torment. They began taking the souls of fallen humans into their afterlife via two new planes they created as conduits—Tiovin created the Ethereal Plane, a shimmering realm of dreams and possibility through which virtuous souls ascended to Heaven, while Taovan created the Shadow Plane, a dark reflection of reality through which damned souls descended to Hell.

As their minds were once one, they were able to agree to disagree on their views of the world and set a challenge to the other, one the other already had in its mind. The two entities would never set foot upon the lands themselves, honoring the principle that mortals must have free will to make their choices meaningful. The only influence that they would have on humankind would be exhibited through angels and devils, those sent back from the afterlife to guide and tempt respectively. It was in fact through those very angels and devils that the two would begin to influence the world and reveal themselves to humans, giving the faithful the ability to work magics no others could.

The angelic messengers first sent back to Mazariim to do works of good and spread the word of Tiovin are known as the Divine Missionaries, said to be some of the purest of good souls to be found, as they lived good and just lives without the promise of salvation or Heaven. These individuals had died before Tiovin revealed himself, living virtuously simply because it was right rather than for reward. Tiovin selected these souls and offered them a choice—remain in Heaven's peace, or return to the mortal world to teach others the path to salvation. Those who chose to return became angels incarnate, walking among mortals with divine purpose.

These angels would come to almost every corner of the world, letting far-off peoples know the truth of God, and that leading a life of honesty and purity would lead to gifts beyond the imaginations of man. They healed the sick with touches, fed multitudes with miracles, and spoke with authority about the nature of virtue and sin. Their words carried weight because they had lived as mortals and died as mortals before being elevated to divine service. It was around five hundred years later that the worship of entities like Tiovin and Minerva would begin to overtake the worship of the old elemental gods and powerful dragons, as people found personal relationship with deities who cared about morality more compelling than propitiation of indifferent natural forces.

Over the centuries, the faithful of Tiovin began to encounter the faithful of Taovan, devil-worshiping cultists that tempted the innocent and tried to make the good into evil. The rivalries between the two faiths would continue from 2500 BW until the next millennium, with increasing frequency and violence. Minor conflicts erupted as Tiovinian priests exposed devil-worshipers and Taovanian cultists assassinated particularly effective clergy. The tension reached a fever pitch around 1750 BW just as the first cities and nations of humankind were forming, when the very nature of human civilization hung in the balance.

In the end, the War of Souls would be won by Tiovin through a combination of military force, popular support, and divine intervention. Tiovinian paladins hunted down devil cults and destroyed their shrines. Inquisitors exposed corruption in positions of power. Common people, given a choice between a faith promising mercy and one promising domination, chose mercy in sufficient numbers to tip the balance. His faith would quickly spread through human civilizations as the Taovanians would move to the shadows, working from that point forward in whispers and secrets rather than open worship. Tiovin's worship would continue to flourish alongside the other newly developed entities, at times at odds with the dogma of some such as Polyvin and Xannith, and at times the willing ally of other good-aligned faiths like Paladine and Hinterhin.

In 555 BW, the remnants of the old civilization of Sabonaa began to splinter into other smaller nations, including what is today Naarlam. The ancient empire had collapsed under the weight of internal corruption and external pressures, and from its ruins emerged new kingdoms with new identities. The year 472 BW would see the founding of one of the greatest nations of Tiovin, ironically one that has strayed from what most Tiovinians would consider the good path. The Holy Theocracy of Tentigo began as a reform movement dedicated to purifying Tiovinian worship and enforcing strict adherence to scripture. They quickly transformed into a militant theocracy that would soon set its militaristic sights on its neighbors, conquering Cecastaa and other smaller cities near its borders in campaigns justified as spreading the true faith.

They would continue to occupy the neighboring lands until the War to End All Wars, during which many of the Tiovinian forces and cities were destroyed or barely survived the onslaught of Zuel's orcish hordes. The Tentigan military, stretched thin across occupied territories, suffered tremendous casualties. Almost a century after the War, Cecastaa would break free of Tentigo through a combination of popular uprising and military revolt, and set its sights on other faraway lands, leading Crusades into other parts of the world to try and convert the many heathens that lived without the glory of Tiovin. These crusades were often brutal, offering conversion or death, and earning Tiovinian faith a complicated reputation in regions that experienced forced evangelization.

After the War to End All Wars, Tiovin's followers suffered a sharp drop in followers and worshipers. After all, during the War, the many entities fought for the side of humans, elves, and dwarves alike, yet Tiovin did not step foot into the prime material plane. Mordrion manifested an avatar and fought alongside dwarves. Minerva empowered her priests with devastating magic. Even lesser entities made their presence felt directly. But Tiovin remained absent, honoring the ancient pact with Taovan that neither would directly intervene. Some said Tiovin wanted humans to prove themselves, to demonstrate that virtue could triumph without divine rescue. Others said he had abandoned the world and slipped into ambivalence, no longer caring about the mortals he claimed to love.

The abandonment was felt most keenly by non-human races who fought and died defending human cities. Elves questioned why they should honor a human god who would not defend his own people. Dwarves pointed to Mordrion as superior, a god who actually cared enough to fight. Halflings wondered why they had risked their lives when the supposed lord of heaven watched from a distance. This resentment persists eight centuries later, with Tiovinian missionaries often receiving cold receptions in non-human lands.

Still, eight centuries after the War, Tiovin remains the most popular deity of humankind, its churches and temples in almost every human land, its healers helping the infirm and its inquisitors seeking out devils, demons, undead, and the followers of the Devil himself, Taovan. The faith has proven resilient, adapting to local cultures while maintaining core principles. From arctic tribes to desert nomads to urban merchants, humans find in Tiovin a deity who represents their highest aspirations and judges their deepest failings. The eternal contest with Taovan continues, fought not with armies but with individual choices, each human soul a victory or defeat in a war that will never end.

Heaven awaits those who live justly. Hell awaits those who embrace evil. And between these eternal destinations, humanity struggles to choose which path to walk.
