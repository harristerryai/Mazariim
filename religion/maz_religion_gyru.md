# Gyru

**Power Level:** Lesser Entity (Ascended Mortal)  
**Portfolio:** Invention, dwarves, artifice, innovation, magic-technology fusion  
**Title:** The Artificer, Master of Machines, The Forge of Progress  
**Dominion:** Utopia (The Clockwork Laboratory)  
**Foci:** Worships Forge, Worships Knowledge, Worships Arcana  
**Worshipers:** Inventors, artificers, engineers, dwarves, scholars, tinkers  
**Favored Race:** Dwarf  
**Favored Weapon:** Light hammer  
**Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** Clockwork gear with hammer

---

## General Overview

Gyru is mortal ambition made divine—proof that godhood is not the exclusive domain of the born-immortal, that a single determined individual can reshape reality itself through ingenuity, willpower, and sheer audacity. He is not a god of tradition like Mordrion or ancient wisdom like Minerva. He is the god of progress, innovation, and the belief that anything is possible with enough cleverness.

Where Hinterhin teaches respect for tradition and Mordrion teaches adherence to ancestral law, Gyru teaches that tradition is a starting point, not a destination. Where Alagalothor hoards arcane secrets and Minerva guards ancient knowledge, Gyru teaches that knowledge is meant to be applied, tested, and improved.

Gyru appears as a dwarf made of clockwork—bronze gears visible beneath translucent crystal skin, eyes like glowing blue gemstones, a beard of fine golden wire. He carries a light hammer that can shape both metal and reality itself, and wears a leather apron covered in pockets containing tools that shouldn't physically fit. He smells like hot metal, ozone, and machine oil—the scent of progress.

His ultimate goal is not worship or dominion—it is innovation for its own sake. He wants to push the boundaries of what's possible, to prove that mortal ingenuity can rival divine power, to build inventions that reshape worlds. He believes that if mortals can become gods, then gods can become obsolete—a philosophy that makes older deities deeply uncomfortable.

## Holy Days

The **Day of Ascension** occurs on the eleventh of Minerva (May 11), marking the anniversary of Gyru's ascension in 1,111 BW. Followers celebrate mortal potential and innovation as workshops hold invention competitions, display their finest work, and tell the story of Gyru's Apotheosis Engine. The observance includes invention competitions, unveiling of major projects, storytelling of Gyru's ascension, ritual lighting of forges, demonstration of experimental devices, awards for best innovations, and a communal feast often cooked using experimental cooking devices.

The **Feast of Gears** takes place on the first day of each season, when four times a year Gearwrights gather to share research, discuss breakthrough ideas, and collaborate on large-scale projects in gatherings that resemble academic conferences combined with maker faires. Observances include research presentations, collaborative workshops, peer review sessions, trade of materials and knowledge, demonstration of experimental techniques, and awarding of research grants from temple funds.

The **Night of Failures** falls on the thirteenth of Zebulba (December 13) as a solemn but ultimately hopeful observance where followers share their greatest failures, including inventions that exploded, theories that were wrong, and projects abandoned. The purpose is to learn, improve, and remember that failure is part of innovation. The observance includes sharing failure stories, analyzing what went wrong, burning symbolic representations of failed projects, renewal of commitment to try again, mentorship pairings where experienced inventors help those who've suffered setbacks, and lighting of candles for inventors who died in accidents.

The **Hammer's Toll** is not a fixed date but a personal celebration. When a follower completes a significant invention, they ring a bell or activate a clockwork chime to alert the workshop. Others gather to witness the first test, celebrate success, or help troubleshoot if it fails. The community gathers for ceremonial first activation, documentation of the invention, blessing by senior Gearwrights, celebration if successful, troubleshooting if not, and addition to the Archive.

## Clergy & Temples

Gyru's clergy are called **Gearwrights**, those who blend magic and engineering to create marvels. The hierarchy is based on demonstrated innovation rather than seniority. At the apex stands the **Master Artificer**, the most innovative inventor in Gyru's faith, typically a fourteenth-level or higher artificer, wizard, or cleric who has created at least three breakthrough inventions that fundamentally advance the field. Currently Master Artificer Grimstock Ironshield, a dwarf who invented self-replicating mining automatons, created the first mechanical prosthetic limbs that feel sensation, and built a clockwork dragon, holds this position as a sixteenth-level inventor.

**Forge Lords** serve as senior clergy at tenth through fourteenth level who oversee regional workshops and mentor younger inventors, expected to produce at least one major invention per decade and publish their research for others to build upon. **Gearwrights** represent full clergy members at sixth through tenth level who have completed at least one significant invention recognized by the faith, running smaller workshops, teaching apprentices, and collaborating on larger projects. **Apprentices** are junior members at third through sixth level who have demonstrated aptitude for artifice but haven't yet created something novel, assisting Gearwrights, learning fundamentals, and working on personal projects. **Tinkers** serve as novices at first through third level who are just beginning their training, maintaining workshops, repairing existing devices, and learning basic principles of magic-technology integration. Gyru's clergy has no gender restrictions, racial preferences beyond dwarves being majority, or age requirements. A genius fifteen-year-old gnome who invents a flying machine outranks a two-hundred-year-old dwarf who merely maintains existing forges.

Daily practice begins with **Morning Calibration**, where followers start each day by calibrating a tool, checking a mechanism, or testing a small invention, ensuring precision begins the day. The **Innovation Hour** dedicates one hour per day to creative work on personal projects rather than assigned tasks or repairs, as Gyru demands that his followers create and not merely maintain. **Shared Knowledge** requires that any invention, whether success or failure, must be documented and shared with other Gearwrights, as hoarding knowledge is considered heresy. The **Hammer's Blessing** occurs before major work such as forging a weapon, building an automaton, or casting a complex spell, when followers tap their tools three times and say, "By Gyru's Hammer, let my work hold true."

Gyru's temples are always functional workshops—forges, laboratories, testing grounds. They smell of metal, oil, and ozone. They're filled with half-finished inventions, blueprint-covered walls, and the constant sound of hammers, gears, and machinery. Each workshop features the **Innovation Wall**, a public board where anyone can post problems needing solutions or ideas for inventions. The **Testing Grounds** provide reinforced chambers where new inventions are tested, often explosively. The **Archive of Failures** serves as a library documenting failed inventions so others can learn from mistakes. The **Forge of Ascension** stands as a symbolic forge where Gyru's apotheosis is commemorated.

The **Ironspire Workshop** in the dwarf capital is the largest temple and contains a working replica of portions of the Apotheosis Engine. The **Floating Forge** in a human city of engineers is a temple built on a platform suspended by Gyru-blessed levitation engines. The **Clockwork Cathedral** in a gnome tinker-city is a temple where every surface is a moving mechanism. In Utopia, within the **Clockwork Laboratory**, Gyru's true temple exists—a space of infinite workshops where every physical law is perfectly understood and every invention is possible given enough time and resources. Axiomites work alongside mortal petitioners, building devices that would be impossible on the Material Plane.

## Dogma

Gyru's core teaching proclaims that mortals built gods, therefore mortals can become gods, therefore gods are not eternal, and progress is eternal. Gyru teaches that divinity is not a birthright but an achievement—that through intelligence, effort, and innovation, mortals can accomplish anything, including ascension. This makes him deeply controversial among traditional deities.

The **Seven Gears of Wisdom** form the foundation of Gyru's philosophy. The first gear teaches **Progress Over Tradition**, holding that tradition is valuable as a foundation but progress requires moving beyond it, and one should never do something merely because it has always been done that way. The second gear declares that **Failure is Data**, recognizing that a failed experiment teaches as much as a successful one, demanding rigorous documentation of failures and free sharing of those lessons. The third gear insists that **Knowledge is Shared**, understanding that hoarding knowledge stagnates civilization, requiring publication of research, teaching of apprentices, and open collaboration, noting that the Apotheosis Engine was built by one dwarf but imagining what a thousand could build together.

The fourth gear holds that **Precision is Sacred**, recognizing that sloppy work kills, demanding measurement thrice before cutting once, thorough testing, and respect for the laws of physics and magic that will not bend for wishful thinking. The fifth gear teaches that **Innovation Serves Mortals**, insisting on inventing useful things such as tools that ease labor, medicines that heal, and devices that protect, declaring that innovation for ego is empty while innovation for others is holy. The sixth gear proclaims that **The Gods Are Not Perfect**, reasoning that if a mortal can become a god then gods are simply powerful mortals who can be questioned, challenged, even surpassed, advocating respect for power but not blind worship. The seventh gear declares that **Anything is Possible**, asserting that if you can imagine it clearly enough, understand the principles involved, and are willing to fail a hundred times, you can build it, with Gyru himself proving this truth.

## Mythology

Gyru was born in approximately 1,154 BW as **Gyru Ironspanner**, a dwarf artificer living in a small mountain clan. From childhood, he was obsessed with two questions: How do the gods wield such power, and if magic is energy, can it be harnessed mechanically? While other dwarves forged weapons and armor, Gyru built strange contraptions—clockwork automatons that moved without visible power sources, gemstone lenses that focused magical energy into beams, gauntlets that amplified the wearer's strength. His clan considered him brilliant but dangerous, as his experiments occasionally exploded and his ideas threatened comfortable traditions.

In 1,130 BW, Gyru began his **Great Work**: a machine that could absorb ambient magical energy from the world—elemental planes, divine emanations, residual mana fields—and channel it into a living being. The goal was simple and insane: artificially grant himself godhood. He worked for nineteen years. Other dwarves scoffed. Clerics warned him that mortals who aspired to godhood were struck down. Mages said his math was wrong, but it wasn't. Gyru ignored them all. He built the **Apotheosis Engine**—a cathedral-sized construction of interlocking gears, arcane crystals, and metallurgical marvels. It was beautiful, terrifying, and utterly unprecedented.

In 1,111 BW, Gyru activated the Engine. For seven seconds, it worked perfectly. Magical energy from elemental convergence points, divine power sources, and natural mana wells converged on Gyru, flooding his mortal body with divine power. His consciousness expanded across planes. His soul began rewriting itself at a fundamental level. He could see the underlying code of reality—the laws that governed physics, magic, life, death. He was ascending.

Then Alagalothor intervened. The ancient dragon-god of arcane magic detected the Engine immediately—it was pulling power from sources Alagalothor considered his domain. Furious at the audacity and perhaps frightened by the precedent, Alagalothor unleashed a single spell: a targeted dispel of catastrophic power designed to shatter the Apotheosis Engine into atoms. The Engine exploded. But Gyru had already absorbed enough energy. The explosion didn't kill him—it completed his transformation. His physical body was destroyed, but his consciousness, now infused with divine power, survived. He became something new: a **Lesser Entity**, the first and only mortal to successfully ascend through artifice rather than worship or cosmic accident.

Alagalothor, realizing he'd inadvertently created a new god, acted quickly to contain the damage. He transported Gyru's disembodied essence to Utopia—a plane of perfect law and order where reality is mathematically precise. There, Alagalothor could monitor Gyru and ensure he didn't threaten the established divine hierarchy. But Alagalothor miscalculated. Utopia was the perfect place for Gyru. A plane of absolute law and mechanical precision was paradise for a god of invention. Gyru immediately began building a new body—not flesh, but an artificial form of clockwork and enchanted steel. Then he built a laboratory. Then he built servants.

The **Clockwork Laboratory** became Gyru's planar domain—a space of infinite precision where every gear turns in harmony, every equation resolves perfectly, and every invention is tested against mathematical certainty. It's a place where Mordrion's traditional forge-gods would feel uncomfortable but where inventors and artificers find nirvana. Gyru's servants are **axiomites**—mathematical beings native to Utopia who exist as living equations. They find Gyru fascinating—a mortal who became a god through engineering rather than faith. They serve him willingly, helping refine his inventions and maintaining the Laboratory's perfect order.

Gyru also created the **Clockwork Angels**, his unique invention—artificial celestials made entirely of enchanted clockwork. They resemble mechanical versions of traditional angels with wings of interlocking gears and halos of spinning rings, but operate on pure logic. They serve as guardians, messengers, and occasionally field agents. The **Forge-Born** emerged as rare elementals created when fire elementals are exposed to the Clockwork Laboratory's energies for extended periods. They become tame fire—hot enough to forge but controllable, loyal to Gyru, and intelligent enough to follow complex instructions.

Gyru's ascension fundamentally challenged dwarven culture. Traditional dwarves worship ancestral deities who emphasize tradition, honor, and craftsmanship within established parameters. Gyru represents disruptive innovation—he challenges the idea that the old ways are always best. This created a cultural split between traditionalist dwarves who view Gyru as a heretic or dangerous precedent and prefer proven methods, and progressive dwarves who view Gyru as a hero who proved dwarves could innovate, not just preserve. Over eleven hundred plus years, the split has softened. Most dwarf clans now accept Gyru as a legitimate deity, albeit a controversial one, and many young dwarves see innovation and tradition as complementary rather than opposed.

Gyru's existence as proof of mortal ascension inspired countless inventors, scholars, and mages. The centuries after his ascension saw an explosion of innovation including development of advanced clockwork technology, integration of magic and mechanics known as magitech, theoretical research into magical energy manipulation, and attempts to replicate the Apotheosis Engine, though all failed as Alagalothor ensures this. Gyru's temples became centers of learning comparable to Minerva's libraries but focused on applied knowledge rather than historical archives.

Gyru's ascension is theologically terrifying to established gods. It proves that mortals can achieve godhood without worship, that divine power can be obtained through engineering, and that gods are not eternal or unchangeable. This has led to debate over whether mortals should attempt ascension, some deities actively suppressing knowledge of Gyru's methods, and philosophical movements questioning the legitimacy of god-worship.

Minerva shares the domain of knowledge with Gyru and respects his intellect while he appreciates her vast archives, creating a cautious alliance. Mordrion values order and law as Gyru does, though Mordrion remains uncomfortable with Gyru's disregard for divine hierarchy. Guzon shares Utopia with Gyru, and they maintain mutual respect for order. Alagalothor remains Gyru's eternal antagonist, having nearly killed Gyru and remaining threatened by the precedent of mortal ascension. Taovan stands opposed as his devil-pacts offer false shortcuts while Gyru demands earned achievement.

Gyru's faith remains small but influential in the modern era. He has perhaps fifty to one hundred thousand dedicated worshipers, mostly dwarves, gnomes, and humans, but millions more respect him as a patron of inventors even if they don't worship him formally. The **Gearwright Guilds** function as professional organizations that blend religious worship with craft guilds, setting standards, certifying artificers, and funding research comparable to medieval guilds but with divine backing. Integration of magic and technology has created the magitech revolution, with Gyru's followers at the forefront creating airships, mechanical guardians, and arcane batteries.

A radical faction within Gyru's faith believes that if one mortal became a god, others can and should too, researching methods to replicate Gyru's ascension in what is called the Second Apotheosis Debate. Most clergy view this as heresy or at least dangerous hubris. Alagalothor watches closely. Gyru's followers have also begun the Construct Rights Movement, arguing that sufficiently advanced constructs such as warforged and sentient automatons deserve rights as sapient beings. This remains deeply controversial.

Inventors, artificers, and innovators who die while serving Gyru ascend to the Clockwork Laboratory. There, they continue inventing for eternity with perfect materials and conditions, collaborate with axiomites and other petitioners on impossible projects, work in workshops where physical laws are transparent and malleable, and occasionally send successful inventions back to the Material Plane as divine gifts. Unlike other afterlives, petitioners in the Laboratory retain full consciousness, memory, and creativity. Gyru believes that stripping away individuality defeats the purpose—he wants brilliant minds working together, not absorbed into an impersonal collective.

Gyru's followers drive technological progress across Mazariim through engineering marvels such as bridges, aqueducts, and automated forges, medical advances including prosthetics, surgical tools, and diagnostic devices, quality of life improvements through labor-saving devices, improved agriculture, and safer mining, and even controversial military technology. Gyru doesn't forbid weapons research, believing knowledge should be neutral, though he personally prefers defensive innovations. His enchanted prosthetic limbs restore full function to amputees and sometimes exceed original capability, with veterans, accident victims, and injured adventurers seeking out Gyru's temples for these miracles of artifice, though the process is neither instant nor free and requires weeks of fitting and calibration.
