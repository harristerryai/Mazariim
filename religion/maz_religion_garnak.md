# Garnak

**Power Level:** Greater Entity  
**Portfolio:** Beasts, savagery, the hunt, primal instinct  
**Title:** The Midnight Stalker, The Beast Within, The First Hunter  
**Dominion:** The Savage Lands  
**Foci:** Worships Hunt, Worships Nature, Worships Survival  
**Worshipers:** Primitive races, beasts, lycanthropes, druids, rangers, barbarians, orc hunters  
**Favored Race:** None (revered by all primal races)  
**Favored Weapon:** Spear  
**Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** Bloody tooth and claw crossed

---

## General Overview

Garnak is the embodiment of primal savagery—the instinct to hunt, to feed, to survive. Unlike other deities who teach civilized philosophy or divine morality, Garnak teaches only one law: the law of the hunt. The strong hunt. The weak are hunted. The cycle continues eternally.

He awakened in 1666 BW during the Age of Awakening, when the primal forces of Mazariim reached critical mass and coalesced into divine consciousness. Garnak was not born from mortal worship like some entities, nor did he arrive from another universe—he emerged directly from the savage heart of Mazariim itself, from every predator that ever killed, from every prey animal that ever fled, from the eternal dance of fang and claw that has existed since the first creatures walked the world.

Where Dirgin represents the earth beneath, Vuleala represents the wind above, and Iquinias represents the waters between, Garnak represents the life that hunts upon it all. He is not merely the god of predators—he is the god of the hunt itself, the ancient ritual that binds predator and prey in eternal dance.

Garnak appears as a massive, shifting figure—sometimes humanoid, sometimes bestial, always terrifying. His most common form is that of a towering figure draped in the pelts of a thousand kills, his face hidden behind a helm made of antlers, tusks, and fangs. His eyes glow with amber light—the eyes of a wolf in the darkness, the eyes of a tiger stalking through jungle shadows. When he moves, it is with perfect silence, with the grace of the apex predator. When he strikes, it is with terrible efficiency.

His realm, the **Savage Lands**, exists as a primordial wilderness untouched by civilization—eternal forests, endless plains, towering mountains, deep jungles. Here, the hunt never ends. Here, petitioners who arrive spend eternity as either hunter or hunted, or both in cycles. Those who prove themselves exceptional hunters can ascend to become **Animal Lords**—demigods who embody specific predators such as Lord of Wolves or Lady of Tigers and rule domains within Garnak's wilderness.

Unlike entities focused on good or evil, Garnak is perfectly neutral. He does not care about morality—he cares only about the hunt. A paladin hunting orcs and an orc hunting villagers both honor Garnak in their own way. The kill is what matters. The hunt is what matters. Everything else is weakness and distraction.

## Holy Days

Garnak's followers do not celebrate fixed-calendar holidays but instead observe event-based rituals tied to the hunt itself. The **First Kill** occurs when a young hunter makes their first solo kill, serving as a rite of passage where the young hunter tracks, stalks, and kills a dangerous beast alone, usually a wolf, boar, or bear. They return with proof such as the heart or head, and the tribe or pack celebrates by feasting on the kill. The young hunter is given their first blood-mark scar and recognized as a true hunter.

The **Great Hunt** takes place in autumn as preparation for winter, when the entire community goes on a coordinated hunt to stockpile meat for the cold months ahead. This is the largest hunt of the year, targeting herds of deer, elk, or bison. The hunt lasts three to seven days, and the resulting feast lasts another three days, with offerings made to Garnak by burning the heart of the largest kill.

The **Blood Moon** is declared on the first full moon after a legendary kill such as a dragon, behir, or purple worm. When a hunter slays such a legendary beast, the hunter recounts the tale of the kill in detail, often taking hours, and the community honors Garnak by re-enacting parts of the hunt through ritual combat and dance. The hide or trophy of the beast is displayed, and younger hunters are encouraged to touch it for luck.

The **Hunt Eternal** marks the death of a great hunter, when their body is taken into the wilderness and left for scavengers. This is not disrespect but the ultimate honor, as the hunter becomes prey one final time, feeding the beasts they once hunted, while their spirit joins Garnak in the Savage Lands to hunt forever. Followers gather at dawn the next day to howl, roar, or make animal calls in tribute.

The **Winter Howl** occurs on the winter solstice, the longest night of the year, when Garnak's followers venture into the wilderness to hunt nocturnal predators such as wolves, owls, mountain lions, and winter wolves. They hunt through the entire night, and at dawn they return to howl in unison, thanking Garnak for another year of successful hunts. This is the one true fixed holiday, though it remains focused on the hunt rather than prayer or ceremony.

## Clergy & Temples

Garnak's clergy are called **Huntmasters** or **Bloodstalkers**, and they are among the most dangerous mortals on Mazariim. They are not priests who preach from temples—they are hunters who lead by example. They track, they kill, they survive. That is their sermon.

The hierarchy is brutally simple. At the apex stands the **Pack Leader**, the eldest and deadliest hunter in a region, usually a druid, ranger, or barbarian of tenth level or higher who has spent decades perfecting the art of the hunt. There is no formal appointment—one becomes Pack Leader by being recognized as the best hunter in the territory. Below them are the **Huntmasters**, experienced hunters at sixth through tenth level who lead smaller groups and train younger hunters while organizing major hunts, leading raids, and maintaining the hunting grounds. **Bloodstalkers** serve as mid-level hunters at third through sixth level who have proven their skill and earned the right to wear the blood-mark of Garnak, a ritualistic scar in the shape of crossed tooth and claw. At the base are **Trackers**, novice hunters at first through third level who are learning the ways of the hunt, assisting in tracking, carrying supplies, and learning from their elders.

Garnak's clergy are overwhelmingly druids, rangers, and barbarians, though occasionally clerics who focus on the Nature or War domains find their way to his worship. Lycanthropes make up a significant portion of his clergy, as they embody the beast within civilized form. Garnak's priests wear little—leather armor, fur pelts, bone jewelry, and the trophies of their greatest kills. The highest-ranking priests wear cloaks made from the pelts of legendary beasts: dire wolves, owlbears, displacer beasts, even young dragons. To wear such a cloak is to declare that the wearer has hunted and killed this creature and challenges others to dare face them.

Garnak has no traditional temples. His temples are the wilderness itself—deep forests, mountain ranges, untamed jungles, windswept plains. Any place where civilization has not tamed the land is sacred to Garnak. That said, his followers maintain **Hunting Lodges**—simple structures built in remote locations where hunters gather to share stories, prepare for hunts, and honor Garnak. These lodges are crude by civilized standards: log walls, thatch roofs, fireplaces, and racks for drying meat and curing pelts. The walls are adorned with trophies—skulls, antlers, fangs, claws—and the air always smells of smoke, blood, and leather.

Within each lodge is a **Hunter's Shrine**—a simple stone altar marked with the crossed tooth-and-claw symbol of Garnak. Here, hunters offer portions of their kills, usually the heart or liver of a particularly impressive beast. The offering is burned, and the smoke carries Garnak's favor to those who hunted well. Sacred groves also exist—ancient forests or mountain valleys where legendary hunts took place, where great beasts were slain, where packs of worshipers gather during holy days. These places are marked by standing stones carved with kill-counts, by totems made from the bones of mighty creatures, by circles of skulls that watch the forest with empty eyes.

## Dogma

Garnak's teachings are simple and brutal. The **Law of the Hunt** declares that all life is divided into two categories: hunter and prey. The strong hunt the weak. The weak flee or die. This is not cruelty—it is the fundamental law of existence. To deny this law is to deny life itself. Hunt or be hunted. Kill or be killed. The cycle is eternal. Honor the hunt.

**Respect Your Prey** teaches that the prey gives its life so that the hunter may live. Waste nothing. Honor the sacrifice. Garnak teaches respect for prey. Hunters must use every part of a kill—meat for food, hide for clothing, bone for tools, sinew for rope. To waste a kill is the highest insult. The prey died to feed you; honor that death by using it fully.

**The Hunt is Sacred** proclaims that one should not hunt for sport, not kill for glory. Hunt because you must. Kill because you hunger. The hunt is not entertainment—it is survival. Garnak despises trophy hunters who kill for amusement. The hunt is sacred because it sustains life. Those who hunt purely for pleasure or to display prowess dishonor the prey and insult Garnak. However, hunting dangerous prey to prove one's skill is acceptable—there is a difference between hunting a deer for sport versus hunting a dragon to test yourself.

**Strength is Earned, Not Given** holds that the strong survive while the weak perish. Do not aid the weak—they must become strong on their own, or they deserve to fall. Garnak's followers are Social Darwinists. They believe strength must be earned through struggle. To coddle the weak is to doom them to a life of dependence. Better to let them struggle, adapt, and become strong—or die and feed something else. This philosophy puts Garnak's clergy at odds with entities like Tiovin, Hinterhin, and Paladine, who value compassion and aid.

**Civilization is Weakness** teaches that cities make you soft, walls make you afraid, and comfort makes you prey. Garnak teaches that civilization is a trap. It offers safety, but safety breeds weakness. His followers often live on the fringes of society, venturing into the wilderness to hunt, returning to sell pelts and meat, then leaving again. Some never return at all, choosing to live as hermits or joining nomadic tribes that roam the wilds.

## Mythology

Garnak did not arrive from another universe, nor was he created by mortal belief. He awakened from the primal heart of Mazariim itself. For millions of years, life had hunted and been hunted on Mazariim. Predators stalked prey. Prey fled or fought. The cycle repeated endlessly, and with each hunt, a tiny fragment of consciousness accumulated—a primal awareness of the hunt itself. By 1666 BW, during the great Age of Awakening when many entities emerged, that accumulated consciousness reached critical mass and became Garnak.

He awakened fully formed, already ancient, already wise in the ways of fang and claw. He did not need worshipers to exist—he had existed in every predator since the dawn of time. Now he simply had a name, a will, and the power to reward those who honored the hunt. His first act was to claim the **Savage Lands**, a realm on the Outer Planes that mirrored the primordial wilderness of early Mazariim. Here, he established his eternal domain, where the hunt never ends and where the greatest hunters would someday come to prove themselves forever.

Garnak's worship spread quickly among primitive races—orcs, goblins, bugbears, gnolls, lizardfolk, and others who lived close to the land and hunted to survive. They recognized in Garnak the primal truth they already knew: hunt or be hunted. Druids and rangers also gravitated toward Garnak, particularly those who rejected the softer teachings of entities like Hinterhin (who values community and harvest) or Minerva (who values knowledge and beauty). These wilderness wanderers saw in Garnak a harsh but honest deity who did not preach impossible ideals—only the law of survival. Lycanthropes, particularly werewolves and weretigers, venerate Garnak as the patron of the beast within. They see their curse as a blessing—a chance to embrace their inner predator and hunt as they were meant to hunt.

When Zuel the Devourer arrived on Mazariim in 1866 BW, a theological conflict emerged among the orcs. Zuel taught conquest, war, and domination—take what you want, destroy what resists. Garnak taught the hunt, survival, and respect for prey—hunt what you need, honor the kill. Some orc tribes saw no contradiction—Zuel for war, Garnak for the hunt. Others chose sides. War-orcs followed Zuel and sought glory through conquest. Hunter-orcs followed Garnak and sought glory through legendary kills. To this day, the divide remains. Some orc tribes worship both, seeing them as complementary aspects of orcish strength, while others are exclusively Zuelites or Garnakites. Garnak and Zuel neither cooperate nor fight—they coexist uneasily, each believing they represent the true orcish soul.

Garnak did not directly participate in the War to End All Wars. He is not a god of organized war—he is a god of the hunt, and wars are messy, political things that have little to do with primal survival. However, his followers fought on both sides. Orcish hunters served in Zuel's armies, hunting elves and dwarves as they would hunt deer. Human and dwarven rangers fought against the orcs, tracking them through the wilderness and picking them off one by one. Garnak blessed them all—they were all hunting, after all. The only moment Garnak took direct interest was when Quarzel (a war deity) was destroyed by Zuel in the final battle. Garnak respected Quarzel's strength but did not mourn his death. In the wilderness, the weak die. Quarzel was weak. The hunt continued.

In the eight centuries since the war, Garnak's worship has remained steady among primitive races, druids, rangers, and lycanthropes. Unlike entities whose power waxes and wanes with civilization, Garnak's power is constant—because the hunt is constant. As long as creatures hunt and are hunted, Garnak endures. His influence is particularly strong in the Steppes of Broken Stone, the Wildwood, the Northern Tundra, and other untamed regions where civilization has not yet imposed its will.

Garnak respects Dirgin, the ancient earth deity. Dirgin provides the land; Garnak provides the hunt upon it. Druids who worship both are not uncommon. The wind goddess Vuleala and Garnak share a professional respect. Wind carries scent to hunters and carries hunters across plains. Vuleala respects Garnak's honesty. Mau the Oracle observes all things, including the eternal hunt. Garnak neither likes nor dislikes Mau, but acknowledges that Mau sees truth in all things, including the hunt.

The halfling god Hinterhin represents everything Garnak opposes. Hinterhin teaches comfort, safety, and mutual aid. Garnak teaches struggle, danger, and self-reliance. They are philosophical opposites, though not active enemies. The merchant god Polyvin teaches trade, diplomacy, and civilization. Garnak finds this pathetic. His followers avoid Polyvin's clergy whenever possible. The god of mercy and compassion Tiovin is fundamentally incompatible with Garnak's harsh philosophy. Garnak sees Tiovin's teachings as weakness. Tiovin sees Garnak's teachings as cruelty. Their clergy clash regularly. The god of honor and justice Paladine opposes Garnak's might-makes-right philosophy. Paladins often hunt down Garnak's more savage followers, particularly lycanthropes who terrorize villages. Garnak finds paladins delicious prey—armored, skilled, and satisfying to hunt.

Garnak despises Taovan. Taovan corrupts and enslaves. Garnak believes in earning strength through struggle, not buying it through devil-pacts. Taovan's followers hunt for power, not survival—they are false hunters. The god of nobility and aristocracy Xannith represents hereditary power and unearned privilege. Garnak finds this insulting. Strength must be earned, not inherited.

Garnak endures because the hunt endures. As long as creatures kill to survive, as long as predator stalks prey, Garnak's power remains absolute. He is not a god to be loved—he is a god to be respected, feared, and honored. Hunt well, or be hunted.
