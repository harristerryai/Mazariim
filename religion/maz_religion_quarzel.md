# **Quarzel** (KWAR-zel)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Destroyed 1 BW)  
**Portfolio**: War, fear, tyranny, glory through conquest  
**Title(s)**: The Warlord, The Conqueror, The Iron Fist, Lord of Titans  
**Dominion**: Fields of Battle (Outer Planes) — *now masterless*  
**Foci**: Worships War, Worships Strength, Worships Tyranny  
**Worshipers**: (formerly) Warlords, tyrants, soldiers, mercenaries, conquerors, gladiators  
**Favored Race**: None  
**Favored Weapon**: Greatsword  
**Alignment**: Lawful Evil  
**Symbol**: Blood-red banner with crossed swords  
**Status**: **Destroyed in 1 BW by Zuel the Devourer**

---

## General Overview

Quarzel is dead.

This simple fact defines everything about Quarzel's legacy. Unlike other deities who endure through the ages, Quarzel's story has an ending—and that ending is annihilation.

But before his destruction, Quarzel was one of Mazariim's most terrifying deities, the god of war as domination, of conquest through fear, of empires built on the broken backs of the defeated. Where Zuel teaches that the strong consume the weak, Quarzel taught that the strong rule the weak. Where Paladine teaches honorable combat to protect the innocent, Quarzel taught total war to subjugate the defiant.

Quarzel awakened in 1293 BW during the Age of Awakening, born from the collective consciousness of countless wars, conquests, and tyrannical empires. He was not the first war deity on Mazariim—Zuel had already arrived from Oerth in 1866 BW—but Quarzel represented a different aspect of warfare: organized, disciplined, systematic conquest. Zuel was a rampaging force of destruction; Quarzel was a calculating warlord.

His form was titanic and terrifying, a humanoid figure standing twenty feet tall, clad in black-and-crimson plate armor forged from the blood of conquered nations. His face was hidden behind a helm shaped like a screaming skull, and his eyes burned with cold blue fire. In one hand he carried an enormous greatsword dripping with the ichor of defeated gods; in the other, a blood-red banner that never stopped bleeding.

His servants were the Titans—fifty-foot-tall humanoid warriors of living stone and metal, each one capable of crushing armies. These beings served as Quarzel's generals, leading mortal warlords and enforcing his will across Mazariim. To see a Titan on the battlefield was to know despair.

Quarzel's dominion, the Fields of Battle, was an eternal battlefield on the Outer Planes—an endless expanse of blood-soaked earth where the spirits of warriors fought, died, and rose again to fight forever. Here, Quarzel's petitioners waged eternal war, proving their strength and earning glory. But when Quarzel was destroyed, the Fields of Battle fell into chaos. Some say it still exists, now a masterless realm where warbands of the damned fight without purpose, waiting for a new warlord to claim the throne.

Quarzel's worship spread quickly among warlords, tyrants, and conquerors who sought divine sanction for their brutal campaigns. His teachings were simple: might makes right, the strong rule, the weak submit or die. Quarzel did not promise peace or prosperity—he promised glory through victory and eternal war.

For 1,292 years, Quarzel reigned as one of Mazariim's most feared deities. His followers built empires, crushed rebellions, and carved their names into history with the edge of the sword. The month of Quarz (March) was named in his honor, a grim reminder that spring—the season of renewal—was also the season of war, when armies marched and blood watered the fields.

And then, in the final year of the War to End All Wars, Quarzel made a fatal mistake: he opposed Zuel the Devourer.

## Holy Days

The first day of Quarz (March 1) brought the Day of Iron, marking the beginning of the war season. This was a day of martial preparation when armies drilled, weapons were blessed, and plans for spring campaigns were finalized. Weapon blessings were performed at dawn, with War-Priests calling upon Quarzel to imbue blades and armor with his favor. Military parades displayed the might of Quarzel's armies through city streets, reminding citizens who truly held power. Gladiatorial games entertained the masses while demonstrating martial prowess, and in the darkest temples, captured enemies were sacrificed on altars of black stone, their blood offerings to the Warlord.

The fifteenth of Quarz (March 15) was celebrated as the Feast of Victory, a day dedicated to recounting past conquests. Warlords gathered in great halls to recount their greatest victories, each tale more brutal than the last. Subordinates honored their commanders with gifts and pledges of loyalty. Victory feasts featured the finest plunder from recent campaigns, and promotion ceremonies elevated the most successful warriors to positions of greater authority. Distribution of plunder to loyal soldiers reinforced the doctrine that conquest brought reward, that service to Quarzel meant wealth and power.

Whenever a lunar eclipse occurred, followers observed the Red Eclipse, considered a powerful omen of Quarzel's favor. Warband leaders would launch major offensives during eclipses, believing Quarzel walked among them and blessed their blades. Night battles were fought under the blood-red moon, armies clashing in darkness guided only by the eclipse's eerie light. Midnight raids struck at enemies caught unprepared, and blood sacrifices were made on outdoor altars beneath the eclipsed moon, the crimson light seeming to drink in the offerings.

Each region celebrated the Breaking of Chains on the anniversary of a major conquest specific to their area—the fall of a great city, the subjugation of a kingdom, the crushing of a rebellion. These celebrations reminded the conquered that resistance was futile and that Quarzel's dominion was eternal. Public executions of rebels served as grim reminders of the cost of defiance. Parades of enslaved prisoners showed the fruits of conquest, and re-enactments of the original battle allowed victors to relive their triumph while the defeated were forced to watch their humiliation replayed.

The thirtieth of Quarz (March 30) was known as the Final Stand, originally a day honoring warriors who died in glorious last stands, fighting to the death rather than surrendering. After Quarzel's destruction in 1 BW, his remaining followers transformed this into a memorial day for Quarzel himself—a day of mourning and vows of vengeance. The few cultists who still worship the dead god gather in secret on this day, performing mourning rituals and silent vigils. They swear vows to resurrect Quarzel, believing that through enough bloodshed and conquest, they can reconstitute his divine essence and bring their god back from oblivion.

## Clergy & Temples

Quarzel's clergy were called War-Priests or Blademasters, and they were as much military commanders as they were spiritual leaders. Quarzel did not value prayer or meditation—he valued victory. A priest who won battles was blessed. A priest who lost was forsaken. The hierarchy was rigid and brutal, organized around proven success in warfare rather than theological study or spiritual devotion.

The supreme commander of Quarzel's faith bore the title of the Iron Fist, usually a warlord of legendary prowess who had conquered nations and crushed armies. The last known Iron Fist was General Kargath Ironscar, a human tyrant who conquered a dozen kingdoms before the War to End All Wars. His fate after Quarzel's death remains unknown, though some believe he went mad when his connection to his god was severed, wandering the ruins of his fallen empire as a broken shell of his former glory.

War-Bishops served as regional commanders, clerics of tenth level or higher who oversaw military campaigns and temple-fortresses. They were expected to lead armies personally and demonstrate their strength in battle, for a War-Bishop who commanded from the rear was considered weak and unworthy of Quarzel's blessing. Blademasters functioned as field commanders, clerics and fighters between fifth and tenth level who served as the backbone of Quarzel's military structure. They trained soldiers, led battalions, and enforced the brutal discipline that made Quarzel's armies feared across Mazariim.

Sword-Priests were junior clerics between third and fifth level who blessed weapons, tended to wounded warriors, and performed battlefield rituals. They accompanied armies on campaign, ensuring that Quarzel's favor flowed to his faithful. Initiates were aspirants of first or second level who served as squires, messengers, and support staff until they proved themselves in combat. Many initiates died before reaching higher ranks, but those who survived became hardened veterans steeped in Quarzel's brutal teachings.

Quarzel's clergy were overwhelmingly clerics specializing in the War domain and fighters, with occasional paladins—or more accurately, antipaladins—who swore oaths of conquest. There were no bards singing of mercy, no healers focused on compassion, no pacifists preaching peace. Only warriors. Only conquerors.

War-Priests wore black-and-crimson armor or robes emblazoned with crossed swords, their vestments designed for battle rather than ceremony. High-ranking priests wore cloaks soaked in the blood of defeated enemies, magically preserved to remain wet and red—a grotesque display meant to inspire fear and demonstrate their prowess. The holy symbol, a blood-red banner with crossed swords, was carried into battle as a standard, and to capture an enemy's Quarzel banner was considered a great victory.

Quarzel's temples were fortress-monasteries, massive military installations designed to train soldiers, forge weapons, and serve as bases for conquest. They featured extensive barracks and training grounds where warriors honed their skills in brutal drills that often left the weak dead or maimed. Armories were filled with weapons blessed by Quarzel, each blade and axe consecrated to bring death to the Warlord's enemies. Altars of Victory stood in central halls where offerings of captured weapons and armor were made, trophies of conquest piled high as testament to Quarzel's power.

The Hall of Conquerors in each major temple was a vast chamber where the names and deeds of victorious generals were inscribed in blood-red script on black marble walls. To have one's name inscribed in the Hall of Conquerors was the highest honor a follower of Quarzel could achieve—proof that one's conquests would be remembered forever.

The largest temple was the Iron Citadel in the Kingdom of Vareth, a now-fallen empire that once stretched across a third of a continent. This towering fortress served as the headquarters of Quarzel's faith, a massive complex of walls, towers, training yards, and halls where the Iron Fist commanded the god's earthly forces. After Quarzel's destruction, the Iron Citadel fell into ruin. It is now said to be haunted by the ghosts of tyrants who still await their god's return, their spirits endlessly drilling phantom armies and plotting conquests that will never come.

Sacred sites included battlefields where great conquests occurred. Quarzel's followers would erect monoliths on these sites, inscribed with the names of the victorious generals and the armies they crushed. These standing stones served as permanent reminders of Quarzel's power and the futility of resisting his chosen champions.

## Dogma

Quarzel's teachings were brutal and uncompromising, a philosophy of domination that justified any atrocity in pursuit of conquest. The strong rule while the weak submit—this is the only law that matters. Quarzel taught that power was the ultimate arbiter of truth. Those with strength had the right to rule; those without strength had the duty to obey. Morality, justice, compassion—these were illusions invented by the weak to restrain the strong. The universe cared nothing for fairness or mercy. Only strength mattered, and those who possessed it were entitled to take whatever they desired.

A warrior's worth is measured in victories. Conquer, or be conquered. Quarzel valued conquest above all else. To build an empire, to crush rebellions, to subjugate rival nations—this was glorious. To defend one's homeland, to fight for survival, to resist tyranny—this was cowardice. Only through aggressive expansion could a warrior prove their worth. A general who fought only defensive wars was merely delaying the inevitable. True glory came from taking what belonged to others and making it your own through force of arms.

Let your enemies fear you, and they will submit before the first blow. Quarzel taught that fear was the most effective weapon in a conqueror's arsenal. A warlord who inspired terror could control populations without lifting a sword. Public executions, mass enslavements, scorched-earth campaigns—these tactics broke the will of enemies and made future conquests easier. His followers were encouraged to be ruthless, to show no mercy, to ensure that their reputation preceded them so that cities would surrender at the mere sight of their banners.

An army without discipline is a mob, and a mob is slaughtered. Unlike Zuel, who valued raw strength and savage fury, Quarzel valued organized, disciplined warfare. His armies fought in tight formations, followed complex strategies, and executed orders without question. Quarzel's War-Bishops were master tacticians who studied the campaigns of great generals, learning from both victory and defeat. His followers prided themselves on their military precision, considering the berserker rage of Zuel's orcs to be wasteful and inefficient.

If you are defeated, you deserved it. If you are enslaved, you were too weak to resist. Accept your fate. Quarzel had no sympathy for the conquered. To be defeated was proof of weakness, and weakness was unforgivable. His followers justified slavery, oppression, and genocide with this logic: the strong survive, the weak do not, and this is natural law. Those who fell before Quarzel's armies had proven themselves unworthy of freedom, and their subjugation was therefore just and proper.

## Mythology

Quarzel awakened in 1293 BW during a period of intense warfare on Mazariim. Kingdoms rose and fell, warlords carved out empires, and armies clashed across continents. This concentration of martial energy—the collective will to conquer, to dominate, to rule—reached critical mass and coalesced into Quarzel. He was not summoned or created but emerged spontaneously from the accumulated violence and ambition of mortal civilizations, the living embodiment of organized warfare and systematic conquest.

He manifested on a battlefield in the midst of a siege, appearing as a titanic figure wreathed in crimson fire. The armies on both sides stopped fighting and stared in awe and terror as this being of pure martial power materialized before them. Quarzel spoke only once, his voice echoing across the blood-soaked plain like the clash of ten thousand swords: "I am Quarzel. I am war. Serve me, and I will grant you victory. Defy me, and I will grant you oblivion."

The commanders of both armies immediately swore allegiance, falling to their knees before this terrifying deity. Quarzel blessed them both—and then commanded them to resume the battle. The one who won would become his first high priest. The one who lost would be forgotten. The battle raged for three days and ended with General Vorgath the Conqueror victorious, his rival slain and his army triumphant. Quarzel elevated Vorgath to the rank of Iron Fist, and thus Quarzel's faith was born in blood and conquest, as all things in his service would be.

Over the next twelve hundred years, Quarzel's worship spread across Mazariim like a plague of steel and fire. His followers built empires, conquered kingdoms, and enslaved entire populations. Some of the most brutal regimes in history were led by Quarzel's War-Bishops, their names still spoken with fear and loathing centuries after their fall.

The Iron Dominion of Vareth ruled from 800 BW to 3 BW, a militaristic empire that conquered half the continent through overwhelming force and systematic brutality. The Dominion's legions were legendary for their discipline and their cruelty, leaving devastation in their wake. The Crimson Legion operated from 400 BW to 50 BW as a mercenary army that served the highest bidder and toppled governments for profit, caring nothing for ideology or justice—only victory and payment. The Slavers' Coalition from 200 BW to 3 BW was a network of warlords who captured and sold tens of thousands of people into bondage, treating sentient beings as mere commodities to be bought and sold.

Quarzel's influence was felt everywhere war and tyranny existed. Even those who did not worship him acknowledged his power, and many sought his blessing when launching campaigns of conquest. But his greatest conflict was not with the forces of good, but with a rival god of war.

When Zuel the Devourer arrived on Mazariim in 1866 BW, Quarzel saw him as both rival and kindred spirit. Both were war deities, but they represented fundamentally different philosophies of warfare. Zuel taught savage, chaotic warfare—rampaging destruction, overwhelming force, total annihilation of everything in one's path. Quarzel taught disciplined, organized warfare—strategic conquest, fear-based control, systematic subjugation that left infrastructure intact for exploitation. Zuel destroyed; Quarzel conquered.

For centuries, the two deities coexisted uneasily. Some warlords worshiped both, seeing no contradiction between Quarzel's discipline and Zuel's fury, believing that different situations called for different approaches to war. Others chose sides, with Zuel's followers being mostly orcs, goblins, and barbarians who valued individual prowess and savage strength, while Quarzel's followers were mostly humans, hobgoblins, and militaristic cultures who valued organization and strategy. The tension between the two faiths simmered for over a millennium, building toward an inevitable confrontation.

That confrontation came during the War to End All Wars. When Zuel's orcish hordes invaded Mazariim between 3 BW and 1 BW, Quarzel faced a terrible dilemma. On one hand, Zuel represented chaos and destruction—everything Quarzel's ordered, disciplined approach to warfare opposed. On the other hand, Zuel's invasion was itself a form of conquest, which Quarzel valued above all else. The philosophical conflict was stark.

Ultimately, Quarzel chose to oppose Zuel, not because he cared about the fate of the civilized races or felt any compassion for those being slaughtered, but because Zuel's victory would leave no kingdoms to conquer. If Zuel destroyed everything, if he reduced the entire continent to burning rubble and scattered survivors, there would be no empires left to rule, no populations left to subjugate, no glory left to claim. Quarzel needed civilization to exist so his followers could conquer it. Zuel's apocalyptic vision threatened to destroy Quarzel's entire purpose for being.

In the final year of the war, in 1 BW, when Zuel's avatar led a massive army toward Fort Hope, Mordrion the Dwarf Father manifested his own avatar to confront Zuel in direct combat. Quarzel joined Mordrion, along with several lesser entities, believing that together they could defeat Zuel and claim victory. They would save civilization—not out of kindness, but to preserve it for future conquest. It was a catastrophic miscalculation.

The confrontation took place on the plains outside Fort Hope, a battle that would be remembered for millennia. Zuel's avatar—a towering figure of muscle, rage, and malice—faced Mordrion's avatar and Quarzel side-by-side. The battle was apocalyptic. Divine power clashed with divine power. The earth shattered beneath their feet. The sky burned with divine fire. Mountains in the distance crumbled from the shockwaves of their blows.

And then, in a moment of terrible clarity, Zuel destroyed them all.

Mordrion's avatar was shattered, his divine essence scattered and forced back to his dominion on Mount Celestia. The lesser entities were obliterated, their souls consumed by Zuel's overwhelming hunger. But Quarzel's fate was far worse than mere banishment.

Zuel did not merely banish Quarzel's avatar—he destroyed Quarzel's divine essence entirely. With a single, overwhelming strike fueled by the accumulated rage of the orcish hordes and Zuel's own inexhaustible fury, Zuel tore through Quarzel's godly form and consumed his power. Quarzel's scream echoed across Mazariim as his existence was erased, not just his physical form but his very essence as a deity. The Warlord was unmade.

The Fields of Battle, Quarzel's dominion on the Outer Planes, fell into immediate chaos. His Titans crumbled to dust, their divine animating force severed. His War-Bishops felt their connection to their god snap like a broken rope, and many went mad from the shock of losing the divine presence that had guided them for decades. Quarzel was gone, truly and completely destroyed.

Ironically, moments later, Demious Stoneshaper—a dwarven hero wielding a legendary weapon—defeated Zuel's avatar, ending the war and saving civilization. But by then, Quarzel was already dead. He had died for nothing, his sacrifice meaningless, his power consumed by the very rival he had sought to defeat.

Quarzel's destruction left his followers in complete disarray. Some War-Bishops refused to believe he was truly gone and continued to worship him, insisting he would return from whatever dark place consumed gods go. Others abandoned the faith entirely, seeking new gods or descending into nihilism, their entire worldview shattered by the proof that even gods could die. A few turned to Zuel, worshiping the god who had killed their own deity, believing that Zuel's power proved his superiority and that serving the strongest was the ultimate expression of Quarzel's own teachings.

The Iron Citadel fell into ruin, its vast halls empty, its forges cold. Quarzel's empires collapsed without divine support, conquered in turn by those they had once oppressed. His name became a cautionary tale whispered among the faithful of other gods: even gods can die.

The question of whether Quarzel can still grant divine power is hotly debated among scholars of theology and divine magic. The orthodox view, held by most clerics and priests of other deities, is that Quarzel is gone and cannot grant spells, cannot manifest, cannot answer prayers. His remaining worshipers are deluded, clinging to the corpse of a dead god.

The heretical view, held by some remaining Quarzelites, is that belief itself has power. If enough mortals believe in Quarzel, pray to Quarzel, and perform rituals in his name, perhaps they can sustain a fragment of his essence—a divine echo that can still grant limited power. They point to the fact that some clerics who worship Quarzel can still cast spells as proof that something of the Warlord remains.

The truth, known only to a few powerful entities like Mau the Oracle and Alagalothor, is more nuanced. Quarzel's divine essence was destroyed, but his myth endures. Clerics who worship Quarzel can still gain power—not from Quarzel himself, but from the conceptual force of war, tyranny, and conquest that he once embodied. In effect, they are drawing power from the idea of Quarzel, from the archetype of the Warlord, not from the god himself. The power is real, but its source is different from what his worshipers believe.

A small but fanatical cult called the Crimson Spear believes that Quarzel can be resurrected. They believe that if they conquer enough kingdoms, spill enough blood, and inscribe enough names in the annals of war, they can reconstitute Quarzel's essence and bring him back from oblivion. Their leader, High War-Bishop Malachar Ironvein, a human cleric and warlord of fifteenth level, has spent the last three hundred years building a secret army in remote mountain strongholds. He believes that the next great war—greater even than the War to End All Wars—will generate enough divine energy to resurrect Quarzel and restore him to his former glory.

Some scholars believe Malachar is insane, driven mad by the loss of his god. Others fear he might be right, that the accumulated violence of a sufficiently massive war could birth a new god of conquest—if not Quarzel returned, then something similar, equally terrible.

Quarzel's dominion, the Fields of Battle, still exists on the Outer Planes—but it is now a masterless realm. Warbands of petitioners, spirits of dead warriors who worshiped Quarzel, continue to fight endless battles, driven by instinct and habit. Without Quarzel to direct them, to give their eternal warfare purpose, they simply fight because fighting is all they know. Some believe that if a mortal warrior of sufficient power were to enter the Fields of Battle and claim Quarzel's empty throne, they could become the new god of war—inheriting Quarzel's portfolio and his remaining worshipers. Several warlords have attempted this over the centuries, journeying to the Outer Planes to claim divine power. None have succeeded. None have returned.

Quarzel's relationships with other entities were largely defined by enmity. Zuel destroyed Quarzel and consumed his essence, and Quarzel's remaining followers hate Zuel with burning passion. When they encounter Zuelites, they attack on sight, believing that destroying Zuel's worshipers might somehow weaken the god who killed their patron. The feeling is mutual—Zuel's followers view Quarzelites as weaklings who worship a dead god, clinging to a corpse while the strong march forward.

Mordrion and Quarzel were enemies even before the final battle. Mordrion valued community, craftsmanship, and defense; Quarzel valued conquest, domination, and aggression. The philosophical gulf between them was absolute. Mordrion's followers helped defeat Quarzel's empires during the War to End All Wars, and they celebrated his death as proof that tyranny cannot endure forever.

Hinterhin, the halfling god of peace and harvest, despised Quarzel with quiet intensity. Quarzel's armies raided halfling villages, enslaved farmers, and burned crops without thought or remorse. Hinterhin's followers rejoiced when Quarzel fell, seeing it as cosmic justice for centuries of suffering. Paladine, the god of justice and honor, was Quarzel's perfect antithesis. Paladine fought against Quarzel's tyrannical followers for centuries, his paladins standing as bulwarks against conquest and oppression. Paladine's followers see Quarzel's death as proof that justice eventually triumphs, no matter how long evil seems to prosper.

Rivoleen, the gnomish goddess of freedom, hated Quarzel for his emphasis on tyranny and slavery. Rivoleen's followers actively worked to undermine Quarzel's empires, liberating slaves, sabotaging supply lines, and spreading sedition among conquered populations. They consider Quarzel's destruction one of their greatest victories, though they had no direct hand in it.

Some entities had more complicated relationships with the Warlord. Taovan, the god of corruption and evil, sometimes cooperated with Quarzel. Both valued domination and tyranny, though Taovan used deception and corruption while Quarzel used brute force. After Quarzel's death, some of his followers turned to Taovan, who welcomed them and incorporated their martial discipline into his own schemes. Pyrantor, the mindless god of fire and destruction, appreciated Quarzel's emphasis on warfare, as fire and war often go hand-in-hand. But Pyrantor is chaotic and elemental while Quarzel was lawful and methodical, so they rarely cooperated directly.

Quarzel's legacy endures despite his death. The month of Quarz still bears his name, a permanent scar on the calendar. The Fields of Battle still exist, waiting for a new master. The Crimson Spear cult still works in secret, plotting conquest and resurrection. And somewhere, in the hearts of warlords and tyrants who never heard Quarzel's name, the whisper remains: might makes right, the strong rule, the weak submit.

Quarzel is dead. But war is eternal.
