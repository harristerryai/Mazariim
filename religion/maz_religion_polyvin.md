# Polyvin

Power Level: Greater Entity (Native, awakened 374 BW)
Portfolio: Luck, fate, portals, merchants, travel, opportunity
Titles: Lord of Fortune, The Wanderer's Friend, Master of Doors
Dominion: Towers of Chance / City of Fortune
Foci: Worships Luck, Worships Travel, Worships Trickery
Worshipers: Halflings, merchants, gamblers, travelers, adventurers, the hopeful
Favored Race: Halfling
Favored Weapon: Dagger
Alignment: Neutral Good
Symbol: Golden coin with a door

---

## General Overview

Polyvin is the god of second chances—the belief that fortune favors the bold, that opportunities appear when you least expect them, that a closed door is merely an invitation to find another way. He is not the god of wealth (that's Xannith's corruption) or greed (which he opposes vigorously). He is the god of fair trade, honest prosperity, and the luck that comes to those who try.

Where Xannith teaches that everything has a price and exploitation is virtue, Polyvin teaches that everyone deserves a fair chance, that rising tides lift all boats, and that prosperity shared is prosperity multiplied. Where Xannith hoards, Polyvin circulates. Where Xannith enslaves through debt, Polyvin liberates through opportunity.

He awakened in 374 BW during an era of economic hardship. Xannith's influence had created a society of extreme inequality—massive wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, grinding poverty for the rest. Merchants were taxed into bankruptcy, trade routes were monopolized, and common folk had no hope of rising above their station.

Into this despair came Polyvin—a god who promised that luck could change, that fortune could turn, that even the poorest halfling or most desperate merchant might stumble upon opportunity if they kept trying. His message resonated powerfully, especially among halflings (who had always believed in luck and second chances) and honest merchants (who were tired of being exploited by Xannith's Chains of Gold).

Polyvin's rise was meteoric. Within decades, he had converted a small but significant number of Xannith's followers—those who had pursued wealth but still possessed a conscience. His temples (which he calls Fortune Halls) appeared in cities across Mazariim, offering not predatory loans but honest partnerships, not exploitation but opportunity.

Polyvin's domain, the Towers of Chance (also called the City of Fortune), is a bustling planar metropolis where deals are made, fortunes are won and lost, and travelers from across the multiverse come to seek opportunity. Unlike Xannith's Abaddon (where wealth brings only dissatisfaction), Polyvin's domain is a place of genuine prosperity—not because people are given everything, but because they are given chances.

His servants are varied: Luck Dragons (small, playful dragons that bring fortune), Trickster Fey (who open unexpected doors), and Merchant Angels (celestial beings who facilitate fair trades). Unlike the fiends that serve evil deities, Polyvin's servants genuinely want to help mortals—though they also enjoy a good gamble and are known for their mischievous sense of humor.

Polyvin appears as a halfling dressed in traveler's clothes—practical boots, a sturdy cloak, and a wide-brimmed hat. He carries a coinpurse that never empties (but only ever dispenses fair payment, never excess) and a ring of keys that can open any door (literal or metaphorical). His eyes twinkle with mischief, and he always seems to be on his way somewhere interesting.

His ultimate goal is not power or worship but opportunity for all. He wants a world where anyone—regardless of birth or wealth—can succeed through hard work, cleverness, and a little bit of luck. He is the patron of dreamers, risk-takers, and those who refuse to accept that their circumstances are fixed.

## Holy Days

The Lucky Day is celebrated on 1 Polyvin (March 1) and is the holiest day of Polyvin's calendar. Followers believe this day is especially blessed with good luck. Fortune Halls host massive gambling tournaments, merchants offer lucky sales, and people take risks they've been postponing—asking for promotions, proposing marriage, starting businesses. The observances include gambling tournaments, risk-taking challenges, luck blessings where clerics bless coins, dice, and trinkets, and feasts where everyone shares their luckiest story.

The Day of Open Doors is celebrated on 15 Polyvin (March 15) and is dedicated to new opportunities. Fortune Halls offer free career counseling, help people find new jobs, and connect merchants with partners. It is traditional to open a new door on this day—start a new project, meet new people, or take a journey. The day features career fairs, networking events, free counseling, symbolic door-opening ceremonies, and travel blessings.

The Traveler's Toast occurs four times a year, on the first day of each season. Polyvin's followers gather to honor travelers—those who have left on journeys, those who have returned, and those who died on the road. It is a bittersweet celebration: joy for opportunities taken, sorrow for those lost. The observances include toasts to travelers, sharing travel stories, blessings for upcoming journeys, and lighting candles for those lost on the road.

The Fortune's Reversal is not a fixed date but a personal celebration. When someone's luck dramatically changes—winning big, losing everything, narrowly avoiding disaster—they visit a Fortune Hall to thank Polyvin or seek guidance. Clerics perform divinations to help interpret the reversal. Personal visits to Fortune Halls, divination rituals, offerings of coins (won or lost), and prayers for continued luck or recovery from bad luck mark these occasions.

## Clergy & Temples

Polyvin's clergy are called Fortunates or Door Openers—those who help others find opportunity. The hierarchy is loose and merit-based, not wealth-based. The Grand Fortunate serves as the spiritual leader of Polyvin's faith, chosen not by wealth or seniority but by a roll of dice blessed by Polyvin himself. Currently Finnegan Quickfoot, a halfling cleric of 13th level, holds this position. He won it despite being relatively young and poor. His selection is seen as proof that Polyvin truly values luck over everything else.

Fortune Masters are regional leaders (9th-13th level) who oversee Fortune Halls and coordinate charitable ventures. They are expected to be successful but not wealthy—Polyvin values those who prosper through honest work and share that prosperity. Door Openers are mid-level clergy (5th-9th level) who travel the world helping people find opportunities—connecting merchants with buyers, introducing adventurers to patrons, helping refugees find new homes. They are wanderers by nature.

Lucky Ones are junior clergy (3rd-5th level) who staff Fortune Halls, offer advice, and perform minor blessings. They are trained in both commerce and divination. Aspirants are novices (1st-3rd level) who are still finding their own luck. They assist senior clergy and learn the art of recognizing opportunity.

Polyvin's clergy wear traveler's clothing—practical, durable, and comfortable. They favor greens, browns, and golds (the colors of coins and growing things). The holy symbol (a golden coin with a door) is worn prominently, often as a pendant or belt buckle. High-ranking clergy carry symbolic keys representing doors of opportunity.

Polyvin's temples are called Fortune Halls—welcoming buildings designed to feel like a combination of tavern, marketplace, and community center. The Coin Room is where Fortunates offer financial advice, small loans (interest-free or low-interest), and help people plan business ventures. The Door Gallery is a hall filled with symbolic doors representing different opportunities (travel, trade, adventure, etc.). Worshipers are encouraged to open a door (literally turn a handle) and see what opportunity Polyvin reveals through divination. The Traveler's Rest provides free lodging for travelers, adventurers, and merchants. The Lucky Table is a gaming hall where gambling is encouraged (but cheating is severely punished). A portion of winnings is donated to Polyvin's charities.

The largest Fortune Hall is the Grand Bazaar of Chances in a major halfling settlement—a massive complex that combines temple, marketplace, inn, and gambling hall. It's a bustling center of commerce where deals are made, fortunes are won, and opportunities are found.

Sacred sites include crossroads (where travelers choose paths), marketplaces (where opportunity and commerce meet), ports and docks (where journeys begin), and sites where lucky events occurred (where someone found unexpected fortune).

## Dogma

Polyvin's teachings are optimistic and practical. He teaches that fortune favors the bold. Luck comes to those who try. You cannot win if you do not play. Polyvin teaches that opportunities do not appear to those who sit idle—they appear to those who take risks. Attempt the impossible. Start the business. Take the journey. Ask the question. The worst answer is no, and even a no teaches you something.

When one path is blocked, seek another. There is always another way. Polyvin is the god of adaptability. His followers do not despair when plans fail—they look for alternatives. Lost your job? Find a new one. Business failed? Start a different one. Path blocked? Take a detour. Polyvin teaches that setbacks are not endings—they are redirections. Every door closed is another door opened.

Wealth hoarded is wealth wasted. Prosperity shared is prosperity multiplied. Polyvin teaches that generosity creates more opportunities for everyone. If you succeed, help others succeed. If you find fortune, share it. This is not altruism—it is enlightened self-interest. A rising tide lifts all boats, and you benefit when your community prospers. His followers are taught to share their fortune.

Judge people by their effort, not their birth. The poorest halfling today may be the wealthiest merchant tomorrow—if given a chance. Polyvin opposes hereditary privilege (Xannith's domain). He teaches that everyone—regardless of race, class, or background—deserves an opportunity to prove themselves. This philosophy makes him extremely popular among the poor and working class, who see him as their advocate against the aristocracy. Every being deserves a fair chance.

Luck is sacred. Cheating is blasphemy. Those who cheat at games cheat at life—and Polyvin remembers. Polyvin takes gambling and fair play very seriously. His followers are taught that cheating—in games, in business, in life—is the highest sin. Those caught cheating face Polyvin's curse: they become cosmically unlucky until they make amends. Dice always roll against them. Coins always land tails. Doors jam when they try to open them. Cheat at your peril.

## Mythology

Polyvin awakened during a time of economic crisis in 374 BW. Xannith's influence had created a system where wealth was concentrated, opportunities were monopolized, and common folk had no hope of advancement. Merchants were crushed by debt, travelers were robbed by bandits hired by corrupt officials, and luck seemed to favor only the already-rich.

A halfling merchant named Garrick Twocoins was on the verge of bankruptcy. He had been an honest trader, but Xannith's Chains of Gold had sabotaged his business, spread false rumors, and bribed officials to deny him trading licenses. Facing ruin, Garrick knelt at a crossroads and prayed—not to Xannith (whose price he could not pay), not to Tiovin (whose mercy he did not seek), but to luck itself. If there is any force in the universe that gives second chances, he prayed, I could use one now.

And Polyvin answered. A door appeared in the middle of the crossroads—a literal, free-standing door that had not been there moments before. Garrick opened it and found himself in a bustling planar city (the Towers of Chance). A figure approached—a halfling traveler who introduced himself as Polyvin, Lord of Fortune. You seek a second chance? he asked. I offer chances. What will you do with yours?

Garrick returned to Mazariim with Polyvin's blessing, a coinpurse that never emptied (but only ever held fair payment), and a new trade route that no one else knew existed. Within a year, his business was thriving. Within a decade, he had founded the first Fortune Hall. Within a century, Polyvin's worship had spread across the world.

From the moment of his awakening, Polyvin was Xannith's enemy. He represented everything she despised: generosity, fairness, shared prosperity. She represented everything he despised: exploitation, hoarded wealth, inherited privilege. The two faiths engaged in economic warfare. Xannith's followers sabotaged Polyvin's trade ventures, bribed officials to shut down Fortune Halls, and spread propaganda claiming Polyvin was a god of gamblers and beggars. Polyvin's followers exposed Xannith's corruption, offered alternatives to her predatory loans, and helped people escape debt slavery.

The conflict continues to this day. In some nations, both faiths are legal and compete openly. In others, one or both are banned depending on who controls the government. But Polyvin's message resonates powerfully with the common folk, while Xannith's appeals to the already-wealthy. It is a battle between the elite and the masses—and Polyvin is slowly winning.

Halflings have always believed in luck—it is central to their culture. When Polyvin awakened, they recognized him instantly as their patron. While Hinterhin remains the primary halfling deity (god of hearth, home, and harvest), Polyvin is a close second, especially among traveling halflings, merchants, and adventurers. Polyvin's philosophy aligns perfectly with halfling values: adaptability, optimism, generosity, and the belief that anyone can succeed if given a chance. His worship is so widespread among halflings that some scholars joke, Show me a halfling merchant who doesn't pray to Polyvin, and I'll show you a liar.

During the War to End All Wars (3 BW – 1 AW), Polyvin's followers provided logistics and support. They organized supply lines, negotiated truces, helped refugees find safe passage, and ensured that soldiers had food and equipment. They did not fight—that was not their way—but they enabled those who did. After the war, Polyvin's Fortune Halls played a crucial role in economic recovery. They offered low-interest loans to rebuild businesses, helped displaced people find new homes, and facilitated trade between formerly warring nations. Polyvin's philosophy—that prosperity shared is prosperity multiplied—proved correct. Nations that embraced his teachings recovered faster than those that clung to Xannith's hoarding.

In the 811 years since the war (811 AW to Present), Polyvin's influence has grown steadily. He is now one of the most popular deities among the common folk, second only to Tiovin (mercy) and Hinterhin (home). His Fortune Halls are centers of community life, offering not just worship but practical help—financial advice, job placement, dispute mediation, and traveler's aid.

His greatest modern achievement is the Free Traders' Guild, a coalition of Polyvin-worshiping merchants who oppose monopolies, advocate for fair trade laws, and provide alternatives to Xannith's exploitative practices. They are not as wealthy as Xannith's Chains of Gold, but they are far more numerous—and far better liked.

Polyvin maintains close alliances with several entities. Hinterhin, the halfling god of hearth and home, is Polyvin's closest ally. Both value community, generosity, and the simple joys of life. Their churches cooperate extensively. Tiovin, the god of mercy, appreciates Polyvin's generosity and charity. Tiovin's clerics often work with Polyvin's Fortunates to help the poor. Rivoleen, the gnomish goddess of freedom and innovation, shares Polyvin's opposition to tyranny and exploitation. They cooperate frequently. Paladine, the god of justice, respects Polyvin's fairness and his opposition to corruption. Paladins often defend Fortune Halls from Xannith's saboteurs. Minerva, the elven goddess of knowledge, values Polyvin's emphasis on opportunity through learning. Elven scholars often study in Fortune Halls.

Polyvin's primary rival is Xannith. They represent opposite philosophies of commerce and wealth. The rivalry is intense and ongoing. Taovan is another rival—Polyvin's optimism (everyone deserves a chance) directly contradicts Taovan's cynicism (everyone can be corrupted). They clash ideologically.

Polyvin has neutral or indifferent relationships with several entities. Garnak, the hunt-god, and Polyvin have little interaction. Garnak values earned strength; Polyvin values luck. They neither cooperate nor oppose each other. Mau, the Oracle, observes Polyvin's games of chance with interest but does not interfere. Most Old Gods are indifferent to Polyvin—he is a young deity focused on mortal concerns, not cosmic forces.
