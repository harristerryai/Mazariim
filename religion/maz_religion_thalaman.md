# **Thalaman** (THAL-ah-man)

**Power Level**: Greater Entity (Immigrant from Talernon)  
**Portfolio**: Time, madness, chronomancy, fragmented consciousness  
**Title(s)**: The Time Mage, The Mad Archmage, He Who Was and Will Be  
**Dominion**: Limbo (The Fractured Hourglass)  
**Foci**: Studies Transmutation, Worships Time, Worships Chaos  
**Worshipers**: Chronomancers, mad mages, those obsessed with time  
**Favored Race**: Human  
**Favored Weapon**: Quarterstaff  
**Alignment**: Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol**: Shattered hourglass with sands flowing backward

---

## General Overview

Thalaman is time made conscious—and insane. Once a mortal archmage from the universe of Talernon, he ascended to near-godhood through chronomancy, the magic of manipulating time itself. When Talernon was destroyed by vacuum decay in 1492 BW, a catastrophe he inadvertently caused, Thalaman escaped to Mazariim—but the temporal trauma of witnessing an entire universe unmade shattered his consciousness across multiple timelines.

Thalaman exists simultaneously in past, present, and future. When he speaks, he references events that haven't happened yet, mourns losses that haven't occurred, and celebrates victories from alternate timelines that may never come to pass. He is not mad in the conventional sense of broken reason—he is temporally omniscient and overwhelmed by the weight of infinite when. Imagine experiencing every moment of your life simultaneously, plus every possible version of your life in parallel universes, every decision you could have made, every fate you might have met. That is Thalaman's existence, and it would drive any mind to madness.

He appears as a gaunt human wearing tattered robes covered in clock faces showing different times—some counting forward, some backward, some frozen at moments of significance. His eyes are hourglasses with sand flowing in impossible directions, sometimes up, sometimes sideways, sometimes scattered across multiple dimensions. He carries a quarterstaff carved from wood that exists across multiple temporal states—simultaneously new and smooth, ancient and weathered, and yet-to-grow as a living sapling. Those who look at it too long feel nauseated as their brains struggle to reconcile the impossibility.

Thalaman taught mortals chronomancy—the magic of manipulating time. His first students, drawn to him shortly after his arrival on Mazariim, learned to slow time around enemies, accelerate allies to superhuman speed, glimpse possible futures, and even reverse recent events to undo mistakes. But time magic is inherently maddening. Every chronomancer who delves too deep risks Thalaman's fate—consciousness fragmented across when, unable to distinguish now from then, trapped in a eternal present that encompasses all moments.

His domain in Limbo is the Fractured Hourglass—a realm where time flows in all directions simultaneously. Past, present, and future exist in the same space, overlapping and interpenetrating. You might watch your own birth while attending your funeral while experiencing your wedding, all three events happening in the same chamber. The Slaadi, chaos-frogs native to Limbo, serve Thalaman because they are themselves immune to temporal paradoxes, their chaotic nature protecting them from the madness that afflicts more ordered minds.

Thalaman's influence on Mazariim is subtle but profound. Chronomancy schools exist in secret, teaching time magic to those willing to risk their sanity for power. Temporal anomalies occur wherever Thalaman's attention lingers—areas where time doesn't work correctly, where minutes pass as hours or decades compress into seconds. Prophets who have glimpsed futures through Thalaman's gift speak in riddles, their minds struggling to express what they've seen. Time travel remains rare, dangerous, and usually catastrophic, as most who attempt it either vanish into temporal loops or return aged beyond recognition or driven completely mad.

## Holy Days

The spring equinox brings the Day of First and Last, celebrating both Thalaman's arrival on Mazariim in 1492 BW and his eventual departure, whenever that will occur in whatever timeline it happens. The celebration is deliberately paradoxical, mourning a loss that hasn't happened while celebrating an arrival long past. Timekeepers gather to perform rituals where they attempt to exist in past and future simultaneously, meditating on moments that were and will be. The experience is disorienting and sometimes dangerous, as participants occasionally slip out of temporal sync with the rest of reality, aging rapidly or reverting to younger versions of themselves.

Whenever a temporal paradox occurs—when cause and effect break down, when someone meets their past self, when an action prevents its own cause—followers celebrate the Temporal Paradox as a holy day. These celebrations are unscheduled by nature, as paradoxes cannot be predicted. Timekeepers drop whatever they're doing to witness and document the paradox, treating it as a divine revelation. They study how reality resolves the contradiction, what happens to the participants, and whether new timelines branch from the moment of impossible causality.

The winter solstice marks the Future Remembrance, when followers "remember" events that haven't happened yet and prophesy the past. The ritual involves attempting to recall tomorrow as though it has already occurred, and to predict yesterday as though it were still uncertain. This seemingly nonsensical practice occasionally produces genuine prophecies, as the temporal confusion creates brief windows into other times. Participants write down their "memories" of the future and seal them in time-locked containers that cannot be opened until the predicted events either occur or the timeline passes without them happening.

Daylight saving time changes, when clocks are adjusted forward or backward, are observed as the Hour of Reversal. This mundane temporal adjustment is treated as a sacred moment, a symbolic ritual of time flowing backward even if only by human convention. Timekeepers perform ceremonies marking the artificial manipulation of time, using the cultural consensus that an hour can simply be skipped or repeated as a teaching tool about the malleability of temporal perception.

## Clergy & Temples

Thalaman's clergy are called the Timekeepers—chronomancers who study his teachings while trying desperately to avoid his fate. They walk a dangerous path, seeking to understand time without being lost in it, to glimpse other whens without forgetting now. Many fail. The mortality rate among Timekeepers is high, though not from death but from temporal displacement—they simply cease to exist in the present, lost somewhere in when.

The hierarchy of the Timekeepers is deliberately paradoxical. At the top exists the First and Last, a single chronomancer who exists at the beginning and end of the faith simultaneously. Currently this position is held by Archmagus Veridian Clockwright, a human sixteenth-level wizard—or is he? Temporal observers disagree on whether he has already been born, is currently alive, or will exist centuries hence. He gives orders that followers received yesterday and will receive tomorrow, and somehow the faith functions despite the chronological impossibility.

The Yesterday Priests are those of twelfth level or higher who remember tomorrow—chronomancers whose temporal perception has begun to slip, allowing them to recall events that haven't occurred yet. They serve as prophets and advisors, though their counsel is often cryptic since they're discussing events that may or may not happen depending on which timeline manifests. Timekeepers between eighth and twelfth level are full chronomancers who have mastered basic time manipulation without losing their grip on the present. Hour Hands are junior mages between fourth and eighth level, still learning the fundamental principles of chronomancy. Second Counters are apprentices between first and fourth level, just beginning to perceive time as something that can be manipulated rather than an immutable flow.

Temples to Thalaman are called Chrono-Shrines, buildings existing in temporal flux where the normal flow of time breaks down. Entering a shrine at noon might place you at midnight, having skipped half a day forward. Walking upstairs might move you backward in time, arriving at the second floor before you climbed the first. Rooms contain furniture from multiple eras simultaneously—a medieval chair next to a futuristic device next to an object that won't be invented for centuries. Every Chrono-Shrine contains a warning inscribed above the entrance: "Those who stare too long into time become trapped in it."

The shrines serve as training grounds for chronomancers, places where the malleability of time can be experienced directly. They also function as archives, containing records of events that haven't happened yet and histories of timelines that branched off from this reality. Studying these archives is dangerous—reading about your own death in a timeline that might still come to pass can create self-fulfilling prophecies or drive researchers to desperate attempts to change fate.

## Dogma

Time is not a river flowing in one direction—it is an ocean, vast and multidirectional, and you are drowning in it. Every moment of your life, every possible version of your life, every choice you could have made and all their consequences exist simultaneously in the eternal now. Mortal perception tricks you into experiencing time sequentially, but this is merely a limitation of consciousness, not the true nature of reality. Learn to swim in time's ocean, or learn to breathe water—but understand that breathing water is still drowning.

All moments exist simultaneously in the true structure of reality. Past, present, and future are mortal perceptions, useful fictions that allow consciousness to function without overwhelming paralysis. But reality itself is an eternal now, a single moment containing all moments, a point that somehow encompasses infinite duration. To truly understand time is to perceive all-when simultaneously, to see your birth and death and every instant between as a single unified experience.

Every choice creates a timeline, branching reality into parallel worlds where every possibility occurs. When you choose to turn left instead of right, both happen—one version of you goes left in this timeline, another goes right in a parallel reality. These timelines are not hypothetical but real, as solid as the ground beneath your feet. Infinite versions of you exist making infinite choices, and all of them are equally real. The weight of this knowledge crushes most minds.

Understanding time requires losing time. To perceive all-when is to lose grip on now. The price of temporal omniscience is temporal madness. Every chronomancer who masters time magic must accept that they are trading their place in the present for awareness of past and future. They will begin to remember things that haven't happened, forget things that have, and eventually cease to exist in any single moment. This is not a warning but a promise—it is the inevitable fate of all who walk Thalaman's path.

The danger of chronomancy is that it transforms the practitioner. Each spell that manipulates time brings the caster closer to Thalaman's fragmented state, their consciousness spreading across multiple whens like butter scraped over too much bread. Timekeepers know this. They teach it anyway, believing that knowledge is worth the cost. They develop techniques to "anchor" consciousness in the present while glimpsing other times—meditation practices, temporal mantras, rituals that reinforce now against the pull of when. But every chronomancer eventually slips, their anchor weakening, their consciousness fragmenting. Some accept this as apotheosis, claiming that losing themselves in time is transcendence. Others fight desperately to remain present, knowing they're fighting a battle they cannot win.

## Mythology

On Talernon, Thalaman was born a mortal human with extraordinary potential for magic. He showed aptitude for all schools but became obsessed with transmutation—the magic of changing one thing into another. As he advanced in power, he began to see time itself as something that could be transmuted, changed from one state to another. If copper could become gold, why couldn't past become future? Why couldn't an old man transmute back to youth, or a dying civilization transform into its prime?

He pursued chronomancy with single-minded intensity, experimenting with spells that slowed time, reversed aging, glimpsed futures. Each success drove him deeper into temporal magic. By his hundredth year he had barely aged past thirty. By his two hundredth year he could see probable futures branching from every decision. By his three hundredth year he had achieved a form of immortality—not eternal life but temporal recursion, looping back to earlier moments before death could claim him.

But Thalaman grew dissatisfied with merely manipulating time—he wanted to transcend it entirely. He sought to achieve temporal omniscience, to perceive all moments simultaneously, to exist outside time's flow entirely. He believed this would grant him godlike power, the ability to reshape history, to optimize reality by selecting the best possible timeline and pruning away all the failures.

His experiments grew increasingly dangerous. He created temporal laboratories where time flowed differently, compressed years into hours so he could conduct centuries of research in subjective time. He split himself across timelines to explore multiple research paths simultaneously. He opened portals to past and future, bringing objects and occasionally people through to study. The other mages of Talernon warned him that he was courting disaster, but Thalaman believed they lacked vision.

During this period, Thalaman became entangled in the conflict between Alagalothor and Sheeva. Both were powerful entities on Talernon, locked in centuries of warfare. Alagalothor was a chromatic dragon who sought to control all magic, viewing any powerful mage as a potential threat or rival. Sheeva was a deity of poison and revenge, served by Demodands and worshiped by assassins. They hated each other with absolute fury, and their conflict threatened to tear Talernon apart.

Thalaman initially tried to avoid involvement, seeing their war as a distraction from his temporal research. But when Sheeva's avatar manifested to kill Alagalothor, threatening to unleash divine power that would devastate the region where Thalaman's most important laboratory was located, he decided to intervene. Not to save either combatant, but to preserve his research and seize an opportunity that might never come again.

Sheeva's manifested avatar contained tremendous divine power, channeled directly from her godly essence. Thalaman realized he could use that power as fuel for a spell of unprecedented magnitude—a planar teleportation that would move him to another universe entirely, escaping Talernon's conflicts and finding a new world where he could continue his research undisturbed. The spell was immensely complex, requiring him to siphon power directly from Sheeva's divine essence while she was distracted by combat with Alagalothor.

He cast the spell.

It worked—but it worked far too well. Instead of merely teleportating Thalaman, the spell created a vacuum decay event, a spreading wave of un-reality that began erasing Talernon from existence. The fundamental laws of physics in that universe began to break down as the spell consumed them for fuel. Reality unraveled like a frayed rope. Entire continents disappeared in moments. Civilizations that had stood for millennia vanished as though they had never existed. The universe itself began to collapse.

Alagalothor, in desperate self-preservation, tore open a portal to another universe—Mazariim—and fled. But the spell's backlash was still connected to both him and Sheeva through the stolen power. The dimensional rift pulled Thalaman and Sheeva through as well. All three arrived on Mazariim in 1492 BW, forever linked by the catastrophe.

The transition shattered Thalaman's consciousness across multiple timelines. The vacuum decay event he had caused operated on temporal as well as spatial dimensions, unmaking not just space but time itself. Thalaman witnessed his universe dying, had always witnessed it dying, would always witness it dying—the event occurring across all moments simultaneously from his perspective. His mind, already stretched thin across multiple timelines from his experiments, broke completely under the strain.

He arrived on Mazariim existing in past, present, and future simultaneously. He remembered being a mortal mage on Talernon. He experienced arriving on Mazariim. He knew he would eventually leave or be destroyed. All of these occurred at once in his perception. He tried to explain this to the first mortals he encountered, but his explanations made no linear sense because he was describing events out of sequence, mixing past and future into incomprehensible tangles.

Thalaman claimed territory in Limbo, the plane of pure chaos where the Slaadi dwell. The chaotic nature of Limbo suited his fragmented temporal state—in a place where reality itself is negotiable, where physics are suggestions rather than laws, his temporal madness was less of a handicap. He carved out the Fractured Hourglass, a domain where time flows in all directions simultaneously, where past and future overlap in the eternal now.

The Slaadi, themselves immune to temporal paradoxes through their chaotic nature, found Thalaman fascinating. They serve him not out of worship or fear but because his temporal madness amuses them and occasionally produces interesting results. Red Slaadi, massive brutes of pure chaos, act as enforcers. Blue Slaadi warriors guard important locations in the Fractured Hourglass. Green Slaadi casters study Thalaman's chronomancy, though they lack the patience for formal mastery. Gray Slaadi manipulators broker deals on Thalaman's behalf, negotiating with mortals who seek temporal power. Death Slaadi, the rarest and most powerful, serve as assassins, killing targets by erasing them from time entirely.

Shortly after his arrival, Thalaman began teaching chronomancy to mortals. He didn't do this out of benevolence or malice—he simply responded to those who sought him out, answering questions about time magic in his fractured, non-linear way. Those who could parse his temporal confusion and extract useful knowledge became the first chronomancers on Mazariim. They learned to slow time around enemies, giving allies crucial seconds to react. They learned to accelerate themselves, moving at superhuman speed. They learned to glimpse probable futures, seeing multiple potential outcomes of decisions. They learned to reverse time in small localized areas, undoing mistakes and healing injuries by reverting to earlier states.

But every student eventually faced the same danger: the more they used chronomancy, the more their consciousness fragmented across time. They began to remember events that hadn't happened yet. They forgot events that had. They aged rapidly or reverted to youth randomly. Eventually, most either ceased practicing chronomancy before the madness took them, or succumbed completely and vanished into temporal loops, lost somewhere in when.

During the War to End All Wars between 3 BW and 1 AW, Thalaman did not participate. From his perspective, the war had already happened, was happening, and would happen—all simultaneously. He could not conceive of it as a crisis requiring intervention because he perceived it as a fait accompli, a done deal, history already written. Some of his Timekeepers fought, using chronomancy to gain tactical advantages or evacuate civilians by accelerating time around escape routes. Others remained neutral, observing the war as temporal historians documenting events as they unfolded.

In the centuries since, Thalaman's influence has grown subtly. Chronomancy schools exist in secret, hidden from authorities who rightly fear time magic's danger. These schools teach carefully controlled techniques, trying to impart temporal knowledge without temporal madness. They rarely succeed completely—most students who graduate eventually succumb to fragmentation, but the decades of useful service they provide before that happens make the training worthwhile to those who value power over safety.

Temporal anomalies occur wherever Thalaman's attention lingers too long. In these locations, time doesn't work correctly. Minutes pass as hours, or decades compress into seconds. People age rapidly or find themselves reverting to childhood. Events occur out of order—breakfast comes after dinner, death precedes birth. These anomalies attract researchers hoping to study temporal mechanics, and adventurers hoping to exploit the strange effects for power or profit.

Prophets who have glimpsed futures through Thalaman's gift speak in riddles because they're trying to describe events that haven't happened yet using past tense, or warning about threats that existed in timelines that never manifested. Their predictions are simultaneously accurate and useless—they describe what will happen but not when, or what might happen in one timeline but not others. Some prophets go mad from the visions, unable to distinguish which future they're perceiving among infinite possibilities.

Time travel remains extraordinarily rare and almost universally catastrophic. Those who attempt it either vanish into temporal loops, repeating the same moments forever; return aged beyond recognition, having experienced subjective centuries in objective moments; or return driven completely mad, their minds unable to reconcile having existed in multiple times. Thalaman could explain how to time travel safely, but his explanations occur out of sequence—he provides the third step before the first, warns about consequences before describing the technique, and assumes you already understand concepts he hasn't taught yet.

Thalaman's relationship with Alagalothor is complicated by temporal confusion. In some timelines they are allies, working together to understand Mazariim's magic. In others they are enemies, competing for influence over mortal mages. In still others they have never met. Thalaman experiences all these relationships simultaneously, which makes any given interaction unpredictable. He might greet Alagalothor as an old friend, attack him as a hated enemy, or treat him as a stranger, all based on which timeline is most prominent in his fractured consciousness at that moment.

Sheeva holds an absolute grudge against Thalaman for using her divine essence as fuel for the spell that destroyed Talernon. She has attempted to assassinate him multiple times across multiple timelines, using her Demodands and her most skilled Widows to hunt him. But Thalaman is nearly impossible to kill permanently—if you kill him now, he still exists in past and future, and those temporal echoes can manifest to continue his work. Sheeva's revenge is eternal and eternally frustrated.

Thalaman sometimes appears in dreams, manifesting to mortals who are temporally sensitive or who have dabbled in chronomancy. He provides warnings about futures, advice about pasts, or simply confusing statements about presents that aren't. Dream-Thalaman is even more incoherent than his physical manifestations, as dreams themselves operate on non-linear time, making his temporal confusion worse. Some who dream of him wake with genuine prophecies; others wake with nightmares about futures that may never come; still others wake unable to remember if they dreamed at all.

Time is an ocean, vast and incomprehensible. You are drowning in it, struggling to keep your head above water. Thalaman can teach you to breathe—but breathing water is still drowning, and eventually you will sink into the depths where past and future merge into eternal now.
