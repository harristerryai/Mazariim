# **VULEALA** (voo-LAY-lah)
## Mother of Rain • The Sky Mistress • The Capricious Wind

**Portfolio**: Air, skies, weather  
**Power Level**: Greater entity (Old God)  
**Title(s)**: Mother of Rain (to Elves), Sky Mistress, The Four Winds  
**Dominion**: Plane of Air  
**Foci**: Worships Air (Elementals), Worships Tempest, Worships Nature  
**Worshipers**: Sailors, sky riders, weather mages, farmers  
**Favored Race**: None  
**Favored Weapon**: Javelin  
**Alignment**: N  
**Symbol**: Cloud pierced by lightning

-----

General Overview

Vuleala is the wind—invisible, everywhere, ever-changing. She coalesced from the atmospheric storms of a distant gas giant, eternally in flux, never the same from one moment to the next. Vuleala is gentle breeze and raging hurricane, clear skies and deadly storms, life-giving rain and destructive lightning. She is capricious as weather itself.

Unlike her brother-elements (Pyrantor's mindless burning, Iquinias's cold depths, Dirgin's patient stone), Vuleala possesses something approaching mood. Not consciousness—she does not think—but temperament. The wind changes direction without warning. Calm mornings become violent afternoons. Promised rain never arrives, or arrives as devastating flood. Vuleala is unpredictable, and that unpredictability feels almost intentional.

Mortals who depend on weather—sailors, farmers, nomads—offer prayers and sacrifices to Vuleala. They do not expect her to listen, but they hope favorable winds might find them by chance. More often than not, they are disappointed. The wind goes where it wills.

Yet Vuleala is not entirely indifferent. On rare, perfectly still days when no wind blows, her clergy claim she is "listening." On these sacred occasions, prayers seem more likely to be answered, weather predictions prove accurate, and impossible wishes sometimes come true. But such days are rare—perhaps twice a year, perhaps less. Most of the time, Vuleala howls across the world, heedless and free.

Elves call her Mother of Rain and credit her with bringing life-giving precipitation to forests. Sailors curse her name during storms and praise it when favorable winds fill their sails. Farmers watch the skies and make offerings, hoping rain will come—not too much, not too little—but Vuleala rarely grants such balance.

Holy Days

The spring equinox marks The First Wind, celebrating the return of warm winds and the planting season. Communities fly kites, release birds, and scatter flower petals to the wind. Children's laughter on this day is said to please Vuleala, and families gather on hilltops to feel the season's first gentle breezes after winter's harsh gales.

The Calm occurs on rare windless days, unpredictable and sacred. When the wind completely stops, everything halts. These sacred days occur perhaps once or twice a year. Skysingers declare them immediately, and communities rush to offer prayers during the stillness. No work is done, no journeys undertaken—only prayer, meditation, and waiting for the wind to return. During these moments, reality seems to hold its breath, and prayers offered have an uncanny tendency to be answered.

Stormwatch falls during hurricane and tornado season, varying by region. During dangerous weather seasons, communities hold vigils asking Vuleala to spare them or at least send warning before striking. Skysingers stay awake for days, watching clouds and listening to wind direction, maintaining constant prayer and burning incense to honor the Mother of Rain.

The autumn equinox brings The Last Breeze, as cold winds return and communities celebrate the summer's end while preparing for winter. Offerings of grain, herbs, and flowers are scattered to the wind, thanking Vuleala for rain and asking for gentle winter winds. This festival includes feasting and storytelling about the year's storms survived and blessings received.

Clergy & Temples

Vuleala's clergy are called Skysingers (or Windwhisperers among nomadic cultures). They wear flowing robes of white and gray that billow dramatically in breezes, often adorned with feathers, wind chimes, and ribbons that dance in the slightest air current. The Skysingers serve multiple roles, attempting to predict storms, droughts, and winds by reading clouds and atmospheric signs as weather prophets. Some claim the ability to summon or disperse storms as storm callers, though whether this is Vuleala's power or coincidence is debated among scholars and skeptics alike. They perform ritual dances to encourage favorable winds for sailing, planting, or cooling as wind dancers, their movements mimicking the flow of air currents. During droughts, communities hire Skysingers to perform multi-day ceremonies pleading for rain as rain bringers, though success rates are inconsistent at best.

Most Skysingers are druids, sorcerers with air bloodlines, or clerics who channel Vuleala's elemental might. They rarely stay in one place long—like the wind itself, they wander from settlement to settlement, following seasonal weather patterns and offering their services where needed. Senior Skysingers are those who have survived lightning strikes, tornadoes, or other violent weather events, bearing the scars as badges of honor and proof of Vuleala's attention.

True temples of Vuleala cannot exist, for she cannot be contained. Instead, her worshipers maintain shrines on hilltops, mountaintops, and open plains—anywhere the wind blows freely. These consist of standing stones, wind chimes, prayer flags, and offering platforms exposed to the elements year-round. The most elaborate shrines feature hundreds of colorful flags inscribed with prayers, which flap and tear in the wind. As flags disintegrate, prayers are released into the air, carried on Vuleala's breath to wherever she roams. Worshipers continuously add new flags, creating ever-changing tapestries of color and sound.

Some coastal and island cultures build wind towers—tall stone structures with openings designed to create specific sounds as air flows through them. The music these towers make is considered Vuleala's voice, and Skysingers interpret the notes as prophecy. Certain combinations of tones are said to predict storms, droughts, or favorable sailing conditions, though the accuracy of these interpretations varies widely.

Dogma

Skysingers teach principles drawn from observing the wind, understanding that adaptation and acceptance are the keys to surviving in a world where weather changes without warning. Do not resist change. Do not cling to what was. The wind does not fight obstacles—it flows around them, over them, through them. Followers are urged to be adaptable, flowing like air through life's challenges rather than breaking against them like rigid structures in a storm.

You cannot control everything. You cannot predict everything. Sometimes the wind favors you, sometimes it opposes you, sometimes it ignores you entirely. Skysingers teach acceptance of this fundamental truth, reminding the faithful that struggling against forces beyond mortal control only wastes strength. Better to adjust one's sails than curse the wind's direction.

Air is the first thing you breathe at birth and the last thing you release at death. Between those breaths is your life. Never take breathing for granted. Honor the air that sustains you, for every breath is a gift from Vuleala, unearned and freely given. The faithful practice breathing meditations, consciously acknowledging each inhalation and exhalation as a sacred act.

Storms do not last forever. Droughts eventually end. The darkest clouds give way to clear skies. Whatever troubles you now will pass like wind through trees—loudly, violently perhaps, but inevitably gone. This teaching offers comfort during hardship, reminding believers that no condition, good or ill, lasts permanently. Change is the only constant, and suffering will blow away as surely as last season's leaves.

Climb mountains, scale towers, rise above the mundane. From high places, you see further, breathe clearer air, and feel the wind's true power. Perspective changes everything. Skysingers encourage pilgrimages to high elevations, where the wind blows strongest and the concerns of the lowlands seem small and distant. Physical height mirrors spiritual elevation, and those who seek wisdom should seek altitude.

Mythology

On a gas giant so massive that Mazariim could fit in its Red Spot, eternal storms raged for millions of years. Winds reached thousands of miles per hour. Lightning the size of continents arced between cloud layers. In this endless atmospheric chaos, Vuleala was born around twenty-five thousand years before the War to End All Wars, not from a single storm but from the concept of wind itself. She coalesced slowly, gathering power from every gust and gale until achieving a form of existence. When solar radiation eventually stripped the gas giant's atmosphere, Vuleala's essence scattered across the cosmos like dandelion seeds on wind, eventually finding Mazariim. Here, she claimed the Plane of Air, an infinite sky where winds blow in impossible directions and clouds take solid form.

When the first living things emerged from Mazariim's oceans onto land around fifty thousand years before the War, they took their first breath of air. In that moment, Vuleala became essential to mortal life. Every breath drawn by every living creature since has been a tiny acknowledgment of her existence. Primitive mortals did not immediately worship wind—it seemed too intangible, too everywhere and nowhere to honor properly. But when hurricanes destroyed villages, when droughts killed crops, when favorable winds allowed fishing expeditions to return safely—then they understood. The wind was a power that could not be ignored.

During the period from five thousand to twenty-five hundred years before the War, Vuleala was widely worshiped by nomadic peoples, sailors, and farming communities. Unlike Pyrantor (who demanded burning offerings) or Iquinias (who claimed drowning victims), Vuleala's worship was gentle: prayer flags, wind chimes, songs sung into the breeze. She seemed less dangerous than the other elementals. This was a misconception. Vuleala's storms killed thousands. Hurricanes leveled cities. Tornadoes tore apart settlements. Droughts starved populations when rain refused to fall. Yet because these disasters felt random rather than malicious, people continued to see Vuleala as capricious rather than cruel. They hoped to earn her favor through devotion.

The Elven people particularly embraced Vuleala worship, calling her Mother of Rain. Forest druids performed rain dances, planted sacred groves to encourage precipitation, and studied wind patterns to predict weather. To this day, many elven druids honor Vuleala alongside Minerva, and some claim that elven forests receive more favorable weather than other lands—whether by Vuleala's rare attention or simple geographic coincidence remains debated.

As patron deities emerged around twenty-five hundred years before the War, elemental worship declined. But like Iquinias, Vuleala remained relevant because her domain remained essential. Farmers still need rain. Sailors still need wind. Weather still determines survival. Modern Skysingers maintain their traditions, wandering from community to community offering weather predictions and storm rituals. Their accuracy varies wildly—sometimes they seem genuinely prophetic, other times completely wrong. Is this Vuleala granting visions, or simply the Skysingers' skill at reading atmospheric patterns? No one knows with certainty.

Vuleala has no formal allies among the entities, but wind touches everything and connects all places in ways that transcend friendship or enmity. Iquinias and Vuleala work together to create rain, storms, and ocean winds as water and air intermingle. Pyrantor and Vuleala feed each other—wind fans flames, flames create wind through convection in an endless cycle. Dirgin is shaped by Vuleala's erosion over eons, mountains worn down grain by grain. These are not friendships but natural processes, elemental interactions that predate consciousness or choice.

Vuleala has no enemies in the traditional sense, though those who seek to control weather through magic rather than prayer sometimes find their spells disrupted by unexpected winds. Vuleala does not appreciate mortals trying to command her domain, and arrogant weather mages have been known to suffer sudden squalls or inexplicable calm at the worst possible moments. Minerva maintains a respectful relationship with Vuleala through her elven followers, who honor Mother of Rain in their forest rituals and receive what seems to be favorable treatment in return.

What remains certain is that on the rare days when the wind completely stops, when perfect calm descends—those days feel sacred. Reality seems to hold its breath. And prayers offered during such stillness have an uncanny tendency to be answered. The wind whispers secrets, carries prayers, and clears away the old, as the Skysinger proverb teaches. Listen when it speaks, dance when it laughs, and shelter when it rages.
