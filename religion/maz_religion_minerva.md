# Minerva

- Power Level: Greater entity
- Portfolio: Elves, nature, magic
- Title(s): Kesir'Atar
- Dominion: Elysium
- Foci: Worships Nature, Worships Life, Worships Arcana
- Worshipers: Elves, druids, rangers, healers
- Favored Race: Elves
- Favored Weapon: Short sword
- Alignment: CG
- Symbol: Elf shadow looking down in humility

---

## General Overview

Minerva (mih-ner-vha) is the patron deity of the elves of Mazariim. It embodies the highest ideals of elvenkind and throughout history has always tried to guide and protect its chosen people. Unlike many entities that remain distant from their followers, Minerva has walked among the elves, spoken directly to their leaders, and even divided portions of its essence to reside within the seven leaders of the elven nations.

Minerva's church emphasizes the protection and advancement of the elven nations as well as the protection of the world and nature itself. Spiritual advisers help guide the leaders of the elven lands, from the village elder to the seven Artharans (High Leaders). This guidance is not tyrannical but collaborative—Minerva offers wisdom while respecting the autonomy of elvenkind to make their own choices.

Minerva appears as an androgynous elf, slightly taller than a normal elf, with soft features and a faint golden glow to its skin and long blonde hair. It is always dressed in simple green and brown robes, emphasizing humility despite its divine power. Over the centuries Minerva has interacted with its people more than almost any other entity. Part of its current avatar exists in the leaders of each of the elven nations, granting them enhanced magical abilities and connecting them directly to Minerva's wisdom.

Known to be a humble deity, Minerva is viewed quite favorably by almost all elves, and in fact by most other sentients of other races. Dwarves usually respect Minerva but think it holds the hands of the elves too much in life, believing that the elves are coddled rather than challenged to grow through adversity. Humans who are one with nature, especially rangers and druids, often hold Minerva in high regard or call it their patron deity. Many halflings worship Minerva alongside Hinterhin and show nothing but respect for the elven religion and ways. Although gnomes do not generally dislike Minerva, they do not understand many of its ways or views, finding elven spirituality too focused on tradition and nature rather than innovation and progress.

Followers of Minerva pray at various times of day, with sunrise and sunset most popular, giving thanks for the day to come or for the day just past. These prayers are not formal rituals but personal moments of reflection and gratitude, often conducted outdoors where the elf can feel the touch of wind and sun.

## Holy Days

Elves, even those who do not give Minerva much thought, celebrate four traditional holidays which occur on the solstices and equinoxes. **The Spring Awakening** marks the spring equinox when nature returns to life after winter's dormancy. Elves celebrate by planting new groves, performing rituals to bless the growing season, and gathering in sacred forests to sing songs of renewal.

**The Summer's Height** falls on the summer solstice, the longest day of the year. This celebration honors the peak of life and growth, with festivals featuring archery competitions, displays of magic, and feasts of fresh fruits and vegetables. Druids perform rituals to ensure the continued health of the land.

**The Autumn Balance** occurs on the autumn equinox, acknowledging the turning of the seasons toward darkness and rest. Elves give thanks for the harvest and prepare for the coming winter through ceremonies that honor both life and death, growth and decay, as natural parts of the eternal cycle.

**Enti'nim** (Longest Night of Cold) marks the winter solstice and new year, the longest night when darkness holds greatest sway. This is the most important holiday in the Minervan calendar, a time of deep reflection and communal gathering. Elves spend the night in meditation, storytelling, and prayer, waiting for the return of the sun. The celebration continues for two more days until **Apaire'aur** (Morning of Victory) on the third day of the New Year, which celebrates the triumph of light over darkness and the promise of spring to come. Feasts, dances, and gift-giving mark this joyous conclusion to the winter solstice observance.

Scattered throughout the year are other more minor or regional holidays, whose predominance depends on nationality and reverence of traditional elven ways. Some elven communities celebrate the blooming of specific sacred trees, the return of migratory birds, or anniversaries of historical events important to their particular region.

## Clergy & Temples

The church of Minerva is made of two parts—the Cundo'elda (Guardians of the Elves) and the Cundo'dori (Guardians of the Land), respectively split between the clerical and druidic clergy. Both groups work together in helping govern and guide the elven lands, with neither claiming superiority over the other despite their different approaches to serving Minerva.

The Cundo'elda consists of the clerics, oracles, and inquisitors of the church and is generally hierarchy-based depending on both caster level and age, with some elder clerics reigning higher than younger clerics that have more powerful magical abilities. Humility and respect for other church members is an additional major theme that the Cundo'elda practice, with very few major power struggles in the history of the elven church. When disputes arise, they are settled through meditation, discussion, and appeals to Minerva's guidance rather than through political maneuvering or combat.

The Cundo'dori, on the other hand, consists of the druids and rangers of Minerva and is a very structured faith where position relies solely on caster level, reflecting the amount of reverence and favor with Minerva to the druids. Although druids might have conflicts regarding the leadership of druid circles, leadership is always passed to the most powerful spellcaster via tests of magic in front of the entire druid circle. These tests are not combative but demonstrate mastery of nature magic, understanding of ecological balance, and wisdom in applying druidic power. At times, elven druids develop conflicts with other races in their lands when they find especially egregious abuses of nature and the land.

Clerics, druids, rangers, inquisitors, and oracles all fill the ranks of the Minervan clergy, as well as holy vindicators, loremasters, envoys of balance, and at the top of the clergy, hierophants. Interestingly, there are very few paladins of Minerva—the elven approach to good tends toward protecting nature and preserving balance rather than the rigid codes of honor and justice that define most paladins.

Temples of Minerva vary greatly around the world. In the elven lands of the Ya'sherim, towering cathedrals made of marble are the centerpiece of the capital as well as other major cities, with soaring architecture that incorporates living trees and flowing water into the sacred spaces. The elves of the plains region have offerings and prayer times in a large, mobile teepee that travels with their nomadic communities. Druids almost always worship in a place of natural beauty and very often have permanent menhir stones set up with prayers and protections upon them. These sacred groves are considered as holy as any cathedral, and indeed some elves prefer the natural temples to the constructed ones.

The traditional garb of Minervan clergy is simple robes in varying shades of browns (clerics) and greens (druids). For holidays and special ceremonies, there are more elaborate or revealing outfits, usually in the same colors but more form-fitting with sections left bare, especially for female elves. These ceremonial garments celebrate the natural beauty of the elven form and the connection between elf and nature. Interestingly, some of the more reverent druids tend to come to ceremonies adorned in a few simple pieces of jewelry or adornments, almost completely naked, viewing clothing as an unnecessary barrier between themselves and the natural world.

All races of sentients, save goblinkind, are welcome in the clergy of Minerva, with a large number of halflings and humans, especially druids, worshiping the elven deity. This openness reflects Minerva's belief that respect for nature and dedication to balance transcend racial boundaries, though elves naturally make up the majority of the faithful.

## Dogma

The core teaching of Minerva calls followers to be true to the world and nature and to their elven brothers and sisters. This loyalty extends not just to individual elves but to the ideals of elvenkind—wisdom, beauty, harmony with the natural world, and the preservation of ancient knowledge and traditions.

Followers are instructed to shun the possession of land and material things, as they tie down immortal souls and bind the will. Elves are meant to be free, to wander, to experience the world without the burden of ownership. While practical necessities are acceptable, the accumulation of wealth and property for its own sake is seen as a corruption that leads elves away from their true nature.

The faithful must ward against those who would destroy the lands and the elves and those who create war and chaos. This is not a call to passive defense but to active protection—druids who prevent deforestation, clerics who defend elven communities from invaders, rangers who patrol the borders of sacred groves. Violence is acceptable when necessary to protect, but it should never be the first choice.

The doctrine calls followers to commune with the natural world and each other, learning from experiences and letting wisdom guide their path. Knowledge gained from books is valuable, but wisdom comes from direct experience—walking in forests, observing the cycles of nature, listening to the stories of elders, and learning from both success and failure.

Finally, followers are instructed to be ever-vigilant against the scourge of the world: orcs and goblinkind. This teaching is the most controversial aspect of Minerva's dogma, reflecting deep historical wounds from conflicts with these races. While some modern followers interpret this as vigilance against any who would threaten elven communities, traditionalists maintain that orcs and goblins are fundamentally opposed to elvenkind and can never be trusted.

## Mythology

In 3402 BW the entity known as Minerva would make first contact with the elves. In an event that would become known as the Enlightening, Minerva gathered the elders and shamans of the major elven nations, whisking them away for a mere blink of an eye in Mazariim's time, but what was a month in time elapsing on the plane of Elysium. It was here that Minerva gave them all a small portion of its knowledge as well as giving them the wisdom of many lifetimes, each sharing much of their life experiences with each other.

During the Enlightening, the elven leaders experienced direct communion with Minerva's consciousness. They saw visions of the past—the ancient history of their people before recorded time—and glimpses of possible futures. They learned advanced techniques of magic, agriculture, architecture, and governance. Most importantly, they gained an understanding of the interconnectedness of all living things and the role elves were meant to play as stewards and protectors of the natural world.

This event began the ascension of the elves in almost all aspects of their lives. Before the Enlightening, elves were simply one race among many, living in tribal communities with shamanistic traditions. After the Enlightening, they rapidly developed sophisticated civilizations, blending advanced magic with deep respect for nature. Their cities grew in harmony with the forests rather than replacing them. Their magic drew upon natural forces rather than dominating them.

Since that time, the church of Minerva has been the leader of the elven nations. While tribes have always been governed by a tribal elder or elder council, these leaders have always given their deference to the clerics and druids of Minerva. This is not a theocracy in the traditional sense—secular leaders maintain authority over day-to-day governance—but spiritual advisers wield enormous influence, and major decisions are made in consultation with the clergy.

Since the beginning of its interaction with the elves, Minerva has guided its people, and at times has even walked the elven lands in its avatar form. These visitations are rare and occur only during times of great crisis or profound importance. When Minerva manifests, it speaks to the assembled elven leaders, offering guidance and sometimes performing miracles—healing blighted lands, blessing sacred groves, or revealing hidden threats.

In the time of the War to End All Wars, Minerva faced a terrible dilemma. The entity did not want to leave its children undefended during the greatest conflict Mazariim had ever seen. Rather than send its avatar to the battlefield where it could be defeated and leave Minerva banished from the material plane for decades, Minerva chose a different path. It sent a portion of its avatar to each of the seven leaders of the elven nations, giving them fantastic powers of magic and leadership.

This unprecedented act divided Minerva's essence among seven living vessels, greatly empowering the Artharans while leaving Minerva itself temporarily weakened. The seven leaders gained abilities far beyond normal elves—enhanced spellcasting, supernatural wisdom, the ability to communicate with each other across vast distances, and direct access to Minerva's guidance. United through their shared connection to Minerva, they led the elven forces with unprecedented coordination and strategic brilliance.

To this day, this portion of Minerva resides in the current seven leaders of the elven nations, passing on to the next worthy leader automatically when a current leader dies. The transfer is instantaneous and unavoidable—the moment an Artharan dies, Minerva's essence within them seeks out the elf most worthy to lead their nation and enters them. This has occasionally resulted in unexpected leaders, as Minerva's judgment of worthiness does not always align with political expectations. Some Artharans have been young, or from unexpected backgrounds, or even initially reluctant to lead, but all have proven themselves wise and capable once touched by Minerva's essence.

This continuing presence of Minerva within the seven leaders ensures that the elven nations remain connected to their patron deity in a way that few other races experience with their gods. The Artharans are not merely chosen by Minerva—they carry part of Minerva within them, making them living bridges between the divine and mortal realms.
