# Zuel, The Devourer

**Portfolio:** Orcs, goblinkind, conquest, destruction  
**Power Level:** Greater Entity (Original Universe: Oerth)  
**Title(s):** The Devourer, The Bloody Hand, He Who Never Stops  
**Dominion:** Abaddon (Plane of Endless War)  
**Alignment:** Neutral Evil (NE)  
**Symbol:** Bloody handprint over crossed axes  
**Favored Weapon:** Greataxe  
**Worshipers:** Orcs, goblins, hobgoblins, ogres, warmongers, raiders  
**Favored Race:** Orc, Goblin  
**Foci:** Worships War, Worships Strength, Worships Destruction

-----

General Overview

Zuel (ZOO-el) is hunger given divine form—an entity of insatiable conquest who teaches that the strong survive by consuming the weak. Once known as Gruumsh on the world of Oerth, he was transformed into Zuel when ripped from his home universe during an apocalyptic battle with Moradin (who became Mordrion). The transition weakened him—he is no longer the immortal god he once was—but it has not diminished his fundamental nature: endless war, ceaseless expansion, the strong devouring the weak until only the strongest remains.

Where Mordrion uplifts dwarves through craftsmanship and community, Zuel drives orcs through conquest and cruelty. Where other evil deities scheme or corrupt, Zuel simply takes. Subtlety is for the weak. Diplomacy is cowardice. There is only the raid, the battle, the conquest. Today's enemy is tomorrow's corpse or slave—these are the only two fates that matter.

Zuel appears as a massive orc warrior, easily ten feet tall, his body scarred from countless battles. His most distinctive feature is his missing left hand—severed by Moradin's axe in the battle that brought him to Mazariim. The stump never healed, constantly weeping blood that never quite falls. Some whisper that Zuel's severed hand still exists somewhere, an artifact of terrible power that might restore him to his former godlike status. His single eye (the other socket empty, another gift from Moradin) burns with red fire, and when he speaks, it is the sound of clashing swords and dying screams.

Since his defeat at the end of the War to End All Wars (1 BW), Zuel has been rebuilding. His avatar was banished for 101 years, returning in 102 AW weakened but unbroken. For over seven centuries, he has been slowly gathering strength, whispering to orc chieftains, blessing raids, rewarding conquest. Every dwarven hold that remains in orcish hands. Every human village burned. Every skull added to an orcish war-shrine. These are prayers to Zuel, and he is listening.

Zuel teaches that might makes right—the strong rule because they can, with no other justification needed. Conquest never stops; today's victory is tomorrow's stagnation. Compassion is weakness; kill enemies or enslave them, for there is no third option. Peace is death; only in battle does one truly live. His faithful are primarily orcs, goblins, and hobgoblins—races that were primitive and disorganized until his arrival gave them purpose: destroy everything that is not us. But his worship has spread beyond greenskins to human raiders, mercenaries who fight for plunder rather than cause, and warmongers who revel in destruction for its own sake.

Holy Days

The Day of Arrival falls on 15 Minerva (March 15), commemorating Zuel's awakening in Mazariim in 1866 BW. Orcish tribes conduct massive raids on this date, considering it especially blessed. Dwarven and human settlements increase defenses annually, knowing that this day brings coordinated attacks across multiple regions. Blood Priests lead ceremonies before dawn, channeling Zuel's power into warriors who then spend the day proving their strength through combat and conquest.

The Blood Moon occurs during every full moon, when orcish war-bands conduct night raids and offer captives to Zuel. The larger the raid's success, the greater Zuel's favor. These monthly observances keep orcish raiders in constant practice, ensuring that martial skills never dull even during periods when major campaigns are not underway. Prisoners taken during Blood Moon raids are considered especially sacred offerings, often sacrificed in elaborate rituals designed to channel their life energy to Zuel.

Victory's Eve is celebrated on 2 Quarz (January 2), the night before the War to End All Wars officially ended—when it seemed Zuel would win. Orcs celebrate this as the day we almost had them, feasting on stores saved throughout winter and planning revenge for the defeat that came the following day. The celebration is bittersweet, commemorating both the heights of Zuel's power and the bitter taste of defeat, serving as motivation for the inevitable next war.

The Broken Hammer falls on 3 Quarz (January 3), the anniversary of Zuel's defeat. Rather than mourning, orcs conduct brutal internal combat tournaments—proving that despite defeat, they remain strong. Winners earn the right to lead the next year's raids, with the bloodiest and most skilled combatants receiving blessings directly from Blood Priests channeling Zuel's approval. This holiday transforms the memory of defeat into a celebration of enduring strength.

The Bleeding Feast occurs on 30 Mord (June 15), coinciding with the dwarven holiday of The Forging—a deliberate mockery. While dwarves celebrate craftsmanship, orcs celebrate destruction, burning stolen dwarven goods in massive bonfires. Weapons, armor, tools, and artifacts painstakingly crafted over years are destroyed in minutes, each item's destruction considered a prayer to Zuel. The feast that follows features meat roasted in the flames of burning dwarven treasures, symbolically consuming both the physical and cultural achievements of their hated enemies.

Clergy & Temples

Zuel's clergy are called Blood Priests or Thrak'zur (axe-blessing in orcish). Unlike the organized hierarchies of civilized religions, orcish spiritual leadership is brutally simple: the strongest priest is the leader until someone stronger kills him. The structure is entirely might-based, with the High Thrak or High Priest being the single strongest and most successful priest in a region, holding the title only until killed by a challenger. War-Blessed are senior clergy who have led successful raids or summoned Zuel's favor in battle, commanding respect through demonstrated prowess. Blood-Marked are initiated clergy who have scarified themselves to Zuel, bearing ritual scars that prove their devotion. Meat-Givers are aspirant clergy, warriors training to become priests by offering enemy corpses to Zuel and proving themselves worthy through repeated acts of violence.

There is no theological training, no years of study, no written doctrine. A warrior becomes a Blood Priest through a brutal process: first by slaying an enemy in Zuel's name, then by offering the corpse on a crude altar, next by scarifying themselves and cutting Zuel's symbol into their own flesh, and finally by successfully channeling divine rage in subsequent battle. If the warrior survives and proves effective, they are recognized as Thrak'zur. If they fail, they were unworthy and their death proves Zuel's judgment correct.

Blood Priests wear no robes or formal garb—only trophies of conquest including belts of enemy skulls, cloaks made from flayed skin, armor decorated with bones, war paint made from blood (preferably dwarf or human), and crude crowns of twisted iron and teeth. The only consistent element is the Bloody Handprint: every Blood Priest bears Zuel's symbol somewhere prominent—painted on armor, tattooed on chest, burned into flesh with red-hot iron.

Before battle, Blood Priests perform the Devouring, ritually consuming raw meat (ideally from enemies) and channeling battle-rage into warriors, whipping them into a frenzy that makes them fearless and savage. After victory, they conduct the Offering, dragging ten percent of slain enemies to crude altars where the corpses are burned or left to rot as sacrifice. Captured enemies undergo the Breaking, publicly maimed, tortured, and enslaved to demonstrate strength's dominion over weakness—not killed outright, but broken to show that death is mercy compared to Zuel's justice. Any orcish warrior may issue the Challenge, confronting a Blood Priest for their position in combat to the death, with the winner earning Zuel's favor and the loser proving their unworthiness.

Sacred items include the Hunger Banner, a blood-soaked battle standard carried into war, Bone Totems which are crude sculptures of skulls and weapons marking Zuel's territories, and the Red Drum, a war drum made from stretched skin and beaten to call Zuel's warriors to battle.

Zuel has no temples in the civilized sense—only war-shrines which are crude altars in conquered lands, usually built from the rubble of what was destroyed to create them. These shrines are constructed from ruins of conquered settlements around a central altar stained black with old blood. Skull-piles rise beside them, with larger piles indicating holier sites. Weapons taken from defeated enemies are driven into the ground like grave markers. There is no roof or walls—Zuel does not hide.

The Bleeding Throne, whose location remains unknown, is Zuel's greatest shrine, built somewhere in the deep mountains where his mortal followers cannot easily reach. It is said to be a throne carved from a single enormous skull (dragon? titan?) eternally dripping blood, where Zuel's avatar sits and plans future conquests. Every orcish settlement has at least a small shrine—a pile of rocks with Zuel's symbol painted in blood, surrounded by trophy skulls. In occupied dwarven holds, the ultimate desecration occurs: orcish forces convert the finest crafted halls into crude war-shrines, destroying dwarven art and replacing it with bone-piles and blood-altars.

Dogma

The strong take. The weak die. This is law. Zuel teaches that power is the only currency that matters, that possession and strength are synonymous, and that might provides its own justification. Battle is prayer. Conquest is worship. Victory is salvation. Every act of violence in Zuel's name is a sacred offering, every enemy slain a hymn sung in blood, every territory conquered a temple raised to his glory.

Zuel embodies hunger that never stops, and his followers must devour worlds in his image. Mordrion taught dwarves to build; Zuel teaches orcs to destroy. Building requires permission; destruction requires only strength. The asymmetry is intentional—creation is weakness because it can be undone, but destruction is power because it is permanent.

Peace is slow death. War is life at its fullest. Better to die in glorious battle than rot from age. Zuel's faithful are taught to view old age as shameful, a sign that a warrior has not fought hard enough or often enough. The ideal death comes on the battlefield, weapon in hand, screaming defiance at enemies even as life drains away.

Show no mercy. Expect none. Only the pitiless survive. Compassion is a luxury the strong pretend they can afford, but it is actually weakness disguised as virtue. Those who show mercy will eventually face enemies they should have killed, and their kindness will be the blade that ends them.

Your axe is your voice. Your strength is your worth. Prove both or perish. Words are wind; only actions demonstrate truth. A warrior's worth is measured entirely by what they can take and what they can hold. Philosophy, morality, law—these are constructs the weak invent to constrain the strong.

Zuel reminds his followers that he was once god of one world. He will be god of this one. Every conquest brings that day closer. His exile from godhood is temporary; his return to divine power inevitable. Every prayer offered in blood, every enemy slain, every territory claimed brings him closer to reclaiming what Moradin stole from him.

Mythology

On the world of Oerth, in a universe governed by different divine laws, two gods waged eternal war: Moradin, lord of the dwarves and embodiment of creation and craft, and Gruumsh, lord of the orcs and embodiment of destruction and conquest. Their battle was not a single event but an eternal state—every gain by one immediately countered by the other. Moradin's dwarves built fortress-cities; Gruumsh's orcs burned them. Gruumsh's hordes expanded across continents; Moradin's champions drove them back. For what must have been eons, they clashed. Neither could win. Neither would yield. They were cosmic opposites locked in perpetual stalemate, until the day everything changed.

In 1866 BW, during what should have been just another battle, something went catastrophically wrong. Perhaps both gods mustered more power than ever before. Perhaps their simultaneous strikes hit at a metaphysically precise angle. Perhaps the universe itself had tired of their endless conflict. Gruumsh's spear tore through divine defenses and ripped out Moradin's left eye. Moradin's axe cleaved through impossible flesh and severed Gruumsh's left hand at the wrist. The wounds should not have mattered—they were gods, such injuries meaningless. But the exchange released such catastrophic power that reality itself fractured. The quantum foam that separates universes ruptured. Both gods were torn from Oerth and hurled through the void between worlds, tumbling through infinite possibilities until they crashed into Mazariim's universe.

The transition was agony. Mazariim's laws of magic and divinity were different. What had been immortal gods became mortal entities—vastly powerful but vulnerable, eternal but killable. Gruumsh became Zuel—transformed, diminished, but unbowed. When he awoke in this new reality, he immediately understood his situation: he was no longer invincible, his power greatly reduced, and his eternal enemy had also arrived. But Zuel retained certain advantages including his memories of godhood (he knew what he had been), his divine essence (weaker but present), his fundamental nature (conquest, destruction, hunger—unchanged), and the ability to grant power to worshipers. And one critical insight: this universe was unfinished territory. On Oerth, every land was claimed, every tribe had allegiance. Here, primitive orcs existed without divine guidance. They were unclaimed, aimless, weak. They were perfect.

From 1866 to 3 BW, Zuel spent centuries building what would become the greatest threat Mazariim had ever faced. He wandered the deep places—mountains, caverns, wastelands—seeking out orcish tribes. To each, he revealed himself: I am Zuel. I am strength. Follow me and devour the world. The orcs, who had been scattered and primitive, suddenly had purpose. Zuel taught them forging iron weapons, coordinating large forces, building siege engines, and channeling divine rage in battle. But more importantly, he gave them hunger. Before Zuel, orcs raided for survival. After Zuel, they raided because conquest was sacred. Every enemy slain was prayer. Every city burned was worship. War became their religion, and Zuel was their god.

He also recruited goblins, hobgoblins, ogres, and other monstrous races. What had been disorganized banditry became an army over one million strong. Meanwhile, Mordrion was doing the opposite—helping dwarves end their civil wars, teaching cooperation and craftsmanship. The two entities barely interacted during this period. Each knew the other had arrived, each focused on building strength, each waiting for inevitable confrontation.

When Zuel finally marched to war in 3 BW, it was apocalyptic. The invasion began with Zuel's million-strong horde erupting from the mountains simultaneously across multiple fronts. Coastal cities faced frog-daemons and massive tentacled sea-monsters (how Zuel commanded such creatures remains unclear—perhaps deals with elemental powers, perhaps artifacts from Oerth). Dwarven holds that had stood for millennia fell within months. Human kingdoms fractured as refugees overwhelmed infrastructure.

By 2 BW, the losses were catastrophic. Most dwarven holds were overtaken, the dwarven population decimated. A dozen coastal cities were wiped from maps. Human and elven forces were driven back despite desperate resistance. Gnomish realms barely held with Rivoleen's inspired defenses. Halfling lands sheltered millions of refugees as Hinterhin blessed fields to produce impossible harvests. The sheer scale was unprecedented. Mazariim had seen wars before—the Dwarven Wars, the War of Souls—but nothing like this. This was existential.

In autumn of 1 BW, the war's final year, Zuel made his greatest gamble. His avatar manifested—a divine projection of his full power—and personally led a countless army toward Fort Hope, where the allied races had gathered for last stand. The final battle was spectacular and terrible. The coalition that formed was unprecedented—multiple lesser entities (Quarzel among them) fought at Mordrion's side, and victory seemed certain.

Then Demious Stoneshaper stepped forward. A dwarven warrior who had fought the entire war, who had lost his hold, his family, everything—but who carried Mordrion's blessed warhammer, passed to him by the dying avatar. The weapon blazed with divine light, channeling all of Mordrion's remaining power in this world. The battle between Stoneshaper and Zuel lasted hours—a mortal wielding divine weapon against entity in avatar form. Every clash shook mountains. Finally, Stoneshaper struck true: the warhammer connected with Zuel's chest and shattered the avatar.

Zuel was banished. His consciousness ripped from the material plane, unable to manifest avatar for 101 years. Without their god's presence, the orcish horde broke. What had been unstoppable army became fleeing mobs. The allied races pursued, reclaiming territory, driving orcs back into dark places. The war ended officially on 3 Quarz (January 3) 1 AW. Demious Stoneshaper became king of the northern dwarven realms. A new age began. But Zuel was not destroyed—only banished.

In 102 AW, Zuel's avatar returned, weakened but coherent. He found his orcish followers scattered, diminished, hiding in mountains and badlands. Many holds he had conquered had been reclaimed. His cult was a shadow of its former strength. He began rebuilding. Slowly. Patiently (as much as Zuel knows patience). What followed was a slow, grinding recovery. Orc tribes reconnected with Zuel's worship. Raids resumed, smaller but constant. Some dwarven holds remained in orcish hands—too deep, too fortified to reclaim. Every generation of orcs was raised on Zuel's teachings. Blood Priests emerged to lead warbands. The hunger never stopped—only weakened.

By the modern era in 811 AW, signs suggest Zuel's power is returning. Increased coordination among orcish tribes appears across multiple regions. Larger and more frequent raids test border defenses. Reports surface of Blood Priests conducting mass rituals not seen in centuries. Orcish warbands carry advanced weapons that suggest someone is teaching them sophisticated forging techniques. Rumors spread of something stirring in the deep holds—the ones never reclaimed.

Mordrion's faithful watch nervously. The War to End All Wars was 811 years ago. Everyone who fought in it is long dead (except ancient elves and dragons). New generations don't remember. They don't understand. But Zuel remembers. And he is hungry.

Zuel's eternal enemy is Mordrion, carried across universes. Their conflict is personal, absolute, and incapable of resolution. Zuel will never stop trying to destroy dwarves. Mordrion will never stop protecting them. This is existential—neither can change their nature. During the War, some humans sided with Zuel (especially Taovanian cultists who saw him as tool for power). Zuel neither liked nor cared about these alliances—humans were means to end. Currently, he views humanity as secondary enemy after dwarves but still worthy of conquest.

The patron deities Minerva, Hinterhin, and Rivoleen all opposed him in the War. He hates them as obstacles but doesn't prioritize them—they're not dwarven, thus not primary targets. If they get in his way, he'll destroy them. Otherwise, he ignores them. Garnak, the orc deity who awakened in 1666 BW, has a complicated relationship with Zuel. Garnak represents orcish strength and glory; Zuel represents conquest and destruction. Some orc tribes worship both, seeing no contradiction. Others choose sides. Zuel and Garnak neither cooperate nor fight—they coexist uneasily, each believing they're the true orc-father.

The war deity Quarzel fought against Zuel in the final battle and was destroyed. Quarzel's followers hate Zuel for their god's defeat. Zuel is indifferent—Quarzel was obstacle, obstacle removed. The death, madness, and aberration entities Zebulba, Og'thallith, and Ry'le are irrelevant to Zuel. He doesn't scheme with them or oppose them—they're simply not factors in conquest. His focus is material domination, not spiritual corruption or cosmic horror.

Asti opposes Zuel as positive energy versus destructive force. During the War to End All Wars (ending 1 BW), Zuel's orcish armies destroyed many of Asti's temples and healing sanctuaries across Mazariim, viewing them as obstacles to conquest. This conflict was purely about territorial dominance—Zuel cared nothing for Asti's theology, only that the temples provided refuge to enemies. Dragons present a complicated relationship. Some chromatic dragons worship Zuel or align with him. Metallic dragons oppose him. He finds dragons useful when they serve and obstacles when they don't—simple relationship based entirely on utility.

An estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 orcs across Mazariim worship Zuel as primary deity, with another 200,000 to 300,000 goblins, hobgoblins, and ogres. Perhaps 10,000 humans and other races who follow strength-based philosophies have adopted his worship. They gather in mountain holds that were once dwarven fortresses, in badlands too harsh for civilized races to survive, in the Underdark's deep cavern systems, in border regions from which they launch constant raids, and even in mercenary companies where human warriors adopted Zuel-worship after fighting alongside orcs.

Dwarven hold reclamation remains a major ongoing conflict—dwarves slowly retaking holds lost during the War. Every reclaimed hold is victory for Mordrion, defeat for Zuel. Progress is measured in decades. Despite defeat 811 years ago, orcish raids never stopped. Small war-bands, hit-and-run tactics, constant pressure on borders continue. Not organized conquest—just eternal hunger manifesting as perpetual low-intensity warfare.

The current campaign year of 811 AW brings troubling signs. Scouts report increased orcish coordination. Ancient battle-drums echo in mountains. Blood Priests perform rituals not seen in centuries. Is Zuel preparing for another war? Or is this another generation of raids like countless before? Dwarven kings aren't taking chances—holds fortify, militias drill, smithies work overtime forging weapons. Because everyone knows: Zuel never stops. He only waits.

Zuel's message, carved in dwarvish runes on every conquered hold, reads: Dwarves built these halls. Orcs took them. This is the natural order. Mordrion teaches you craft; I teach orcs destruction. Your god and mine are eternal enemies. Your holds will become my shrines, your bones will decorate my altars. This is not threat—it is promise.
