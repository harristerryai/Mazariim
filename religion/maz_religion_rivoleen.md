# **RIVOLEEN** (RIV-oh-leen)
## The Liberator • The Enlightened One

**Portfolio**: Gnomes, freedom, innovation  
**Power Level**: Greater entity  
**Title(s)**: The Liberator, The Enlightened One  
**Dominion**: Nirvana  
**Foci**: Worships Freedom, Worships Arcana, Worships Knowledge  
**Worshipers**: Gnomes, tinkerers, free folk  
**Favored Race**: Gnome  
**Favored Weapon**: Light crossbow  
**Alignment**: NG  
**Symbol**: Open birdcage with rainbow feathers

---

## General Overview

Rivoleen is the patron deity of gnomekind, awakening around 1780 BW—less than a hundred years after the arrival of Mordrion. She is an introspective and philosophical entity who favors enlightenment, thought, creativity, and above all, freedom. Rivoleen teaches that creativity flourishes in freedom, and that all beings deserve autonomy and the right to pursue their own paths.

Unlike more direct or interventionist deities, Rivoleen guides her followers subtly through philosophy, inspiration, and moments of clarity. She does not demand worship or obedience but instead encourages her followers to question, explore, and think for themselves. This has made gnomish society uniquely thoughtful and inventive, with a strong grasp of perspective on life and the universe.

Rivoleen's symbol—an open birdcage with rainbow feathers floating away—captures her core philosophy: true beauty and potential can only be realized when beings are free to fly. She opposes all forms of tyranny, slavery, and forced conformity, and her followers often work to liberate the oppressed and defend the rights of individuals.

Gnomish inventors and tinkerers invoke Rivoleen's name when seeking inspiration for new creations. Her worship encourages both intellectual pursuits and practical innovation, making gnomes some of the most inventive people in Mazariim. The steam-powered technologies that gnomes developed and shared with dwarves are seen as gifts from Rivoleen's inspiration.

Rivoleen's dominion, Nirvana, is a peaceful plane of enlightenment and contemplation where souls achieve perfect understanding of themselves and the universe. Those who enter Nirvana find themselves in an endless realm of gentle hills, quiet libraries, and workshops where they can pursue knowledge and creation for eternity, free from all constraints and suffering.

## Holy Days

The first of Mord (December 1) brings the Day of Questions, marking the beginning of winter with a day of philosophical debate and questioning. Communities gather in temples to discuss profound questions, challenge old assumptions, and share new ideas. No topic is off-limits during these debates—followers question the nature of reality, the purpose of existence, the definition of freedom, and even the teachings of Rivoleen herself. The goddess encourages this questioning, believing that a faith that cannot withstand scrutiny is not worth holding. Young gnomes often participate in their first serious philosophical debates on this day, presenting arguments they have prepared for months. The debates continue from morning until late into the night, with breaks for meals and contemplation.

The twenty-first of Riv (April 21) is celebrated as the Festival of Flight, honoring freedom and the release of caged birds. Gnomes craft beautiful mechanical birds throughout the spring, intricate creations of clockwork and colored glass that gleam in the sunlight. At dawn on the Festival, communities gather in open fields and release these mechanical birds into the sky as symbols of liberation, watching them soar and circle before returning to be collected and stored for next year. It is also traditional to free any caged creatures if it is safe to do so, releasing songbirds, rabbits, or other small animals that have been kept as pets or for other purposes. Many followers of Rivoleen use this day to help someone in need of freedom—whether paying off someone's debt, helping them escape a bad situation, or encouraging them to break free from their own fears and self-imposed limitations.

The fifteenth of Gyru (June 15) brings the Tinker's Jubilee, a joyous celebration of invention and creativity. Gnomes showcase their latest inventions in grand exhibitions, displaying everything from practical tools to whimsical toys to experimental devices of uncertain purpose. Competitions are held for the most ingenious gadgets, with categories for practical utility, aesthetic beauty, and sheer audacious creativity. Winners receive not gold or prizes but respect and the opportunity to have their inventions studied and replicated by others. Rivoleen's clergy, the Seekers, bless workshops throughout gnomish communities, offering prayers for inspiration to struggling inventors and celebrating the successes of those whose creations have improved life for others. The day concludes with a feast where gnomes share their discoveries with the community, explaining how their inventions work and encouraging others to build upon their ideas.

The thirtieth of Quarz (January 30) is observed as the Great Debate, a mid-winter tradition where gnomes engage in friendly but intense debates on philosophical, scientific, and ethical topics. Unlike the Day of Questions which welcomes all questions regardless of preparation, the Great Debate features structured formal arguments where participants have spent months researching their positions. Topics are announced a season in advance, allowing debaters time to gather evidence and construct arguments. The debates follow strict logical rules, with moderators ensuring that participants support their claims with reason rather than emotion or rhetoric. Winners receive not material prizes but respect and the opportunity to have their ideas published in the Librum Volantis and shared throughout gnomish communities. Many of the most important philosophical advances in gnomish thought originated in Great Debates.

The first of Quarz (January 1) marks Enlightenment Eve, when the new year begins with reflection and goal-setting. Gnomes spend the evening in meditation, contemplating the past year and identifying what they wish to learn or create in the year ahead. Many visit temples to write their goals in special journals kept by the Seekers, creating a record of their intentions that can be reviewed in future years. It is customary to make resolutions to pursue knowledge and freedom, committing to study a new subject, learn a new skill, or help liberate someone from oppression. The midnight moment when the old year becomes the new is observed in silence, with gnomes sitting in contemplation as one year passes into the next, symbolizing the eternal nature of learning and growth.

When a gnome comes of age around forty years old, they participate in the Rite of the Open Cage, a ceremony in which they symbolically choose their own path in life. The young gnome stands before an ornate birdcage while family and community gather to witness. A Seeker asks them the traditional question: "What cage will you refuse to enter?" The initiate then states what they will not allow to constrain them—fear, tradition, others' expectations, greed, hatred, despair, or any other force that might limit their freedom and growth. They open the cage, releasing either a mechanical bird or a real one, and step forward into adulthood as a free individual, acknowledged by their community as one who has chosen their own path.

## Clergy & Temples

Rivoleen's clergy are called Seekers, or Gendor'vol in Gnomish, meaning "those who walk the path." They wear robes of brilliant colors—often featuring rainbow patterns or gradients—symbolizing the diversity of thought and the beauty of freedom. Many carry journals, scrolls, or mechanical gadgets as symbols of their devotion to knowledge and innovation. Unlike most clergy who distinguish themselves through elaborate vestments, Seekers pride themselves on wearing practical robes that allow them to sit comfortably for long debates, work in workshops without snagging fabric on machinery, and move freely through gardens and libraries.

The Seekers serve multiple roles in gnomish society, functioning as philosophers and teachers who lead discussions, teach critical thinking, and preserve knowledge for future generations. They work as counselors, helping individuals find their purpose and make choices aligned with their true selves rather than the expectations of family or society. They serve as advocates for freedom, opposing tyranny wherever they find it, defending the rights of individuals against the demands of the collective, and sometimes aiding escaped slaves or refugees fleeing persecution. They act as inspirers of innovation, blessing workshops, encouraging tinkerers who are struggling with difficult problems, and celebrating new inventions that push the boundaries of what is possible.

Unlike hierarchical religions where authority flows from high priests and councils, Rivoleen's clergy operate with great autonomy. Each Seeker follows their own path, and there is no central authority dictating doctrine or commanding obedience. Regional gatherings occur several times a year for philosophical debates and the sharing of ideas, but these are meetings of equals where no Seeker can command another. The closest thing to leadership comes from respect earned through wisdom, and elder Seekers are deferred to not because of their position but because their years of study and contemplation have given them valuable perspective.

Many followers of Rivoleen keep a Question Journal, a personal notebook where they record questions, observations, and philosophical musings. Seekers actively encourage this practice, teaching that asking the right questions is often more valuable than knowing the answers. Questions lead to investigation, investigation leads to knowledge, and knowledge leads to freedom from ignorance and superstition. Some gnomes spend their entire lives filling dozens of journals, which are preserved in temple libraries for future generations to read and consider. The most profound Question Journals are studied like sacred texts, with later generations adding their own annotations and responses to questions posed centuries earlier.

Temples of Rivoleen are open, airy structures designed to inspire thought and creativity rather than awe or submission. They feature libraries, workshops, observatories, and meditation gardens, serving as community centers for learning, debate, and invention rather than places of ritual and ceremony. The architecture emphasizes light and space, with large windows, skylights, and open courtyards that allow natural light to flood every chamber.

Each temple includes a Hall of Questions, a debate space where gnomes and visitors of any race gather to discuss philosophy, science, and the nature of existence. The hall is arranged in a circular pattern so that no speaker stands above another, with comfortable seating and excellent acoustics that allow even whispered arguments to be heard clearly. The Workshop of Wonders serves as a communal space filled with tools, materials, and half-finished inventions where tinkerers can work and share ideas. Resources are held in common, and any visitor may use the tools to pursue their own projects, with the understanding that they will share their discoveries with others. The Garden of Contemplation provides a peaceful outdoor space with walking paths, fountains, and quiet alcoves for meditation, where those overwhelmed by questions or struggling with difficult problems can find peace and clarity.

The largest temple to Rivoleen stands in the gnomish capital of Seergov, a massive complex that houses the greatest library in gnomish lands. The Library of Ten Thousand Questions contains not only books but also mechanical models, experimental prototypes, and artistic works created by generations of gnomes. Seekers from across Mazariim make pilgrimages to Seergov to study in the library and add their own contributions to its ever-growing collection.

## Dogma

The teachings of Rivoleen, as recorded in the Librum Volantis (The Book of Flight), begin with the principle that freedom is sacred. All beings deserve the right to choose their own path, to make their own decisions, to succeed or fail by their own efforts. Slavery, tyranny, and forced conformity are abominations that must be opposed wherever they exist. A society that takes away individual freedom—even for the supposed good of the collective—is a society that has lost its way. Rivoleen teaches that the only legitimate use of force is to prevent one being from taking away another's freedom, and even then, the minimum necessary force should be used.

Question everything. Do not accept ideas simply because they are traditional or widely believed. Examine, test, and think critically about all things. The greatest obstacle to knowledge is the assumption that we already know the answer. Followers of Rivoleen are taught to challenge their own assumptions as rigorously as they challenge the assumptions of others, to remain open to the possibility that they might be wrong, and to change their minds when evidence demands it. This applies even to the teachings of Rivoleen herself—the goddess would rather her followers question her than follow her blindly.

Creativity flourishes in freedom. You cannot force innovation or inspiration through threats or mandates. Give people the freedom to experiment, fail, and try again, and wonders will emerge. The greatest inventions come not from careful planning but from playful exploration, from trying things just to see what happens, from combining ideas in unexpected ways. Rivoleen teaches that societies which try to control and direct creativity inevitably stifle it, while societies that embrace chaos and experimentation produce breakthroughs that change the world.

Pursue knowledge for the joy of learning, not for power or profit. The universe is vast and full of mysteries—explore them with curiosity and wonder. Those who seek knowledge only to gain advantage over others have missed the point entirely. True learning is its own reward, expanding one's understanding of reality and one's place within it. Rivoleen's followers are encouraged to study subjects that fascinate them regardless of whether that knowledge has practical application. Some of the most important discoveries in history came from people investigating questions that seemed pointless at the time.

Respect all perspectives. Every individual sees the world differently based on their experiences, their nature, and their limitations. Listen to others, consider their views, and recognize that diversity of thought makes society stronger. A community where everyone thinks alike is a community that has stopped growing. Disagreement and debate are healthy when conducted with respect and genuine curiosity about why others believe what they believe. The goal is not to win arguments but to understand truth more fully, and truth is often found in the synthesis of competing perspectives.

Invent and share. Create new things, solve problems, and push the boundaries of what is possible. But do not hoard your discoveries for personal gain or competitive advantage—share them freely so all can benefit. Knowledge that is kept secret benefits only the few who hold it, but knowledge that is shared multiplies its value infinitely. When one gnome discovers something useful and teaches it to ten others, who each teach ten more, the entire world becomes richer. Rivoleen teaches that those who patent inventions to prevent others from using them are betraying the spirit of creativity itself.

Find your purpose. Every being has a unique path to walk, a contribution only they can make to the world. Do not live according to others' expectations or follow a path chosen by your parents, your society, or your circumstances. Discover what brings you joy and fulfillment, and pursue it with passion. A life lived in service to someone else's vision is a life half-lived. Rivoleen wants her followers to be fully themselves, to express their unique nature, and to find the work that makes them feel most alive.

## Mythology

Rivoleen awakened around 1780 BW, during a time when gnomes were still developing their identity as a distinct people. Before her arrival, many gnomes worshiped the Old Gods—particularly Dirgin the earth entity, Pyrantor the fire god, and the other elemental forces. While these entities provided protection and power, they offered little in the way of philosophical guidance or moral teaching. Gnomes followed the Old Gods out of fear and pragmatism, making offerings to appease vast forces that neither knew nor cared about individual gnomes.

When Rivoleen first made contact with the gnomes, she appeared not as a commanding figure demanding worship but as a humble gnomish scholar carrying a journal filled with questions rather than answers. She walked among the gnomes in their cities and villages, sitting in workshops and gardens, asking them what they believed, why they believed it, and whether they had ever questioned their assumptions. She did not criticize those who worshiped the Old Gods but simply asked what they received in return for their devotion, and whether beings who could not speak to them or care about them truly deserved to be called gods.

Her arrival sparked a philosophical revolution among gnomes. Within a generation, the worship of the Old Gods faded dramatically, though some still honor them out of respect for tradition or for specific practical needs. Rivoleen became the dominant deity of gnomekind, reshaping gnomish culture through her teachings. Her influence led gnomes to become a more peaceful, thoughtful people with a firm grasp of perspective on life and the universe, understanding their place in the cosmos while refusing to be bound by cosmic forces beyond their control.

When Mordrion the Dwarf Father arrived in 1866 BW and began working to end the Dwarven Wars, gnomes found themselves caught in the middle of the conflict. The northern Gormdawi dwarves sought to expand their empire into gnomish lands, seeing the gnomes as weak and their territories as ripe for conquest. This caused great distress among gnomes who valued their independence above all else and had no desire to be absorbed into someone else's empire, even one as orderly and prosperous as the Gormdawi realm.

Rivoleen's teachings during this period emphasized patience, diplomacy, and the importance of freedom. She encouraged her followers not to respond to aggression with aggression but to make clear through words and defensive preparations that they would fight if forced to do so. Gnomish communities fortified their borders and prepared clever traps and defenses, making it clear that conquering gnomish lands would be far more costly than it appeared. At the same time, Rivoleen's Seekers engaged in diplomatic efforts, reaching out to both sides of the Dwarven Wars to argue for peace.

When Mordrion finally brokered peace in 1849 BW, ending the Dwarven Wars and establishing the Day of Unity that united the dwarven clans, gnomes were relieved and grateful. The end of the conflict allowed gnomes to focus on more industrious matters—tinkering, invention, and the pursuit of knowledge rather than the preparation for war. Rivoleen taught her followers to learn from the experience, noting that freedom must sometimes be defended but that the goal of defense is always to return to peace as quickly as possible.

Following the end of the Dwarven Wars, gnomes entered a golden age of innovation spanning from 1849 BW to 3 BW. Inspired by Rivoleen's teachings that creativity flourishes in freedom, they used magic and engineering to create steam-powered technologies that revolutionized industry and transportation. Gnomish workshops produced engines that could power mills, pumps that could drain mines, and vehicles that could travel without horses or wind. These technologies were shared freely with the dwarves according to Rivoleen's teaching that knowledge should be shared rather than hoarded, and dwarves incorporated steam technology into their cities and holds, creating a technological renaissance that benefited both peoples.

Rivoleen's clergy encouraged this innovation through their role as inspirers of creation, blessing workshops and celebrating inventors. The philosophy that creativity flourishes in freedom became the foundation of gnomish society, and the Gnomish Realms became centers of learning and invention where curious minds from all races came to study and share ideas. Universities were established, great libraries were built, and philosophical academies attracted scholars from across Mazariim.

When the War to End All Wars began in 3 BW with the invasion of Zuel's orcish hordes, Rivoleen took a defensive rather than aggressive role. She had no interest in conquest or glory, only in protecting the freedom of her people and others who sought sanctuary. She helped defend the Gnomish Realms from orcish invasion, using her influence to inspire clever tactics, ingenious traps, and defensive fortifications that turned gnomish cities into nearly impregnable strongholds. Gnomish engineers created steam-powered war machines, explosives, and other innovations that helped turn the tide of battle, proving that intelligence and creativity could defeat brute force and overwhelming numbers.

Rivoleen also encouraged gnomes to provide sanctuary to refugees fleeing the War, particularly those escaping slavery or tyranny. Many gnomish communities opened their doors to humans, halflings, and even dwarves seeking safety, offering food, shelter, and protection. Some Seekers traveled into war-torn regions to help escaped slaves reach freedom, risking their own lives to live out Rivoleen's teachings about the sacred nature of liberty. These rescue missions saved thousands of lives and earned Rivoleen's faith deep respect among those who valued freedom.

After the War ended in 1 AW with the defeat of Zuel's avatar, gnomes became even more focused on innovation, family, and the pursuit of knowledge. The steam technologies developed during the War found peaceful applications in mining, transportation, and manufacturing, improving quality of life throughout the Gnomish Realms and in dwarven lands that had adopted the technology. Gnomes continued to defend their homeland from periodic conflicts with giants who raided from the mountains, but these were minor skirmishes compared to the existential threat of the War, and gnomes remained generally peaceful and inward-focused.

Rivoleen's writings and the teachings of her philosopher-disciples became common throughout the Gnomish Realms. The Librum Volantis was copied countless times and distributed to every gnomish community, with new chapters added each generation as Seekers recorded new insights and answered new questions. Her influence spread beyond gnomes, and many humans and halflings adopted her ideals of freedom, creativity, and critical thinking. Some human philosophers traveled to gnomish cities to study at Rivoleen's temples, bringing her teachings back to their own cultures.

Today, gnomes are known throughout Mazariim as brilliant inventors, thoughtful philosophers, and defenders of freedom. Their cities are havens for freethinkers and refugees, places where ideas are valued over tradition and where questioning authority is considered a virtue rather than a vice. Rivoleen's influence continues to shape gnomish culture, ensuring that her people remain committed to the principles of liberty, innovation, and the endless pursuit of knowledge.

Rivoleen maintains excellent relationships with several other entities who share her values. Hinterhin the halfling god of harvest and community is a close ally, as both favor peace, prosperity, and the protection of simple folk from the depredations of tyrants and monsters. Tiovin the human god of protection and heroism shares Rivoleen's ideals of goodness and freedom, and their clergy often work together to oppose evil and aid the oppressed. Minerva the ancient goddess of wisdom respects Rivoleen's commitment to the pursuit of knowledge and harmony with the natural world, though Minerva sometimes finds gnomish tinkering too disruptive of natural patterns.

Rivoleen has a cordial but sometimes strained relationship with Mordrion the Dwarf Father. She respects his dedication to his people and his emphasis on craftsmanship and community, but she sometimes finds his emphasis on order and tradition restrictive. Where Mordrion teaches that society functions best when everyone knows their place and follows established rules, Rivoleen teaches that society flourishes when individuals are free to find their own place and question established rules. The two faiths coexist peacefully, but philosophical debates between Seekers and Mordrion's clerics can become quite heated.

Rivoleen opposes Taovan the god of corruption and greed, Quarzel the dead god of tyranny and conquest, Xannith the entity of madness and chaos, and Zuel the orcish god of destruction. All of these beings promote tyranny in some form—whether through direct oppression, through the corruption of institutions, through the destruction of civilization, or through the madness that destroys free will. She has particular disdain for entities that enslave or oppress others, seeing slavery as the ultimate violation of the principle that all beings deserve freedom.

Rivoleen respects the Old Gods—Dirgin, Pyrantor, Vuleala, and the others—but does not actively promote their worship. She acknowledges their vast power and their role in the fundamental workings of the universe, but she believes that mortals deserve deities who care about them as individuals rather than vast forces of nature that regard individual lives as insignificant. She is indifferent to most cosmic conflicts between good and evil, law and chaos, unless they threaten freedom. She does not care whether the universe is ordered or chaotic, only whether beings within it are free to choose their own paths.

The cage door is open. Freedom is not given—it is chosen.
