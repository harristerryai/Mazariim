# Halfling History: Before the Harvest Awakening
## The First Age of Halflings (100,000 BW - 3,402 BW)

This document details halfling (Hin) history across the same long-span structure used for elven history, but reframed around halfling cultural turning points. **Hinterhin's awakening (2,971 BW)** is treated as a spiritual-cultural consolidation, not a sudden revelation event. Halfling history is most reliably preserved in village genealogies, seasonal rites, and travel-tales carried by traders, midwives, and wandering hearthkeepers, rather than in centralized imperial records (and where records exist, they tend to be practical: tallies, seed lists, marriage oaths, and festival routes).

**Calendar Note:** Halfling history uses the standard BW/AW calendar system. Unlike elves (who use Elven Reckoning from Minerva's awakening) or dwarves (who use Dwarven Reckoning from Mordrion's arrival), halflings never developed an alternate dating system—a reflection of their practical, outward-facing culture that prioritizes cooperation over distinctiveness.

---

## THE ANCIENT TIMES (~100,000 - 20,000 BW)

### **~100,000 BW — The Separation**

The five mortal races (elves, humans, dwarves, halflings, gnomes) become genetically distinct. Early Hin exist as small, hardy river- and hedgerow-folk—adapted to concealment, patience, and cooperative survival. Their earliest legends emphasize *not being found* (by storms, predators, or tyrants) as the first great magic of the small folk. Halfling oral culture develops as "story-ritual": tales that carry practical instruction (weather signs, soil lore, safe crossings) and moral expectation (share food, keep vows, never waste the land).

### **~50,000 BW — Stone, Seed, and Shelter**

Halfling clans master stone tools, controlled fire, and the earliest forms of mixed subsistence: trapping, small-game hunting, foraging, and proto-gardening. Instead of large permanent sites, they prefer *seasonal hearth-circles*—places returned to each year where tools are repaired, seeds are traded, and marriages arranged. While elven clans develop similar technologies in parallel, halflings distinguish themselves through an emphasis on portability and redundancy—multiple small caches rather than single large stores.

### **~30,000 BW — The Hidden Roads**

As populations spread, Hin develop the first "quiet routes" between valleys: paths that avoid open ridges, keep water within reach, and pass through defensible choke points. These routes become the skeleton of later Shalazar trade trails and refuge-lines. Where elven migration during this period (the Scattering) follows geographic features openly, halfling migration follows concealed paths that later generations will maintain as sacred trust.

### **~25,000 - 20,000 BW — First Contact with the Old Gods (By Sign, Not by Temple)**

As elemental entities awaken across Mazariim, halfling shamans interpret them through immediate cause-and-effect: rain that arrives after the right song, soil that fattens after the right offering, a birth that survives because the midwife remembered the old charm. Hin religiosity begins as *practice* more than doctrine—spirits acknowledged in the language of seasons and necessities, long before formal priesthoods exist.

Where elven shamans receive visions from Mau the Oracle and learn divination through observation of stars, halfling shamans focus on smaller, closer signs: the behavior of insects before rain, the color of soil after flood, the timing of bird migrations. This practical spirituality creates a foundation for the agricultural religion that Hinterhin will later formalize.

### **~20,000 BW — The First Marked Fields**

Rather than building great temples like the elves (who construct the Circle of Watching Stars and other monumental sites during this era), Hin mark the land modestly: boundary stones for shared orchards, small shrines at irrigation points, and "hearth posts" that record seasons by carved notches. The earliest halfling calendars are agricultural and communal: a memory device for when to plant, when to hunt, and when to store.

## THE AGE OF CLANS (~20,000 - 12,000 BW)

### **~18,000 BW — Gardens Become Villages**

Hin clans transition from seasonal camps to semi-permanent villages at fertile river bends and sheltered hillsides. Their key innovation is not large-scale agriculture, but *redundant agriculture*: many small plots, many seed varieties, many contingency plans. This same era sees elven clans in river valleys transitioning to agriculture, but where elves develop population centers that grow to 200-500 inhabitants, halfling villages rarely exceed 100—a deliberate choice favoring resilience over scale.

### **~16,000 BW — The Borrowed Bridle**

Contact with larger peoples (especially plains folk and the edge of wemic territories) yields an early halfling cultural trait: learn from everyone, imitate no one completely. Hin adopt pack-animal logistics and mobile craft traditions while keeping village hearth-life central. As elven plains clans form the Wemic Alliance during this period, halflings observe and adapt useful techniques without entering formal alliances themselves—a pattern of quiet learning that will characterize Hin relations with all larger peoples.

### **~15,000 BW — The Two Hearthlines**

The two hereditary lines recognized later as Tala'nitya (Lightfoot) and Tala'beleg (Stout) begin to be acknowledged socially: one line tending outward-facing roles (trade, diplomacy, craft mastery), the other anchoring the inward strength of villages (field labor, building, defense). Halflings treat the distinction as complementary rather than hierarchical—a duality that mirrors the later elven distinction between city and forest culture, but emerges among halflings nearly 12,000 years earlier.

### **~14,200 BW — The First Great Bake (A Shared Miracle)**

Hin legend preserves an early cultural unifier: a famine narrowly avoided when multiple clans coordinate seed-sharing and communal storage across a region. The "miracle" is logistical and moral: every family eats because every family shares. This event occurs in the same era that elves found Elwendor (their first city with over 1,000 permanent residents), highlighting the divergent paths: elves consolidate into urban centers while halflings develop networks of mutual aid between dispersed villages.

### **~13,500 BW — Names that Carry Work**

Family names and bynames become linked to trades (baker, healer, gardener, etc.), turning social identity into a living inventory of village capability. The Hin become a people who remember *who can do what* as carefully as others remember royal bloodlines. While Elwendor scholars develop written language during this same period, halflings perfect oral systems for encoding practical knowledge—a choice that will persist even after literacy becomes available.

### **~13,000 BW — Halfling Contact (Remembered from the Inside)**

Where other histories describe "halflings appear," Hin tales describe "the tall-folk finally noticed." Early relations with elven neighbors are characterized by mutual agriculture and boundary negotiation rather than conquest. Elven records from this period note that they initially mistook halflings for "deformed or cursed kin" before recognizing them as a separate race. Halfling tales, by contrast, suggest the Hin had been aware of elves for generations and simply saw no reason to make contact until the tall-folk wandered into halfling gardens.

Relations are peaceful from the start—halflings pose no territorial threat and share elven reverence for nature and agriculture. Some halfling clans settle alongside elven villages, while others maintain independence. Intermarriage never occurs (species barrier), but cultural exchange flourishes, establishing patterns that will endure for millennia.

### **~12,000 BW — The Hearth Oaths**

Hin clans formalize non-royal governance through oaths sworn at communal fires: shared defense, seed tithes, guest-right, and dispute mediation. This becomes the cultural root of later Greenwarden judicial authority. The same year sees elven druids consecrate Iaurtorn as supreme ceremonial ground for wood elven druids—both races independently developing formal spiritual-political institutions, though through very different models.

## THE AGE OF KINGDOMS (~12,000 - 6,000 BW)

### **~11,800 BW — The First Hedgewardens**

Before formal clergy, certain families become recognized as protectors of borders, storehouses, and "quiet routes." They serve as scouts and mediators rather than soldiers—more likely to prevent a fight than win one. This emergence parallels the rise of the first true elven druids during the same period, as Gruhathyrir's influence intensifies across Mazariim. Hedgewardens share the druids' connection to nature but lack formal divine power—they are community servants rather than priests.

### **~11,200 BW — The Orchard Leagues**

Multiple villages form cooperative leagues around orchards and long-term crops (which require years of shared planning). These leagues become halfling proto-institutions: seasonal councils, rotating leadership, and enforceable norms about storage and distribution. While elves form the Starwood Confederation (first multi-clan nation) during this era, halflings resist consolidation into larger political units, preferring networks of obligation over hierarchies of command.

### **~10,800 BW — The Walking Market**

Hin traders develop itinerant market-circuits: a traveling fair that moves from village to village on predictable dates, spreading news, tools, marriages, and songs. This innovation—commerce as community-building rather than profit-seeking—becomes central to halfling identity. The Walking Market tradition will persist into the modern era, evolving but never disappearing.

### **~10,200 BW — The Quiet Wars**

Halfling history records conflict in the negative: years when the bread is hidden, the children moved, the songs go un-sung. Hin prefer avoidance, sabotage of supply lines, and negotiated exits over decisive battles. This cultural memory later shapes their response to larger continental wars. While elves wage holy war against Zebulba's shadow cults during this period (the Brotherhood of the Black Flame destroyed after decades of conflict), halflings experience the era as one of careful hiding and quiet resistance against unnamed threats.

### **~9,400 BW — The Sodroof Tradition**

The iconic rounded, partially underground homes become widespread: warm in winter, cool in summer, and nearly invisible from a distance. Architecture reflects worldview: survive quietly; persist through storms and armies alike. This development occurs as elven architecture grows increasingly monumental—the contrast between towering elven spires and hidden halfling burrows becomes a defining difference between the races.

### **~9,100 BW — The Granary Codes**

Village law evolves around storage: what is owed in lean years, how disputes are mediated, and when outsiders can be fed without endangering the winter. These codes become the foundation of halfling legal tradition—practical, food-focused, and communal. The same period sees devastating plague (the Bloodtear Plague) sweep through elven coastal settlements; halfling communities, more isolated and self-sufficient, largely escape the worst effects.

### **~8,200 - 8,000 BW — The Seed Sailors**

Coastal contacts increase as elven shipwrights develop true oceangoing vessels. Hin do not found great ports, but become essential to provisioning: preserving food, seed stock, and herbal medicine for longer journeys. Halfling expertise in food preservation makes them invaluable partners for maritime ventures, establishing a pattern of supporting rather than leading larger enterprises.

### **~7,900 BW — Two Stories, One Hearth**

By this point, Hin culture has a stable duality: outward-facing circuits (markets, diplomacy, craft) and inward-facing stability (fields, kinship, dispute law). Both are treated as sacred duties. This cultural balance mirrors the emergence of "Twin Kingdoms" among elves during the same period—both races developing complementary internal structures, though through very different social forms.

### **~7,500 BW — Shalazar Founded**

Halflings consolidate more densely into the island region that will bear the name Shalazar ("Land of Small Folk"). For Hin, this is remembered as "the choosing of the good ground": a cultural commitment to agriculture, shared labor, and peaceful living. The founding occurs through peaceful negotiation with elven neighbors—halfling clans receive territory in exchange for agricultural tithes and military alliance.

Shalazar is not a nation in the elven sense (no capital, no king, no formal borders) but a patchwork of villages linked by oath and custom within the broader elven sphere of Vand'ardhon. The island's isolation provides natural defense, while its fertile soil and favorable climate create conditions for the agricultural abundance that will later be attributed to Hinterhin's blessing.

Halflings prove exceptionally skilled farmers, increasing food production across the region. Many elves view halflings as junior partners in civilizing Vand'ardhon—a perception the Hin quietly accept while maintaining their own sense of distinct identity. This successful integration will make elves uniquely accepting of halflings throughout Mazariim's history.

## THE AGE OF MITHRIL (~6,000 - 4,000 BW)

### **~6,000 BW — The Little Iron**

While others chase mithril (discovered by elven miners in northern peaks), Hin perfect practical metallurgy: durable tools, hinges, plows, and needles. Halfling innovation shows up in "small multipliers"—tools that let a village do more work with fewer hands. Where elven smiths craft legendary weapons and armor from mithril, halfling smiths craft better spades and kitchen knives from iron. Both approaches reflect cultural values: elven pursuit of excellence versus halfling pursuit of sufficiency.

### **~5,700 BW — The Rootcellars**

Food preservation advances: fermentation, smoking, cold storage, and shared rootcellars become halfling strategic infrastructure. In later wars, these hidden storehouses save tens of thousands. The techniques spread beyond Shalazar as halfling traders share knowledge with trusted partners—an early example of Hin soft power through practical expertise.

### **~5,400 BW — The Neighbor Compacts**

Halflings become unusually skilled treaty-makers: boundary accords, water rights, shared grazing. They prefer agreements that can be remembered and repeated at seasonal festivals—oral contracts witnessed by community rather than written documents held by individuals. This period coincides with the Philosopher Wars among elves (intellectual conflict between city and forest factions); halfling communities sometimes serve as neutral ground for negotiations, beginning a long tradition of Hin mediation.

### **~5,100 BW — The Herbwise Tradition**

Healers and midwives unify a long oral "green book" tradition—recipes, antidotes, and childbirth rites—carried by memorized verse and call-and-response teaching. While elven scholars in Elwendor begin systematic arcane research during this era, halfling healers perfect a different kind of knowledge: practical, embodied, and passed through generations of practitioners rather than recorded in texts.

### **~4,800 BW — The Flood Years**

Inundations and strange seasons teach Hin that weather can be a slow enemy. This becomes a proto-theological itch: someone is needed who tends the sky and the soil together. The same catastrophic flooding destroys four major elven coastal cities (the Drowning Cities), killing 3,000-4,500 elves. Halfling communities, built in sheltered hillsides rather than coastal lowlands, suffer less severe damage but remember the era as proof that even the best preparation cannot prevent all disaster.

### **~4,600 BW — The Peninsular Crossings**

Halfling clans expand along peninsulas and river deltas, forming "bridge villages" that broker trade between inland and coast. This expansion coincides with elven development of the Peninsula Kingdoms in the same regions—the two peoples settle overlapping territories, establishing patterns of coexistence that persist into the modern era.

### **~4,300 BW — The Sling Schools**

Village defense formalizes around militia drills and slings—cheap, accurate, and culturally aligned with "protect without invading." Halfling warriors train not to conquer but to defend: hit-and-run tactics, knowledge of terrain, and the ability to disappear when outnumbered. This defensive orientation reflects both practical necessity (halflings cannot match larger races in direct combat) and cultural preference (victory through survival rather than domination).

### **~4,000 BW — Dragons as Distant Weather**

Hin tales treat dragons like storms: real, dangerous, and best endured through preparation, concealment, and community response. While elves experience increasing contact with dragons during this era (metallic dragons visiting settlements, offering cryptic advice), halflings prefer to observe from a distance. Dragon cults never gain traction in halfling communities—the Hin see no reason to worship what they cannot control or understand.

## THE AGE OF TWILIGHT (~4,000 - 3,402 BW)

### **~3,900 BW — The Great Replanting**

A generational project spreads resilient grains and orchard stock across Shalazar, reducing famine risk and increasing village interdependence. This coordinated agricultural program represents the most ambitious collective action in halfling history to date, requiring cooperation across dozens of villages over decades. The success of the Great Replanting strengthens faith in communal institutions and creates the agricultural surplus that will support population growth over the following centuries.

### **~3,800 BW — The Valley Exchanges**

Halfling trade circuits deepen with elven valley communities: seeds and food for crafted tools, woodwork, and arcane-adjacent curios. As elves colonize the Valley of Bounty (later Yavaw'dae) during this period, halfling traders become essential intermediaries, connecting the new settlements with established networks. Valley elves develop particular appreciation for halfling partners, creating relationships that remain strong into the modern era.

### **~3,700 BW — The Last Old Clans**

Some Hin clans refuse consolidation and remain fully itinerant. They become the keepers of "outside stories," later invaluable when wider-world threats arrive. While elves formalize the Seven Nations during this period, halflings maintain space for those who reject even the loose structures of Shalazar village life. These wandering clans preserve ancient knowledge and maintain connections to distant lands that settled halflings rarely visit.

### **~3,600 BW — The Questioning Time**

Halflings begin asking openly whether the seasons themselves have a patron, and whether peace can be protected without becoming tyrants. This spiritual questioning parallels a similar crisis among elves during the same period—both races sense that the Old Gods (Mau, Vuleala, Gruhathyrir, and others) provide power but not purpose. The Hin, like the elves, hunger for a patron who actually cares about their specific people.

### **~3,500 BW — The Hearth Priors**

Respected elders emerge who serve as judges, festival callers, and oath-witnesses across multiple villages. These proto-clergy lack divine power but command moral authority, settling disputes and coordinating community action. The Hearth Priors create a structure that Hinterhin's Greenwardens will later inherit and formalize.

### **~3,450 BW — The Weather Wounds**

A run of bad harvests and violent storms in Shalazar creates the cultural conditions for a unifying harvest-and-sky patron. Hearth Priors organize unprecedented mutual aid, but the crisis reveals the limits of purely mortal coordination. Many halflings begin praying to unnamed powers, hoping that someone will answer.

### **~3,402 BW — The Darkness Before Dawn**

Hin tradition holds that the world grew restless long before the great continental wars; the land "felt thin," and the songs became more urgent. This sense of impending change mirrors elven experience during the same period—both races stand on the precipice of transformation. For elves, the answer comes immediately when Minerva awakens and makes first contact. For halflings, the answer will come 431 years later.

# Halfling History: The Harvest Awakening Era
## The Age of Hearth and Harvest (3,402 BW - 1 BW)

This era is framed around halfling consolidation of identity and civic religion. It overlaps the same world-span as the elven "Age of Gold," but halfling history treats it as the maturation of village institutions rather than a sudden transformation event.

---

## THE ENLIGHTENING & EARLY CHANGES (3,402 - 3,300 BW)

### **3,402 BW — The Elven Enlightening**

Minerva awakens and makes first contact with the elves of Vand'ardhon in "The Enlightening." Halflings in Shalazar observe the transformation of their elven neighbors with interest and some wariness. The sudden formalization of elven religion, the emergence of the Cundo'elda and Cundo'dori, and the cultural renaissance that follows all affect halfling communities—but the Hin do not rush to join.

Some halfling druids who share groves and river rights with elves begin attending Minervan ceremonies out of neighborly respect. A few convert entirely, finding that Minerva's teachings about balance and stewardship align with halfling values. But most Hin wait, sensing that their own answer has not yet arrived.

### **~3,350 - 3,300 BW — Minerva's Respect Spreads Among Hin Druids**

As Minerva's church influences regional druidic practice, Hin druids who already venerate earth-and-water powers adopt Minerva as an honored ally—particularly where elves and Hin share groves and river rights. This is not conversion but syncretism: halfling druids add Minerva to their practice without abandoning the Old Gods or their hope for a specifically halfling patron.

The relationship proves mutually beneficial. Minervan druids share advanced agricultural knowledge; halfling druids share practical expertise in food preservation and small-scale farming. The alliance strengthens both communities without requiring halflings to subordinate their identity to elven religion.

---

## THE AGE OF CONSOLIDATION (3,300 - 2,900 BW)

### **~3,300 BW — The Granary Accord**

Villages standardize mutual aid obligations across Shalazar: if one valley fails, another shares; if a storm ruins one orchard, others replant. This creates halfling resilience as a cultural trait. The Granary Accord is not a single document but a network of oral agreements, renewed at each year's harvest festival and witnessed by Hearth Priors from multiple communities.

### **~3,200 BW — The Quiet Roads Become Sacred**

Trade paths are ritually maintained; hearth-shrines mark safe crossings. These routes later double as refugee corridors. The Walking Market tradition, now over 7,000 years old, becomes explicitly religious—the market's circuit is a pilgrimage, and those who maintain the roads perform sacred service.

### **~3,100 BW — Population and Prosperity**

Shalazar's population reaches approximately 40,000—small by elven standards but unprecedented for halflings. Agricultural surplus, peaceful relations with elven neighbors, and the Granary Accord's mutual aid create conditions for sustained growth. Villages remain small (50-200 inhabitants typical) but multiply across the island's fertile regions.

### **~3,000 BW — The Tiovin-Taovan Sundering**

When Tiovin and Taovan split from their dual origin and establish Heaven and Hell respectively, the cosmic event ripples through all mortal peoples. Halflings, lacking a patron deity, experience the Sundering as distant thunder—aware that something momentous has occurred but uncertain what it means for them. Some Hearth Priors begin incorporating Tiovin's teachings about mercy and community into their practice, while firmly rejecting Taovan's doctrines of domination.

---

## THE HARVEST AWAKENING (2,971 BW)

### **2,971 BW — Hinterhin Awakens**

Hinterhin awakens as patron of halflings, harvest, nature, and family. The Hin remember this as the first time "the land answered back" consistently: predictable blessings, teachable weather wisdom, and a shared moral code that scales beyond a single village. This is the seed of the Greenwardens (Duni'hin) as spiritual and judicial leaders.

Unlike Minerva's Enlightening (which gathered elven leaders to Elysium for a month of direct divine contact), Hinterhin's awakening manifests through the land itself. Crops grow more abundantly. Weather patterns become more predictable. Prayers for rain are answered. The divine does not descend to speak with chosen representatives; instead, the divine rises through the soil and seasons, touching every halfling who works the earth.

The Hearth Priors—elders who had long served as informal spiritual leaders—find their prayers suddenly answered with divine power. They become the first Greenwardens, though the title and formal organization develop over the following decades.

According to halfling legend, Hinterhin ran his deific hands through the soil of Shalazar, making it incredibly fertile and productive. He waved his hands to make the weather perpetually beautiful and bountiful. Whether through divine intervention or natural fortune, Shalazar is indeed blessed with rich farmland and favorable growing conditions from this point forward.

---

## THE FIRST GREENWARDENS (2,970 - 2,800 BW)

### **~2,970 - 2,930 BW — The First Greenwarden Circles**

The earliest Greenwardens unify village rites: planting vows, harvest tithes, guest-right, mediation practice, and seasonal festival circuits that keep distant villages culturally synchronized. Unlike elven clergy (organized into formal hierarchy with the Cundo'elda and Cundo'dori), Greenwardens operate as a loose network—each village has its own Greenwarden(s), and they gather occasionally for regional festivals but resist centralized authority.

The Greenwardens wear simple robes in earth tones—greens, browns, and golds—decorated with wheat sheaves, flowers, and other natural symbols. Many carry wooden walking sticks carved with family crests and blessed by Hinterhin.

### **~2,930 - 2,900 BW — The Field Laws**

Village justice becomes standardized: disputes resolved by restitution, labor, and oath-repair rather than imprisonment or execution. Halflings become known (even to outsiders) as difficult to rule, because they rule themselves. The Field Laws codify practices that had developed over millennia, giving them divine sanction through Hinterhin's blessing.

The Greenwardens serve as both spiritual and judicial leaders: spiritual guides leading festivals, blessing harvests, and performing marriages and naming ceremonies; community leaders mediating disputes, organizing communal work, and ensuring village needs are met; agricultural experts teaching farming techniques, predicting weather, and blessing fields; and healers tending to the sick and injured using herbal remedies and divine magic.

### **~2,900 BW — The Festival Web**

A dense calendar of local and shared holy days develops—dozens per year—aligned with agricultural cycles, reinforcing identity without central government. Halflings celebrate more holidays than any other race in Mazariim, observing over three dozen throughout the year.

The major holidays crystallize during this period: First Planting (1 Quarz/January 1) blessing seeds and planting first crops; Midsummer Feast (21 Hin/July 21) celebrating the longest day; Harvest Home (1 Zuel/October 1) marking the first major harvest; Festival of Lanterns (30 Mord/December 30) lighting the longest night; and Hearthfire Day (15 Gyru/June 15) celebrating community and family.

## THE AGE OF WISDOM (2,800 - 2,000 BW)

### **~2,800 BW — The Seed Codices (Oral)**

Knowledge about crops, irrigation, soil rotation, and herbal remedies becomes standardized through memorized "seed songs." These are not written texts but oral traditions passed through generations of Greenwardens—call-and-response teaching that encodes practical agricultural knowledge in memorable verse. A trained Greenwarden can recite thousands of lines covering every aspect of farming, from when to plant each crop to how to treat common diseases.

### **~2,600 BW — The Hearth Courts**

Greenwarden-led dispute resolution becomes a recognized institution even among non-halfling neighbors; outsiders sometimes request Hin mediators. The halfling reputation for fairness, patience, and practical wisdom makes them valuable neutral parties in conflicts between larger powers. Elven valley communities and human traders alike learn to trust Greenwarden judgment.

### **~2,400 BW — The Guest-Right Doctrine**

Hospitality becomes halfling moral law: feed the traveler, but never endanger the winter stores; protect the guest, but never enable the thief. The Guest-Right Doctrine balances generosity with prudence, reflecting halfling values of community and survival. Hinterhin's teachings emphasize that "the traveler at your door may be Hinterhin himself in disguise"—a reminder that hospitality is sacred duty.

### **~2,200 BW — The Herbwise Codification**

The ancient Herbwise Tradition (dating to ~5,100 BW) receives divine sanction and systematic organization. Greenwarden healers compile oral knowledge into comprehensive training programs, creating a class of halfling healers whose expertise rivals the best elven and human physicians. Unlike arcane or divine healing magic, Herbwise healing relies primarily on practical medicine—knowledge of plants, anatomy, and treatment that any dedicated student can learn.

---

## THE AGE OF TESTING (2,000 - 1,400 BW)

### **~2,000 BW — The Lean Generations**

A sequence of hard years tests halfling institutions. Villages that violate the Field Laws fracture; those that keep oaths endure, reinforcing the culture's deep hostility to hoarding and petty tyranny. The Lean Generations prove that Hinterhin's blessing does not guarantee prosperity—it provides tools and guidance, but halflings must still work together to survive.

### **~1,900 BW — The Border Bargains**

Hin negotiate survival alongside stronger neighbors through treaties and careful trade rather than conquest. As human civilizations expand and compete (Azala founded in jungle regions, Sabonaar culture developing along coasts), halflings position themselves as useful partners rather than valuable targets. The Border Bargains establish patterns of halfling-human relations that persist into the modern era.

### **~1,700 BW — The Sling and the Plow**

Village defense becomes formal militia duty: protect fields, escort travelers, and defend storehouses. Every adult halfling receives basic training in sling and knife, but the emphasis remains defensive. Halfling military doctrine focuses on making invasion unprofitable rather than winning battles—hidden stores, evacuated villages, and guerrilla resistance ensure that any aggressor pays more than they gain.

---

## THE AGE OF ARRIVALS (1,866 - 1,377 BW)

### **1,866 BW — New Roads, New Fears**

As wider-world contacts intensify (including the cosmic arrival of Mordrion and Zuel from Oerth), Hin culture doubles down on resilience: keep the seed stock pure, keep the oaths clean, keep the routes hidden. News of gods arriving from other universes filters through elven and dwarven contacts; halflings respond not with theological crisis but practical preparation. Whatever threatens the wider world may eventually threaten Shalazar—best to be ready.

### **~1,600 BW — The Market Lanterns**

The "Festival of Lanterns" tradition spreads as both navigation aid and cultural marker: if you see lanterns at the hedges, the Hin are here. What began as practical lighting for winter market-circuits becomes religious observance, honoring the longest night and guiding Hinterhin's blessings into the new year. The Festival of Lanterns will become one of the four major holidays in Hinterhin's calendar.

### **1,414 BW — Dragons and Distant War**

As draconic and planar stories spread (Paladine the Platinum Dragon arrives from Krynn, introducing paladin traditions to Mazariim), halflings adapt by strengthening refuge plans and stockpiles rather than fielding armies. Some halflings take interest in Paladine's teachings about protection and justice, but formal paladin traditions never take root in halfling communities—the code is too rigid, the lifestyle too martial for Hin temperament.

### **1,377 BW — The Bitter Songs**

Tales of dark elves and deeper shadows filter into Shalazar with the arrival of Eilistraee and drow refugees from Faerun. Hin respond with secrecy, solidarity, and travel warnings. The Bitter Songs—ballads about treachery, hidden enemies, and the importance of vigilance—enter the halfling oral tradition, reminding each generation that not all who smile mean well.

---

## THE AGE OF SHADOWS (1,400 - 400 BW)

### **1,400 - 1,000 BW — The Watchful Harvests**

Hin villages maintain hidden routes and refuge caches. Halfling heroes of this period are scouts, healers, and negotiators—those who prevent conflicts or mitigate their damage rather than those who win battles. As elves monitor the growing threat of Zuel's orc horde on distant Nigal, halflings quietly prepare for whatever crisis may come.

### **1,000 - 500 BW — The Long Vigil**

The culture's suspicion of necromancy and hoarding intensifies, reinforced by horror stories carried by traders. Zebulba's death cults, which periodically emerge among other races, find no foothold in halfling communities—the Hin reject any power that requires taking from others or defying natural cycles of life and death. Hinterhin's teachings about the sacredness of harvest and natural abundance directly contradict necromantic philosophy.

### **~699 BW — The Xannith Warning**

When Xannith awakens as entity of greed, halfling Greenwardens recognize an immediate threat to their values. Xannith's teachings—that wealth should be hoarded, that exploitation is virtue, that the strong should dominate the weak—represent everything halflings oppose. Greenwardens begin explicitly warning against Xannith's influence, though the entity's formal cult rarely targets halfling communities (too poor, too communal, too resistant to exploitation).

### **400 - 4 BW — The Countdown**

The Hin quietly prepare for storm-years: seed reserves, evacuation paths, and treaties renewed. As the wider world teeters toward war, halflings stockpile food, identify hiding places, and coordinate with elven allies. The Greenwardens organize preparation without creating panic—a balance of vigilance and normalcy that reflects Hinterhin's practical wisdom.

### **374 BW — Polyvin Awakens**

A second halfling deity awakens: Polyvin, Lord of Fortune, patron of luck, travel, merchants, and second chances. Unlike Hinterhin (who represents hearth, home, and harvest), Polyvin represents the halfling spirit of adventure—the traders, the travelers, the risk-takers who venture beyond village borders.

Polyvin awakens during an era of economic hardship caused by Xannith's corrupting influence on regional commerce. Where Xannith teaches that wealth should be hoarded and the poor exploited, Polyvin teaches that fortune favors the bold, that prosperity shared is prosperity multiplied, and that everyone deserves a fair chance. His message resonates powerfully with halflings, who have always believed in luck and community.

Within decades of his awakening, Polyvin converts many of Xannith's followers—those who pursued wealth but still possessed a conscience. His temples (called Fortune Halls) appear in cities across Mazariim, offering not predatory loans but honest partnerships, not exploitation but opportunity. Halfling merchants and travelers especially embrace Polyvin, finding in him a patron for the outward-facing aspects of Hin culture that Hinterhin's agricultural focus does not fully address.

The two halfling deities complement each other perfectly: Hinterhin for the settled life of farm and family, Polyvin for the wandering life of trade and adventure. Most halflings honor both, emphasizing one or the other depending on their circumstances and calling.

---

## THE WAR TO END ALL WARS (3-1 BW)

### **3 BW — The Fields of Refuge**

Hinterhin's faith is remembered as saving countless refugees through hidden convoys and blessed harvests. Halflings become the logistics backbone of survival: food, routes, and shelter. When Zuel's million-strong horde erupts from mountains across Nigal, devastating human and dwarven civilizations, halflings on distant Shalazar face a choice—hide and hope the storm passes, or help those who flee.

They choose to help. Hinterhin blesses the fields of Shalazar and surrounding regions to produce far beyond their natural capacity, feeding millions of refugees. Halfling farmers work day and night to harvest and distribute food. Greenwardens organize massive relief efforts, coordinating supply lines across hundreds of miles.

Additionally, Hinterhin uses his influence over weather and luck to hide refugee convoys from orcish scouts, guiding them to safety through fog and misdirection. While other entities fight with sword and spell, Hinterhin fights with bread and sanctuary. The war proves that nurturing and protecting life can be just as heroic as battle.

Polyvin's followers contribute to the war effort through logistics and trade. They organize supply lines, negotiate truces, help refugees find safe passage, and ensure that soldiers have food and equipment. They do not fight—that is not their way—but they enable those who do.

### **2 BW — The Greenwarden Networks**

Village-to-village coordination becomes militarily relevant: information relays, safehouses, and rationing protocols. The same systems that halflings developed over millennia for mutual aid and trade prove invaluable for wartime logistics. Greenwarden networks move refugees, supplies, and intelligence faster than formal military structures can match.

Halfling scouts—trained in concealment and terrain knowledge since the Sling Schools era—provide reconnaissance that saves thousands of lives. They cannot fight orc warbands directly, but they can ensure that orc movements are tracked and refugees warned in time to flee.

### **1 BW — The Last Winter Stores**

The war's end is remembered less as a battlefield victory and more as "the winter we survived." When Demious Stoneshaper shatters Zuel's avatar at the Battle of Fort Hope, halflings celebrate not with triumphant songs but with quiet thanksgiving—another harvest, another season, another year of life. The Last Winter Stores become a metaphor for halfling resilience: survival through preparation, community, and faith.

# Halfling History: The Modern Era
## Post-War Recovery & Current Times (1 AW - 842 AW)

---

## AFTERMATH OF WAR (1 AW - 101 AW)

### **1-10 AW — The Years of Rebuilding**

Hin communities recover through replanting, refugee resettlement, and rebuilding quiet routes. The Greenwardens increasingly serve as judges in mixed communities where post-war law is unstable. Halfling expertise in agriculture, food preservation, and community organization proves invaluable for rebuilding devastated regions.

Many refugees who sheltered in halfling communities during the war choose to stay, creating the first significant non-halfling populations in Shalazar. These immigrants are welcomed according to Guest-Right tradition, gradually integrating into halfling society while maintaining their own cultural identities. The post-war period establishes Shalazar as a place of refuge and recovery.

### **11-50 AW — The Spreading Hearths**

Halflings begin traveling beyond Shalazar in greater numbers, settling in human and elven lands, opening inns and farms, and sharing their agricultural expertise. Where pre-war halfling communities outside Shalazar were rare, post-war communities become common—small halfling neighborhoods appear in most major cities, and halfling-run inns and farms dot the countryside.

Hinterhin's worship spreads with his people. Temples and shrines (modest, welcoming structures with community kitchens and gathering halls) appear wherever halflings settle. Non-halflings, especially farmers and those who value community, begin honoring Hinterhin alongside their own deities.

Polyvin's Fortune Halls play crucial roles in post-war economic recovery. They offer low-interest loans to rebuild businesses, help displaced people find new homes, and facilitate trade between formerly warring nations. Polyvin's philosophy—that prosperity shared is prosperity multiplied—proves correct. Nations that embrace his teachings recover faster than those that cling to hoarding.

### **51-101 AW — The Generation Turns**

By 101 AW, the war generation passes leadership to those born after the conflict. Halfling communities maintain oral traditions about the war—the heroes, the hardships, the importance of preparation—but daily life returns to normal rhythms of planting, harvest, and festival.

When Zuel and Mordrion's banishment ends in 101 AW, allowing them to manifest avatars again, halflings respond with quiet vigilance rather than panic. Seed stores are checked, evacuation routes reviewed, and Greenwarden networks tested—but no crisis materializes. Zuel does not immediately manifest, and life continues.

---

## RECOVERY & INTEGRATION (102 AW - 400 AW)

### **102-200 AW — The New Normal**

Halflings become culturally central far beyond their numbers: orchard-making, soil restoration, food preservation, and conflict mediation. Halfling expertise commands respect in every nation, and "call a halfling" becomes common advice for agricultural problems, kitchen emergencies, or community disputes.

The diaspora population grows steadily. By 200 AW, approximately 15,000-20,000 halflings live permanently outside Shalazar—small compared to Shalazar's 50,000+, but significant for a people who rarely traveled before the war. These diaspora communities maintain connections to Shalazar through the Walking Market tradition (now spanning continents) and regular pilgrimage to major festivals.

### **201-400 AW — The Long Peace**

The longest sustained peace in recorded history brings prosperity to halfling communities. Population in Shalazar reaches approximately 65,000 by 400 AW, with another 30,000+ in diaspora communities. Agricultural techniques continue improving; Greenwarden healers achieve recognition as among the finest physicians in Mazariim; halfling inns become famous for hospitality and comfort.

Polyvin's influence grows steadily during this period. His Fortune Halls become centers of community life, offering not just worship but practical help—financial advice, job placement, dispute mediation, and traveler's aid. The Free Traders' Guild, a coalition of Polyvin-worshiping merchants who oppose monopolies and advocate for fair trade, becomes a significant economic force.

---

## THE MODERN ERA (400 AW - 842 AW)

### **400-800 AW — Prosperity and Complacency**

With the world's later crises, Hin communities emphasize resilience: keep the seed, keep the oath, keep the hearth. But four centuries of peace breed complacency. Military readiness declines. Young halflings grow up knowing war only as grandparents' stories. The careful preparations that defined pre-war culture become traditions rather than necessities.

Shalazar's population reaches approximately 75,000 by 800 AW, with diaspora communities totaling another 40,000+. Halflings remain respected for their agricultural expertise, hospitality, and mediation skills, but they are also seen as quaint, rustic, perhaps irrelevant to the great affairs of nations.

### **811 AW — The Orceri Incursion**

The invasion of Orceri (orcs from an alternate timeline) shatters the Long Peace. Halfling communities are not primary targets, but the crisis reminds everyone—including halflings themselves—that threats can emerge suddenly and devastatingly.

Halfling response follows traditional patterns: food production increases, refugee networks activate, Greenwarden healers deploy to assist allied forces. But the crisis also reveals how much has been forgotten. Young halflings must learn emergency protocols their great-grandparents knew by heart. The Orceri Incursion serves as wake-up call.

### **815 AW — The Rebirth of Akovar**

The discovery of Akovar and freeing of Asti sends magical shockwaves across all planes. Halfling druids report disturbances in natural cycles; Greenwardens experience unusual weather patterns; something fundamental has changed in the world's magical structure. The ley-line network's reawakening affects Shalazar's blessed fertility in ways not yet fully understood.

### **819 AW — The Elemental Crisis**

Noctuminalis's Elemental Crisis brings earthquakes, floods, and storms to regions across Mazariim. Halfling communities suffer damage but respond with characteristic resilience—mutual aid, emergency food distribution, rapid rebuilding. The crisis reinforces traditional values of preparation and community cooperation.

### **832 AW — Dual Crises**

The Shadow Realm crisis and zombie plague in the Lands of Honor occur far from Shalazar, but Greenwarden networks provide support—supplies, healing expertise, and refugee assistance. Halfling involvement is modest but meaningful, demonstrating that Hin values extend beyond their own communities.

### **835-838 AW — Ley-Line Development**

The Astral Circle's creation of mortal-crafted ley-lines interests halfling druids, who see potential for enhancing agricultural productivity and stabilizing weather patterns. Greenwarden representatives participate in research efforts, contributing expertise in natural cycles and ecological balance.

The Fire & Ice crisis in Chumbawamba (838 AW) reveals dangers of ley-line expansion—the network's magical energies can revitalize dormant threats. Halfling advisers advocate for careful surveying and gradual implementation, reflecting traditional Hin caution about rapid change.

### **839 AW — Gods of the Fall**

The proto-entity crisis threatens all of Mazariim, including halfling communities. When five entities crash into Varkonan lands and Khemset seeks to drain all magic from the world, halflings face potential catastrophe—their agricultural bounty depends partly on ambient magic and Hinterhin's blessing. If magic fails, Shalazar's fertility might fail with it.

Halfling contributions to the global response follow traditional patterns: food supply, logistics, healing support, and refugee assistance. Some halfling scouts and adventurers participate directly in the quest to collect Machine of Gyru parts, but most Hin support the effort from home—keeping society functioning while heroes save the world.

### **842 AW — The Coming of the Lines (Current)**

Noctuminalis's endgame—transforming Mazariim into a land of undead—represents everything halflings oppose. The shadow dragon's five simultaneous catastrophic plans threaten not just life but the natural cycles that define halfling existence. Undeath is anathema to harvest; permanent darkness would end agriculture; the destruction of divine patrons would orphan both Hinterhin and Polyvin's followers.

Halfling response to the crisis is total commitment within traditional limits. Food production maximizes. Refugee networks prepare for unprecedented displacement. Greenwarden healers deploy wherever needed. Polyvin's Fortune Halls coordinate economic resources for the war effort.

The crisis's resolution brings relief but also loss. Halfling casualties are proportionally modest (the Hin are not front-line fighters), but some communities suffer significant damage, and the trauma of near-apocalypse affects everyone. As 842 AW ends, halflings face uncertain future with hard-won wisdom: stay prepared, value community, and never assume peace is permanent.

## MILITARY SUMMARY (1 AW - 842 AW)

Hin military practice emphasizes militia defense, scouting, and logistics rather than standing armies. Slings and ambush defense remain culturally preferred. Halflings do not field formal military units in the sense that other races do; instead, every village maintains a militia capable of basic defense, and specialists (scouts, healers, logistics coordinators) train for specific support roles.

Total military-capable halflings (trained militia, active scouts, Greenwarden healers with combat-adjacent skills): approximately 8,000-10,000 across all communities. This represents a smaller percentage than most races, but halfling military doctrine does not require large numbers—the goal is making invasion unprofitable, not winning battles.

---

## CULTURAL SHIFTS (1 AW - 842 AW)

The key shifts are reputational: from "small folk under elven shadow" to "small folk who keep everyone fed." Before the war, halflings were often overlooked or patronized by larger races. After the war, they are respected for their contributions to survival and recovery.

Other significant shifts include increased mobility (diaspora communities now common worldwide), economic influence (Polyvin's Fortune Halls and Free Traders' Guild provide alternatives to exploitative commerce), and religious integration (both Hinterhin and Polyvin worshiped by non-halflings who appreciate their values).

Core halfling values remain consistent across all eras: community over individual, preparation over reaction, survival through cooperation, and celebration of life's simple blessings. The modern halfling is more traveled and more connected to the wider world than ancestors who rarely left their villages, but still cherishes the hearth, the harvest, and the company of family and friends.
